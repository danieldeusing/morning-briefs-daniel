Opus 4.8: the coding leap that counts

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.8 on 28.05. — just 41 days after 4.7, the fastest minor cadence the house has ever run. The pitch is tightly code-focused this time: better agentic coding chops, "high effort" as the default for hard tasks, and a safety claim that matters more to us developers than any benchmark digit.

The vendor-reported numbers: 88.6% SWE-bench Verified (4.7: 87.6%), 69.2% SWE-bench Pro (4.7: 64.3%), 74.6% Terminal-Bench 2.1 (version 2.1 — not directly comparable to 2.0 numbers, and competitors report the bench on different harnesses, so cross-model comparison is limited). Two independent runs (Vellum, llm-stats) reproduce the same numbers.[1][2] Pricing stays at $5 / $25 per 1M tokens — no upgrade surcharge, and fast mode is 3× cheaper than 4.7.

SWE-bench Verified vs. Pro: Both test whether a model fixes real GitHub bugs independently. "Verified" is the older, human-reviewed set — now suspected of leaking into training data (contamination). "Pro" is the newer, harder set with fresher tasks. Think of Verified as a test whose answers half-leaked: high scores, but tells less about real ability. That's why the Pro jump (64.3 → 69.2) is the truer story.

The "4× fewer mistakes" claim — and why it matters

Anthropic claims Opus 4.8 is "roughly four times less likely" than its predecessor to let its own code mistakes slip through uncommented.[3] This is not a benchmark, but a self-critique metric: does the model let obvious bugs in its own output pass? For agentic loops (model writes, reviews, self-corrects), this is more practically relevant than two percentage points on SWE-bench — an agent that can't see its own mistakes spins expensive error loops. Caveat: "4× fewer" is relative; without a base rate it says little. Wait to see if it holds in the wild.

SWE-bench Verified — take with care

The public Verified leaderboard leads (as of 28.05.) with Mythos Preview at 93.9%, ahead of Opus 4.8 (88.6%) and Opus 4.7 (87.6%).[4] Before we celebrate 90s: contamination debate swirls precisely on this board. A model with training exposure to Verified tasks shines here without generalizing better in reality. Lab numbers are vendor-reported and (except 4.8 via the reruns) not backed by independent measurement on the same board — treat the bar chart as a rough map, not gospel.

Mythos: the cyber model that's "soon" [5][6]

Along with Opus 4.8, Anthropic confirmed that Mythos — until now available only to a handful of partners — will ship more broadly "in the coming weeks". Mythos is the other story: a model whose strength is explicitly coding and cyber. In the preview (07.04.), it autonomously found a 16-year-old hole in FFmpeg's H.264 codec, chained Linux kernel bugs to full root access, and built a browser exploit chaining four vulnerabilities into a JIT heap spray that broke renderer and OS sandboxes.

Anthropic engineers without security training asked it "find an RCE" at night and had a working exploit by morning. That's precisely why it wasn't sold to paying customers at first, but given through Project Glasswing to defenders — Amazon, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Linux Foundation, Microsoft, Palo Alto Networks.

Heads up For you as a developer: when a model of this caliber becomes broadly available, the supply-chain balance tips. "Turn N-day into exploit" gets cheap — patch windows shrink. Dependency hygiene (SBOM, quick patching, fewer transitive deps) stops being compliance theater and becomes self-defense.

Harnesses & IDEs: what's moving

Cursor — Composer 2.5 + Auto-Review try it

Cursor rolled out Composer 2.5 on 18.05. (steadier on long tasks, follows complex instructions more reliably) and on 29.05. an Auto-Review run mode: shell/MCP/fetch calls on an allowlist run instantly, sandboxable ones in sandbox, the rest go to a classifier subagent. Why try it: fewer approval clicks on long agentic runs without blindly allowing everything — the middle ground that Claude Code's --dangerously-skip-permissions doesn't offer.

cursor.com

Claude Code — Dynamic Workflows

Research Preview: Claude plans a workflow and orchestrates it over ten to hundreds of subagents in the background, with built-in verification and saved progress across CLI/Desktop/VS Code. Small but useful: /simplify is now /code-review and reports correctness bugs at a chosen effort level.

Open Weights & Miscellany

Qwen3-Coder-Next local-ready

Notable for local setups: Qwen3-Coder-Next claims 70.6% SWE-bench Verified (SWE-Agent scaffold) at 256K context under permissive license.[8] As a MoE with 80B total parameters / only 3B active, it's fairly efficient; a single 24-GB GPU will only fit with aggressive 2–3-bit quantization and noticeable quality loss — at 4-bit, VRAM demand is closer to ~46 GB. This puts an open-weight model at a tier that a year ago required a cloud API. Worth a look on your Ollama setup — the gap to frontier closed models remains (88.6 vs 70.6), but for offline/privacy workloads this is the right class.
