Claude writes 80% of Anthropic's code

This is the story of the day — and it comes not as a product launch, but as an essay. On June 5, the Anthropic Institute published "When AI builds itself", and the hook speaks directly to us developers: Over 80% of the code merged into Anthropic's own codebase is now written by Claude.[1] For comparison, the lab cites "low single digits" at Claude Code's launch in February 2025. From ~2% to >80% in two years.

The second number is almost tangible: A typical Anthropic engineer merge in Q2 2026 writes around eight times as much code per day as in 2024.[1][2] And Anthropic pushes further: In real Claude Code sessions, their best model (Myth Preview) made the better choice 64% of the time compared to humans during deliberately hard decision moments in April 2026 — back in November 2025 it was still 51%.[1]

Recursive Self-Improvement (RSI): AI building AI. Picture a carpenter who doesn't just build furniture, but trains a better apprentice carpenter, who in turn trains an even better one. Anthropic's definition: "an AI system that completely autonomously designs and develops its own successor". Important: Anthropic itself says they're not there yet — but closer than most institutions are prepared for.

And then the twist that makes headlines: Anthropic calls for a option to globally pause frontier development — "we believe it would be good for the world to have the option to slow or temporarily pause frontier AI development", they would themselves pause "if other developers at or near the frontier also did so in a verifiable manner".[1][3]

Skepticism filter: what the 80% really means

My read, which Gary Marcus shares clearly: The framing is overblown. Marcus calls the essay a "bait and switch" — Anthropic tries "to strike terror into everyone's hearts… but all they have really shown is just faster coding — entirely under human control". His key point: "The results in the blog are about RSI, not AGI."[4]

Three sober caveats on the 80% figure before it lands somewhere as "AI replaces developers":

Skepticism Filter First — what counts as "authored"? Anthropic doesn't define this sharply in the essay. If every line of Claude code accepted by a human counts as "written by Claude", 80% is less spectacular: that's steered pair programming, not autonomous coding. Second — the loop runs with humans in the loop. Every merge is reviewed and approved by an engineer; that's the definition of "under human control". Third — timing. Framing your own progress as a precursor to RSI and calling for a pause button is also positioning: it makes Anthropic both the frontier player and the responsible voice at once.

What remains when you strip away the hype framing is still real and relevant for Daniel: A frontier lab runs much of its own development with its own coding agent. That's the most honest available statement about how far agentic coding really carries in a top engineering team — not in demos, but in production.

The model behind it: Claude Opus 4.8

Who writes 80% of the code is concretely: Claude Opus 4.8, released May 28.[5] Yesterday's Build Brief pivoted to Microsoft's MAI-Code-1-Flash; Opus 4.8 got somewhat buried, but belongs exactly here because it's the model doing the work in the RSI essay.

The numbers, plainly: 1M-token context (API/Bedrock/Vertex; 200K on Microsoft Foundry), 128K output, price unchanged at $5 / $25 per million input/output tokens.[6] No price jump despite better coding metrics — that's the relevant news for cost math.

Claude Opus 4.8 — Coding Benchmarks (vendor/secondary sources, as of 06/06/2026)

On SWE-Bench Verified, Opus 4.8 reports 88.6% (Opus 4.7: 87.6%) — a gain of just one point. On the harder SWE-Bench Pro, the jump is bigger: 69.2% versus 64.3% on 4.7.[7] That's exactly the point:

SWE-Bench Verified vs. Pro: Both are collections of real GitHub bugs where the model must write the patch itself that makes the tests green — like a mechanic fixing a stranger's car and having to pass the test drive at the end. Verified is the older, easier collection; Pro is newer, harder, and less "burned" into training. When one benchmark is nearly full (everyone near 90%), it says nothing about differences anymore — then the harder one counts.
Bench skepticism SWE-Bench Verified visibly saturates: Opus 4.8 at 88.6%, Anthropic's Myth Preview leads the leaderboard already at 93.9%.[8] From here on, these are mostly vendor numbers; Artificial Analysis's independent coding index loads its table via JavaScript, a clean fetch yielded only the top row (GPT-5.5 xhigh = 59), and Opus 4.8 couldn't be isolated from that.[9] So: the values are plausible and consistent across two secondary sources, but I'd only treat SWE-Bench Pro as independently measured.

Simon Willison sums Opus 4.8 as "a modest but tangible improvement" — and highlights something more important for agentic work than any bench point: 4.8 is more honest, flags uncertainty more often, and makes fewer unfounded claims.[6] For long autonomous runs (where overconfident-false models get expensive), that's the more relevant improvement.

Cursor 3.7: Design Mode in the browser try

A day after 3.6 (Auto-review, in the 06/05 brief), Cursor shipped 3.7 on June 5.[10] The highlight is Design Mode in the Cursor browser: You click two or more UI elements directly in the rendered browser, and Cursor sees "the selected elements, their code, the surrounding layout, and the visual relationships on the page" — edits can be clicked, drawn, or dictated via voice.

Less exciting for backend folks, but the SDK update (06/04) is: Custom Tools, Auto-review routing, and nested subagents plus flexible storage options (JSONL/Custom Store). If you're building your own agents on Cursor's harness, you can now nest subagents in subagents — the same orchestrator pattern GitHub's Agent HQ and Claude Code already use.

Why try it If you're building UI: Design Mode is the first browser-native "show don't tell" workflow that reads the code context, instead of just guessing from a screenshot. Skip it for pure API work.

It's Saturday, June 6th. Today itself is light on news, but yesterday Anthropic published the most interesting claim for developers all year: Claude now writes the lion's share of Anthropic's own code.

The main story is an essay titled "When AI builds itself". In it, Anthropic says more than eighty percent of code flowing into its own codebase is written by Claude. I read that skeptically, and I'm not alone. The well-known critic Gary Marcus calls the essay a bait and switch. Three sober points: First, it's unclear what exactly counts as written by Claude. Second, a human reviews and merges every line. Third, it's also positioning to present yourself simultaneously as the frontrunner and the responsible voice. What remains is still an honest data point about how far agentic coding really carries in a top-tier team.

The model doing this work is Claude Opus four point eight, released May twenty-eighth. It has a context window of one million tokens and costs the same five dollars per million input tokens and twenty-five for output. On the test SWE-Bench Verified, it reaches eighty-eight point six percent, but this test is nearly maxed out and says little anymore. More meaningful is the tougher SWE-Bench Pro at sixty-nine point two percent. Simon Willison calls the model a modest but tangible improvement and highlights that it's more honest and less likely to make unsubstantiated claims. For long autonomous runs, that's more valuable than any benchmark point.

My bottom line: The eighty-percent claim is inflated as a headline, but useful as a data point. It shows that the pattern of agent writes, human reviews and merges already scales.
