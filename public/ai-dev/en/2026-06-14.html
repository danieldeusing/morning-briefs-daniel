US Export Control: Anthropic Shuts Down Fable 5 + Mythos 5 for Everyone [1][2][3]

The biggest AI-Dev story of the day: On June 12, the US government invoked national security and issued an export control directive prohibiting access to Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5. Anthropic immediately disabled both models for all customers—because the company cannot separate Foreign Nationals from US users in real time, and the directive locks out every non-US citizen, whether inside or outside the US, including its own foreign staff.[1]

The kicker: Fable 5 had launched just three days earlier (June 9) and seized the SWE-Bench-Verified crown at 95.0%.[3] As of June 14, the ban remains in place; Claude Code falls back to Opus 4.8. All other Anthropic models remain unaffected per Anthropic.[1]

Direct impact on you You're a Foreign National in Brazil—the directive blocks Fable 5 for every non-US citizen. Even if the shutdown were lifted tomorrow, you couldn't access Fable 5 right now. Practically speaking, little changes in your Claude Code routine: the harness runs on Opus 4.8 again, the model you were already shipping on.
Export-control suspension: US export controls govern which entities a US company can "export" certain technologies to—and "export" here means not just physical shipment overseas, but also mere access by a foreign national, even on US soil. A directive can force a company to kill a product overnight. Since Anthropic can't reliably filter an API call by citizenship, the only option was the hard stop: off for everyone, rather than off for some.

Why the shutdown: Anthropic's read is that the government suspects a method to circumvent or "jailbreak" Fable 5.[1] Anthropic publicly disputes this—a narrow, potential jailbreak doesn't justify a full recall of a frontier model.[2] How long the ban holds is unclear; for now, the nominally best coding model of the week is simply unavailable.

Skeptic reflex: The 95.0% on SWE-Bench Verified (human-curated, thus more robust than vendor slides) remains historically noteworthy—but useless as a buying signal while the model is locked. And it was never "solves 19 of your 20 tickets": public benchmark tasks carry contamination risk, and 95% on the leaderboard isn't 95% in your monorepo. The real lesson today is different—a top-tier model can vanish overnight on regulatory grounds, which makes heavy reliance on a single frontier model a liability.

For context, what was withdrawn: Fable 5 priced at $10 per 1M input tokens and $50 per 1M output tokens (Anthropic said "less than half" the Mythos preview), with 1M token context and 128K token max output; in Claude Code it carried the ID claude-fable-5 and arrived with v2.1.170—both now disabled.[1]

Claude Code: Sub-agents up to 5 levels, new lifecycle hooks [4]

Surrounding the Fable 5 launch, Claude Code got several releases. v2.1.169 brought a /cd command for switching working directories mid-session, a --safe-mode, a post-session lifecycle hook for self-hosted runners, and MCP-policy enforcement.[4]

v2.1.172 followed up: nested sub-agents up to 5 levels deep and a plugin marketplace search.[4] Practically for you: deeper sub-agent chains mean an orchestrator-agent can spawn agents that themselves delegate—useful for tiered research/build pipelines, but watch the token costs; they multiply per tier.

Gemini CLI & Code Assist: Free/Pro access ends June 18 [5][6]

This is the story that demands a decision from you today. Google is sunset-ing the consumer tiers of Gemini Code Assist and the Gemini CLI on June 18, 2026: the IDE extensions will stop serving requests for "Gemini Code Assist for individuals."[5]

Affected: the free tier (individuals), Google AI Pro, and Google AI Ultra—including Gemini-CLI usage. Unaffected: anyone with a Gemini Code Assist Standard or Enterprise subscription keeps full IDE extension and CLI access.[6]

Migration path: Google points to the Antigravity CLI, unveiled at Google I/O on May 19—a Go-based command-line tool plus SDK for custom agents, powered by Gemini 3.5 Flash. Antigravity 2.0 comes with new tiers (AI Ultra $100/month, Premium $200/month, down from $250).[6]

What to do If you're using Gemini CLI free or via AI Pro/Ultra: you have until 06-18. Either switch to Antigravity CLI, review a Standard/Enterprise subscription—or use this break as a moment to consolidate your coding agents anyway (Claude Code, Cursor, OpenCode keep running). If Gemini never seriously factored into your loop, ignore this.

Pure Google domain (model/product strategy) belongs in ai-usecases; here only the dev-tool consequence.

Kimi K2.7 Code—open coding model, vendor-hyped [7]

Moonshot AI released Kimi K2.7 Code on June 12—with open weights under Modified-MIT license, so downloadable and self-hostable. Architecture: 1 trillion parameters as a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), 32 billion active per token, 384 experts, 256K context window.[7]

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE): Like a hospital with many specialists—when a question arrives, only the two or three relevant experts wake up, not the whole staff. The model has 1 trillion "parameters" total, but computes only 32 billion per token. Upshot: lots of knowledge in memory, manageable compute per response.

Via the public API it costs $0.95 / 1M input and $4.00 / 1M output (cached input $0.19)—roughly a tenth of Fable 5's output price.[7] Moonshot also reports ~30% fewer reasoning tokens than K2.6.

Skeptic reflex (critical here): All headline figures are at launch first-party, i.e., measured by the vendor itself—independent results are pending. Moonshot reports +21.8% on "Kimi Code Bench v2" (62.0 vs. 50.9), +31.5% on MLS Bench Lite, and claims to beat Opus 4.8 on "MCP Mark Verified" (81.1 vs. 76.4).[7] Some of these are vendor-owned benchmarks—treat them as marketing until third-party measurements confirm them. On independent leaderboards (SWE-Bench Verified, LiveCodeBench), K2.7 Code is not yet listed as of June 14.

try Why still worth a look: open weights + rock-bottom API pricing + long context make it an interesting local/cheap fallback alongside Claude Code—especially for bulk tasks where output costs dominate. Calibrate expectations until independent numbers arrive.

Benchmark landscape: two boards, two winners [8][9][10]

Outside SWE-Bench Verified, the picture is mixed—which is exactly the point: which model "leads" depends heavily on which benchmark.

WebDev Arena (humans vote anonymously between two web-app outputs): as of 05-24 with 328,594 votes across 81 models, claude-opus-4-7-thinking leads with 1567 Elo, closely followed by claude-opus-4-7 (1562) and claude-opus-4-6-thinking (1542). Qwen3.7-max sits at #4 with 1541, GPT-5.5 only at ~1505 (#11).[8][9]

LiveCodeBench v6 (less contamination-prone due to constant fresh competition tasks): as of 06-14, Qwen3.7 Max is out front with ~91.6%, then Kimi K2.6 (89.6%), Seed 2.0 Pro (87.8%). Claude Opus 4.8 is not in the top 10 here (Fable 5 wasn't even listed yet and has been locked since 06-12).[10]

Elo (Arena): the same score as chess. Each model duel where humans pick the better output shifts points; models that win more rise. It measures felt quality in head-to-head, not an objective pass rate—so a model can lead on the Arena but rank low on SWE-Bench.

Reading: Anthropic dominates on "fix real bugs" (SWE-Bench) and "web-app feel" (Arena), while Alibaba/Qwen and open models dominate fresh algorithm tasks (LiveCodeBench). With the Fable 5/Mythos ban, however, SWE-Bench's top performer drops as a usable option—of available Anthropic models, Opus 4.8 now leads (88.6%). No board is the whole truth—and aggregator sources (llm-stats, benchlm) supply data, not the raw measurement; the ~1505 for GPT-5.5 is an estimate.
