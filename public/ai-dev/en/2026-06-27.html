Anthropic shuts down Fable 5 + Mythos 5 worldwide [1][2]

The biggest move this month isn't a release — it's a withdrawal. On 12.06. at 17:21 ET, Anthropic received a US export directive and reacted immediately: both top models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, were disabled for all customers — "to ensure compliance," in Anthropic's own words.[1] This is Anthropic's own product; I'm reporting it as neutrally as possible because the scope is independent of ownership.

The directive targets use "by any foreign national, whether within or outside the US, including foreign Anthropic employees," according to Anthropic.[2] Since model access can't be cleanly filtered by nationality, the only compliance answer was: global shutdown. Anthropic cites a "narrow, non-universal jailbreak" — getting the model to read a codebase and fix vulnerabilities — as the trigger and publicly disputes whether that justifies a recall of a commercial model.[1]

Export-control directive: A government order that prohibits the export of certain technology to certain recipients — like a ban on shipping advanced tech. Here it hit software that isn't physically shipped but accessed via API — so all access had to go offline.

What this means for you: All other Claude models (Opus 4.8, Sonnet, Haiku, Claude Code) continue running unchanged — Anthropic emphasizes this explicitly.[3] But it's the sharpest possible lesson in vendor lock-in: the two models at the very top of the independent SWE-Bench Verified list (Mythos 5 95.5 %, Fable 5 95.0 %) are inaccessible overnight — not from a bug or price, but from a government order.[4] If you've pinned your agent workflow to a frontier model, you should have a fallback path by now.

GLM-5.2: the self-hostable bridge [5][6][7]

When a frontier model can disappear overnight, vendor independence shifts from a nice idea to insurance. That's exactly where GLM-5.2 from Zhipu/Z.ai lands at the right moment: API since 13.06., weights since ~17.06. on HuggingFace — under MIT license with no regional restrictions.[5] You can self-host it without anyone pulling the plug.

Architecture: 753B parameters total, ~40B active per token (MoE), with 1M-token context window.[5]

MoE (Mixture of Experts): Instead of running the whole giant model for every word, it's split into many "experts" and only a small fraction activates per token — like a large office where each task only brings the two relevant colleagues to their feet instead of all hundred. Result: the punch of a 753B model with the compute cost closer to ~40B.

On benchmarks, GLM-5.2 is surprisingly close to Opus 4.8, per Z.ai's blog: SWE-Bench Pro 62.1, FrontierSWE 74.4 (Opus 4.8: 75.1), Terminal-Bench 2.1 81.0 (Opus 4.8: 85.0).[5] Important caveat: the Opus comparison numbers come from Z.ai's own blog. Independently confirmed is the #1 ranking among open-weights on the Artificial-Analysis-Intelligence Index (score 51) — that's a vendor-neutral measurement.[6] Day-1 integration into Claude Code, Cline, OpenCode, Roo Code, Goose, Crush, and Kilo Code.[7] Try this Want a frontier fallback with zero vendor risk: GLM-5.2 is today's most plausible self-hostable choice — worth the read.

MAI-Code-1-Flash for Copilot is GA [8][9]

As of 26.06., MAI-Code-1-Flash — Microsoft's in-house coding model — is generally available for GitHub Copilot Business + Enterprise.[8] It's not positioned as a new frontier heavyweight, but as a cheap, fast workhorse for Copilot's agentic workflows, metered by consumption.

Microsoft positions it explicitly against Claude Haiku 4.5: vendor numbers cite +16 points on SWE-Bench Pro (51.2 % vs. 35.2 %), +28.9 on IF Bench, and "up to 60 % fewer tokens" on SWE-Bench Verified — at just 5B parameters by secondary sources.[9] All these numbers are Microsoft-reported; an independent SWE-Bench Verified entry is missing. The token efficiency is the real pitch: if you run large agent sweeps, you pay per token, not per score.

Context: Microsoft now controls another standard model within its own stack — as with Copilot's shift from Premium Request Units to "GitHub AI Credits" on 01.06., platform-pricing lock-in grows, not shrinks.

Cursor swallows Continue — export data by 15.07. [10][11]

Cursor has acquired Continue (announced ~22.06.) — and shut the product down. It's an acqui-hire: the team moves to Cursor, the open-source repo (`continuedev/continue`, 34K stars) goes read-only with final release v2.0.0.[10] Continue users have until 15.07. to export their data — then it's over.[11]

Quirky detail: Cursor itself is set to be acquired by SpaceX in an announced ~60B$ deal (expected to close Q3 2026) — so Continue would pass to SpaceX too.[10] For developers: if Continue is wired into a workflow, export now and migrate to an actively maintained path — Cline and Kilo Code are the obvious open-source successors.

Pattern of the week Three of the four big stories are consolidation or withdrawal — Anthropic pulls models, Microsoft builds its own stack, Cursor/SpaceX buys out an open-source competitor. The only movement toward more options comes from China (GLM-5.2, Kimi). That's the real story of the month.

Kimi K2.7-Code: strong claims, independent unconfirmed [12][13]

Moonshot AI released Kimi K2.7-Code on 12.06.: 1T parameters total, 32B active per token (384 experts, 256K context), under Modified MIT on HuggingFace (~595 GB weights).[12] On vendor-owned benchmarks it jumps significantly over K2.6: +21.8 % on Kimi Code Bench v2, +31.5 % on MLS Bench Lite; vendor-reported SWE-Bench Pro 58.6.[13]

Benchmark caution For Kimi K2.7-Code, there is (as of ~14.06.) no independent third-party entry on standard suites — SWE-Bench Verified, SWE-Bench Pro, Terminal-Bench, LiveCodeBench. The jumps cited are all self-measured. On vendor benchmarks (Kimi Code Bench, MLS Bench), training contamination is the standard risk. Treat the numbers as hypothesis, not measurement — until an independent entry surfaces.

It's Saturday, June twenty-seventh. The tooling side is quiet, but the model stack is in the middle of upheaval — and the biggest story of the month isn't a new model, it's a disappearing one.

On June twelfth, Anthropic shut down its two strongest coding models, Fable five and Mythos five, worldwide for all customers. The trigger wasn't a crash, but a US export directive that couldn't be filtered by nationality — so the only answer was to take both models completely offline. Anthropic disputes the order publicly but shut down for safety anyway. All other Claude models keep running. Still, it's the sharpest lesson in vendor lock-in you can imagine: the two models that ranked independently at the very top of the SWE-Bench list became unusable overnight.

The open-weights world provides the bridge. It's a Mixture-of-Experts model with over seven hundred billion parameters, but only around forty billion of them work per token. On the independent Artificial-Analysis Index, it's number one among open models. If you're looking for a fallback with zero vendor risk, you should look at it.

It's not a frontier heavyweight but a cheap, fast workhorse with supposedly just five billion parameters. Microsoft's benchmark numbers look good, but they're all self-measured — an independent entry is missing.
