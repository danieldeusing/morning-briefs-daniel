SWE-bench Verified: Anthropic takes the whole top [1]

The SWE-bench Verified leaderboard on llm-stats (page dated July 5, 2026) shows something you rarely see: the top five spots are all Anthropic. Claude Fable 5 leads with 95.0%, followed by Claude Mythos Preview (93.9%), Opus 4.8 (88.6%), Opus 4.7 (87.6%) and Sonnet 5 (85.2%). The best non-Anthropic entry in the top 10 is a tie between Gemini 3.1 Pro and DeepSeek-V4-Pro-Max at 80.6% each on rank 8, with MiniMax M3 closing out the field at 80.5%.

SWE-bench Verified: A test course of real, human-validated GitHub bugs. Like a driving test in real traffic instead of a practice lot — the model gets a bug report plus the repo and has to write a patch that makes the test suite pass. "Verified" means every task was manually checked for solvability.
Read skeptically A board full of models from a single vendor is a warning sign, not a victory. Vendor-adjacent scores always raise two questions: training contamination (SWE-bench tasks come from public repos — if they leak into training data, you're measuring memorization, not capability) and real-world transfer (95% on curated bugs doesn't mean 95% on Daniel's actual legacy code with half-documented dependencies). The ranking is plausible; the absolute numbers aren't gospel.

Two numbers on the board are explicitly not reliable. First, the name/score conflict around Mythos: the directly fetched llm-stats table lists "Mythos Preview" at 93.9%, while a second (unverified) secondary source claims "Mythos 5" at 95.5% — whether that's the same model under two names or two separate runs remains unclear; I'm using the directly checked figure here. Second, GPT-5.5 is missing entirely from the top 10, which pushes its real Verified score below the 80.5% cutoff — contradicting the circulating figures of 82.6% and 88.7%, which also contradict each other. The only reliable number for GPT-5.5 is 58.6% on the harder SWE-bench Pro (vellum.ai, vendor-reported) — don't confuse Pro with Verified.

Fable 5: gone 19 days, back globally since 07/01 [2]

Context for the bench lead: Fable 5 was blocked for non-US users from 06/12 to 06/30 — a US Commerce Department export-control directive following a reported jailbreak incident. The Commerce Department lifted the controls on 06/30, and since 07/01 Fable 5 has been back on the Claude API globally. That explains why its top spot looked "frozen" in the meantime. Pricing per multiple sources: $10/Mtok input, $50/Mtok output (with the usual 90% prompt-cache discount). For Pro/Max/Team, Fable counts toward up to 50% of the weekly limit through 07/07, after which it's usage-credits only — so in daily use it's a special-case model, not a default.

Timeline referenced via multiple outlets (Forbes/CNBC/VentureBeat); no vendor primary source directly fetched — read as context.

GitLab: the "AI paradox" — writing faster, shipping slower [3]

GitLab's "2026 AI Accountability" report (06/23, 1,528 developers and tech buyers across six countries, conducted by The Harris Poll) names exactly the feeling many have day to day: 79% say their individual productivity has risen with AI, 78% produce code faster — but the overall delivery process is not speeding up at the same rate.

The reason is the headline finding: 85% say AI has shifted the bottleneck from writing to reviewing and validating code. On top of that, the governance gap: 80% admit their organization adopted AI tools faster than the rules for them, and 92% report governance problems with AI-generated code.

AI writes faster → more code to review → review becomes the bottleneck

My take: This is the most honest AI-coding number of the week, and it matches my own harness reality. The leverage is no longer in generation — that problem is solved — it's in fast, trustworthy review. Anyone with time to invest today is better off putting it into review automation, test coverage, and small, checkable diffs rather than even faster generation. The fastest autopilot is worthless if a human still has to proofread every line afterward.

JetBrains AI Pulse: who's actually working with what [4]

JetBrains' AI Pulse survey (fieldwork January 2026, over 10,000 developers, eight languages) measures actual work usage, not hype. GitHub Copilot leads worldwide with 29%, Claude Code sits at 18% (24% in the US/Canada) — tied with Cursor, also at 18%.

The real story is the slope: Claude Code jumped from around 3% (April–June 2025) to 18% in January 2026 — roughly 6x in half a year, with the highest loyalty scores in the market (CSAT 91%, NPS 54). Copilot's lead is real, but it's the incumbent bundled with the IDE; the growth is happening elsewhere.

NPS (Net Promoter Score): How many users actively recommend a tool minus how many warn others away, on a scale from −100 to +100. Like a restaurant rating that only counts whether guests would recommend it to friends. +54 is unusually high for developer tools.

Standing thread — open-weights coding models

The open front keeps moving even as the top stays proprietary. DeepSeek-V4-Pro-Max at 80.6% is the strongest open-weights entry in today's Verified top 10 — the only open number I could cross-check against a directly fetched leaderboard, making it the most reliable one here.

Everything else is vendor-reported and should be read with caution: GLM-5.2 (Z.ai) claims to lead the open ranking on SWE-bench Pro with 62.1% (ahead of GPT-5.5's 58.6%) — Pro, not Verified. Kimi K2.7 Code (Moonshot, 06/12) cites 81.1% on MCP Mark Verified and 62.0% on Kimi Code Bench v2; a circulating SWE-bench Verified figure (~60.4%) is single-sourced and unconfirmed — deliberately not presented here as measured. MiniMax M3 (06/01) combines near-frontier coding (SWE-bench Pro 59.0), 1M context, and native image/video input — rank 10 on the Verified board.

Context For Daniel's local stack (Ollama/Gemma/Qwen), this changes little in the short term — the interesting open models are large and don't run comfortably locally. But the gap to the top is shrinking: 80.6% open vs. 95.0% proprietary is a lead of about 15 points, not entire worlds anymore.
