Grok 4.5 is public — and strikingly cheap

xAI (under the SpaceX umbrella since February) publicly rolled out Grok 4.5 on 07/08. Price: $2 input / $6 output per million tokens, cached input $0.50 — roughly 3–4x cheaper than Claude Opus 4.8. marktechpost.com · openrouter.ai, retrieved 07/10.

On the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index, Grok 4.5 lands at 54 points — fourth place among the major labs' flagships (full ranking below). The real selling point is elsewhere: on "agentic tool use," AA measures 33% for Grok 4.5 — the best score of any model it tracks, ahead of GPT-5.5 and Claude Sonnet 4.6. artificialanalysis.ai, 07/08.

Agentic tool use: Can the model not just write text but independently operate tools — search for a file, run a script, read the result, plan the next step? Think of the difference between a colleague who tells you what to do and one who sits down at the computer themselves. For coding agents, this is the core discipline.

Skeptical on the coding numbers: xAI cites Terminal-Bench 2.1: 83.3% and SWE-Bench Pro: 64.7% (versus GPT-5.5's 58.6%).[1] Two catches. First, these are vendor-reported numbers with no independent audit (as of 07/09). Second, xAI benchmarks against GPT-5.5 — not GPT-5.6, which arrived hours after Grok's announcement. The benchmark fight xAI wins here is already a round behind.

My take: Try Grok 4.5 is worth trying because the price is right and the agentic-tool-use number comes from an independent source (AA), not just xAI. Test it as a cheap agent backend — but hold off on believing the coding self-praise numbers until SWE-Bench Pro confirms them independently.

[1] Vendor-reported, cross-checked via technext24.com; no independent SWE-Bench Pro line for Grok 4.5 yet (as of 07/10).

Meta Muse Spark 1.1: 1M context and computer use

Meta Superintelligence Labs launched Muse Spark 1.1 on 07/09 — by Meta's account, its most capable model for coding and agentic tasks. Three capabilities stand out: a 1-million-token context window with active context compaction, multi-agent orchestration (main agent + subagents), and computer use. API price: $1.25 input / $4.25 output per million tokens, $20 starting credit. ai.meta.com · datacamp.com, retrieved 07/10.

Computer use: The model clicks and types on a screen itself — moving the cursor, filling out forms, instead of just producing text output. Per Meta, Muse Spark decides on its own when a script is more efficient than clicking directly. Context compaction means: rather than letting the 1M-token window fill up, the model actively manages its memory — remembering what it has done and pulling back earlier work steps as needed.

Skeptical: This is so far a capability announcement, not a measured one. No independent coding benchmark exists for Muse Spark 1.1 yet (as of 07/10) — neither on SWE-Bench Verified nor Pro. The 1M-context and computer-use claims are Meta's own. marktechpost.com, 07/09.

My take: Try The price sits between Grok 4.5 and OpenAI's Terra tier, and the 1M-context-plus-computer-use combo at this price is competitive. But: only take it seriously after your own tests — without an independent number, it's a promise, not a result.

GPT-5.6: the independent numbers are in

GPT-5.6 (Sol/Terra/Luna) went into full rollout yesterday; today the first numbers from Artificial Analysis are in — the "independent re-measurement" this brief flagged yesterday. On the AA Intelligence Index it scores Sol 59, Terra 55, Luna 51; Sol sits one point below Claude Fable 5 (60). On the AA Coding Agent Index, Sol leads with 80 points, topping all three coding evals tested. artificialanalysis.ai, 07/09.

Does yesterday's skepticism still hold? Yes. AA is an independent aggregator, but the Coding Agent Index measures — as METR already noted — primarily shell-agentic coding, not the file-editing this brief relies on. And on the one board that measures exactly that — SWE-Bench Verified — GPT-5.6 doesn't appear at all yet (as of 07/10). Pricing stands at: Sol $5/$30, Terra $2.50/$15, Luna $1/$6 per million tokens. qcode.cc · aipricing.guru, retrieved 07/10.

Source situation — disclosed OpenAI's own pages (openai.com, platform.openai.com) kept returning HTTP 403 on retrieval. The preview and pricing details come from cross-checked secondary sources and are dated accordingly — not read from the primary page.

My take: The AA numbers are better than pure vendor marketing, but they confirm yesterday's pattern — GPT-5.6 is strong on shell agents, not measurably better on the Verified board. For model choice: keep Terra in mind as the affordable middle option, use Sol only where shell-agentic performance matters.

JetBrains AI for Teams and Organizations

JetBrains announced "AI for Teams and Organizations" on 07/07: not a new model bet, but a vendor-agnostic governance layer. It connects the AI tools developers already use via two open protocols — MCP for external tools, ACP for external agents — and layers shared context, reusable agent workflows, and org-wide cost/policy control on top via "JetBrains Central." blog.jetbrains.com · infoworld.com

MCP & ACP: MCP (Model Context Protocol) is a standard plug through which an AI model talks to external tools and data sources — like USB for AI tools. ACP (Agent Client Protocol) does the same for entire agents: it lets an external coding agent dock into the IDE, regardless of vendor. So JetBrains isn't building the next model — it's building the power strip.

Commercially, JetBrains is shifting enterprise customers from AI licenses to on-demand AI credits, rolling out from July 2026. infoworld.com, retrieved 07/10.

My take: This is the more honest answer to this week's consolidation (three model launches, Cursor under SpaceX). Anyone who doesn't want to bet on a single lab gets a layer from JetBrains that makes models and agents interchangeable — the opposite of the big vendors' lock-in strategy. For teams with governance obligations (cost, data protection), the most interesting move of the week.

OpenAI itself declared SWE-Bench Verified broken

Context for all the numbers above: the most important coding board, SWE-Bench Verified, has officially been considered contaminated since 02/23 — by OpenAI's own account. OpenAI stopped reporting on it after finding that ~59% of the tasks its models failed contained flawed tests, and now recommends SWE-Bench Pro. openai.com (403, via blockchain.news), 02/23.

Benchmark contamination: A benchmark is an exam for models. "Contaminated" means the questions (or their solutions) leaked into the training material — the model has already seen the answers. Improvements then no longer measure ability, but memorization. In OpenAI's own words: progress on Verified "increasingly reflects how much the model was exposed to the benchmark during training." The evidence: current top models fall from ~85–88% on Verified to roughly 60–70% on the contamination-resistant Pro — Opus 4.8 88.6%→69.2%, GPT-5.5 82.6%→58.6%.
SWE-Bench Verified — Top 7, as of 07/10/2026 (llm-stats.com)

GPT-5.6 and Grok 4.5 are still missing entirely. And the model ranking? On the AA Intelligence Index v4.1 (methodology as of 06/15, Grok 4.5 added 07/08), among available models, Claude Opus 4.8 (56) leads ahead of GPT-5.5 xhigh (55) and Grok 4.5 (54). Claude Fable 5 tops the overall table at 60, but is flagged as unavailable/restricted (falls back to Opus 4.8). GPT-5.6 Sol (59) just joined and isn't reflected in this table yet — the ranking is simply in flux this week. artificialanalysis.ai, 06/15./07/08.

Looking for a third opinion? It's chaos: on LiveCodeBench, three trackers show three different #1s today — Gemini 3 Pro Preview (91.7%, AA), Qwen3.7 Max (91.6%, llm-stats v6), DeepSeek-V4-Pro-Max (93.5%, llm-stats main). And Aider's Polyglot board? Still led by gpt-5 (high) at 88.0% — tested on 08/23/2025, page last updated 11/20/2025. Eight months old; unusable as a current signal. artificialanalysis.ai · aider.chat, retrieved 07/10.

My take: This is exactly why the headline says "the benchmarks disagree." Use SWE-Bench Verified at most as an internal Claude-to-Claude comparison (the relative ordering among Claude models is plausible), but don't draw any cross-vendor conclusion from it. For new models, only SWE-Bench Pro counts right now — and there are still no independent numbers for GPT-5.6, Grok 4.5, or Muse Spark.
