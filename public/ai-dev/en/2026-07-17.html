Gemini 3.5 Pro: delayed — and coding is the reason [1][2][3]

Update since yesterday Yesterday's brief had Gemini 3.5 Pro on the calendar as a rumor for today, with the caveat that Google had confirmed neither date nor specs. Today the answer is in, and it's a no: Bloomberg reported on 07/16/2026 that Google is "months behind schedule" with Gemini 3.5 Pro — its own flagship. The delay is said to stem from Google taking more time to improve the model's abilities, particularly at programming. In late June, Google reportedly resharpened Gemini's training data specifically to boost coding ability — "the results were disappointing."[1]

We didn't just take that at face value — we checked. Google's own API model list, fetched this morning, simply has no gemini-3.5-pro. What's actually there: Gemini 3.5 Flash as the generally available top model, Gemini 3.1 Pro only as a Preview — and the only stable Pro model is still the old Gemini 2.5 Pro.[3] No model entry, no changelog entry, no model card. A model you can't call hasn't launched.

False report Several aggregator sites are listing a "Gemini 3.5 Pro GA" for today — some with specs like a 2M context and a "Deep Think" layer. None of it is confirmed by Google, and the model list directly contradicts it. The date originated in the rumor mill, got passed along by aggregators as a target date, and was then cited by further aggregators as fact. It's a clean case study in how a number without a primary source becomes "news" — and a reason why every number here carries a dated source.

Worth noting: this deadline has already slipped once before. In its own Gemini 3.5 post back in May, Google wrote that 3.5 Pro was "already being used internally," and that it looked forward to rolling it out "next month" — meaning a June target.[4] "Next month" has since become at least two months. And the market was listening: Alphabet closed down 4.4% on the day of the report, wiping out roughly $200B in market value.[2] What that means for the stock is covered in the stocks-crypto brief — here, it's the technical finding that matters.

Why coding, of all things, is the sticking point

It's worth pausing a second on the justification, because it's unusually honest for the industry. Google isn't delaying over safety, or capacity — it's delaying because the code wasn't good enough. My reading, and I'll defend it: coding is the one skill you can't write your way around. In a summary or an essay, "good" gets blurry — a polished answer can read as competent. Code is ruthlessly binary: it compiles or it doesn't, the tests pass or they don't, the repo builds or it doesn't. There's no phrasing that makes a bug charming.

That's exactly why "more and better training data" is no longer a reliable lever here. When a lab resharpens its coding data and the results still disappoint, the obvious explanation isn't "wrong dataset" — it's that the cheap part of the curve has been grazed bare. For our own toolbox, that's good news with a bitter aftertaste. Good, because it means nobody's going to leapfrog you unannounced next week — your setup ages more slowly than the marketing suggests. Bitter, because the next big jump apparently doesn't come on demand even for a lab with Google's resources. Anyone picking their toolchain today based on what's supposedly coming in three months is choosing based on a number nobody can substantiate.

Kimi K3: 2.8 trillion parameters — and open starting 07/27 [5][6]

While Google is stuck on its flagship, Moonshot AI launched Kimi K3 on 07/16/2026: a 2.8-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model built on two proprietary components called Kimi Delta Attention and Attention Residuals, with a 1-million-token context. The sentence that matters most to us is in the same blog post: the full weights are expected to be released by 07/27/2026.[5]

MoE sparsity (16 of 896): The model never computes with all 2.8 trillion parameters. It keeps 896 "experts" on hand and switches on only 16 of them per token — under 2% of the building. Picture a huge library where, instead of walking every aisle for each question, an information desk sends you straight to two or three shelves. Result: a lot of knowledge in the building, but only a fraction of the compute load per request. That's the trick that makes 2.8 trillion parameters affordable to serve at all.

What we're deliberately not explaining: Moonshot's announcement doesn't spell out, in verifiable detail, what exactly "Kimi Delta Attention" and "Attention Residuals" do differently, and no independent analysis exists yet. These are vendor terms, not documented mechanisms — we name them but won't invent an explanation for them.

The independent read is in, and it's good: on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index, Kimi K3 scores 57 points, putting it at rank 4 of 189 models — behind Claude Fable 5 (60) and GPT-5.6 Sol (59), roughly on par with Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5.[6] Moonshot itself is upfront about it: overall performance "still trails the strongest proprietary models."[5] A vendor that doesn't dress up its own ranking earns some benefit of the doubt.

Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index — frontier and Kimi K3, as of 07/16/2026

The chart shows why this is more interesting than "rank 4" sounds: it's a photo finish. Three points separate a model whose weights will be downloadable in ten days from the proprietary frontier. Worth watching On pricing, Moonshot's own API charges $3.00 per million input tokens ($0.30 on a cache hit) and $15.00 per million output tokens.[5]

Skepticism Moonshot's own table lists DeepSWE 67.5, Terminal-Bench 2.1: 88.3, and Program Bench 77.8. Those are vendor numbers — K3 isn't on SWE-Bench Verified or LiveCodeBench yet.[5] And the index above is a composite grade: it averages many tasks into one number, like a GPA rather than a single math grade. It says "roughly this smart," not "can handle your repo." The independent coding numbers needed for a coding verdict are still missing — realistically, those will only arrive once the weights are out and third parties can measure for themselves.

Kimi K2.7 Code: the first open model in Copilot's picker [7][8][9]

The K3 launch isn't a standalone event — it's the second half of a movement that has already arrived in a tool millions of people use. Since 07/01/2026, Kimi K2.7 Code has been generally selectable in GitHub Copilot's model picker — in GitHub's own words, "the first open-weight model offered as a selectable option in the Copilot model picker." Initially for Pro, Pro+, and Max; since 07/07/2026 also for Copilot Business and Enterprise.[7][8]

Open weights: "Weights" are the model's learned numbers — the outcome of training, essentially the brain as a file. If they're open, you can download that file and run the model on your own hardware, with no request ever reaching the vendor. It's the difference between a recipe you're allowed to cook yourself and a restaurant that only ever hands you the finished plate. Heads up: "open weights" isn't the same as open source — the training data and training code usually stay locked away.
Practical snag On Business and Enterprise, Kimi K2.7 Code is off by default. GitHub says so explicitly: plan administrators must first enable the policy in Copilot settings before anyone in the organization can even select the model.[8] So if you're on a company Copilot plan and can't find the entry, you haven't clicked the wrong menu — it's a policy setting.

On the technical side: 1 trillion parameters total, 32B active, MoE with 384 experts (8 per token), 61 layers, 256K context, Modified MIT license.[9] Billing runs "at provider list pricing under usage-based billing," and the model is hosted by GitHub itself on Microsoft Azure.[7] That last part is the real punchline: open weights no longer mean "self-hosters only." They're now arriving as a convenient menu entry inside a closed product — and anyone who wants to can still download the same model family. Having both paths open at once is something no proprietary model can offer.

Clarification: GPT-5.6 is not ChatGPT's default [10][11]

An assumption that's spread since 07/09 and is simply wrong: that GPT-5.6 is now ChatGPT's default model. It isn't. The default remains GPT-5.5 Instant, in place since 05/05/2026 — the GPT-5.6 launch didn't change that status.[10] You only reach GPT-5.6 Sol via the reasoning setting on a paid plan; the family (Sol as the flagship, Terra for everyday use, Luna as the budget option) has been available across ChatGPT, Codex, and the API since 07/09, at $5/$30 (Sol), $2.50/$15 (Terra), and $1/$6 (Luna) per million input/output tokens.[11]

Why this matters in practice: anyone who quickly tests something in the browser and draws conclusions about 5.6's abilities is, as a rule, testing 5.5 instead. That's exactly the kind of quiet false assumption that renders a model comparison worthless.
