Kimi K3: good at code, middling at the rest [1][2]

Update since yesterday Yesterday's brief carried K3 with an explicit caveat: the Intelligence Index is a composite grade, and a coding verdict was missing the independent numbers. Those are in now, and they shift the picture. Artificial Analysis — the evaluator that runs the models itself rather than copying vendor tables — puts Kimi K3's Coding Index at 76.24, the highest score in the comparison field. On the general Intelligence Index it stays at 57.11 and rank three to four, and on the Agentic Index at 50.07.[1]

Coding Index vs. Intelligence Index: Both are averaged grades, but from different exams. The Intelligence Index averages nine very different tasks — math, knowledge, logic, a bit of code — into a single number, like a report-card GPA across every subject. The Coding Index only averages the programming tests — the grade in math alone. A kid can shine in math and stay average on the overall GPA. That's exactly the case here. Important: the two numbers sit on different scales — 76 isn't "better than" 57, it's a different exam.

The second independent confirmation comes from a completely different direction, and I like it methodologically even better: on Arena.ai, K3 sits at #1 in the Frontend Code category — passing Claude Fable 5, up from rank 18, where predecessor K2.6 stood.[1][2] Arena.ai works like a blind taste test: users submit the same task, get two anonymous answers, and vote on which is better. There are no test questions a vendor could know in advance and bake into training. The price for that is subjectivity — what gets scored is what people like, not what passes the tests. Two methods with opposite weaknesses, both landing on the same result: that's about the best evidence you can get ten days before the weight release.

Skepticism What's not included in that scoring: Moonshot's own table. DeepSWE 67.5, Terminal-Bench 2.1: 88.3, ProgramBench 77.8 are vendor numbers, self-measured — and the evaluator who checked them notes they were collected on mixed harnesses (sometimes KimiCode, sometimes Claude Code, sometimes Codex, depending on the benchmark), which "limits direct model-to-model conclusions."[3] Translation: these are cars compared on different tracks. K3 still doesn't appear on SWE-Bench Verified.

My read, and I'll defend it: this gap isn't an accident, it's a design choice. Whoever tunes a model for code pays for it in breadth. For our toolbox, that's better news than an evenly-strong model — when programming, nobody cares whether the thing can write sonnets. The honest caveat: an index score says the model does well on bounded tasks. Whether it holds up in your ten-year-old repo with half-documented dependencies is something no leaderboard measures. That's something everyone can only find out for themselves starting Jul 27.

Gemini 3.5 Pro: day two with no launch [4][5]

Brief, because the story ran in full here yesterday and nothing has moved since — but it still deserves a sentence, because the non-event is itself the finding. Our own pull of Google's developer model list this morning: there is still no gemini-3.5-pro. What's listed is unchanged — gemini-3.5-flash as the generally-available top model, gemini-3.1-pro only as a preview, and the old gemini-2.5-pro as the only stable Pro model.[4]

Google's last statement of its own on the topic still comes from its I/O post in May: 3.5 Pro was already in internal use, and the rollout was coming "next month" — meaning June.[5] The report attributing the delay to disappointing coding results comes from Bloomberg (Jul 16); the page isn't directly reachable from here, so we're relying on two outlets that cite the report. That's reporting about reporting, and we're flagging it as such.

The connection to the story above is too neat to skip: in the same week a lab with Google's resources is holding back its flagship because the code wasn't good enough, an open model out of Beijing leads the Coding Index. It doesn't follow that Google has fallen behind — it simply hasn't published anything to judge. Something else does follow: coding ability is precisely the bottleneck the labs are now being sorted by, and it isn't something you can buy with data-center scale.

NVIDIA Nemotron 3 Embed: open embeddings, ranked #1 [6][7]

Possibly the most practically useful release of the week for your own toolchain got lost in the Kimi excitement. On Jul 16, 2026, NVIDIA released Nemotron 3 Embed — a family of open embedding models in three variants: 8B in BF16, 1B in BF16, and 1B in NVFP4 (quantized). Per NVIDIA, the 8B model ranks #1 on the RTEB leaderboard at 78.46 NDCG@10 (rounded to 78.5% in the summary); the 1B variant scores 72.4%.[6]

Embedding model: a translator from text into strings of numbers. Every scrap of text — a paragraph, a function, a ticket — becomes a list of numbers, arranged so that texts with similar meaning get similar lists. Like a library where books aren't shelved alphabetically but by topic: stand in front of the "cooking" shelf and every cookbook is within reach, no matter what it's called. That's exactly what every RAG system and every code search needs — retrieval happens through that closeness, and only then does the language model come into play. A bad embedding model hands the best LLM the wrong paragraphs.

NVIDIA explicitly names code retrieval and agent memory as target use cases alongside classic RAG.[7] Agent memory is the case anyone working with coding agents feels first: whatever yesterday's session knew has to be stored somewhere and found again tomorrow — a retrieval problem, not a context-window problem.

NVFP4 quantization: like saving a photo as a JPG — you throw away precision so the file gets smaller and loads faster. Instead of storing every number in the model at full precision, NVFP4 uses four bits per value. NVIDIA puts the loss on this model at 0.38 RTEB points, or 99.5% of original performance.[7] For local use, that's a very good trade.

Worth trying For a local setup, the 1B NVFP4 variant is the interesting candidate: small enough for your own machine, with a quantified rather than guessed quality loss. The honest test stays the same — index your own code corpus, measure hit quality against your current solution. No leaderboard replaces that.

Two caveats First: the "#1" claim is made by NVIDIA's own blog post. The leaderboard behind it — RTEB — is at least not run by NVIDIA; it's a community-maintained benchmark hosted on Hugging Face's MTEB leaderboard that deliberately mixes open with private datasets so nobody can game it by memorization.[7] That makes the comparison more credible than a purely in-house measurement, but it's no substitute for independent verification. Second: the post compares exclusively against open models (Qwen3-Embedding, EmbeddingGemma) and against its own prior generation — no direct comparison against the commercial embedding vendors (OpenAI, Cohere, Voyage). If you're already using one of those, this release doesn't answer whether switching is worth it.

On licensing: NVIDIA writes "open weights" and "open and commercially available" — the post doesn't name the specific license. So before any commercial use, read the model card, not the blog post.

Codex Micro: OpenAI sells a keyboard [8][9]

On Jul 15, 2026, OpenAI put its first piece of hardware on sale, and it's smaller and stranger than expected: Codex Micro, a $230 macropad with 13 mechanical keys, built with keyboard maker Work Louder, meant to control Codex agents through the ChatGPT desktop app. Six of the keys are illuminated "Agent Keys" that show the agent's state by color: white = idle, blue = thinking, green = done, red = error. On top of that come programmable command keys, a dial for reasoning depth, and a joystick for saved workflows.[8] By Jul 16, the order page read "sold out"; the estimated shipping date for the first wave is Jul 24.[9]

Why this is more than a gadget: you don't build a status light for something that answers instantly. The four colors are an admission that agent work has become mostly about waiting — you kick off a run, go do something else, and want to catch out of the corner of your eye whether it's still thinking, done, or has driven into a wall. Many of us have half-baked workarounds for that: a terminal ping, a desktop notification, a second monitor. The reasoning dial points the same direction — it treats compute effort as a quantity you dial in by hand per task, like a volume knob.

And the objection: the thing only works with the ChatGPT desktop app — $230 of hardware tied to exactly one vendor, in a field where people switch harnesses every few months. A Stream Deck costs less and doesn't care if you switch tools next quarter. The one-day sellout also says little as long as the unit count is unknown.

Side note The timing is unintentionally funny: OpenAI is stepping into the hardware business while Apple is suing the company over alleged theft of trade secrets — the case concerns a separate, unreleased, screenless device reportedly designed by former Apple engineers.[8] The lawsuit has nothing to do with the macropad, but it explains why the first product is so pointedly small.

Also new: two emergency brakes — a session-wide limit on web searches (default 200, tunable via CLAUDE_CODE_MAX_WEB_SEARCHES_PER_SESSION) and a cap on subagent spawns (default 200, CLAUDE_CODE_MAX_SUBAGENTS_PER_SESSION, /clear resets the budget) — both against runaway loops.[10] If /fork is in your muscle memory, you'll now get something different than before; this is the kind of change worth reading up on deliberately.
