SWE-Bench saturates — rank no longer discriminates

The one number making the rounds right now: a secondary tracker puts Claude Mythos 5 at the top of SWE-Bench Verified with 95.5%, followed by Fable 5 at 95% and Opus 4.8 at 88.6% (as of 07/19). Two things before you lean on that. First: it rests on one source — the official leaderboard at swebench.com loads its table via JavaScript and returned no numbers on fetch, so there's no independent confirmation. Second, and more important: even if the figures are accurate, they barely tell you anything anymore. When the top models all sit somewhere between 88 and 96 percent, the ranking is measuring noise, not ability.

benchlm.ai · swebench.com · as of 07/21/2026
Benchmark saturation & contamination: A benchmark is like a test with a fixed set of questions. As long as some students score 40 and others 90, the test discriminates well. But if everyone scores 95, it stops being useful for ranking — that's saturation. And if the questions leaked beforehand and students memorized the answers, the test measures memory instead of skill — that's contamination. On SWE-Bench (models are asked to fix real GitHub bugs), both effects are colliding right now: the tasks are years old and potentially sitting inside the training data.

Three benchmarks, three stories

The suspicion is confirmed the moment you look at the tougher sibling. On SWE-Bench Pro — harder, curated tasks — Claude isn't narrowly ahead, it's far ahead: 80.3% for Mythos 5 versus 64.6% for GPT-5.6 Sol, and Sol is the expensive tier of the GPT family. That too rests on a single source (the Pro leaderboard page was rate-limited), so treat it as a direction, not a ranking. And LiveCodeBench isn't readable at all: three trackers report three different "current" leaders as of three different dates. So there's no LiveCodeBench ranking here — a number that contradicts itself is worse than a gap.

The most reliable read is Artificial Analysis's Coding Agent Index, because it measures agentic work (multi-step: reading files, editing, running tests) rather than isolated code snippets. There, GPT-5.6 Sol (max) leads with 80 points, Grok 4.5 follows with 76; on the broader Intelligence Index, Sol trails Claude Fable 5 by just one point (59 to 60). That's the more useful picture: no winner by decimal point, but a tight top pack where the harness and the task matter more than the model's name.

That's why we're deliberately building no bar chart today. A clean chart needs clean, comparable numbers — and that's exactly what today's benchmark landscape doesn't offer. The honest advice: test two or three candidates against your own repo, not against someone else's leaderboard.

Open weights catch up: GLM-5.2 at Opus level

Not breaking news — but exactly the thread yesterday's pricing focus left out. GLM-5.2 from Z.ai (Zhipu) has been out since 06/13, with weights on Hugging Face since 06/16, under an MIT license. It's a ~744-billion-parameter MoE model with a 1-million-token context, and API input runs around $1.40 per million tokens. For anyone running LLMs locally, it's currently the most interesting open model — with an asterisk on "locally" (see below).

eigent.ai · artificialanalysis.ai · as of 07/21/2026
Open weights & MoE: "Open weights" means the trained model parameters are downloadable and you can run the model yourself — not the same as open source, since the training code and data often stay closed. "MoE" (Mixture of Experts) is the trick that lets a model be huge without computing the full network for every word: instead of one giant brain, there are many specialists, and a router wakes up only a handful per token. The catch: all 744 billion parameters still have to sit in memory. This is a server model, not a laptop model.

The numbers — with the same skepticism

On SWE-Bench Pro, GLM-5.2 hits 62.1, putting it practically level with Claude Opus 4.8 (~63) and ahead of GPT-5.5 (~58.6); this figure is confirmed across two sources. On Terminal-Bench 2.1 it scores 81.0 against Opus 4.8's ~85. And on Artificial Analysis's Intelligence Index v4.1, it leads the open weights with 51, ahead of MiniMax-M3 (44), DeepSeek V4 Pro (44) and Kimi K2.6 (43). Important context: this is closing in, not passing — and precisely at the top, where the gaps are small, saturation and contamination concerns weigh heaviest. Still, the story is real: the gap between open and closed has shrunk to a few points on coding. try If you need sovereignty or on-prem, GLM-5.2 is the candidate to know — try it on a rented GPU server, not your MacBook.

Devin SWE-1.7: speed as a feature

Cognition unveiled SWE-1.7 on 07/08, the in-house coding model behind Devin. The hook is speed: 1,000 tokens per second, delivered via Cerebras inference hardware. On its own FrontierCode benchmark it's said to land "within a few points" of GPT-5.5 — a vendor number on a vendor benchmark, so read with caution.

More interesting than the self-reported score is the second move: Devin now makes Claude Sonnet 5, Kimi K2.7 and GLM 5.2 selectable as models in the desktop app and the CLI. That's the real trend behind the story — harnesses are becoming model-agnostic, and the in-house model becomes the fast default option rather than the only choice. Given a choice, people pick per task.

Claude Code: effort is a dial, not a switch

Anthropic published a guide to model and effort choice in Claude Code on 07/07, and one conceptual clarification in it is worth flagging: "effort" is not a fixed low/medium/high token knob. It continuously controls behavior — how many files the agent reads, how deeply it verifies, how many steps it takes before checking in. So higher effort doesn't mean "same work, more tokens," it means "a more thorough approach that happens to cost more tokens."

The post's illustration cites roughly a 7x token spend at high versus low effort on the same prompt — though the page explicitly notes these curves are illustrative only, not real benchmark data. try The practical takeaway: match effort deliberately to the task — high for the stubborn debugging case, low for routine work — instead of leaving it maxed out and multiplying the bill tenfold.

Two Copilot threads converge here. The message is the same as yesterday: the era of the flat-rate plan is ending; budget per run.
