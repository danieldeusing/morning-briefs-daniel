Google's three-model drop: two to use, one to watch

On Tuesday, July 21, Google released three models in one go. The flagship is Gemini 3.6 Flash: $1.50 per million input tokens, $7.50 per million output tokens, available from day one in the Gemini API, AI Studio, Android Studio, and the Gemini app. Google isn't selling it on price alone but on efficiency: the model reportedly needs 17% fewer output tokens than 3.5 Flash, and up to 65% fewer in individual benchmarks. That's a vendor claim from the launch post, not an independent measurement — but the math behind it holds up.

blog.google · As of 2026-07-23
Token price × token usage: A model costs you twice — once for every word fragment you feed in, once for every one that comes out. In agent work, the second line item dominates, because an agent produces pages of intermediate reasoning before it changes a single line of code. Picture a taxi whose per-mile rate drops while it also takes shorter routes: the two effects multiply, they don't just add up. That's exactly Google's argument — a lower price per token combined with fewer tokens consumed.
Diligence Trade press additionally reports that 3.6 Flash's knowledge cutoff moves forward from January 2025 to March 2026 (techtimes.com, July 22). Google's own announcement page says nothing about this — we checked it directly. Treat this as a press claim, not a confirmed spec; if your use case depends on the knowledge cutoff, query the model itself before relying on it.

Flash-Lite: the cheapest one allowed to click

Gemini 3.5 Flash-Lite comes in at $0.30/$2.50 per million tokens — and has "Computer Use" not as an add-on product but as a built-in tool. That's the real story: the ability to operate a UI on its own is moving into the cheapest price tier. For tasks where an agent needs to doggedly click through a web form or an admin interface, that changes the math considerably.

blog.google · As of 2026-07-23
Computer Use: Instead of just returning text, the model gets to operate a computer — it receives screenshots and responds with "click here," "type this," "scroll down." Like an intern whose monitor you turn toward yourself instead of dictating commands to: they see the same screen you do and work their way through it. The upside is that it works anywhere there's no API. The cost is slowness and a lot of tokens per click — which is exactly why it makes sense in the budget tier.

Flash Cyber: the model behind the barrier

The third model is the interesting case. Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber is a security model built on top of 3.5 Flash, "fine-tuned to find, validate, and patch vulnerabilities" — and according to DeepMind's own blog, it will be available exclusively to governments and trusted partners via CodeMender. So it exists, it's official, and you can't use it. Google's figure on it: on the V8 JavaScript engine, it found 55 uniquely confirmed issues, versus 47 by 3.5 Flash and 36 by Claude Opus 4.6. That, too, is a vendor measurement without independent verification.

deepmind.google · As of 2026-07-23

My read: the lockdown is the most honest part of the announcement. A model that finds, validates, and patches vulnerabilities is, in the same breath, a model that finds vulnerabilities — and not just for defense. Google's answer to that symmetry is a bouncer rather than a price tag. For you, that means nothing concrete today, except a direction: value is shifting from "model writes code" to "model closes the loop of finding, checking, and fixing." Anyone who wants to build an open equivalent of that loop just got a very clear target description.

Meta's first invoice: a quarter of the top price

On July 9, Meta charged money for one of its own models for the first time. The self-serve Meta Model API prices Muse Spark 1.1 at $1.25 per million input tokens and $4.25 per million output tokens. Mark Zuckerberg stated the positioning openly in a Bloomberg interview: the new API costs "about 25 percent of OpenAI and Anthropic's top models." That's not an aside, that's the strategy — Meta is buying market share with the price tag, not with a benchmark record.

The prices above therefore come from two independent secondary sources published the same day, both citing Meta's announcement — not confirmed by Meta directly. API compatibility: The Meta API speaks both the OpenAI SDK (Chat Completions and Responses) and Anthropic's Messages format. In plain terms: same plug, different outlet. To switch your existing agent over to Muse Spark, you change the base URL and the key — not the code. That's exactly what makes a price war usable for you in the first place: switching costs are close to zero, and you can pit two providers against each other on the same job.

The price ladder, measured

Still at the top, unchanged, is GPT-5.6 Sol, OpenAI's flagship for coding: $5/$30 per million tokens, a 1.05 million token context window, maximum 128,000 tokens of output. That means a million output tokens on Sol costs four times as much as Gemini 3.6 Flash, seven times as much as Muse Spark, and twelve times as much as Flash-Lite. One caveat on availability: OpenAI's own post is titled "Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol" and cites June 26 as the start of a limited preview, while trackers list July 9 as general availability. The source record doesn't support a hard GA date.

Before you rebuild around this: price per token is only half the equation. Two holes in the IDE chain

On July 14, two vulnerabilities were disclosed that hit exactly the tools used here every day. CVE-2026-50510 affects the GitHub Copilot plugin for JetBrains, CVSS 3.1 base score 7.8, and all plugin versions before 1.13.0-251. The advisory's recommendation is unambiguous: users of IntelliJ IDEA, PyCharm, WebStorm, Rider, and Android Studio should update the extension immediately. That's today's two-minute task.

CVE-2026-50520 hits Visual Studio Code: a command injection (CWE-77) with a CVSS of 8.4. One detail that gets lost in the headlines: the attack vector is local. The "remote" in "remote code execution" here means attacker-controlled input, not an attack over the network — so the realistic chain is an opened untrusted repository or a smuggled-in config file, not a port scan. That doesn't make it harmless, it just clarifies what to watch for: untrusted code in the editor is still untrusted code.

PyTorch 2.13: FlexAttention on the Mac

PyTorch 2.13 was released on July 8 — 3,328 commits from 526 contributors since 2.12. The point that matters for local experiments: FlexAttention now runs on Apple Silicon (the MPS backend). On sparse attention patterns, the gains are large: on a 1×8×32768×64 shape with a 256-element sliding window (0.8% density), FlexAttention takes about 35 ms versus about 431 ms for SDPA — a factor of 12.3. A smaller case with length 8192 and a 64-element window comes in at a factor of 4.15.

pytorch.org · As of 2026-07-23
Attention & "sparse": "Attention" is the step where every word in the context looks at every other word to decide what matters right now — at 32,000 words, that's over a billion pairs. "Sparse" means most of those pairs don't matter at all; each word only looks at its 256 neighbors. Like a classroom where not everyone talks to everyone, just to the people sitting next to them. FlexAttention doesn't even compute the uninteresting pairs, while the older SDPA stubbornly carries them along anyway — hence the speedup factor. try If you run local models on a Mac, this is the reason to install 2.13.

The rest of the release notes are everyday utility: nn.LinearCrossEntropyLoss cuts peak memory usage by up to a factor of 4, a new torchcomms backend and overlapping FSDP2 communication target distributed training, and there are wheels for Python 3.15.
