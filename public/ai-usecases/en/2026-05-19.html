Top story · I/O Tuesday

Before the keynote: what's actually new vs. what already shipped in April

One hour before I/O, a bit of expectations hygiene helps. Three items will get airtime tonight — and two of them are not, strictly speaking, new: Gemma 4 already shipped on April 2, 2026 — four sizes (E2B, E4B, 26B-MoE, 31B-Dense), Apache 2.0 license (a switch from the Gemma-3 custom license). 140+ languages, native function-calling, video and audio input. The 26B-MoE runs on smartphone hardware with ~4B active parameters. That is the only structural shift that really matters for the DE Mittelstand today — a Google model with no licensing friction on your own hardware. (blog.google, huggingface.co)

What's actually new tonight: the Gemini Intelligence layer as an agentic substrate under Android, plus Chrome Auto-Browse (live since January, now with tighter integration), Maps 3D navigation, Gboard Rambler. The biggest hardware piece: the Android XR glasses preview with an OEM stack — Samsung "Jinju" (audio-only, Snapdragon AR1, 50 g, $379–499) as the only variant with 2026 volume; Samsung "Haean" (Micro-LED, $600–900) targeting 2027; XREAL Project Aura (70° FOV, ~$1,000) as developer hardware; Warby Parker and Gentle Monster as consumer eyewear in 2026; Gucci in 2027. (wareable.com, androidauthority.com)

My read The XR platform is the story everybody will tell — but the material news is Gemma 4 with Apache 2.0. A glasses display variant at >$600 as developer hardware is not a 2026 market-opener; the sub-$1,000 audio-only Samsung "Jinju" is the Ray-Ban competitor. If you're standing up an on-prem stack or a sovereign inference layer in Germany, you've had a usable Google building block since April 2. Comparable license freedom previously came only from Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek and Meta — each with their own license quirks.

Standing thread · Economy & work

Gartner data point of the week: 80% layoffs, 0% measurable ROI

Published May 5: Gartner reports that ~80% of organizations piloting or rolling out autonomous business capabilities are reporting layoffs — and those layoffs do not translate into measurable ROI. Gartner nonetheless forecasts AI-agent software spending at $206.5B (2026) and $376.3B (2027). Sample: 350 business executives globally, Q3 2025. Methodology flag Gartner's survey is executive self-report, not telemetry. When a CEO says "we cut headcount and did not measure ROI," no ROI is not the same as negative ROI — it can just as well mean "we are measuring the wrong things." Even so, the read is uncomfortable: the hardware cycle (NVIDIA Q1 tomorrow) is running visibly ahead of measured value creation.

Standing thread · Regulation

Article 50 guidelines draft: combined techniques, no single standard

Published May 8: the Commission's draft Article 50 transparency guidelines explicitly avoid picking C2PA or SynthID. The draft proceeds from the position that "no single technology currently meets the legal requirements" and calls for combined techniques: metadata + invisible watermarks + fingerprinting/logging. Second clarification: the "obviousness test" under Art. 50(1) borrows the "average consumer" yardstick from consumer law — code assistants for professional developers and NPCs in games may be exempted. Consultation closes June 3. (digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu, insideglobaltech.com)

Combined watermarking techniques: Instead of one "digital postage stamp," several methods running in parallel — metadata inside the file (e.g. C2PA), an invisible signature embedded in the image, audio or video (e.g. SynthID), plus a central log of who generated what when. Like a letter with a postage stamp plus watermarks in the paper plus an intake stamp at the post office — any one layer alone would be forgeable.

Digital Omnibus AI: political agreement May 7 — what's actually decided

The Council and Parliament reached provisional agreement on May 7 (formal adoption still pending). What is agreed: Annex-III high-risk deadline extended to December 2, 2027; Machinery Directive removed from AI Act scope (a DE industry-lobby win); SME relief extended to firms up to 750 staff / €150M revenue; new bans on nudifier apps from December 2, 2026. Reactions: industry says "not far enough," civil society says "watering down." (consilium.europa.eu, whitecase.com)

MedTech pivot: association now focuses on MDR revision, not AI Act

Marginally new vs. the May 18 brief: MedTech Europe didn't just protest on May 7 — it has strategically switched fronts. The association is now focused on the ongoing MDR/IVDR revision as its alternate repair track, because the Digital Omnibus denied the carve-out for medical devices (which the Machinery Directive received). Position: no dual conformity assessment; embed AI requirements inside the MDR framework. For medical-AI pitches: two conformity pathways remain reality, but the MDR revision agenda is the next political battleground. (medtecheurope.org)

EU Tech Sovereignty Package May 27 — what the tender already tells us

The package remains scheduled for May 27. The most concrete lever isn't the still-unpublished CADA draft but the Commission's exploration of restricting US cloud providers for sensitive government data (health, finance, justice). Important context: on April 17, the €180M sovereignty tender was already awarded — to Post Telecom/OVHcloud/CleverCloud, STACKIT, Scaleway (all SEAL-3) plus Proximus with S3NS/Mistral (SEAL-2 only). CISPE calls the S3NS award (Thales × Google Cloud) "sovereignty washing"; Brussels stays the course anyway, because Mistral needs scalable hyperscaler infrastructure. Read the tender closely and you know what's coming May 27: not a pure EU-only door, but tiered SEAL levels. (cnbc.com, commission.europa.eu)
