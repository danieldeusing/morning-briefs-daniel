Top Story · The Deployment Layer Is Being Bought

OpenAI's "Deployment Company": ~$4B, and the Tomoro acquisition

On May 12 OpenAI launched the Deployment Company — a majority OpenAI-owned subsidiary funded by 19 global investment firms, consultancies and system integrators with ~$4B in initial capital. Lead investor TPG, co-lead founding partners Advent, Bain Capital, Brookfield. The partners explicitly include Bain & Company, Capgemini and McKinsey — exactly the consulting heavyweights that normally dominate enterprise implementation. Alongside it OpenAI acquired Tomoro (an applied-AI consultancy founded in 2023 in alliance with OpenAI, based in London), bringing ~150 experienced forward-deployed engineers and deployment specialists from day one.[1] cooley.com · @OpenAI

The logic: buy in highly skilled engineers to clear "one of the biggest bottlenecks in enterprise AI adoption." The unspoken point: AI is sold as high-margin software, but it actually needs labor-intensive, highly skilled services to fit onto customer data and workflows — the Palantir model of embedded engineers.

My take This is the most important use-case news of the month — and it didn't come from a keynote stage. When OpenAI and Anthropic industrialize the forward-deployed-engineering layer with billions and buy up SI giants, it reshapes the market twiceD pitches into. Two readings, both true: (1) threat — the labs + Bain/Capgemini/McKinsey take large enterprise accounts directly. (2) validation — the most expensive AI scarcity isn't the GPU, it's the human who gets the model into the workflow. That's exactly twiceD's product. The German Mittelstand stays too small and too sovereignty-sensitive for a TPG-funded McKinsey vehicle — that's the gap.

Anthropic's counter: a $1.5B JV with Wall Street capital

Anthropic countered with its own $1.5B joint venture — backers include Anthropic, Blackstone and Hellman & Friedman, with Goldman Sachs as a founding partner. Target segment explicitly: PE-owned mid-market firms — regional banks, regional health systems, manufacturers. Same forward-deployed model here: engineering teams sit with the customer and build tools into existing workflows.[2] cnbc.com

Forward-deployed engineering (FDE): Instead of selling software over the counter and leaving the customer alone, the vendor sends its own engineers straight into the company — they sit inside the customer's team, learn its data and processes, and build the solution to fit on site. Like a furniture store that doesn't just deliver the cabinet but sends a carpenter to install it in your home. The model made Palantir big; now everyone is copying it.

SAP Sapphire Madrid · the credible customer cases

Levi Strauss: 1,000+ agents, wholesale orders from days to minutes

The hardest verifiable customer case of the Madrid week comes from Levi Strauss: 1,000+ AI agents in production, 4,000 employees trained. Concrete effect: 80% of orders already flow through automatically; the remaining 20% — from smaller retailers via handwritten note, email or unstructured document — used to take 2–5 days to process, now 20–30 minutes. Context: 170-year-old company, consolidating 9 ERP instances onto RISE with SAP/S/4HANA, global rollout expected in 2027.[3] news.sap.com · cio.com

Why it matters The 2–5-days-→-20–30-min number is exactly the pattern a mid-sized company can rebuild: a high-frequency, document-driven ordering process with a clear before/after. Not an "AI strategy" pitch, but a single workflow with measurable throughput. That sells better than any survey percentage.

Aeropuertos Argentina and the vendor stage

The Madrid keynote (a rerun of Orlando) also showed the Aeropuertos Argentina agent: weather, track-sensor and maintenance data orchestrated in the winter-ops workflow, with effects like a noticeable cost reduction and less admin time, two airports in production. Lockheed Martin and ExxonMobil were on stage too, but with strategy statements and no quantified KPIs.[4] sapinsider.org

Methodology flag All Sapphire numbers are vendor communication from the SAP stage. Useful as documented context, but anyone putting a number on a pitch slide should add "SAP/customer figure, Sapphire 2026." The gold standard remains: named company + verifiable before/after.

Standing Thread · Regulation

High-risk classification guidelines: draft from May 19

Published yesterday: the Commission put its long-awaited draft guidelines on classifying high-risk AI systems under Article 6 of the AI Act (EU 2024/1689) out for consultation — 148 pages, in three parts: general principles, the Annex I product-safety route, and the Annex III use-case route. Key point: the Article 6(3) filter (the exception for purely preparatory/narrow-procedural tasks) is read narrowly — the Commission stresses that exceptions to fundamental-rights-protection rules must be interpreted restrictively. Consultation open until June 23, 2026.[6] digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

Annex III use-case route: The AI Act lists specific deployment fields (e.g. hiring, credit scoring, critical infrastructure) where an AI system automatically counts as "high-risk" — with hard obligations (risk management, logging, human oversight). Like a list of rooms in the house where protective gear is mandatory: as soon as your system works in one of those rooms, the strict rules apply, no matter how harmless it looks. The narrowly read filter means: fewer easy ways out.

What becomes real by year-end

For context: December 2, 2026 is the next hard deadline — the transition for legacy GPAI ends, the watermarking obligation kicks in, and the new bans on non-consensual intimate material and CSAM generation take effect. Annex III high-risk has been deferred via the Digital Omnibus to December 2, 2027.[7] insideprivacy.com

Project Ideas for twiceD

Three researched proposals — with the market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · Document order agent for the Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: At many mid-sized firms, orders, complaints and dispatch notices arrive as PDF, email or fax and get typed into the ERP by hand. A "document agent" reads these unstructured inputs, checks them and posts them as a clean order. Value: Levi's cut exactly this process from 2–5 days to 20–30 minutes — what's saved is expensive clerical time and the error rate of manual re-keying.
Prototype (V0): (1) A real stack of the customer's documents as a test set. (2) Extraction via an existing IDP model (layout + fields), not built from scratch. (3) Validation logic against master data (customer, SKU, price). (4) A write adapter into the target ERP for one document type. MVP deliberately narrow: one doc type, one customer, a human confirms before posting.
Market & gap: The IDP market is large (≈ $14B in 2026, ~26% CAGR) with mature vendors — Rossum (transactional LLM, writes straight to the ERP), ABBYY, Google Document AI, AWS Textract, and newly agentic LlamaIndex/LlamaParse.[10] But those are horizontal platforms: the customer has to wire up workflow, integration and validation themselves. The gap is the last mile — fitting it into one concrete German-Mittelstand process, on-prem for sensitive supplier data, with no per-page SaaS subscription. Hands-on instead of self-service.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. The heavy model comes from the platform; twiceD builds the adapter + validation + operations. V0 doable solo in 4–6 weeks, rollout with 1 contractor. Maintenance: low, as long as the scope stays narrow (a new doc type = a new mini-project, not ongoing overhead). Sovereignty variant via an open model on-prem (see idea 3). rossum.ai · llamaindex.ai

2 · AI Act high-risk audit (Annex III routing)

ELI5 & value: The EU AI Act classifies some AI uses as "high-risk" — with hard obligations. Many mid-sized firms simply don't know whether their HR screening, credit scoring or predictive maintenance falls under it. An audit maps each use case to the right route and says what to do. Value: avoided fines and retrofit risk before the August 2, 2026 / December 2, 2026 deadlines, documented and audit-ready.
Prototype (V0): (1) An inventory of all the customer's AI systems (often there is none). (2) Classify each system against the May 19 guidelines draft + Annex III. (3) Article 6(3) filter check (read narrowly!) — does it fall out after all? (4) A gap list + obligations per system as a report. MVP = a structured template + an LLM-assisted question workflow, no tool-building.
Market & gap: SaaS checkers and registers exist — Vanta, Holistic AI, ActReady (SMB focus, free risk classifier), the official EC AI Act Compliance Checker — plus boutique advisers like McKenna.[11] The SaaS tools deliver a generic register; interpreting the brand-new 148-page guidelines for the concrete case, in German, hands-on, is what they don't do. The gap is translating regulatory text into a concrete to-do list for one specific Mittelständler.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Very high — the lowest tech barrier of the three. Advisory-heavy, no software to operate, so no ongoing maintenance. Repeatable as a 2–4-week fixed-price package. AI helps read/map the guidelines; Daniel sells judgment + German language + liability awareness. Risk: not legal advice — frame it as a technical audit, bring in a lawyer for the legal question. artificialintelligenceact.eu · ec.europa.eu

3 · Sovereign on-prem AI workplace

ELI5 & value: Instead of sending customer data to a US lab, an open AI model runs inside the customer's house (own server or EU cloud). twiceD builds the application around it — document search, drafting, data extraction. Value: GDPR- and sovereignty-compliant, no per-seat lab license, data never leaves the building. For regulated or data-sensitive mid-sized firms, often the only viable path.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick one bounded use case (e.g. quote search across internal documents, RAG). (2) Stand up an open model on a single GPU — a 70B model fits in ~35 GB of VRAM (INT4) on an A100/H200. (3) A RAG pipeline over the customer's documents. (4) A lean web UI. MVP on a rented EU GPU, later on-prem at the customer.
Market & gap: Sovereignty is a top theme in 2026 — "OpenAI for Germany" (SAP/Delos), 19 EU AI Factories giving SMEs free GPU access, the EU actively pushing open-weight.[12] Open models (Qwen 3.5, Llama 4 Scout, GLM-4.5V) handle ~80% of real tasks at a fraction of API cost per practitioner reports.[13] The gap: the big players sell sovereign infrastructure; missing is the one who builds and operates the finished application on it for the individual Mittelständler.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium — the most demanding of the three, but doable. Standing it up solo in 3–4 weeks; the model heft comes ready-made, twiceD builds RAG + UI + operations. Maintenance honestly higher (model updates, GPU operations, monitoring) — the EU AI Factory GPU access drastically lowers the entry barrier (no data-center budget needed). Scale via a contractor per customer, not via a big team. ec.europa.eu · mentiko.com
