Top story · NVIDIA delivers — the adoption litmus test

Record without China: $81.6B revenue, $91B Q2 guide

NVIDIA reported its first quarter after the US close last night (fiscal year ended 2026-04-26): $81.6B revenue (+85% year over year), of which data center $75.2B (+92%) — "nearly doubling," per CNBC. Non-GAAP EPS $1.87 (+140%), GAAP net income $58.3B, gross margin 74.9% GAAP / 75.0% non-GAAP. A clear beat over its own guidance ($78.0B ±2%) and consensus (~$78.8B). The decisive line is in the guidance: $91.0B ±2% for Q2 (the Street expected ~86) — and that with zero China data-center compute revenue. "No shipments of data-center Hopper products to China" in the quarter (vs. $4.6B a year ago); H200 licenses granted since February, but no revenue from them yet. nvidianews.nvidia.com · cnbc.com

Beat and raise: guides $78.0, delivers $81.6, guides Q2 to $91.0 — with the China zero already priced in. Source: NVIDIA release, 2026-05-20.

The real message: inference becomes the main event

For an applied-AI newsletter, what counts isn't the headline EPS but how management frames the business. CEO Jensen Huang: "Agentic AI has arrived, doing productive work, generating real value and scaling rapidly across companies and industries." The CFO calls Grace Blackwell with NVLink "the king of inference today — delivering an order-of-magnitude lower cost per token." The new inference software Dynamo 1.0 reportedly speeds up Blackwell "by up to 7x"; the next platform, Vera Rubin, is positioned as "built for agentic AI." NVIDIA explicitly names sovereign customers (governments) as a growth driver alongside the hyperscalers. nvidianews.nvidia.com

Inference & tokens: Inference means an AI model actually working — answering a question, reading a document — not being trained. Training is the model's schooling; inference is its day job. A "token" is a chunk of text the model processes; "cost per token" is the unit price. When that drops, every productive AI use gets cheaper.
Blackwell + Dynamo→ cost/token −1 order of magnitude→ more mid-market workflows pencil out→ bigger deployment market
My take The pure stock/share-price read belongs in the stocks-crypto brief; here, the adoption read-through is what matters. And it's unambiguous: if inference really gets "an order of magnitude" cheaper, automations that were too expensive in 2024 suddenly pencil out — exactly the small, high-frequency mid-market processes where twiceD plays. The China zero is the confidence signal: demand stands on its own, with no export windfall. Cheaper inference = a bigger addressable market for embedded deployment, not for yet another model.

Study · proving the ROI is still the problem

Gartner: layoffs don't correlate with AI returns

This week's most-cited study comes from Gartner: about 80% of organizations piloting or rolling out autonomous or agent capabilities report workforce cuts — yet the cut rate is nearly identical at firms with high ROI and those with modest/negative outcomes. Translation: cutting staff does not correlate with better AI returns. A separate Gartner survey (Apr 7, 782 infrastructure/operations leaders) backs the skepticism: only ~28% of AI use cases meet the ROI expectation, 20% fail outright. fortune.com · gartner.com

Methodology flag Both are self-reports, not audited financials. "ROI" is what respondents claim. The first study surveyed 350 executives at firms with ≥$1B revenue (fielded Q3 2025), the second 782 I&O leaders (Nov–Dec 2025) — both large enterprises; the mid-market can look different. Sponsor: Gartner's own research.

My take in one sentence: the message isn't "AI doesn't work," it's "most firms can't even tell whether it worked" — because they never measured the before and after. That's a gap, not a verdict (see the project ideas).

Standing thread · regulation

EU high-risk guidelines: the lawyers respond

Over the past 48 hours the major firms dissected the 148-page draft from May 19 (classifying high-risk AI under Art. 6, consultation until June 23) — Bird & Bird, Hunton, Baker McKenzie, plus the IAPP. Recurring point: even when final, the guidelines are non-binding — only the ECJ can rule authoritatively; they were originally due Feb 2, and the delay feeds the legal uncertainty. In parallel, the Digital Omnibus deal (Council/Parliament, May 7) is not yet formally adopted (expected ~July), so the deadlines are provisional. iapp.org · lw.com

Annex III: The AI Act lists use-case fields — applicant screening, lending, critical infrastructure — where an AI system automatically counts as "high-risk," with hard duties (risk management, logging, human oversight). Like a list of rooms where protective gear is mandatory: the moment your system works there, the strict rules apply. The filter that gets you out (Art. 6(3), "preparatory tasks only") is read narrowly in the draft.

US: a frontier-model order is imminent

Per CNN (May 20) and Axios, a Trump executive order on "frontier models" is imminent: a voluntary framework with 90-day pre-launch government access to "covered frontier models," plus defense/national-security components — prompted by demonstrated cyber/exploit capabilities in newer frontier models. Not yet signed; the White House calls it "speculation." It builds on the National AI Legislative Framework of March 20 (a push for federal preemption). cnn.com

Brazil: PL 2338 still stuck

The AI framework bill PL 2338/2023 (Senate-passed Dec 10, 2024, risk-tiered) is in its decisive phase. A vote in the Chamber's special committee was floated for May 19, with the floor on May 27 — but the timeline is contested; parliamentary trackers now point to December. No confirmation that the vote took place. camara.leg.br · diap.org.br

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with a market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · "Proof-of-pilot" — ROI-instrumented AI piloting

ELI5 & value: A mid-market firm wants to test AI in a process but ends up not knowing whether it paid off — because no one measured the "before." "Proof-of-pilot" is the neutral party that delivers only the measurement: capture a baseline (cycle time, error rate, hours, cost per transaction), define the success metric up front, run the pilot with clean before/after (or A/B), and give an honest go/no-go afterward. Value: the client stops pouring its AI budget blindly into a second pilot and knows, robustly, after 6–8 weeks whether the first one delivered. Exactly the gap Gartner's "only ~28% meet the ROI expectation" describes.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick a running or planned AI use case at the client. (2) Baseline kit: a lean measurement scheme (metric definition, sample, collection path) — no tool-building, a sheet plus one or two scripts. (3) Pre/post or A/B collection over 4–8 weeks. (4) A go/no-go report with one number and a recommendation. MVP deliberately narrow: one use case, business outcome (not model metrics), twiceD does not build the AI — that preserves neutrality. Building blocks: the client's existing telemetry/logs, a stats package (Python/pandas), optionally CloudZero/Vantage for the token-cost side.
Market & gap: The AI-consulting market is large (~$14.07B in 2026, ~26.5% CAGR per Business Research Insights). The "value realization" practices at McKinsey QuantumBlack, BCG X and Accenture target exactly this — but they're enterprise-priced and structurally conflicted: they sell the build, so they grade their own homework. SaaS/FinOps tools (Olakai, CloudZero, Vantage) measure usage, adoption and token cost — not the business before/after. The German mid-market AI shops (KIMI, Digital Colliers) sell fixed-price pilots — again, the build. Gap: the neutral, build-agnostic, fixed-fee, mid-market-sized measurement that grades only the outcome — nobody owns it. businessresearchinsights.com · olakai.ai
Feasibility (solo + AI): High — the most advisory-heavy, tech-light of the two. No software to operate, so no ongoing maintenance; repeatable as a 6–8-week fixed-fee package. AI helps set up the collection and the analysis; Daniel sells judgment + neutrality + German-language delivery. Scaling: one contractor per parallel engagement. Risk: neutrality only holds if twiceD does not also build in the same engagement.

2 · Annex-III-safe decision-support assistant (one function)

ELI5 & value: Many AI features in HR (applicant pre-screening) or credit/supplier risk fall under the EU AI Act's "high-risk" tier — with an expensive compliance burden. twiceD builds an assistant for one such function that deliberately stays in the permitted lane: pure decision support (a human always decides), no scoring/ranking, scoped to the Art. 6(3) exemption. Value: the client gets the assistant's productivity without the full high-risk burden (risk-management system, conformity assessment, EU registration).
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick a function where summarizing beats scoring — e.g. applicant summaries or supplier-risk prep notes. (2) RAG over the client's documents, output strictly as a draft/note, never as a score. (3) Human-in-the-loop hard-wired + audit log. (4) A scoping dossier that justifies the Art. 6(3) classification. MVP narrow: one process, no profiling, a lawyer confirms the classification. Building blocks: an open model on-prem/EU-cloud (sovereignty), a RAG framework, no homegrown model.
Market & gap: The adjacent EU AI Act compliance market is growing fast (~$609.4M in 2026 → ~$10.5B by 2035, 37.3% CAGR per Dimension Market Research). The big vendors — SAP SuccessFactors, Personio, Workday, HeyMilo (HR), SCHUFA/Creditreform (credit) — bolt AI on as a scoring feature that is itself Annex-III high-risk (Annex III explicitly lists recruitment/scoring); the burden lands on the customer. Governance startups (Trail/Munich, DataGuard, Holistic AI) only document the burden — they don't remove it. Gap: nobody builds a mid-market-sized assistant deliberately designed to stay in the Art. 6(3) lane. dimensionmarketresearch.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium — the more demanding of the two. A V0 (a RAG assistant for one process) is doable solo in a few weeks; maintenance is moderate (model/document updates), and predictable. The catch: the "compliant by design" claim is technical scoping, not legal advice — a lawyer must confirm the Art. 6(3) classification per deployment, or the promise carries liability. Scaling via a contractor per client. The on-prem/open-model variant is the sovereignty lever here.

It's Thursday, May twenty-first. After the US close last night, NVIDIA reported its first quarter — and it's a record. But the story that matters more to us is the tension between that record and a second piece of news the same day.

First, NVIDIA. Revenue came in at eighty-one point six billion dollars, up eighty-five percent from a year ago. The data-center business nearly doubled, to seventy-five point two billion. The guidance for the second quarter is ninety-one billion, well above what the market expected — and here's the striking part: there isn't a single dollar of China data-center revenue in that forecast. So the demand stands on its own, with no policy windfall.

For an applied-AI newsletter, though, what counts isn't the headline number but how management frames the business. And that frame is now inference — the model actually working, not being trained. The finance chief says the cost per chunk of text processed is, with the new generation, a full order of magnitude lower. If that holds, automations that were too expensive two years ago suddenly pay off — exactly the small, high-frequency mid-market processes where twiceD works. Cheaper inference means a bigger market for embedding AI, not for yet another model.

Now the tension. The same day, Gartner reminds us where it falls down. In a survey of three hundred fifty executives, about eighty percent of firms with AI projects are cutting staff — but the cut rate is nearly the same at the winners and the losers. So cutting jobs buys no better return. A second Gartner study found only about twenty-eight percent of AI projects even meet their return expectations. Both are self-reports from large companies, not audited accounts — but the message is clear. Most firms can't say whether their AI paid off, because they never measured the before.

From the SAP Sapphire stage came new customer cases: Ericsson, with eighty-five thousand Joule users and, by its own account, ninety thousand hours saved, and KPMG, aiming to avoid a hundred and twenty million dollars in contract leakage at a single client. Both are vendor claims from the stage, not independently audited results. In Brussels, the law firms tore into the hundred-and-forty-eight-page high-risk draft; their refrain: the guidelines are non-binding, only the European Court of Justice can rule on them. In Washington, a Trump order on frontier models is imminent but not yet signed. And in Brazil, Finep and BNDES are putting about two hundred five million reais behind AI startups, with bids due at the end of May.

From that, two project ideas for twiceD. First, a proof-of-pilot service — a neutral party that sells only the measurement, instruments the before and after of an AI pilot cleanly, and delivers an honest yes or no. Nobody owns that today, because everyone else sells the build and so grades their own work. Second, a decision-support assistant for a single function, deliberately kept inside the AI Act's permitted lane — support, not scoring — so a mid-market firm gets the productivity without the costly high-risk burden.
