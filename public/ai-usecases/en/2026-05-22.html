Top story · Walmart delivers — and the gap shows

$177.8B revenue, new CEO, „AI native"

Walmart reported Q1 FY27 before the US open yesterday: $177.8B revenue (+7.3%, +5.9% in constant currency), GAAP EPS $0.67, adjusted EPS $0.66. US comp sales (ex-fuel) +4.1%, global e-commerce +26%, advertising +37% (Walmart Connect US +44%, ex-VIZIO). The full-year guide (adjusted EPS $2.75–2.85) holds. Notable is the tone: under new CEO John Furner (Doug McMillon has stepped down), Walmart now calls itself „AI native" — AI is no longer a project, it's the operating model. corporate.walmart.com

Sparky: real motion, but no denominator

What matters for us isn't the EPS but the AI assistant Sparky — and the call did bring fresh numbers (not the old Q4 figures): weekly active Sparky users +100% in the quarter, units bought through Sparky ×4 versus the prior quarter, and Sparky's „intelligence/response quality" up +40% this year per Walmart. Sparky now runs in the app, on the web, and in stores (reordering, meal planning). corporate.walmart.com

Methodology flag All growth rates with no stated base: „twice as many users" doesn't say twice what. No absolute user counts, no revenue attribution, and „+40% intelligence" is an internal, undefined quality measure. The catchy figures (+35% larger carts, ~50% of app users tried Sparky) still come from the Q4 call (Feb 2026) — they were repeated as context, not refreshed.
growth rate with no base→ no before/after→ engagement ≠ proven ROI
My take The stock read belongs in the stocks-crypto brief. Here, what counts: +100% users is a real engagement signal, but not a return on investment. Even the company that calls itself „AI native" puts no audited before/after on the table. That's no knock on Walmart — it's the pattern of the whole cycle, and it's exactly the gap twiceD plays in.

Study · The measurement gap

Use doubled, measurement near zero

The week's sharpest stocktake comes from heise (May 19): AI use in German firms has risen from 12% to 26% (2023→2025, Destatis/Eurostat); Bitkom puts adoption even higher at 36% (Sept 2025); 68% of S&P 500 firms mention AI on their earnings calls (FactSet, Q4 2025). Yet the share of firms that formally measure the effect of their AI use is „near zero." As a warning, the piece cites the controlled experiment in which experienced developers were 19% slower with AI help — but felt 20% faster. heise.de

Methodology flag The adoption figures are self-report (Destatis/Eurostat survey, Bitkom CATI), not measured telemetry. The one hard data point is the developer RCT — and it mostly shows how far feeling and measurement diverge. That's precisely the point: without a clean before/after, gut feel replaces the number.

Finance · DE — AI as rationale, not as proof

Commerzbank: „a large part" of cuts due to AI

CEO Bettina Orlopp, per Handelsblatt (May 15), attributes „a large part" of the planned cut of 3,000 jobs to AI; the effect is „larger than assumed a year ago." Those affected are mainly external call centers and IT contractors. What's missing: a named system, a productivity metric, a before/after. handelsblatt.com

Counter-check The same skepticism is backed by a Gartner survey (350 large firms, via Handelsblatt May 5): of the ~80% cutting staff, only about 1 percentage point is directly attributable to AI productivity — the rest is the economy. „Because of AI" in 2026 is often a story that frames a cut you were planning anyway. handelsblatt.com

Standing thread · Regulation

US: frontier order halted just before signing

The Trump executive order on „frontier models" expected yesterday was scrapped hours before signing (Axios/NBC, May 21). It would have asked leading labs to share advanced models with the government up to 90 days before release for a voluntary security review. Trump „doesn't like certain aspects" and fears slowing the US lead over China; advisor David Sacks, plus Zuckerberg and Musk, reportedly pushed against it. No new date. Not to be confused with the already-signed order challenging state AI laws. axios.com · nbcnews.com

My take Instructive for the hype skeptic: even the avowedly light-touch White House couldn't get even a voluntary framework across the line. For twiceD's clients that means: don't count on US federal guardrails near-term — the enforceable duties come from Brussels.

EU: Article 50 transparency — the clock is running

On May 8, the AI Office published a 40-page draft of guidelines on the transparency obligations under Article 50 (consultation until June 3). The duties apply from Aug 2, 2026: users must be able to tell they're talking to an AI; AI-generated content (text, image, audio, video) must be machine-readable marked, and deepfakes labelled. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

Article 50 vs. Annex III: Two different work-sites in the AI regulation. Annex III (high-risk) is about what the AI decides — applicant screening, lending — with a heavy compliance load. Article 50 is far broader and touches almost everyone: it's only about honesty — tell people they're talking to a machine, and mark what the machine produced. Like an ingredient list: not a ban, just a duty to disclose.

Brazil: ANPD principle-based, PL 2338 still stuck

Data-protection authority ANPD signaled a „principle-based" course over rigid rules at the Telebrasil Summit (May 19); its regulatory sandbox now runs as a live test under ANPD supervision through Dec 2026, with an AI guidance document for regulated entities in the works. Meanwhile the framework bill PL 2338/2023 (Senate-approved Dec 2024) sits in the Câmara's special committee — no plenary date in sight. gov.br/anpd · whitecase.com

It's Friday, May twenty-second. A quiet news day, but with one clear line running through all of it: AI use is growing everywhere — yet almost no one is measuring whether it pays off.

The illustration of the day is Walmart. The retail giant reported its quarter before the US open yesterday: revenue of one hundred seventy-seven point eight billion dollars, up seven point three percent, online up twenty-six, advertising up thirty-seven percent. The new chief, John Furner, now calls the company „AI native" — artificial intelligence is no longer a project, it's the operating model.

And he did bring fresh numbers on the in-house AI assistant, Sparky: twice as many weekly users in the quarter, four times as many items bought through Sparky. Sounds strong — but these are growth rates with no base. Twice as many users doesn't tell you twice as many as what. No absolute user counts, no revenue attribution. Even the company that calls itself „AI native" puts no audited before-and-after on the table.

At scale, a German stocktake by heise shows the same thing. AI use in German firms has doubled, from twelve to twenty-six percent in two years. But the share of firms that formally measure the effect is, by this account, near zero. That gap between feeling and measurement is the theme.

The counter-check comes from a Gartner survey: of the roughly eighty percent of large firms cutting staff, only about one percentage point is directly attributable to AI. „Because of AI" in 2026 is often a story that frames a cut you were planning anyway.
