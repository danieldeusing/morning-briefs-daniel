Top story · Hershey builds the measurement in

AI agents steer a $2-billion budget — monthly instead of three times a year

Confectionery giant Hershey has moved its marketing-mix analysis onto an agent-based system, reshaping how it steers more than $2B in combined media and trade budget. Previously the analysis ran roughly 3×/year for about five brands, with results arriving months later. Now the full brand portfolio is modeled monthly (up to 12×/year), with insights in around three weeks. Two specialist vendors deliver it: Tracer cleans and standardizes the fragmented trade/marketing data, and Mutinex runs domain-specialized agents (econometrics, competitive pricing, anomaly detection) on top. adweek.com · bandt.com.au

Marketing-mix modeling (MMM): A statistical method that reads historical sales and ad data to figure out how much revenue each dollar of advertising (TV, online, discounts) actually drove — like a receipt-by-receipt review showing which ad spend paid off and which didn't. Until now a clunky quarterly project; the agents turn it into ongoing measurement.
data chaos→ Tracer cleans→ Mutinex agents model→ monthly budget allocation
My take What makes Hershey interesting isn't "another AI deployment" — it's what got automated: the measurement itself. Most firms deploy an AI tool and then claim the benefit; Hershey quadrupled its measurement cadence and shortened the feedback loop. Caution on the headline number: the +4–5% media-attributable revenue is an expectation, not a measured result. What's real and already evidenced is the operational shift: three analyses a year become twelve, "months later" becomes three weeks. That difference — measured cadence vs. claimed effect — is the theme of the rest of this brief.

Counter-check · The measurement gap

heise: AI broadly deployed, measurement near zero

A heise analysis titled "The measurement gap" sums up the point of the day: generative-AI adoption in German firms rose from 12% (2023) through 20% (2024) to 26% (2025), and EU-wide from 13.5% to 20%. Per FactSet, 68% of S&P 500 firms mentioned AI on their Q4 2025 calls. The share of firms that formally capture the effect, by contrast, is near zero. heise.de

Methodology flag The figures in the analysis are assembled from other sources (adoption statistics, FactSet), not an original survey — the value is in the thesis, not in new primary data. Still a clean bracket: adoption broadly evidenced, impact measurement scarce.

KI-Barometer: named Mittelstand firms, longer rollouts

The equally fresh heise KI-Barometer 2026 (May 18) is a cross-sector interview study of named German firms — among them Wittenstein/baramundi, Deutsche Glasfaser, the food logistics of the Nagel-Group and Protektorwerk, plus a carpentry shop and a bakery. The recurring finding: which applications have proven themselves in daily use is less of a problem than how long the rollout takes — deployments consistently ran longer than planned. heise.de

Methodology flag Qualitative, no public numbers — the metrics sit behind the paywalled report. Useful as a "these DE Mittelstand firms are genuinely in production" pointer, not as an ROI source.

WRITER: the disappointment is growing

For context, clearly dated: a WRITER survey from early April (n=2,400, split evenly between C-level and employees, six countries incl. Germany) finds that 48% call AI adoption a "massive disappointment" — up from 34% a year earlier — and 79% report adoption challenges. businesswire.com · finance.yahoo.com

Methodology flag Vendor-funded: WRITER sells enterprise GenAI — the "adopt, or fall behind" framing serves the business. What's credible is precisely the numbers that cut against its own interest (48% disappointment, 79% challenges); the productivity promises alongside them are self-reported, not measured output.

Standing thread · Regulation

EU: the Art. 50 clock runs toward August 2 — unmoved this week

On the regulatory side nothing material moved in recent days; the calendar, though, is tight. The draft guidelines on the Art. 50 transparency obligations (May 8) are still in consultation until Jun 3. The deployer-side disclosure applies unchanged from Aug 2, 2026: users must be able to tell they're talking to an AI (50(1)), deepfakes (50(4)) and emotion/biometric systems (50(3)) must be labeled — as does the machine-readable marking of AI-generated content (50(2)). The still-provisional Digital Omnibus would defer this 50(2) marking to Dec 2, 2026, but it isn't adopted yet. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu · artificialintelligenceact.eu

What to watch For agentic systems whose interaction can't be reliably predicted, the draft pushes a cautious reading: build disclosure in by default rather than bolting it on afterward. For a GmbH with a customer chatbot that means the August deadline stands, and the marking should be baked in.
Article 50 (transparency): The part of the EU AI Act that doesn't regulate the model but the honesty outward: anyone talking to an AI or seeing AI-generated content must be able to tell — like an ingredient list on the package, only for AI.

CRA & Omnibus: stages incoming, nothing final yet

In parallel, the Cyber Resilience Act: from Jun 11 the provisions on conformity-assessment bodies apply, from Sep 11, 2026 the reporting duties (24h early warning / 72h / 14-day report) via the ENISA platform. The Digital Omnibus on AI presented in May — which pushes several AI Act deadlines back — remains a provisional political agreement; formal votes are expected for June/July. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Art. 50 transparency toolkit for the Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: From Aug 2, 2026 every company in the EU must disclose when a user is talking to an AI or seeing AI-generated content — chatbots, text/image generators, deepfakes. A 50-person GmbH with one customer chatbot doesn't need a corporate governance platform with an org-wide risk registry; it needs that one chatbot labeled in a legally sound way. Value: avoided fines and warning-letter risk at a fixed price, without subscribing to an enterprise suite. Like the inspection sticker on an elevator — you don't certify the whole factory, just the one point that has to be checked.
Prototype (V0): (1) A quick inventory of a pilot customer's AI touchpoints (chatbot, text generator, image tool). (2) Map the concrete Art. 50 duty per touchpoint (50(1) interaction notice, 50(4) deepfake label, 50(2) machine-readable content marking). (3) Build in the notices/banners and the machine-readable marking — existing building blocks: the open C2PA / Content Credentials provenance format for image/video, lean disclosure components written in-house. (4) Audit checklist + evidence docs as the handover. MVP deliberately narrow: one customer, their real touchpoints, German legal context — no org-wide governance registry.
Market & gap: The AI-governance market is small but fast — Precedence Research puts it at ~$420M in 2026 at ~34% CAGR through 2035 (MarketsandMarkets estimates even ~45%; both are analyst estimates, absolute size soft).[1] Plenty of vendors exist — OneTrust, Credo AI, Holistic AI, Saidot, Modulos, in Germany Trail, plus the certifiers (TÜV SÜD, TÜV AI.Lab).[2][4] But all are horizontal, inventory-first governance platforms: "discover ALL your AI systems", org-wide registries, 600+ control libraries — built for enterprise. Art. 50/transparency isn't even the headline topic for most (Trail and Holistic AI don't mention it on their product pages at all). Nobody delivers "make THIS chatbot compliant by August" hands-on, at Mittelstand scale, in German. That's the gap.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Mostly advisory plus a lean, reusable toolkit (checklist + disclosure snippets + marking integration) — no hosted always-on infrastructure, so low maintenance load. V0 solo in 3–5 weeks per customer, sold as a fixed-price "compliance package"; scaling via one contractor per engagement, the framework being reusable. Deadline-driven → a clear demand window through August. Honest limit: the final legal assessment stays with the customer's lawyer — twiceD delivers the technical implementation + evidence docs, not legal advice.
