Top story · Germany bets on a frontier lab

SPRIND puts €125M on the table for "Europe's own OpenAI"

The federal innovation agency SPRIND today opened a staged competition worth €125 million to fund a European frontier-AI lab. The structure is a knockout race over 24 months: Stage 1 funds up to 10 teams at €3M each, Stage 2 narrows to 6 at €8M each, Stage 3 the final 3 at €15.5M each. SPRIND's Jano Costard is candid about the point: "We cannot try to compete with today's Anthropic … we need to create new paradigms." euronews.com

Frontier-AI lab: A lab that trains models at the absolute capability frontier — the GPT/Claude/Gemini class, the most expensive and most capable systems. Like Formula 1 for AI: not the car for the road, but the one on which the next generation of technology gets tested. The catch: that exact class costs billions elsewhere, not millions.
My take Welcome that someone is backing the sovereignty idea with real money — but be honest about the order of magnitude: €125M over 24 months is roughly what a US frontier lab burns through in days of compute. That's why Costard's "new paradigms" line isn't marketing, it's the only available strategy: you can't win this race with money, so you have to want to win it differently. For twiceD the lesson isn't the lab itself (far too big), but the signal: the state now considers sovereignty worth funding — and that creates demand one floor down, among the many small bodies that don't need their own model, just a sovereign application.

The counter-check from the data: broad but shallow

Anyone reading "Germany funds a frontier lab" should set the one fresh, measured number next to it. The IAB establishment panel (around 15,000 establishments) shows: generative-AI use rose from 5% (2023) to 25% (2025) — a fivefold jump. But the depth is missing: 90% deploy off-the-shelf software, only 16% buy tailored solutions, 6% build their own. Large firms (200+) sit at 48%, small ones (under 10) at 21%. handelsblatt.com

Methodology flag A clean official survey (IAB establishment panel), self-reported on use, no productivity or ROI measure. That's exactly why it's valuable: it measures that AI is used, not the claimed effect. The "90% off-the-shelf" number is the real punchline — breadth without in-house work.

The state buys, audits, rations

US HHS turns AI loose on the Medicaid books of all 50 states

The US health department HHS sent letters to the governors of all 50 states: an AI initiative called AERO — reportedly built in part on ChatGPT — is to scan at least five years of audit history and flag chronic noncompliance, "material weaknesses," and delinquent audits across Medicaid, research grants, addiction services, and childcare. Penalties up to withholding or terminating funds. healthcaredive.com · bloomberglaw.com

Methodology flag The deployment is real and live — but so far with no disclosed hit rate or efficiency figure. "AI finds fraud" here is an announcement, not a measured result. And a ChatGPT-based fraud detector over government data immediately raises the question of false positives and auditability.

Japan rations Anthropic access for its megabanks

Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama said — relayed from US Treasury Secretary Bessent — that Japan's government and financial institutions will get access to Anthropic's newest model within two weeks. The three megabanks MUFG, SMBC and Mizuho are expected; the model is prized for finding system vulnerabilities, and Japan's FSA is treating its arrival as a firm-wide management and cybersecurity issue. japantoday.com · nationthailand.com

What to watch What's notable is the order: the market doesn't distribute the model — governments negotiate early access and pass it to strategic banks. AI access becomes a state-steered good — a pattern that also fuels Europe's sovereignty debate (see SPRIND).

Three German states pool sovereign administrative AI

NRW, Hesse and Baden-Württemberg have formed a "KI-Zukunftsbund" (May 7) — for common standards and cloud-independent, sovereign infrastructure. In use: NRW.Genius, an administrative assistant already supporting staff on routine work, research and text tasks, and Hesse's AIGude (still in pilot, regular operation planned for 2026: research, document creation, translation, summarization). haufe.de

Counter-check · Money vs. measured impact

Gartner: $2.59 trillion — a spending curve, not ROI

In parallel with the sector euphoria, Gartner raises its forecast of worldwide AI spending for 2026 to $2.59 trillion — +47% year over year, and up from $2.52 trillion in January. AI infrastructure makes up over 45%; the AI-agent software segment is projected to jump from $86.4 billion (2025) to $206.5 billion (2026). morningstar.com

Methodology flag This is a spending forecast model, not an adoption or ROI survey — no sample, no productivity measure. It's built from supplier and capex signals (hyperscaler investment), so it measures the vendors' investment intent, not realized customer value. Read it as a hype/capex indicator, not as evidence of returns; Gartner is also a paid research vendor.

The adoption number that looks fresh is six months old

The US Census Bureau released its BTOS AI data on May 21: 18% of firms use AI in a business function (32% employment-weighted), ~22% plan to within six months. Sounds fresh — but it isn't: the reference period was Nov 17, 2025 – Feb 8, 2026. census.gov

Careful: stale Freshly released doesn't mean freshly collected. The 18% reflects a state of affairs from 3–6 months ago — don't sell it as a May-2026 snapshot. And a second trap this week: a circulating "Stack Overflow 2026 survey" (487,000 respondents, $185k salaries) does not exist — Stack Overflow's latest survey is the 2025 one; the figures come from a single third-party blog. Exactly the kind of year-mislabeled pseudo-study this brief warns against.
What a measured number looks like For contrast, clearly dated to early May: AIG CEO Peter Zaffino called Anthropic's Claude "88% as accurate as a human expert" on insurance claims in the Wall Street wave — an internal benchmark (so AIG's own number, handle with care), but at least a read-off value rather than a spending forecast. Named in production: JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Citi, AIG, Visa.

Standing thread · Regulation

EU clock unmoved this week — the deadline stays Aug 2

Materially, nothing moved on the regulatory side over the past seven days. The draft guidelines on the Article 50 transparency obligations (May 8) are still in consultation until Jun 3; the disclosure applies unchanged from Aug 2, 2026. The Digital Omnibus on AI (political agreement of May 7) remains provisional — the formal votes are expected for June/July; it would, among other things, push the machine-readable content marking to Dec 2, 2026. It does not push the GPAI enforcement date of Aug 2, 2026. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu · lw.com

Fresh only in the press New this week was the broad coverage (Euronews, May 19) of the planned nudify/CSAM ban: part of the Omnibus, it would from Dec 2, 2026 ban AI systems that produce realistic intimate depictions of identifiable persons without consent. It would be the first EU law to explicitly ban a concrete AI application type — but it isn't adopted yet, so plan for it, don't treat it as law.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with the market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Sovereign case-file assistant for the one small municipality

ELI5 & value: A small municipality or utility has years of files, statutes and case records that staff search by hand. A "case-file assistant" answers their questions from exactly those documents — with a citation back to the original file — and runs entirely on the authority's own hardware; the data never leaves the building. Value: minutes instead of hours of searching, with no new headcount, and — the real selling point — full data sovereignty without needing an IT department for it.
Prototype (V0): (1) A real stack of files/case records from a pilot customer as a test set. (2) On-prem RAG on an open model — existing building blocks: Ollama for the local model, the German Haystack (deepset) for the RAG orchestration, or the open-sourced state tool F13 directly as a fork base. (3) Strictly limit to one workflow (e.g. "question the statutes/building files" with a citation). (4) Source-grounded answers, human decides — no autonomous action. (5) Delivery as a maintained on-prem appliance. MVP kept narrow: one workflow, one organization, on-prem, German, sourced answers — no org-wide platform, no cloud, no autonomous decisions.
Market & gap: Sovereign administrative AI exists at the top and in the middle — Aleph Alpha with PhariaAI / Government Assistant in federal ministries and at the Bundeswehr,[1] at the state level Baden-Württemberg's F13 (open source, €1.50/workstation/month, open to all German administrations),[2] plus NRW.Genius and Hesse's AIGude — and as build-it-yourself kits the open building blocks (Haystack, Nextcloud/KomCloud).[3] Germany already counts ~400 GovTech startups.[4] But: the state tools are built for state workforces, not for the single 5,000-resident municipality; F13 is free, but that municipality still has to host and operate it itself — which it has no staff for. Pharia is ministry-/enterprise-scale. The gap: no one packages a single, named workflow as a turnkey, operated on-prem solution for an organization too small to run F13 or buy Pharia.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. The heavy lifting is already openly available (F13/Haystack/Ollama) — twiceD doesn't build the model, but the fit, the delivery and the operation the small customer can't carry itself. That's exactly why free F13 is not a competitor but a building block. Honest limit: an on-prem appliance per customer means real ongoing maintenance (model updates, per-site operation) — sustainable only if sold as a fixed-price managed service per organization and the scope is kept narrow to one workflow; scaling via one contractor per deployment. With org-wide ambition it tips into a 10-person team — then it's out. [1] aleph-alpha.com · [2] treffpunkt-kommune.de · [3] deepset.ai · [4] egovernment.de

It's Monday, May twenty-fifth. A quiet day for corporate deployments — and yet a clear pattern runs through the fresh news: today it comes almost entirely from the public sector. This week, the state is the buyer.

The hook comes from Germany. The innovation agency SPRIND puts one hundred twenty-five million euros on the table to fund a European frontier lab through a staged competition — a lab that builds models at the absolute capability frontier, the most expensive class. The scale has to be put in perspective honestly: that's roughly what a top American lab burns through in compute in a few days. The counter-check is the one fresh, measured number. The IAB establishment panel shows generative-AI use in German firms rose from five to twenty-five percent in two years, a fivefold jump. But the depth is missing — ninety percent use off-the-shelf software, only six percent build their own. Broad, but shallow.

The public sector acts in three ways this week. In the US, the health department puts an AI called AERO to work scanning the audit books of all fifty states over at least five years for fraud — up to terminating funds. In Japan, the government passes early access to Anthropic's newest model to the three megabanks; the market doesn't distribute the model, the state does. And three German states — North Rhine-Westphalia, Hesse and Baden-Württemberg — pool their sovereign administrative assistants in a "future alliance" for AI.

Then the spending side. Gartner raises its forecast of worldwide AI spending this year to two point five nine trillion dollars, up forty-seven percent. Important: that's a spending forecast model, not a returns survey — it measures what the vendors want to invest, not what reaches the customer. And the seemingly fresh adoption number from the US statistics bureau, eighteen percent, rests on data from half a year ago. Freshly released doesn't mean freshly collected. Beware too of a circulating Stack Overflow survey supposedly from this year — it doesn't exist.

From Brussels, nothing new this week, but a tight calendar. The consultation on the Article 50 transparency guidelines runs until June third, and the disclosure duty applies unchanged from August second. New only was the broad coverage of the planned ban on AI-generated intimate depictions without consent — part of the still-provisional Digital Omnibus.

From that, the project idea for twiceD, straight out of the sovereignty theme: a case-file assistant for the one small municipality or utility. It answers questions from the organization's own files, with citations, and runs entirely on its own hardware — the data never leaves the building. Sovereign administrative AI exists today at the top, with Aleph Alpha in the ministries, and in the middle, for instance with Baden-Württemberg's open-source tool F13. But the single small municipality can neither host nor operate any of it — it lacks the staff. That exact gap could be filled turnkey and operated, and precisely because F13 lies open and free, it's not a competitor but the building block. The honest caveat: operating one solution per organization is ongoing work, so it only makes sense as a fixed-price managed service, kept to one workflow.
