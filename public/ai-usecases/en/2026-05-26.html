Top Story · The Government Builds the Rails

€250M for a sovereign AI cloud — T-Systems, SAP and an SVA consortium

The Federal Ministry for Digital Affairs and State Modernisation (BMDS, minister Karsten Wildberger) has awarded a framework contract worth roughly €250M for a sovereign AI cloud for public administration. Deliberately split between two consortia: 70% to T-Systems + SAP, 30% to a consortium around SVA (with Schwarz Digits/Stackit and Codesphere) — a dual award for resilience. The first application is named "Kipitz": document processing, knowledge management, translations, and faster planning and permitting procedures. A competing challenge from Adesso + Google was withdrawn beforehand. heise.de · cio.de · handelsblatt.com

Sovereign AI cloud / PaaS: A "platform as a service" is the rented workbench on which agencies build their AI applications — compute, models and building blocks, without each office buying its own servers. "Sovereign" means the workbench sits under German/European control and the data never leaves the jurisdiction. Like a rented shared lab instead of a US-rented lab to which a foreign state holds the master key.
My take This is the logical continuation of the week: first SPRIND (money for a frontier lab), now the federal government with the foundation underneath. But read it carefully — an awarded tender is not yet a running benefit; "Kipitz" is an announcement, not a measured agency saving. For twiceD the lesson stays the same as yesterday, only reinforced: value isn't created on the rails but in the trains — the concrete workflows one floor up that the federal mega-contract doesn't cover. The rails create demand but don't build a finished application for the single office.

Measured vs. Claimed

The week's sharpest number: 90% with no measurable effect

Against every spending curve stands an uncomfortable, measured set of findings. An NBER working paper (w34984, "AI, Productivity, and the Workforce") surveyed about 750 executives in the US, UK, Germany and Australia: 69% of firms use AI, but over 80% report no measurable productivity gain over three years, and around 89% saw no change in revenue per employee. The core finding: perceived gains far exceed measured ones. nber.org

Productivity paradox: Everyone feels it's going faster — but the gain doesn't show up in the year-end numbers. Like a new kitchen gadget that feels great but the time you save on cooking gets frittered away elsewhere by evening. The gap between "feels productive" and "is demonstrably more productive" is exactly the one most firms don't measure.
Methodology flag Academic, not a vendor commission (unlike the consultancy studies) — making it the more trustworthy measure. But date it honestly: the paper is from March 2026, so roughly 10 weeks old, not a May snapshot. Executive self-report, not a hard audit of the books. Still the best counterweight to vendor-funded euphoria, precisely because it cleanly separates perceived from measured.

What the exception looks like: Trinio shows real numbers

Right next to it, the counter-test — a rare, fresh story with measured values. Brazilian e-commerce AI Trinio raised R$32 million in a seed round (led by Hi Ventures). Its system routes orders in real time by stock, freight and delivery time. Customer-reported results: +10–15% checkout conversion, −25s average checkout time, −5–15% logistics/operating cost. Named customers: Vivara, Osklen, Reserva, Asics, Casa & Video (50 customers total). exame.com

Methodology flag The figures are customer-reported and relayed via the vendor, not an independent audit — so read with the usual caution. But unlike "$2.59T in spend" or "50+ assistants", this is at least a read-off business value with before/after, not a forecast. Exactly the kind of number 90% of the NBER firms cannot show.

The study landscape fakes freshness

Anyone looking for fresh adoption evidence this week mostly finds relabelled old data. Stanford HAI AI Index 2026 (88% org adoption) compiles prior-year data; McKinsey "State of AI" (March) and Deloitte "State of AI in the Enterprise" (field period Aug–Sep 2025) are pure self-report surveys, months back. Destatis (20% of DE firms) is the Nov 2024 survey; the current round won't deliver until late 2026. nber.org · hai.stanford.edu

Caution: stale Freshly published doesn't mean freshly collected — last week's lesson from the Census BTOS figure still holds. For any adoption percentage this week, find the field period first, not the publication date. Where it's missing, don't sell the number as a May 2026 state.

Regulation · Law vs. Press Release

Brazil enacts what the EU is still negotiating

On 2026-05-21 Brazil published two decrees: 12.975 (Marco Civil, in force immediately) and 12.976, which bans AI-generated or -modified non-consensual intimate content and mandates technical blocking safeguards in AI applications — in force roughly 60 days after publication, i.e. around 2026-07-20. That is an enacted, dated law. gov.br

What to watch The contrast with the EU is the real point: the Digital Omnibus on AI would bring an almost identically worded nudify ban, but it remains provisional — the parliament vote is only expected by ~2026-07-07, and the ban wouldn't take effect until 2026-12-02. Brazil has a law this week; the EU has a statement of intent. Anyone building AI products with image generation in both markets now plans around the Brazilian date.

EU Omnibus: the deferrals get concrete

The provisional agreement of May 7 gets numbers: the Digital Omnibus would push the transparency/marking obligations to 2026-12-02, high-risk standalone (Annex III) to 2027-12-02, embedded high-risk systems (incl. medical devices) to 2028-08-02. What it explicitly does not defer: the GPAI obligations and enforcement on 2026-08-02. consilium.europa.eu · euronews.com

BaFin: shorter AI-driven IT inspections

Germany's BaFin announced (~May 13) shorter, more frequent "IT spotlight" inspections — a response to AI-accelerated cyber risks in the financial sector (BaFin president Branson). No new legal act, but tightened supervisory practice. resultsense.com

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with the market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · AI impact audit for the Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: A mid-size firm has introduced AI somewhere — a Copilot licence, a chatbot, a document tool — and believes it helps. It can't prove it. An "impact audit" cleanly measures exactly one workflow: how long did it take before, how long after, what does the AI tool cost, what does it really save? twiceD delivers a defensible before/after number at the end — and with it the decision to roll out or switch off. Value: no burnt licence budget on a tool that merely feels good, and a number management can argue with internally.
Prototype (V0): (1) Agree one concrete workflow with the customer (e.g. quote generation, complaint handling). (2) Measure the baseline without AI — time per case, error rate, cycle time; instrument via simple logs/time-tracking, no custom model needed. (3) Tap existing usage signals where they exist (Microsoft Copilot Analytics, tool-native telemetry). (4) A/B or before/after over 3–4 weeks. (5) A concise report with a number + recommendation. MVP deliberately narrow: one workflow, one customer, one metric, fixed price — no permanent platform, no org-wide dashboard.
Market & gap: The AI-consulting market is large (~$11–14B in 2026, ~24–29% CAGR),[1] but occupied at the top: BCG (10-20-70 framework, ~$3.6B AI revenue 2025), McKinsey, Accenture, Deloitte — enterprise-priced and aimed at portfolio strategy, not measuring one workflow.[2] The measurement tools below them measure the wrong thing: Arize, LangSmith, Galileo, Fiddler score model quality (faithfulness, drift), not business value.[3] The closest direct competitor, Olakai, is a self-service dashboard across tools — not a human instrumenting one customer workflow on site.[4] Gap: the small German Mittelstand firm that wants a hands-on proof for exactly its one process — and the NBER paradox says almost everyone needs it. Lever on top: BAFA funding reimburses 50–80% of consulting engagements (cap ~€3,500) — a precise fit for a fixed, small audit.[5]
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. It's advisory work with a clear start and end — zero ongoing maintenance, no hosted service, no GPU. A V0 audit is doable solo in 3–4 weeks, and the build-up (measurement templates, report skeleton) amortises across customers. Scaling cleanly via one contractor per engagement; none of it ever tips into a 10-person team. The only risk is sales, not operations — and BAFA eligibility lowers the customer's entry barrier. It's Tuesday, the twenty-sixth of May. The state stays the big AI buyer this week — but today one floor lower than before: this is about the infrastructure, the rails beneath the applications.

The lead comes from Berlin. The federal digital ministry has awarded a framework contract worth roughly two hundred fifty million euros for a sovereign AI cloud. Seventy percent goes to the Telekom subsidiary T-Systems together with SAP, thirty percent to a second consortium around a firm called SVA — deliberately split so that not everything hangs on one vendor. Sovereign means the data stays under German control. The first application is meant to process documents, unlock knowledge and speed up planning procedures. The key caveat: an awarded tender is not yet a measured benefit. For twiceD the value lies not on the rails but in the trains — the concrete workflows one floor up.

That brings us to the sharpest number of the week, and it's sobering. An academic working paper from the American NBER surveyed about seven hundred fifty executives across four countries, Germany among them. Sixty-nine percent use AI. But more than eighty percent see no measurable productivity gain, and around eighty-nine percent no change in revenue per employee. The core: the felt gain is far above the measured one. That's the productivity paradox — it feels faster, but at year-end the gain doesn't show up in the numbers. To be honest about it: the paper is from March, so about ten weeks old.

Right beside it, the rare exception. The Brazilian e-commerce firm Trinio raised thirty-two million reais and shows real before-and-after figures: ten to fifteen percent more completed purchases, a checkout twenty-five seconds faster, five to fifteen percent lower logistics costs. The numbers are customer-reported, so handle with care — but it's at least a read-off business value, not a forecast. Exactly the kind of number most firms in the NBER study cannot show. And a warning: many studies this week look fresh but rest on data from months ago. Freshly published doesn't mean freshly collected.

On the regulatory side, this week shows the difference between a law and a press release. Brazil has enacted, by decree, a ban on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery, complete with a technical blocking duty; it takes effect around the twentieth of July. The European Union is still negotiating an almost identical ban — the Digital Omnibus remains provisional, with the parliament vote only expected in early July. Brazil has the law, Brussels the statement of intent. Unchanged is the second of August: that's when the obligations for general-purpose AI models bite. And Germany's financial regulator BaFin announces shorter, more frequent IT inspections, because AI accelerates cyber risk.

Hence the project idea for twiceD, straight out of the NBER paradox: an impact audit for the Mittelstand. Instead of selling yet another AI tool, twiceD cleanly measures whether an already-introduced workflow really got faster or cheaper — a defensible before-and-after number instead of a good feeling. The big consultancies like BCG and McKinsey do this too, but enterprise-expensive and at portfolio level. The measurement tools beneath them measure model quality, not business value. The small German mid-size firm that wants a hands-on proof for exactly its one process is served by no one — and the state's BAFA programme covers most of the consulting cost. It's pure advisory work with a clear end, so zero ongoing maintenance and doable solo.
