Measured vs. spent

The US AI jump partly sits in the questionnaire

The US Census Bureau published fresh adoption figures yesterday from its Business Trends and Outlook Survey (BTOS): 19.8% of US firms use AI (as of May 3), firms with 250+ employees 37%, and 20–23% expect to adopt within the next six months. The decisive catch is in the fine print: the Bureau widened the question in November 2025 — from AI "in producing goods or services" to AI "in any business function." That breaks the older, narrower time series; part of the optically higher figure is a broader definition, not organic growth. census.gov · census.gov

Time-series break: If you measure the same thing for years and then change the question mid-stream, the before and after numbers are no longer directly comparable. Like counting only your running miles, then next month also counting your walking — your "weekly average" jumps even though you haven't gotten any faster. The question got bigger, not the performance.
Methodology flag This is the most trustworthy adoption source going: government, a sample of ~164,500 responding firms from a registry-based frame, not vendor-funded. But the 19.8% measures self-reported use of AI anywhere in the last two weeks — it says nothing about productivity or ROI. And the Bureau itself flags the question change. Stacking the number against 2024 values compares apples to a bigger pear.

The counter-check from the money side: +47%

Right next to it, the spending curve: Gartner forecasts (May 19) that worldwide AI spending will rise 47% in 2026 to $2.59T, more than 45% of it in infrastructure. That is not an adoption proof but a market model — it shows where money flows, not what it yields. Read against the flat, broadly-defined Census usage, it sharpens the week's picture: the tap is wide open, the measured impact lags behind. gartner.com

Methodology flag A vendor-proprietary forecast model (the Gartner report "Forecast: AI Spending, Worldwide"), not a survey — there are no respondents, only a projection. As evidence of benefit it's useless; as a contrast to the adoption figure it's telling.

And the ROI damper: cutting yields no return

Fitting alongside, a second Gartner finding: AI-driven layoffs create short-term budget room but no measurable return — cut rates were nearly identical at high-return and low-return firms. The gains concentrated where AI amplifies staff rather than replacing them. gartner.com

Methodology flag Vendor-proprietary, just 350 self-selected large-enterprise executives, "ROI" is self-assessed — and honestly dated: fielded in Q3 2025, published only on May 5, 2026. A classic "fresh release, stale collection" gap. Still a useful corrective to the replacement reflex.

Where AI actually ships

Two US healthcare roll-outs — read with care

On the deployment side, the most concrete stories this week come from US healthcare. Labcorp (lab diagnostics) launched "MyLabcorp," a HIPAA-compliant app that uses OpenAI models to explain lab results and track health trends — a real consumer application, not a pilot. In parallel, the ops platform Commure reports agentic AI across more than 500 healthcare organizations (3,000+ sites) and a $70M round at a ~$7B valuation. prnewswire.com · cxodigitalpulse.com

Agentic AI / ambient documentation: "Agentic" means the AI carries out a task across several steps on its own, instead of just answering a question — e.g. preparing and submitting a billing claim. "Ambient documentation" is the AI quietly listening in on the doctor-patient conversation and writing up the record itself. Like an assistant who takes notes and fills in the forms while the doctor talks.
Methodology flag Careful: at Labcorp all the percentages come from a vendor survey (e.g. "41% already use AI for lab results"), not an audit. Commure's "85% of revenue-cycle work without human intervention" is a company claim with no disclosed methodology, and the report ran via an industry aggregator, not the primary source. Awareness yes — proof no.

German angle: Schaeffler brings humanoids onto the floor

The most tangible German industrial point: bearings and automotive supplier Schaeffler intends to deploy humanoids from the startup "Humanoid" in an initial two German plants (box handling, manufacturing testing). Roll-out from Dec 2026 to Jun 2027, with a projected ramp to 1,000–2,000 robots across Schaeffler's sites by 2032. artificialintelligence-news.com

Physical AI / humanoid: Instead of just generating text, here the AI model controls a body with arms and legs — it "sees" the box, grips it and sets it down. The human-like build is meant to fit without rebuilding the factory: a robot that uses the same shelves and aisles as the workforce.
My take Of all today's deployments this is the one with the hardest reality check — but also the most distant number: the 1,000–2,000 is a projection to 2032, not a current installed base, and the report is from mid-May, not yesterday. Still the right signal for the German Mittelstand: physical AI is leaving the demo stage and getting roll-out dates. Not directly addressable for twiceD (hardware integration is a different game), but relevant as an industry thermometer.

Regulation · the clock runs to August

The EU finally says what "high-risk" means

The freshest regulatory point: on May 19 the EU Commission published its long-overdue draft guidelines on the classification of high-risk AI systems (Art. 6 of the AI Act) — with concrete examples of what counts as high-risk and what doesn't. These guidelines were actually due by Feb 2, 2026, so they close a noticeable gap before the high-risk regime starts. A public consultation runs until Jun 23. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

High-risk AI / Annex III: The AI Act sorts systems by risk. "High-risk" is the tier with the hard obligations (documentation, risk management, human oversight) — and Annex III is the list of use fields that automatically qualify: among others, hiring selection, credit decisions, critical infrastructure. Like a mandatory safety inspection that applies only to certain vehicle classes — the trick is knowing whether your system even falls into the class.

A transparency consultation and a BaFin nudge

Two shorter dates: the consultation on the draft Article 50 transparency guidelines (chatbot disclosure, deepfake labelling) closes on Jun 3; Article 50 itself applies on Aug 2. In Germany, BaFin insurance supervisor Julia Wiens (May 21) told insurers to implement the AI Act proactively rather than waiting for deadlines — no new legal act, but a sharper supervisory tone. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu · versicherungsmonitor.de

Omnibus + Brazil: quiet this week

The Digital Omnibus remains procedural: formal adoption by Parliament and Council is expected for Jun–Jul, ahead of the Aug 2 deadline; nothing substantively new since the provisional agreement of May 7. In Brazil, PL 2338 still sits in the Câmara with no dated vote. consilium.europa.eu

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with the market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · AI-Act classification & compliance triage for the Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: A mid-sized firm has an AI system in use somewhere — an applicant pre-screen, a credit-scoring aid, a maintenance forecast — and doesn't know whether it falls under the hard high-risk obligations of the EU AI Act that apply from Aug 2, 2026. A "triage" engagement answers exactly that one question with legal certainty: are you high-risk (Annex III), a GPAI obligee, or merely subject to transparency rules — and what is the minimum you must document and prove by August? Value: no expensive full consultancy for a system that turns out not to be high-risk, and no nasty surprise just before the deadline. Like a pre-inspection check that first tells you whether your vehicle even needs the test.
Prototype (V0): (1) With the customer, inventory the AI-system estate (often 1–3 systems, not 300). (2) Classify each system against a structured decision tree built from the Commission's fresh Art. 6 guidelines (May 19) + Annex III — the guidelines supply the examples, which saves you interpreting from scratch. (3) For each high-risk hit, a tight gap list against Art. 9/11/12 (risk management, documentation, logging). (4) A fixed-price report with a traffic-light verdict + the next 3–5 steps. MVP deliberately narrow: classification + gap list, no running governance platform, no continuous monitoring.
Market & gap: The AI-governance/compliance market is growing fast — Gartner sees AI data-governance spending alone at $492M in 2026, over $1B by 2030.[1] It's occupied by horizontal enterprise platforms — OneTrust, Credo AI, Holistic AI, IBM watsonx.governance, Modulos — that inventory and monitor whole AI portfolios, plus the big consultancies (PwC & co.) running enterprise-priced transformation projects.[2] Both serve the corporation with dozens of systems, not the Mittelständler with three. That's exactly where the pain is greatest: an appliedAI study of 106 AI systems already found in 2023 that 40% were unclear to classify (18% clearly high-risk) — and the Commission itself had expected only 5–15% to be high-risk.[3] That very ambiguity is what the new Art. 6 draft aims to address. Gap for twiceD: the hands-on first verdict for the German Mittelstand, with German contract language and a GDPR reflex, on-prem-aware (don't tip data into a US SaaS suite) — and BAFA-fundable as a tight advisory package.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Pure advisory work with a clear start and end — no hosted service, no GPU, zero ongoing maintenance. A V0 triage is doable solo in 2–3 weeks per customer; the build-up (decision tree from the guidelines, report template, gap checklists) amortizes across mandates and only ages when the guidelines change. Scaling cleanly via one contractor per engagement. The only real risk is the liability question on legal interpretation — scope it cleanly as "technical classification aid, not legal advice" and, when in doubt, pair with a law firm.
