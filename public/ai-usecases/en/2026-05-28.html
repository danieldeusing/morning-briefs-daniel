The consulting pyramid gets a frontier-lab core

KPMG deploys 276,000 employees to Claude

On May 19, KPMG announced a global alliance with Anthropic: Claude is embedded in KPMG Digital Gateway—the central, Azure-based platform where KPMG consultants work and deliver to clients. Reach: 276,000+ employees in 138 countries. The first wave addresses tax and legal ("compliance research that used to take weeks now in minutes"—KPMG Tax Vice Chair Rema Serafi), plus cybersecurity vulnerability work and code modernization via Claude Code in PE portfolio firms. Anthropic also names KPMG a preferred PE partner. On May 26, Fortune contextualized the story around the Tax Summit in Miami as a "Big-Four AI alliance." anthropic.com · kpmg.com · fortune.com

Digital Gateway / "Cowork" / "Managed Agents": Digital Gateway is KPMG's internal toolkit—what Microsoft Office is to a general clerk, Digital Gateway is to a KPMG consultant. Claude sits inside it now: "Cowork" is the chatbot sidekick that reasons along, "Managed Agents" are small AI robots that run an entire task (e.g., tax research) on their own. Like a junior employee who preps everything before the senior partner starts.

PwC one week earlier: 30,000 certified and an "Office of the CFO"

Just a week earlier, on May 14, PwC expanded its Anthropic partnership: Claude Code and Claude Cowork are rolling out across the US workforce—with explicit goal "hundreds of thousands" globally—30,000 US professionals certified, a joint Center of Excellence in place. PwC is also launching an Office of the CFO to serve regulated sectors (banking, insurance, healthcare). Reported customer outcomes (Anthropic PR, no audit): insurance underwriting 10 weeks → 10 days, a COBOL-mainframe project "four times larger than planned" staying on schedule, incident response "hours → minutes," average "up to 70%" delivery improvement. anthropic.com · pwc.com

Methodology flag The "10 weeks → 10 days" and the 70% come from Anthropic's own PR with PwC confirmation—no independent audit source, no disclosed before-and-after comparison, no named customer. What's real: the workforce scale (30,000 + path to scale) and the new CFO business. The ROI numbers are the story being sold.

OpenAI DeployCo: the pyramid finances its own middle-tier replacement

On May 11, OpenAI launched the OpenAI Deployment Company ("DeployCo")—a majority OpenAI–controlled subsidiary with $4 billion in external funding at $14 billion post-money valuation. Mission: station OpenAI engineers in large enterprises (healthcare, logistics, manufacturing, finance) and convert workflows into production AI systems—exclusively on OpenAI models. Lead investor TPG; co-leads Advent, Bain Capital, Brookfield; also Goldman Sachs, SoftBank, BBVA, Warburg Pincus. Notably: McKinsey & Co., Bain & Co., and Capgemini are on the investor list—exactly the consulting firms whose delivery work the vehicle will cannibalize. Operating core: the Tomoro acquisition with ~150 Forward-Deployed Engineers from day one; Axios cites a guaranteed minimum return of 17.5% with capped upside. openai.com · axios.com

Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE): Instead of a consultant delivering a PowerPoint deck, a software engineer who sits on-site for weeks, actually wiring the AI system into the customer's databases and tools. Palantir made the role famous—the job lives on bridging the last mile between model and customer system with code chops.
Our take KPMG and PwC lock into Anthropic, OpenAI builds a delivery subsidiary with McKinsey + Capgemini as co-investors, EY stays with Microsoft. This isn't "tooling choice"—it's the consulting industry's delivery layer being rewired. For twiceD: the frontier-lab stack is now the default for enterprises—"affordable and right-sized for midmarket" is nobody's problem. The gap stays open.

The layer above: process infrastructure goes agentic

Camunda ProcessOS: four agents instead of six months of discovery

On May 20, at CamundaCon (1,200 attendees from 25 countries), Camunda launched ProcessOS in Closed Beta—an AI-driven intelligence layer over the existing Camunda platform. Four agents collaborate: Discover (analyzes real workflows), Re-engineer (designs AI-native processes), Build (builds and deploys), Optimize (improves continuously). Camunda itself sets the speed targets: discovery 2–3 months → 1–2 weeks, re-engineering 1–2 months → 1 week, build/deploy 3–6 months → 2–4 weeks. Early evidence: at Danica (insurance), customer onboarding went "months → days"; at R-KOM (telecom/IT), ticket response "8 h → near-instant." businesswire.com · camunda.com

BPM / process orchestration: Business Process Management is the boring backbone behind "order arrives → gets checked → gets booked → triggers shipping." Tools like Camunda draw it as a flowchart and run it mechanically. "Agentic" now makes each step not rule-based but lets an AI agent decide what's next—e.g., "this invoice looks weird, ask again." Like a factory floor controller that doesn't just count steps but sometimes makes its own call on exceptions.
Methodology flag Closed Beta means: no public run, no independent comparison, the cited before-and-after numbers are Camunda's own customer stories from launch PR. The scale is plausible (BPM market has been drifting this direction for years), but today nobody knows the median. That said, this is the most important story of the week outside the consulting deals: BPM meets agents, and Camunda is playing it with BPMN standard plus AWS/Bedrock hookup—not in a proprietary cage.

Salesforce Agentforce Coworker: AI in every search bar

On May 21, Salesforce announced Agentforce Coworker (Beta)—an AI sidekick living in the Salesforce search bar, also available in Slack, Teams, or ChatGPT, with access to CRM data, workflows, customer history, and open cases. Available immediately in Enterprise, Unlimited, and Agentforce 1 editions. Marc Benioff positioned it as "your new AI coworker in every search bar." salesforceben.com

Our take Salesforce is pitching the same promise as KPMG/PwC, just from the platform angle. Two-way squeeze: top frontier labs flowing through consultants into enterprises, bottom SaaS platforms with embedded agents as default—before consultants can bring in an alternative stack. For midmarket: neither direction is optimized for 200-person firms.

SAP context in the background

Just a reminder—SAP unveiled at Sapphire (May 12–14) the "Autonomous Enterprise" with over 50 Joule Assistants and over 200 specialized agents; RISE customers contractually get three Joule Assistants in year one, GROW customers 20+ from day one, a €100-million partner fund funds rollout partners. Forrester calls it "credible, but it comes with concentration risk." The stack around KPMG/PwC/Camunda only makes sense together with this SAP picture. news.sap.com · forrester.com

Skepticism layer: is anyone actually using this?

Bitkom: DE adoption doubled, but pricier than expected

The Bitkom AI Study 2026 (field weeks 2–6 / 2026, published March 11, n = 604 firms with 20+ employees) gives the most solid DE adoption number: 41% of German companies are actively deploying AI—against 17% in 2024, a real doubling. Firms with 500+ employees hit >60%; midmarket is catching up. What rarely gets quoted: 33% report AI is "more expensive than expected," and 19% have cut headcount as a result. bitkom.org

Our take The most honest number in this brief: no government funding, good sample, asks "what did it cost." The 33% "more expensive than expected" speaks loud where KPMG/PwC/OpenAI are just scaling rollout. For twiceD, that's the door: midmarket that tested AI and learned the enterprise full-stack won't fit, searching for someone who just delivers the tight workflow.

KPMG/UT Austin: 5% of users show high-impact patterns

From the same Fortune story covering the KPMG alliance, the most important skepticism number: a joint KPMG/UT Austin study of 1.4 million real AI interactions found that only 5% delivered "meaningful outcomes." KPMG sells the number as upside ("95% headroom"), read honestly it's the effectiveness gap beneath the entire rollout wave: the delivery stack is being built without clarity on what it delivers. fortune.com

Methodology flag Watch out: "1.4 million interactions" sounds audited, but it's a self-published research note from KPMG with an academic co-author. What "meaningful outcome" means, KPMG defined themselves. Still, the right scale: all serious observers (Bitkom, McKinsey "1% AI-mature," Census-BTOS data) draw the same picture—lots of usage, little cleanly attributable impact.

EU regulation: quiet, but the clock is running

Regulation-wise nothing new since yesterday, but two tight deadlines stay in the pipeline: the Art. 50 transparency consultation (chatbot disclosure, deepfake labeling) closes June 3, the Art. 6 high-risk consultation on June 23 (10 p.m. CET)—the latter being what the Commission has been missing since Feb 2. Both proceed unchanged; the real lever is Aug. 2, when GPAI enforcement and Art. 50 transparency kick in. digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

Brazil quiet: PL 2338 advancing in Chamber

Brazil's AI framework PL 2338 (Senate approved Dec. 10, 2024) sits in the Chamber with no scheduled vote. A phased timeline similar to LGPD is expected; operational planning horizons no earlier than end 2026. ANPD stays SIA coordinator. whitecase.com

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gaps and honest solo buildability

1 · Forward-Deployed Engineering as a Service for German midmarket

ELI5 & Value: A German midmarket firm (50–500 employees) sees KPMG, PwC, and SAP Joule Agents in the press, wants to transform one concrete workflow—invoice intake, first-response support, bid pre-check—and realizes: KPMG is too expensive, OpenAI's DeployCo won't pick up the phone (enterprise size only), SAP/Salesforce agents are generic. A twiceD forward-deployed engagement puts someone on-site for three to six weeks to deconstruct that one workflow, wire it to an existing model, and hand it over ready for production. Value: no 10-year platform license, no 200-slide strategy deck, one working workflow. Like a mechanic retooling a single machine in the factory floor, not redesigning the whole plant.
Prototype (V0): (1) Engagement template: 3 weeks discovery + 3 weeks build + 2 weeks handover, fixed price. (2) Narrow tool selection: Anthropic Claude or OpenAI as cloud default, vLLM/Ollama + Llama-3.x or Qwen as on-prem option, Camunda Community or n8n as workflow layer, LlamaIndex/LangGraph for retrieval. (3) Three pre-built workflow modules: "invoice intake → ERP," "email triage first response," "bid pre-check." (4) Handover package: architecture sketch, data flow diagram, runbook, test set. MVP tight: one workflow, one location, human-in-loop before any external action.
Market & Gap: AI consulting market growing from $11.07 billion in 2025 to projected $91 billion in 2035 (CAGR ~26%).​[1] Supply side consolidating visibly: KPMG (Anthropic, 276k), PwC (Anthropic, 30k certified), OpenAI DeployCo ($4 billion, McKinsey/Bain/Capgemini as co-investors).​[2][3] All targeting enterprise-scale deals—Bitkom shows the catch-up happening in midmarket and 33% of AI-using firms say "more expensive than expected."​[4] Competitors in the niche (appliedAI, university spinouts, Adesso, SAP/Camunda implementation partners) don't cover all three axes at once: on-prem-capable + fixed-price engagement + single workflow in scope. Gap for twiceD: the tight, hands-on fixed-price delivery block with a clear on-prem path (sovereignty as honest sales argument before Aug. 2).
Buildability (solo + AI): High. V0 solo in 6–10 weeks per customer—three weeks on-site, rest remote, one contractor per module. Maintenance light, since project-based not hosted service. Scale via contractor pool; the three workflow modules amortize by customer three. Real risks: (a) sales lift (midmarket flows through chambers of commerce/trade assocs, not LinkedIn ads); (b) liability on production—fence it clearly as "build service, not ongoing ops"—optionally sell a 4–12 week maintenance block afterward. [1] researchandmarkets via businesswire.com · [2] anthropic.com · [3] axios.com · [4] bitkom.org

2 · ProcessOS adapter for midmarket—the open alternative to Camunda

ELI5 & Value: Camunda's ProcessOS promises to shift business processes from hand-coding to "describe what you want and we'll build it"—Closed Beta, enterprise pricing. A midmarket firm running Camunda Community or n8n thinks: this would help my purchase workflow or ticket system, but the ProcessOS ticket is out of reach. A twiceD variant builds the small version of the same idea on open source: a Discover/Re-engineer/Build/Optimize quartet tuned to a single process (purchase order, onboarding, service ticket), delivered with BPMN diagram + runbook. Value: same direction of impact as ProcessOS, but running on your own infrastructure, no cloud lock-in.
Prototype (V0): (1) Stack: Camunda 7/8 Community or n8n as workflow engine, LangGraph or CrewAI for agent orchestration, vLLM + Llama-3.x or Qwen-Coder for on-prem path, Anthropic/OpenAI as cloud alternative. (2) "Discover" agent that reconstructs steps from logs/emails (one week on-site). (3) "Re-engineer" step where you co-review the BPMN proposal. (4) "Build/Optimize" pair with human-in-loop. MVP tight: one process, one data source, no ERP write-ops without approval.
Market & Gap: BPM market 2026 ~$18.7 billion, CAGR ~20.7%.​[5] Consolidation platforms >$21 billion by 2029.​[6] Competitors in the agentic process layer: Camunda ProcessOS, Pega, Appian, Automation Anywhere, UiPath with GenAI add-ons.​[7] All hitting enterprises, all with proprietary components in the smart layer. Gap: BPMN standard instead of proprietary, on-prem without SaaS lock-in, tight engagement vs. 6-month platform rollout. For GDPR-sensitive midmarket or data that can't leave a site, hard differentiation.
Buildability (solo + AI): Medium. Higher than idea 1 because a standard toolkit needs maintenance (open-source components age, on-prem models need updates). V0 solo (8–12 weeks, re-use from customer 2 onward), but maintenance is real—estimate 4–8 hrs/week on model/library upkeep, plus one paid maintenance block per production customer. Risk: "I'm shipping open-source pieces" slides into "I'm the one person keeping this system alive"—fence it clearly as "build + bounded maintenance, then customer-owned or local IT vendor"—.
