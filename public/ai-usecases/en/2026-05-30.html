Search AI becomes default for one billion people

The biggest use-case news from Google I/O 2026 (May 19) is not a new model but a switch: AI Mode — Google's AI-answer mode in search — has "surpassed one billion monthly active users, with queries more than doubling each quarter," and now runs globally on Gemini 3.5 Flash as the default model.[1] Two independent Google blogs cite the same billion-user figure.[1][2]

The point for Daniel: applied AI no longer needs its own product to lure users into — it is the search box everyone uses anyway. This raises the bar for any twiceD solution with end-user touch: if the generic answer is free in the search field, the added value must lie elsewhere — in tying to real company data and workflows.

Antigravity mini-apps Google showed "mini-apps for your own tasks": search builds on-the-fly dashboards, tables, and simulations via Antigravity + Gemini 3.5 Flash. Rollout "in the coming months," US AI Pro/Ultra first.[1] Generative UI on demand — interesting as a pattern, not yet as an available tool.

TD Bank: mortgage pre-adjudication from 15 hours to 3 minutes

Finance The week's cleanest named customer story comes from Canada: TD Bank (Recap, May 21) launched its first agentic AI model for mortgage and HELOC pre-adjudication. Early results: pre-adjudication drops "from an average of 15 hours to an average of less than three minutes".[3]

Pre-adjudication: the pre-approval phase before final credit decision — sorting documents, calculating income, checking numbers against policy, hunting contradictions, then producing a summary for the underwriter. Like an assistant who preps the whole file before a person decides.
Documents in (PDF, scan)→ Agent: classify, calculate income, check policy→ Summary for underwriter→ 15 h → <3 min

Important for skeptics: humans still decide — the agent checks beforehand, it does not adjudicate. This exact pattern (narrowly bounded step, human in loop, hard before-after number from a named bank) is generalizable and becomes twiceD idea #2 below.

US federal mandate collapsed — governance is now self-service

Policy The week's second big story is one that didn't happen. The Trump administration postponed an executive order hours before signature (~May 20–21) that would have set up a voluntary 90-day pre-release security review for frontier models.[7] Rationale per Axios: simple aversion to regulation; Musk and Zuckerberg called Trump the night before.

CAISI: the "Center for AI Standards and Innovation" at US standards body NIST. A kind of TÜV station for AI models — labs voluntarily test their models there before release. The order would have made it an expected mandatory stop; without it, it stays voluntary and non-binding.

What's left is a bottomless gap: no US federal mandate, instead voluntary CAISI commitments (Google DeepMind, Microsoft, xAI named May 5, details later removed from the web).[8] The result — the standard shifts to US states (California, Colorado, Illinois) and the EU.[9] That vacuum is exactly where OpenAI stepped in.

OpenAI Frontier Governance Framework: voluntary because law is absent

Reg · US/EU On Thursday (May 28) OpenAI published its Frontier Governance Framework — a public document explicitly mapping its own safety and security practice to two real regulatory regimes: California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act (SB 53) and the EU AI Act's GPAI Code of Practice.[10] It covers four risk categories: cyber offense, CBRN, manipulation, and loss of control, and builds on OpenAI's existing Preparedness Framework.[11]

SB 53 / Transparency in Frontier AI Act: California's frontier AI law, in force since Jan 1, 2026. "Large frontier developers" (revenue > $500M, models over 10²⁶ computational operations) must publish a public AI safety framework and report critical incidents; up to $1M fine per violation.[12]

My reading: timing is no coincidence. One week after the US federal mandate died, OpenAI publishes voluntarily — but addresses two binding regimes (CA + EU), not the defunct federal level. Governance migrates away from the feds into states and Europe, and labs follow the strictest binding floor. For anyone deploying AI in the EU, the lesson: compliance pressure comes not from Washington but from Brussels and Sacramento — and no one delivers a ready-made evidence package.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI-Act evidence pack for DE mid-market

Pitch & value: When a mid-sizer deploys AI (chatbot, document agent, scoring), EU AI Act and — if US-bound — California law demand proof: what does the system do, what risks, what testing. This week shows the trend: US federal mandate vanished, so real pressure migrates to Brussels and Sacramento, and even OpenAI now documents voluntarily.[10] Customer value: a ready, audit-grade evidence package instead of Excel scrambling and expensive law-firm hours — avoided fine risk (EU high-risk violations sanctionable from Aug 2, 2026) and audit safety.
Prototype (V0): (1) One real customer AI use case as pilot (e.g., a support chatbot). (2) Risk tier against EU AI Act Annex and transparency obligations (Art. 50). (3) Evidence collection semi-automated: system map, test logs, data provenance into a template — building blocks: existing policy packs (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001) as template, an LLM to pre-fill documentation. (4) Audit-ready PDF + update routine. MVP deliberately narrow: one use case, one regime (EU AI Act), no multi-tenant SaaS.
Market & gap: Mature platforms exist — Modulos, Credo AI, Holistic AI, Trustible (deepest EU AI Act coverage 2026), plus incumbents like IBM watsonx.governance, ServiceNow, OneTrust. Governance market ≈ $2.55B in 2026 (from $2.20B in 2025), forecast $11.05B by 2036, ~15.8% CAGR.[14] But: prices ~$50K/year (mid-market) to hundreds of thousands, US-centric, self-service platform — customer must map their own case and fill it in.[14] Gap for twiceD: hands-on delivery not platform sub, German contract/GDPR reflexes, on-prem for sensitive data, fixed price for one use case — exactly what a 50-person machine builder needs and won't pay $100K for a platform.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Core is advisory + template work — near-zero ongoing lift per engagement (one-shot audit + annual refresh). V0 in 3–5 weeks solo, scaling "one contractor per wave of engagements." Risk: liability questions — position cleanly as documentation/prep, not legal advice. futuremarketinsights.com · modulos.ai · fpf.org

2 · Agentic pre-check workflow for finance/insurance mid-market

Pitch & value: At credit unions, insurance brokers, and leasing houses, applications land as PDF stacks (salary slips, balance sheets, policy requests) and get reviewed by hand. A "pre-check agent" reads the file, calculates (income, ratios), checks against policy, hunts contradictions, and hands the underwriter a ready summary — the human decides. Value: TD Bank squeezed this exact pre-check from 15 hours to under 3 minutes.[3] Savings come from expensive underwriter time freed up, error rate drops.
Prototype (V0): (1) One real customer application stack as test set. (2) Extraction via existing IDP model (layout + fields), don't build it. (3) Rule engine: compute income/ratios, check against customer policy, flag contradictions. (4) Summary generator for the reviewer. MVP tight: one app type, one customer, human confirms before any decision — no auto-adjudication. Building blocks: AWS Textract / Google Document AI / Rossum as extraction layer, an LLM for the summary.
Market & gap: IDP market is large (2026 estimates range ~$3–14B by scope) with mature vendors — ABBYY, UiPath, Rossum, IBM, Hyperscience plus hyperscalers (AWS Textract, Azure AI Document Intelligence, Google Document AI). Finance/insurance is ~32.7% of the largest segment; Deloitte pegs cost savings in financial services at 50–70%.[15] Vendors are horizontal extraction platforms — the decision logic (policy check, contradiction hunt, underwriter summary) the customer builds. Gap for twiceD: the last mile — rule-bound pre-check for one concrete DE mid-market workflow, on-prem for sensitive financial data, no per-page SaaS sub.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium–high. Heavy model bought in; twiceD builds rule engine + wiring + ops. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo. Higher ongoing lift than idea 1 (hosting, model drift, new app types) — stays solo-manageable as long as scope stays tight; scale via contractor per customer. On-prem/open-model variant (local OCR + open LLM) is the sovereignty lever against hyperscalers. td.mediaroom.com · coherentmarketinsights.com · futurevault.com

It's Saturday, May thirtieth. Eleven days after Google I/O, applied artificial intelligence shows itself in two speeds — the product races, the rules stall.

Product side first. Google made its AI answer mode in search, AI Mode, the standard — for more than one billion people a month, and the number of queries doubles every quarter by Google's count. Artificial intelligence is no longer its own product, it's just the search box everyone uses anyway. For twiceD that means: you can't outrun the free standard answer, the value has to come from real company data and workflows.

What that looks like in practice, TD Bank in Canada shows us. Its new software agents cut the pre-review of mortgage applications from an average of fifteen hours to under three minutes. Important: the human still decides, the agent only preps the file. That exact pattern — a tightly bounded step, human in the loop, a hard before-after number from a named bank — works as a template.

In the big picture too the center of gravity tilts toward business customers. Enterprise now makes up more than forty percent of OpenAI's revenue. And Anthropic is certifying thirty thousand consultants at PwC on its models. Both companies send engineers straight into customer shops — that's headwind for twiceD, the room stays with mid-market and on-prem operation.

Now the rules side, and here it stalls. The US government postponed an order just days beforehand that would have brought voluntary security review for powerful AI models before release — hours before signature. What stays is a voluntary test station called CAISI at the standards institute. So the US loses the federal mandate, and the standard migrates to individual states and to Europe.

Right into that vacuum stepped OpenAI on Thursday with its Frontier Governance Framework. The document lays bare how its own security practice works — and points not to Washington, but to California's frontier law and the European AI code of conduct. My reading: whoever deploys AI in Europe gets compliance pressure from Brussels and Sacramento, not Washington — and no one ships the ready evidence package for it.

From that flow two twiceD ideas. First, a ready evidence package for German mid-market that documents an AI deployment audit-ready for the EU AI Act — the big platforms like Credo AI or Holistic AI run from fifty thousand dollars a year up and leave the customer to assemble everything themselves. Second, an agentic pre-check workflow for finance and insurance mid-market shops, in the spirit of the TD Bank story — the last mile into a concrete process, which extraction platforms leave open.
