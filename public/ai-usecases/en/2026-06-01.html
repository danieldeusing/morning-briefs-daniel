The big shift: applied AI becomes a delivery business

When you stack the May announcements side by side, a pattern emerges that matters more than any single launch: AI labs have stopped selling only models and APIs, and have started putting people in the customer's office. The bottleneck is no longer model quality — it is the people who can actually fit an AI system into real business processes.

Forward-Deployed Engineer (FDE): an engineer who does not build products at their own company, but sits on site with the customer and fits the technology into their concrete workflows. Picture: not a furniture store selling you a shelf — but a carpenter who comes to your house, measures, and installs it. The term originally comes from Palantir; now every major AI lab is copying it.

The numbers show how hot the role is: postings for FDEs surged roughly 800% between January and September 2025, according to an Indeed/FT analysis. Salesforce alone committed to a team of 1,000 FDEs and set up a partner network (Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, IBM Consulting, Slalom and others) — according to Salesforce, these partners already drive a third of all successful Agentforce rollouts.

My take: This is the strategically most important story for twiceD in months — more important than any model update. The labs are saying with their money that the real value lies in fitting it in, not in the model itself. Exactly that "last mile" is twiceD's territory (see project ideas below).

Meta enters the FDE race late

The latest confirmation came this week: Meta is building a new unit called Enterprise Solutions, according to an internal memo (reported by The Information on May 27). It will embed product managers (who lead customer relationships), data engineers (who prep customer data for Meta's AI), and software engineers (who wire Meta's tools into customer systems) directly with major customers.

Naomi Gleit, Meta's product chief, writes in the memo that the team should build "repeatable playbooks and tooling" so the work scales. The honest subtext: Meta needs to make revenue sense of its massive AI infrastructure spending to investors — and it is late to the game. As one analysis puts it, "every major AI lab now treats this approach as mandatory."

My take: The fact that Meta — hardly an enterprise heavyweight until now — is jumping on this train is the clearest signal that the embedded-engineer model is now industry standard, not experiment. For a small vendor, that means two things: the market is validated (good), but the big players are moving in (caution). The gap stays where the giants do not go — the German mid-market firm below their minimum deal size.

Anthropic & OpenAI: two paths, the same bet

The two leading labs set up structural delivery units almost in lockstep — with different architecture, but the same logic.

Anthropic (May 4) took the joint-venture route with Wall Street: Together with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs (plus Apollo, General Atlantic, GIC, Sequoia), a standalone enterprise AI services firm is being formed. Capital commitments around $1.5B — roughly $300M each from Anthropic, Blackstone, and H&F, roughly $150M each from Goldman and General Atlantic. It places Anthropic's "Applied AI Engineers" in mid-sized firms — starting with companies owned by the participating private equity houses. Anthropic's own description: "A typical engagement starts with a small team working closely with the customer to figure out where Claude has the most impact."

Private Equity / PE-owned firm: investment firms (here Blackstone, H&F) buy whole companies and want to make them more valuable. The JV gets these portfolio companies as its first, guaranteed customer base — a built-in market of hundreds of mid-market firms where Claude can be rolled out directly. Anthropic is openly attacking the traditional consulting industry (McKinsey, Accenture & Co.).

OpenAI (May 11) instead created a majority-owned subsidiary, OpenAI Deployment Company — over $4B in starting capital from 19 investors, plus the acquisition of consulting firm Tomoro (~150 engineers). The FDEs "work with leaders and operational teams, identify impactful use cases, redesign workflows, and build reliable systems."

My take: Anthropic rents a pre-built customer base through Wall Street; OpenAI buys the engineers and keeps control. Both are expensive admissions of the same thing — without hands on the ground, a brilliant model does not become a productive process.

The demand engine: Enterprise > 40% at OpenAI

Why all the delivery buildup? Because enterprise money is real. OpenAI's CRO Denise Dresser said in mid-May that the business unit now accounts for more than 40% of revenue — with "incredible momentum" toward parity with the consumer business by year-end.

Methodology flag This is a self-reported figure by the vendor in an investor context (ahead of a potential IPO), not an audited balance sheet. "> 40% of revenue" and "> 1M business customers" are company claims from a Bloomberg interview — strong as directional signal, read as exact numbers with caution. Context: ~$25B annualized revenue (Feb 2026), > 50M consumer subscriptions.

My take: This one number explains everything else in this brief. As long as the business is growing this fast, any expensive delivery unit pays for itself — and the bottleneck shifts from "do we have a good model?" to "can we get it working at enough customers fast enough?". The latter is craft, and craft sells.

Gemini Spark: applied AI for end users

The counterpoint to the enterprise wave: At Google I/O on May 19, Google unveiled Gemini Spark — the first true 24/7 personal agent from a major vendor. Spark runs on dedicated cloud VMs and keeps working "even when your laptop is closed" (Sundar Pichai). You can send it tasks by email; it researches, compares, books, and completes multi-step jobs in Gmail, Docs, and via the MCP protocol in third-party apps too (Canva, OpenTable, Instacart at launch).

24/7 personal agent: not a chat window that only responds when you type — but an assistant that works like an intern in the background while you are offline, and lets you know when it is done. The difference from earlier assistants: it sustains tasks over hours, not just answers single questions.

Available initially to US subscribers of the priciest tier, "AI Ultra" ($100/month), age 18 and up.

My take: Spark shows that "always-on agent" is landing in the consumer space too — but the price reveals it is still a premium experiment, not a mass product. For twiceD, the more useful lesson: that exact MCP integration with third-party apps is the integration work that companies do not want to do themselves.

Regulation (DE): Bundesnetzagentur takes over

For German users concretely: With the AI Market Supervision and Innovation Promotion Act (KI-MIG), the Bundesnetzagentur — with exceptions — becomes the national market regulator for AI systems. BaFin keeps financial AI; the EU AI Office keeps general-purpose AI models (GPAI).

Market regulator: the office that checks afterward whether an AI system deployed in Germany meets EU rules — and can impose fines. Picture: the TÜV for AI, except not before you sell it, but during live operation.

The EU AI Act deadline for many obligations remains August 2, 2026. If you run agentic workflows in personnel, finance, or health by then, you now have a concrete German contact — and a concrete reason to document your own AI use.

My take: For most mid-market firms, the EU AI regulation is still an abstract bogeyman. Exactly this mix of real deadline and missing know-how is consulting demand — cleanly legitimized by a named authority (see project idea 2).

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · "Forward-Deployed Engineer" for German mid-market

ELI5 & Value: The major labs just proved with billions of dollars that the money is not in the model, but in the person who fits it at the customer's site — the Forward-Deployed Engineer. That is exactly twiceD's natural role: go in, rebuild one concrete process (quote approval, returns, goods receipt) agentic-ally with an off-the-shelf model and get it running at the customer. Value: a tough, half-manual workflow gets compressed from days to hours — with clear before-and-after, without the customer needing to build its own AI team.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick one painful workflow with the customer and real documentation/task set as test case. (2) Build agent on an existing model (Claude/GPT via API), wire tool integrations via MCP to their existing systems. (3) Insert human-confirm gates at critical points. (4) Measure against old cycle time. MVP deliberately tight: one process, one customer, no firm-wide rollout — and nothing home-grown that exists as a building block (model, MCP connectors, agent frameworks like LangGraph).

The vendor roster is heavyweight: OpenAI Deployment Company, Anthropic's Wall Street JV, Google Cloud FDEs, Meta's Enterprise Solutions, Salesforce with 1,000 FDEs plus partner network (Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, IBM Consulting). But all target large customers with minimum deal sizes a German 80-person machine builder will never hit. twiceD's gap: the mid-market firm below the enterprise threshold, who wants a reachable contact instead of a corporate supply contract — DE contract terms, GDPR by reflex, on-prem where needed.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High — this is literally twiceD's business model, now validated by the big players as a multi-billion category. The heavy model comes from the platform; twiceD delivers integration and operational handoff. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo, scaling "one contractor per customer." 2 · AI Act compliance package ahead of the August deadline

ELI5 & Value: As of August 2, 2026, many obligations of the EU AI Regulation take effect, and in Germany the Bundesnetzagentur becomes the regulator. Many mid-market firms already use AI (in personnel screening, credit review, customer service) without knowing if it counts as "high-risk" or what to document. twiceD delivers a lean compliance package: AI inventory, risk rating per application, and a to-do list plus minimum documentation. Value: avoided fine risk (cap €35M / 7% revenue) and peace of mind before the regulator asks.
Prototype (V0): (1) Questionnaire and workshop that lists all a customer's AI applications (including SaaS tools with built-in AI). (2) Rate each application against AI Act risk classes — an AI-powered classification assistant can pre-sort here, humans decide. (3) Per high-risk app, minimum dossier (purpose, data, human oversight, logging). (4) Handoff plus one training session. MVP tight: one firm, audit and dossier, no running compliance ops.
Market & Gap: The action need is real and named — expert bodies explicitly warn of "mid-market action need," and Bundesnetzagentur is the named authority (KI-MIG). Big law firms and Big Four consultancies offer AI Act compliance — but at enterprise rates for enterprise complexity, not for the 50-person shop. twiceD's gap: an affordable, lean package for SMBs that actually does risk-rating instead of just shipping a white paper — technically grounded (twiceD understands how the AI it is rating works), not just legal.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. Mostly advisory and documentation — almost no running maintenance, clearly scoped deliverable. V0 as a pilot audit in 2–3 weeks solo, scaling "one contractor per customer." Risk/limit: twiceD provides technical rating and documentation, not legal advice — refer real legal questions to a law firm.
