AI as vulnerability hunter: Glasswing goes big

Today's applied AI story is not a chatbot demo, but security. Anthropic has expanded its Project Glasswing — launched in April with roughly 50 partners — to about 150 more organizations across more than 15 countries.[1] Partners get access to Claude Mythos, Anthropic's strongest model by their account, and let it comb through their own code bases to find vulnerabilities and generate patches before attackers find those same holes.

Zero-day vulnerability: a security hole in software that nobody knows about yet, and for which there's no patch — the vendor had "zero days" to respond. Picture: an unlocked back window the homeowner doesn't even know exists. Mythos searches vast amounts of code for exactly these windows — and can, by Anthropic's account, "identify thousands of zero-days over several weeks".[2]

New to the mix: sectors that barely showed up in the first round: power, water, health, comms and hardware.[1] Many partners are suppliers — firms whose code runs inside countless other organizations (and governments). Anthropic's sober risk framing: "For most partners we estimate that a major attack could impact more than 100 million people."[1] So far initial partners together have found more than 10,000 severe or critical holes.[1]

My take: This is the most tangible "AI in live ops" case yet — not a promise, but 10,000 real finds. And it's the rare case where the use case itself is the story: the same ability that protects a power grid operator would, in the wrong hands, make that grid attackable. Exactly this dual-use is why the second story of the day exists.

Who gets Mythos now: NATO, ENISA, Okta, Samsung

The partner list brings the scope into focus. Among the new organizations with Mythos access are the US identity service Okta, South Korean conglomerates Samsung, SK Hynix and SK Telecom, the military alliance NATO — and, crucially for Europe, the EU cyber agency ENISA.[2][3]

ENISA: the EU's cybersecurity agency (based in Greece). It's something like the member states' shared IT security advisor — it warns of threats, sets standards and coordinates in emergencies. That it now has Mythos access means: Europe's central cyber defense uses the same AI tool as US corporations.

My take: The ENISA line is the most important for us. While the EU is still arguing over regulating AI in the AI Act, its own security agency is already actively deploying AI for defense. No contradiction, but it shows the pace: operational reality is running ahead of regulation. For anyone advising agencies or critical operators in the EU, "AI-powered code review" has now shifted from nice-to-have to expected practice.

Trump executive order: The state wants to look first

On the same June 2, President Trump signed an AI executive order drawn straight from Mythos's capabilities. Core: leading AI labs — named are Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft and xAI — should voluntarily give their most powerful models to the government for cyber testing up to 30 days before release, so agencies can check in advance what a model can do in cyberspace.[4][5]

Executive Order: a directive from the US president to federal agencies — not a law passed by Congress. Picture: the boss gives his own administration house rules; outsiders (here: AI firms) he can't force, only ask. So the labs' participation is voluntary.

Deliberately set limits: the order explicitly forbids mandatory licensing or pre-release approval — it's a request, not a requirement.[5] The 30 days are a compromise: the first draft proposed up to 90 days, tech firms wanted 14.[4] Within 60 days, DHS, Treasury, the National Cyber Director's office and NIST must define which models are even "powerful enough" for such a test.

My take: This is the most honest response a state could make to the Glasswing finding — and simultaneously the weakest. Honest because it acknowledges the dual-use: if AI reliably finds zero-days, whoever tests a new frontier model first has an advantage. Weak because "voluntary + no licensing mandate" means enforcement relies entirely on lab goodwill. It was triggered by Anthropic deliberately limiting the Mythos preview release in April precisely because of this capability.[4]

cybersecuritydive.com · npr.org

The "clearinghouse" — and a justified criticism

The second pillar of the order is an "AI Cybersecurity Clearinghouse": Treasury (Finance), the NSA and the cyber agency CISA are to — again voluntarily, with industry and critical operators — collect, validate and prioritize vulnerability finds and patches.[6][5] Essentially the government version of what Glasswing does privately.

Commentary Critics fault Treasury leading: "Neither AI nor cybersecurity are core competencies of the Treasury Department."[5] That the operationally seasoned agencies (NSA, CISA) play supporting roles instead of leading hints at internal turf disputes — an efficiency risk, not a detail.

My take: A central clearinghouse is exactly right — the bottleneck with vulnerabilities was never finding them, but coordinated patching across many operators. Whether the agency structure delivers that will turn on execution, not the order itself. For Europe: a comparable EU-side clearinghouse is missing — ENISA could become it.

The EU mirror: Omnibus steers toward adoption

While the US acts by order, the EU reform nears the finish. The AI Act amendment ("Digital Omnibus") provisionally agreed May 7 should be "formally adopted by July 2026" according to law firms — then it becomes law.[7] Until then the old deadline technically stands.

What changes: Standalone high-risk systems (Annex III — AI that decides about people) must meet requirements only from December 2, 2027, embedded products (Annex I) from August 2028. Newly tightened: From December 2, 2026, so-called "nudifier" apps and AI-generated abuse material (CSAM) are explicitly banned — breach penalty up to 35 million euros or 7% of annual turnover.[7]

Heads up — not yet final This remains a provisional agreement pending formal confirmation by Council and Parliament. "More time" for high-risk does not mean "no action needed": technical standards are partly still unfinished — that was the reason for the delay.

My take: The day captures the full span of AI regulation in one picture. US: fast, voluntary, thin enforcement. EU: thorough, binding, slow. Both react to the same phenomenon — AI that does more than expected. For German users the message holds: know the deferred deadline, but start the inventory audit now (see Project Idea 2).

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI-powered supply-chain security audit for mid-market

ELI5 & Value: Most mid-market software is packed with foreign open-source components ("dependencies") — and AI today finds vulnerabilities massively in exactly those components, as Glasswing shows. But the mid-market operator often doesn't even know which components they use. twiceD delivers a repeatable audit: generate the software bill of materials (SBOM), scan with AI for known and novel vulnerabilities, prioritize by actual exploitability, and propose patches. Value: avoided supply-chain attack risk and demonstrable security evidence for customers and procurement — without building an internal security team.
Prototype (V0): (1) Real customer repository as test case. (2) Generate SBOM + scan dependencies with existing tools — open-source scanner (Semgrep/Syft) plus a frontier model for triage, nothing custom-built. (3) Reachability analysis: show only holes actually reachable in code (Semgrep filters to ~2% of hits). (4) Prioritized report + patch suggestions, human confirms. MVP narrow: one repository, one report, no continuous monitoring.
Market & Gap: AI vulnerability scanning market grows from $3.06B (2025) to $3.58B (2026), ~17% CAGR. Mature vendors exist — Snyk, Semgrep, Sonatype, Endor Labs, Mend, Checkmarx — and frontier models (Claude Opus, GPT-Codex) now autonomously find unknown holes in widespread open-source. But: horizontal SaaS tools with per-seat subscriptions; the customer must wire scanner, triage and reporting themselves. Gap for twiceD: the 50–200-person shop without a security team, wanting a concrete finding + action plan instead of a dashboard subscription — GDPR-conscious and on-prem-capable, where code can't leave the building.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High as advisory engagement — the heavy parts (scanner, model) are bought, twiceD provides method, triage judgment and clear reporting. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo, scaling "one audit per customer, contractors as needed". Low maintenance as long as point audits; continuous monitoring with SLA would be a different, team-hungry tier — deliberately out of scope. researchandmarkets.com · cloudsecurityalliance.org · endorlabs.com

2 · AI Act Readiness Package — timed for the December 2027 horizon

ELI5 & Value: The EU defers sharp high-risk obligations for now to December 2027 — and steers toward formal adoption "by July". Takes the August pressure off, but "more time" invites procrastination, and an AI inventory takes months. twiceD delivers a lean package: catalog all AI applications (even hidden ones in SaaS tools), classify each against risk tiers, build minimum dossiers for high-risk cases (purpose, data, human oversight, logging) and a realistic roadmap to the new deadline.
