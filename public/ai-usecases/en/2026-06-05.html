The pattern of the week: consulting becomes deployment [1][2][3]

Three weeks, three mega-alliances, one thesis. KPMG is embedding Claude directly into its "Digital Gateway" for all 276,000 employees across 138 countries — Azure rollout by September.[1] PwC is training and certifying 30,000 US professionals first (out of 364,000 globally), with later global expansion, on Claude, and is spinning up its own "Office of the CFO" business unit around it.[2] EY and Microsoft are putting US$ 1 billion on the table over five years to take customers "from pilot to production" — Finance, Tax, Risk, HR, Supply Chain.[3]

The throughline is not the model — that comes from Anthropic or Microsoft. The throughline is fit: building AI into the actual workflow, with validation, integration, change management. That's exactly the core activity twiceD does. My take: this is both confirmation ("deployment is where the money is") and threat ("276,000 people are now doing it too"). Ignore this shift and you've slept through the biggest move in the AI services market since ChatGPT.

Agentic Deployment: Instead of a chat window that answers questions, you build an "agent" — an AI program that runs through an entire work step on its own (e.g., review an invoice, post it to ERP, ask a follow-up). "Deployment" means: wiring that agent into the company's real systems so it ships work live — the hard part where most projects fail.

The skepticism layer: 5% of 1.4 million

Before euphoria tips, the reality check — and it comes straight from the KPMG deal itself. Study Fortune reports that only 5% of KPMG's 1.4 million AI interactions delivered "meaningful outcomes."[4] That aligns strikingly with the MIT NANDA study "The GenAI Divide": ~95% of GenAI pilots produce no measurable P&L lift; only ~5% move revenue noticeably.[5]

Methodology The 5% figure is self-reported from usage logs or surveys, not audited ROI. "Meaningful" is undefined everywhere, and the MIT study is from 08/2025 — it measures an earlier wave. Even so, the direction holds: adoption ≠ value.

The number that matters most is buried in the fine print of that same study: companies that buy AI from specialized vendors succeed ~67% of the time — in-house builds succeed roughly a third as often.[5] And 88% of agent prototypes never reach production; Gartner expects over 40% of agentic projects to be cancelled by end of 2027.[6] This is not bad news for twiceD — it's the sales pitch. Companies that fail, fail at the fit, not at the model.

Microsoft tunes MAI to a consultant workflow — and wins at 10x lower cost [7]

Deploy At Build 2026 (06-02), Microsoft showcased what may be the most relevant use case of the year for my purposes: "Frontier Tuning." When Microsoft fine-tuned its MAI models on McKinsey's specific tasks, MAI delivered the highest win rate, beat GPT-5.5 on quality — and cost 10x less.[7] (The published coding model MAI-Code-1 belongs in the ai-dev brief — here, only the applied enterprise finding counts.)

The takeaway for mid-market: a generic frontier model is expensive and mediocre at everything. A smaller model tuned on one company's workflow beats it at exactly that task — for a fraction of inference cost. Microsoft frames it as an ownership story: "only the organization retains the advantage of its hard-won workflows … and controls the resulting model."

Frontier Tuning: You take a finished top-tier model and "train" it on a company's real tasks until it masters that organization's way of work. Like a new hire who brings general knowledge but also gets trained on how this house operates — afterward, they're better and cheaper at that job than an expensive generalist.

Mayo Clinic builds its own hospital model [8]

Health Second Build aftermath with signal value: Mayo Clinic and Microsoft are developing a proprietary frontier model specifically for healthcare — trained on de-identified clinical data for earlier detection and personalized treatment decisions.[8] The key point: Mayo owns the model; Microsoft distributes it only via Azure Foundry APIs.

That's the opposite of "drop in an OpenAI API." An institution with valuable data gets its own domain-specific model built and keeps control. For regulated terrain (Health, Finance, Public), that's the blueprint — and exactly the sovereignty logic that's about to matter in Germany.

OpenAI builds its own consulting arm [9]

M&A So there's no doubt where this is headed: OpenAI has launched the OpenAI Deployment Company with over US$ 4 billion in seed capital — a partnership with 19 investors/integrators (led by TPG, with Bain, Advent, Brookfield) — and is acquiring the consultancy Tomoro along with ~150 "Forward Deployed Engineers."[9] The model lab becomes the implementer itself.

Fortune nails the power dynamic — consultancies need the AI labs, but the labs need consultancies only for now; Capgemini strategy chief Fernando Alvarez, for his part, points to domain expertise as the consultants' trump card.[4] VC Chamath calls the alliances "letting the fox into the henhouse": whoever opens their customer data to the lab is handing it the ammunition to replace them later. For twiceD: don't try to compete with the big players for enterprise logos. Play beside them, where they can't reach.

Regulation: the clock on GPAI and Germany's AI-MIG [10][11]

Reg · EU The EU AI Act countdown gets concrete: the Commission's first enforcement actions against GPAI vendors — information requests, model access, recalls — are scheduled for August 2, 2026.[10] Anyone offering a general-purpose AI model must deliver technical documentation, training data summary, and copyright compliance proof by then.

GPAI: "General-Purpose AI" — an all-purpose AI model like GPT or Claude, not built for a single task but for many. The AI Act treats these foundation models separately because they sit under thousands of applications — like an engine that goes into a hundred different cars, so they get their own rules.

Reg · DE In Germany, the AI-MIG (KI-Marktüberwachungs- und Innovationsförderungs-Gesetz) is taking shape: the government draft from 10.02.2026 makes the Federal Network Agency the primary market regulator and creates the competence centre KoKIVO.[11] For a mid-market company using AI, that means: a German authority with enforcement power is coming. Compliance support becomes a sales argument, not a cost center.
