SAP wants to be your agent operating system [1][2]

At SAP Sapphire (May 12, 2026, Orlando), SAP announced the "Autonomous Enterprise" — and it's more than the usual keynote noise. The plan: a unified business-AI platform where more than 50 domain-specific "Joule Assistants" (Finance, Supply Chain, Procurement, HR, Customer Experience) orchestrate 200+ specialized agents that handle individual tasks in the background.[1]

Joule Assistant vs. Agent: Think of the assistant as a shift supervisor who understands how an invoice flows from start to finish — and the agents as individual workers, each doing one task (read receipt, validate master data, post). The assistant distributes the work and ensures a clean outcome.

The real story is the €100 million partner fund: SAP is paying its partner ecosystem to have consultants roll out SAP agents at customer sites and build their own agents in Joule Studio.[1][2] Translation: SAP knows that the technology is worthless unless someone embeds it at a mid-market company — and it's buying delivery capacity. This "last mile" is also twiceD's business.

Remarkable: the building blocks. Claude (Anthropic) as the foundation model for Joule agents in HR/Procurement/Supply Chain, Mistral and Cohere as sovereign model options, NVIDIA as a secure runtime layer, AWS for zero-copy data, Google and Microsoft for agent-to-agent interoperability.[1] SAP is no longer building its own frontier model — it's becoming the conductor of other people's models. That's the more honest bet.

My Take The fund is the signal, not the agent count. "200+ agents" is marketing; €100 million for partner delivery is an admission that software licensing alone doesn't create autonomous operations. Whoever can do the integration wins — and that's not the platforms.

Gopuff: the AI fills the cart before you search [6][7]

U.S. quick-delivery service Gopuff launched "Go" with xAI on June 3, 2026 — an AI assistant that pre-fills the shopping cart as soon as you open the app, fed by "13 years of quick-delivery data" and "hundreds of millions of orders".[6]

Predictive Shopping: Instead of searching for products, you describe a situation ("grill night for four") or say nothing — and the app suggests the cart. Like a waiter who has already prepared your "the usual?" before you sit down at the table.

Technically, Go is built on Grok (reasoning, voice, an "Imagine" image model for a TikTok-like, shoppable product stage); delivery in "as fast as 15 minutes" from 400+ micro-fulfillment centers.[6] A frequently cited privacy commitment — that Grok cannot be trained on customer data — comes from reporting (Axios/PYMNTS), not from Gopuff's announcement itself.[7] This is the kind of detail you'd scrutinize closely in an EU context.

My Take The real value isn't the language model, it's the proprietary order dataset. If you have 13 years of cart history, you can "predict" — if you don't, you have a nice chat window. Transferable to B2B: the data treasure beats the model.

Cognizant × Snowflake: finally, countable hours [3]

At Snowflake Summit 26 (announcement June 3, 2026), Cognizant expanded its Snowflake partnership and — more importantly — delivered verifiable numbers. This is the gold standard this brief seeks: named company, named metric.

Cortex / CoCo: Snowflake is the data-cloud database where many enterprises store their data. "Cortex" is the AI capabilities built directly into it; "CoCo" is Snowflake's agent platform. Instead of copying data out first, AI agents run where the data already lives — shorter path, fewer copies.

The scale numbers: 2,250 users, 30+ use cases, 1.3 million AI requests.[3] The customer results are what matters:

  • Biopharm: up to 70% effort reduction.
  • Sports/Entertainment: ~$85,000 annual savings and 1,300 hours recovered.
  • Telco (North America): 99% less time for change-impact analysis.
  • A+E Global Media: ~200 hours of manual effort saved (Conversational Analytics agent).
Heads Up These are individual cases supplied by the vendor ("up to", "representative"), not a controlled study. "1,300 hours saved" without a baseline is a marketing metric — but at least it's a named, auditable one, tied to a real company. That's more than 95% of ROI slides deliver.

The study of the week: everyone deploys, almost no one profits [4]

The second annual WRITER survey "AI Adoption in the Enterprise" (with Workplace Intelligence) delivers the number that grounds all agent enthusiasm: 97% of executives say their company deployed AI agents in the last year — but only 29% see significant organizational ROI.[4]

Add the friction numbers: 79% of organizations face adoption challenges (double-digit increase vs. 2025), 54% of C-suite say AI "tears apart" their company, 73% of CEOs report stress over AI strategy.[4]

Methodology Flag Client WRITER sells enterprise AI — the survey has an interest in telling the story "adoption is hard, get help". Sample: 2,400 respondents (1,200 C-suite + 1,200 staff), conducted Dec 17, 2025–Jan 25, 2026, US/UK/Ireland/Benelux/France/Germany. Self-report, no measured ROI. But the direction ("gap between deploy and value") aligns with independent observations.
My Take This 97-to-29 scissors is the most important number of the week — and the best sales thesis for twiceD. The market is past the "should we?" phase and into the "why isn't it working?" phase. That's pure consulting demand.

Germany Spotlight: INXM and "Compiled AI" [5]

Berlin story of the week: INXM came out of stealth on June 3, 2026 with €5.7 million in pre-seed (Cherry Ventures + Redstone).[5] Founded in 2025 by ex-CDOs from Isar Aerospace and Volocopter plus n8n/Thoughtworks veterans — an unusually industry-grounded founding team.

Compiled AI: AI designs the business process ("plan"), but at runtime the plan runs deterministically — not re-thought with each execution. Like an architect who creatively designs the blueprint once; construction then follows the plan strictly, without workers improvising.

This is the direct answer to the WRITER gap: industrial businesses don't trust AI agents in core processes because "guesses fresh each time" and "auditable" contradict each other. INXM targets reliable, provable execution — exactly the mid-market's concern.

Standing Thread — Regulation: AI Act Omnibus [8][9]

Important for anyone deploying AI in the EU: On May 7, 2026, EU lawmakers reached a political agreement on the "Digital Omnibus", shifting key AI Act deadlines.[8]

High-Risk System (Annex III): AI in sensitive areas — hiring, credit scoring, law enforcement. These face the strictest rules (risk management, data governance, human oversight). This deadline has now shifted to December 2, 2027 (embedded in regulated products, Annex I: August 2, 2028).[8]

But heads up — the one deadline that stays: Art. 50 transparency obligations still take effect on August 2, 2026 (users must know they're talking to AI / AI outputs must be labelable). Only the watermark obligation for providers shifts to December 2, 2026.[8][9] SME exemptions expand to companies up to 750 employees / €150 million revenue.[9]

My Take The high-risk deadline shift removes pressure — but August 2, 2026 for transparency is near and affects every chatbot/generative deployment. That's a concrete, dated reason for mid-market consulting (see Idea 2 below).

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · NL-Analytics Agent for Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: In mid-market, the business team often waits days for a simple report, because only IT knows SQL. A "ask your data" agent lets a procurement manager ask in plain English — "which three vendors overran plan last quarter?" — and get the number plus source. Value: the A+E/Cognizant story showed ~200 hours of manual effort saved per use case;[3] the "analyst" bottleneck disappears.
Prototype (V0): (1) a real customer data slice (sales/procurement). (2) Text-to-SQL via an existing model, not built in-house — Snowflake Cortex, or an open-source stack (LlamaIndex + DuckDB) for on-prem. (3) a semantic layer (what tables are called what) as guardrails against hallucination. (4) every answer shows the generated query + data source. MVP tight: one data source, read-only, human sees the query before trusting it.
Market & Gap: Conversational analytics is big and fast — the Conversational AI market sits at ~$25.1 billion in 2026 with ~26.7% CAGR.[10] Mature players exist: Snowflake Cortex/CoCo, Databricks Genie, ThoughtSpot.[3] But they're horizontal platforms with cloud lock-in and per-seat pricing — the customer must assemble the semantic layer, wiring, and governance themselves. Gap: the specific German mid-market process, on-prem for sensitive data, a fitted data model instead of a self-service toolkit.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. The heavy model comes from the platform; twiceD builds the semantic layer + wiring + operations. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo, roll-out per customer with 1 contractor. Maintenance moderate (schema-drift upkeep), as long as scope stays tight on a few data sources. cognizant.com · knowledge-sourcing.com

2 · AI Act Audit as Fixed-Price Service

ELI5 & Value: Starting August 2, 2026, EU companies must disclose where AI interacts with humans or generates outputs (Art. 50) — and many mid-market firms don't even know how many AI tools are running internally.[9] twiceD delivers a quick "AI Audit": which systems exist, what risk class, what to do by when. Value: avoided regulatory penalty risk and a clear to-do list instead of panic — an affordable audit, not a year-long subscription.
Prototype (V0): (1) questionnaire + interview guide for system discovery (purpose, data, affected parties). (2) mapping against Annex III categories + Art. 50 triggers, semi-automated with an LLM as classification helper (human decides). (3) output: PDF audit + prioritized action list with deadlines. MVP tight: one mid-market company, inventory only + transparency obligations, no ongoing monitoring.
Market & Gap: The EU AI Act compliance market is small but fast-growing — ~$609 million in 2026 at ~37.3% CAGR;[11] the broader AI governance market ~$2.55 billion in 2026.[12] Players like Credo AI, Holistic AI, Modulos, Trustible are SaaS governance platforms for large enterprises — enterprise pricing, self-service, no hands-on at an 80-person shop.[12] Gap: the mid-market buyer doesn't want a platform license; they want someone to walk through the building, in their language, with data-protection and copyright reflexes.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High — consulting-heavy, nearly zero maintenance (one-shot audit). V0 immediately, no hosting/GPU. Scale via standardized templates + contractor per vertical. Risk: don't deliver legal advice — position as technical inventory + prep for counsel, with a lawyer in your network. artificialintelligenceact.eu · dimensionmarketresearch.com · futuremarketinsights.com
