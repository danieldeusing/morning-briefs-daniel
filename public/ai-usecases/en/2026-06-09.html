WWDC: Apple rebuilds Siri on Gemini—and makes other AI swappable [1][2][3]

The applied-AI story of the year came out of Cupertino yesterday. At the WWDC keynote on June 8, 2026, Apple introduced Siri AI—per Apple, "a completely new version of Siri, deeply integrated into iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, and Apple Vision Pro," that taps personal context from messages, email, and photos and executes actions across apps.[1]

The part Apple's press release notably doesn't name comes from reporting and Apple's developer sessions: the new Siri runs on a custom Google Gemini model. TechCrunch quotes Apple directly: they "worked with Google and the Gemini model family to develop the next generation of Apple Foundation Models."[2] The reported deal volume is around $1 billion/year.[3]

Foundation Model: The big "base brain" an AI assistant runs on—like the engine in a car. Apple is no longer building just its own engine; it's putting a Google-tuned Gemini engine under the Apple hood. The user still sees "Siri," but Gemini is spinning underneath.

Second: via an "Extensions" framework in iOS 27, iPadOS 27, and macOS 27, users should be able to plug in other assistants—ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini—directly into Siri and route queries there instead of to Apple's model.[3] Siri AI also gets a standalone app (like ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini) with conversation history.[2] Rollout: beta this fall, English first, on newer hardware.[1]

Source nuance Apple's official newsroom statement only speaks of a "bold new architecture" for privacy and names neither Gemini nor assistant choice.[1] Both are confirmed via TechCrunch and established Apple press (MacRumors, Bloomberg)[2][3]—but clearly separated: the Gemini backbone is Apple-confirmed (via TechCrunch); the ~$1-billion volume and some Extensions details come from reporting, not Apple's mouth.
My take This is the largest distribution channel for applied AI in existence: Apple had 2.5 billion active devices last quarter[4], with iPhone the lion's share. If end users can soon check "Claude" or "ChatGPT" in the Siri menu, assistant choice becomes a mass-market switch—and Apple takes the fee for standard integration (the Gemini model) while showing the door to competitors. For applied AI, that means: the assistant is no longer an app you download but a swappable system layer. That's exactly what changes how firms deliver AI to their customers.

The EU exclusion is the real twiceD news [5]

Here it gets concrete for Daniel. Apple will not ship Siri AI on iPhone and iPad in the EU at all—and is citing the Digital Markets Act (DMA). Software chief Craig Federighi: "Their refusal to engage constructively on solutions that preserve privacy and security means we currently have no timeline for Siri AI availability on iOS and iPadOS in the EU."[5]

Digital Markets Act (DMA): An EU law forcing large "gatekeepers" like Apple to open their platform to rivals—e.g., requiring third-party assistants to get the same deep device access as Apple's own. Like a rule forcing the superintendent to give every contractor a master key. Apple says: that mandate can't be met without compromising privacy, so it's pulling the feature from the EU.

EU users get Siri AI only on Mac, Watch, and Vision Pro—not the two most-used devices.[5] Apple's DMA reading: it demands "giving nearly unlimited access to AI systems on the device … without ongoing user visibility and control."[5] The Commission rejected Apple's "Trusted System Agent" proposal.

My take Whether you buy Apple's DMA rationale or see it as negotiating theater—the effect is real: the AI flagship feature of 2026 is absent from hundreds of millions of EU iPhones. That creates a gap, not a barrier. If the convenient system assistant doesn't ship in the EU, the value rises for built-in, app-native AI functions—and for consulting that helps a German mid-market firm deliver AI independently, without platform lock-in. Idea 1 below targets exactly this.

Chatbot market share: the number everyone cites—and why it's misleading [6][7]

Fresh market-share numbers are making the rounds this week. Per Momentic (via Similarweb, as of June 8), measured in web visits: ChatGPT 54.7%, Gemini 27.4%, Claude 8.2%.[6] In the US, Claude is higher (12.5%). The most-cited headline: Claude +306% in one quarter—from roughly 203 million web visits in January to 824 million in April 2026.[6]

Web-visit share: The fraction of counted website visits across a fixed set of AI apps. It measures how often someone loads the webpage of a chatbot—not how much actual work flows through it. Like rating a store by how many people walk through the door instead of what they buy.
Methodology flag The number misleads for two reasons. First, it counts only web visits—the better an assistant answers directly, the fewer clicks it generates; roughly 65% of Google searches in 2026 end without a click.[6] Second, it misses the entire API and enterprise consumption, where Claude is especially strong. A competing measure by First Page Sage (06.05., "AI search share," different method) puts Claude at 21.1% instead of 8.2% and Gemini at only 13.1%.[7] Two reputable sources, three times the Claude number diverges—that says more about the measurement than the market.
My take The useful insight isn't the ranking but the spread: consumer reach (ChatGPT/Gemini ahead) and enterprise/API use (Claude ahead) are two different markets. For twiceD, the second one matters—and you can't read it from web visits. Anyone recommending a model should decide not on these headline percentages but on the task, privacy, and cost per task.

Standing thread—Regulation: US backing off, EU stays sharp [8][9][10]

Important correction on a date floating through checklists: the Colorado AI Act does not take effect on 06.30.2026. Governor Polis signed SB 189 on 05.14.2026, shifting the deadline to 01.01.2027 and stripping the hard parts (due diligence against algorithmic discrimination, risk-management duties, impact assessments)—what remains is a narrow transparency core.[8][9] On top of that, a federal court already halted enforcement on 04.27.2026 over a constitutional challenge.[10]

My take This is a pattern, not an outlier: the US state track of AI regulation is crumbling under lawsuits and lobbying. That leaves the EU AI Act as the only sharp, dated line for applied AI—and it's holding: Art. 50 transparency and GPAI enforcement continue rolling from 08.02.2026 (only the high-risk Annex III was deferred to Dec. 2027 via omnibus, see brief from June 8). For an EU operator like twiceD, that's doubly relevant: the binding obligation lands here, not Colorado—and that very asymmetry makes EU-focused consulting valuable.

What this adds up to [6]

Three seemingly separate stories tell one narrative. Apple is making the assistant a swappable system layer—selectable, platform-wide. The market-share debate shows that "which assistant wins" measured by end-user clicks is already the wrong question. And regulation is drifting: the EU stays binding, the US goes softer.

For applied AI, that means a maturity jump: it's no longer "which model is best," but orchestration—which assistant for which task, under which privacy and sovereignty constraints, at what cost per task. That's the question Apple is now putting to 2.5 billion devices[4]—and not answering at all for EU iPhones.

My take This is the best news for a solo shop in weeks: if the market shifts from "model choice" to "model orchestration under rules," fitting-work becomes more valuable than model-building—and fitting is exactly twiceD's discipline. The two ideas below tackle the Apple EU gap and the orchestration layer directly.

Researched proposals—with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · EU assistant bridge for mid-market

ELI5 & Value: Apple isn't shipping its new Siri AI on iPhone/iPad in the EU—the convenient system AI is missing precisely where Daniel's customers sit.[5] Value to the mid-market firm: instead of waiting for Apple's mercy, twiceD bakes the AI function into the customer's app / workflow—an embedded feature (quote assistant, support bot, document helper) that works independent of platform whims and stays sovereign. Concrete problem solved: the AI feature the end user expects in 2026 arrives reliably—without dependence on a gatekeeper pulling it from the EU.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick a tight, valuable use case at the customer (e.g., "turn customer inquiry into quote"). (2) Embed an assistant surface into the customer's existing app/web interface. (3) Model hookup via a gateway allowing EU-compliant endpoints (Azure-EU/Mistral/Claude over EU region). (4) Build GDPR and Art. 50 labeling in from day one. MVP tight: one use case, one customer surface, no app-store assistant, no deep device integration (exactly what DMA blocks—we deliberately skip that). Existing building blocks: Claude/GPT/Mistral APIs in EU region, a web embed, optionally Apple's App Intents for non-EU variant.
Market & Gap: Apple itself confirms the bottleneck—Siri AI doesn't ship on EU iPhone/iPad, Federighi names no timeline explicitly.[5] Platform assistants (Apple/Google) don't solve it for EU mobile; the big consultancies chase the company logos. What stays open: the 50–250-person German firm that wants a concrete, embedded AI function in German, GDPR-compliant, platform-agnostic—without waiting on Apple's EU negotiation. Exactly this "own assistant instead of system assistant" niche is unserved by Apple or the Big 4 hands-on.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. Pure fitting-work on ready model APIs—no model-building, no platform risk. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo, rollout with one contractor per customer. Maintenance light, as long as scope stays to one use case. Honest limit: no true deep device integration possible (that's exactly what DMA forbids Apple)—the bridge lives in app/web, not the OS. 2 · Sovereign multi-model router on open-source gateway

ELI5 & Value: Apple is showing where the market goes: not one model, but choice—users get to pick ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini behind the assistant.[3] For firms, that means: the right model per task, cheapest, most privacy-compliant, one place for cost, logging, and filtering. A "router" is that switchboard. Value: lower costs (cheap model for simple tasks, pricey only when needed), an audit trail for EU compliance, and no vendor lock-in to one provider. Like a circuit breaker that routes current to the right line by need—instead of running everything through one expensive line.
Prototype (V0): (1) Stand up an open-source gateway (LiteLLM) as self-hosted Docker container—speaks 100+ providers over an OpenAI-compatible protocol. (2) Define routing rules per task type (simple → cheap/local model, complex → Claude/GPT). (3) Logging + cost dashboard + Art. 50–compliant labeling. (4) For sensitive data, an EU-hosted or local open model as fallback. MVP tight: one customer, 2–3 models, one routing-rule class, no bespoke guardrail engine. Existing blocks: LiteLLM (open source, self-hostable), EU-region endpoints, optionally local open model for on-prem.
Market & Gap: The AI gateway market is established by 2026—LiteLLM (open source, free self-hosted), Portkey (hosted, on-prem as of April 2026), Kong AI Gateway, and Truefoundry are the named players.[11] The gap: Portkey/Kong target large enterprise programs with their own platform team and hosted subscription; above ~$50K/month LLM spend they pay for themselves, below it LiteLLM is the open-source default.[11] The German mid-market has neither the budget nor the team—they need someone to set up LiteLLM for them, wire it EU-compliant, and run it. This hands-on setup on open-source basis, on-prem-capable, the vendors leave open.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): Medium to high. Build is tractable (LiteLLM is done; twiceD writes routing rules + ops), but it's a hosted service with ongoing load—monitoring, model switching, cost grooming. Honestly: ~3–6 hrs/week per customer, doesn't scale solo unbounded. Viable as long as scope stays tight and one contractor picks up per-customer ops. Sovereignty lever: the on-prem/EU variant is exactly what hosted vendors reluctantly offer. [3] macrumors.com · [11] truefoundry.com · spheron.network
