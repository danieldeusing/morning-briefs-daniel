KPMG × Microsoft: whoever manages agents is selling the scarce thing now

On Tuesday (06-09), KPMG and Microsoft expanded their partnership. At first glance, routine: Microsoft 365 Copilot rolls out across 276,000 KPMG employees. But the important part is in the fine print — KPMG uses Agent 365 to "govern how AI agents are deployed, monitored, and updated," with "centralized control and visibility over agents operating across systems, data, and business processes."

Agent 365: Picture your company running hundreds of invisible interns, all tinkering with email, ERP, and databases at once. Agent 365 is the time clock, security camera, and key ring all in one: it knows which agent exists, what it can do, what it did — and can switch it off. So Microsoft isn't selling the intern; it's selling oversight of the intern.

My take: this is the real market move. Models have become commodity — yesterday Apple/Gemini, the day before the labs. What stays scarce is the control layer: counting, locking down, logging, shutting down. KPMG is now reselling exactly this layer as a "Trusted-AI Framework" to its customers — consulting industrializes itself (the pattern already showed up on 06-08). Microsoft provides the rails, KPMG runs the locomotive.

Sources: microsoft.com · kpmg.com

Integra LifeSciences: the first GxP use case in the deal

In the same release sits the story that's more interesting for Daniel. Integra LifeSciences (med-tech, ~$1.6B in revenue) embeds Copilot into supply chain, regulatory affairs, and medical affairs — with a "dedicated team that makes every deployment responsible, secure, and compliant."

GxP: Umbrella term for "Good x Practice" rules in pharma and med-tech (GMP for manufacturing, GDP for distribution, GLP for labs). Simplified: if software co-decides outcomes at a pill or implant maker, you have to prove it does what it claims — fully logged, validated, auditable. A chatbot that's "usually right" isn't enough for the FDA.

That's what makes this case hard and expensive: deploying a Copilot in a regulated process doesn't mean "install the app" — it means validating every output against GxP rules. The fact that Integra is building its own operating model for this, rather than buying it off the shelf, is exactly the gap that lands in twiceD idea #2 below — because the mid-market med-tech maker doesn't have that team.

Sources: microsoft.com · biospace.com (FY2025 revenue $1.635B)

Brazil snapshot: agentic banking gets measurable

While the Northern Hemisphere debates governance, Brazil's banks deliver numbers. Bradesco's "BIA Tech," used by 80% of developers, lifts dev productivity by 46% according to the bank; its customer assistant BIA scores 85% on resolution. Itaú runs 500+ AI projects with roughly 17,000 developers, Nubank has its homegrown "nuFormer" model in production for credit underwriting.

Methodology flag: these are self-reported numbers from the banks, no independent audit — "+46% productivity" is the standard vendor/PR metric, without definition of what was measured. But the pattern is real: Brazil is skipping the pilot graveyard because Pix and Open Finance have already structured the data. That's the playbook the German mid-market doesn't have — and thus the reason for the hands-on gap.

Source: bnamericas.com

OpenAI Economic Research Exchange: who's actually measuring what AI does to jobs?

On Monday (06-08), OpenAI launched the Economic Research Exchange — a program giving outside economists "carefully controlled, privacy-protected access to OpenAI tools and datasets" to research "how AI changes workers, firms, and economies." It's explicitly seeking people in "causal inference, labor economics, and productivity." Apply by 07-05, decisions by 07-31.

Take Whoever owns the data frames the study. OpenAI is funding research into the employment impacts of its own models — and granting only curated access. Not worthless (real usage data is rare), but it carries the same methodology caveat as any vendor-paid ROI study: a truly neutral audit looks different.

For twiceD, the lesson is less about the program than the admission: even OpenAI doesn't yet know reliably what AI does to productivity. Every "+46%" figure in this brief deserves the same skepticism.

Sources: openai.com · startuphub.ai

Standing Thread — Regulation: the AI Act clock strikes on 08-02

GPAI obligations (for "General-Purpose AI," i.e., all-purpose models like GPT/Gemini/Claude) took effect on 08-02-2025 — the AI Office could request disclosures, model access, and evaluations, but couldn't impose fines. As of 08-02-2026, the fine power kicks in (Art. 101): up to €15M or 3% of worldwide revenue, or in extremis, recalls. The same day also sees standalone high-risk obligations take effect — though the "Digital Omnibus" tentatively delayed them (on 05-06 and 05-07), but hasn't formally decided yet.

Around two dozen model providers have signed the GPAI code of conduct — Anthropic, Google, IBM, Microsoft, Mistral, OpenAI, Cohere — Meta has not, xAI only the safety chapter. For mid-market firms: if you're deploying a high-risk AI system (e.g., in HR, lending, medical devices), start the documentation now, not in August.

Sources: lw.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu

A framing with clear dates: per ZEW survey (7,000+ firms, CEPR column from 02-07-2026), 56% of German firms use or plan GenAI in 2026 — up from 44% (2025) and 26% (2024). Among early adopters, GenAI's share of working time rose from 7.5% to 12.6%.

But: that's GenAI use (chatbot helps type), not autonomous agents. On agent maturity, globally, only 1 in 5 firms has a mature governance model. That exact gap — broad adoption, thin controls — is this week's market opportunity.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Agent Inventory & Governance for German Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Once a mid-market firm has three or four AI agents running (one drafts quotes, one answers tickets, one books in ERP), nobody knows anymore: which agents exist? What can they access? What did they do last week? That's the "shadow IT" of the agent era. Idea: a lean inventory + watchdog — a list of all agents, their permissions, and their logs, with an emergency cutoff. Value: avoided risk (an agent accidentally leaking customer data) and AI Act proof without enterprise platform.
Prototype (V0): (1) Survey all running agents/automations at a real customer (n8n, Make, Copilot Studio, custom scripts). (2) A central registry as a lightweight app — per agent: purpose, data scope, owner, status. (3) Logging adapter that writes every agent call into an audit log. (4) A kill switch (revoke token / disable webhook). MVP deliberately narrow: one customer, read-only audit + manual kill, no auto policy-enforcement. Building blocks: OpenTelemetry for tracing, an existing secrets manager, no bespoke security code.
Market & Gap: The market exists and is growing: AI governance is expected to grow to ≈ $3.59B (36% CAGR through 2033), with agent observability at ≈ $2.2B. Players: Microsoft Agent 365, Cisco AI Defense / Cloud Control, WitnessAI, F5 AI Guardrails (formerly CalypsoAI, acquired by F5). All of them are horizontal enterprise platforms — network layer, enterprise pricing, DIY on top. Gap: only 1 in 5 firms have mature agent governance, and the 20–200-person mid-market shop won't buy Cisco AI Defense. For them: hands-on, on-prem-capable, a concrete toolchain covered instead of "everything."
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. The inventory + audit log is standard CRUD plus tracing — solo in 4–6 weeks, heavy crypto/security comes from existing parts. Maintenance is low as long as scope stays "observe + manual stop"; automatic enforcement would drive load up, so deliberately out. Scaling: one contractor per new customer onboarding. The on-prem flavor is actually the sales lever here (sensitive data never leaves the house). grandviewresearch.com · deloitte.com · witness.ai

2 · GxP-Copilot Validation for Regulated Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Integra is building its own team to make Copilot demonstrably compliant in regulated processes (supply chain, regulatory). An 80-person med-tech or pharma supplier doesn't have that team — but wants the same Copilot. Idea: a packaged validation kit that locks down exactly one Copilot use case (e.g., "SOP authoring" or "complaint triage") for GxP compliance: test protocols, human-review gates, immutable audit trail. Value: customer reaches compliance weeks instead of months — and survives an FDA or Notified Body audit.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick one regulated workflow at a real customer. (2) Validation templates (URS, IQ/OQ/PQ-light) as fillable forms. (3) Capsule one Copilot/LLM call with enforced human-in-the-loop gate. (4) Audit trail: immutably store prompt, output, reviewer, and decision. MVP narrow: one workflow, one document type, validation as consulting + software bundle, not SaaS. Building blocks: existing eQMS integration, Azure OpenAI with data residency, no homegrown model.
Market & Gap: Pharma QMS software market sits at ≈ $2.78B in 2026 (~13% CAGR). Players: Veeva Vault Quality, MasterControl, Sparta TrackWise, Qualio, Dot Compliance, plus AI-native validators like Sware and Validfor ($1.2M Pre-Seed, early 2026, go-live in ~4 weeks). But they're platforms/eQMS — they give you the system, not the canned validation for your Copilot use case. Gap: the "last mile" — hands-on validation of a concrete Copilot workflow at a German mid-market firm, in German legal/audit language, on-prem where needed.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): Medium. The software piece is small (gate + audit log), weight is in domain depth: GxP knowledge needs to be bought in or a validation contractor engaged — that's the honest bottleneck for non-pharma Daniel. Upside: it's mostly advisory + templates, minimal ongoing ops load. Scaling through one GxP contractor per business segment. No validation partner? The idea fails the filter — deliberately idea #2, not #1. intuitionlabs.ai · sware.com
