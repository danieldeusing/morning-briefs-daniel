Nubank: the model belongs to the bank, not OpenAI

Finance · BR On June 11, Nubank — Latin America's largest digital bank — unveiled its AI strategy, and the core is remarkably self-contained: nuFormer, a proprietary, self-supervised-trained foundation model for financial data. It's already running in the core business of lending in Brazil and is being rolled out to consumer loans and the Mexico and Colombia markets.[1]

Foundation Model: Instead of building a separate small model for each question, you train one large model on massive amounts of raw data, and afterward it can do many tasks. Like an apprentice who watches everything in the workshop for years and afterward can handle almost any repair. nuFormer is such an apprentice — only trained exclusively on Nubank's transaction and behavioral data, not the open internet.

The concrete numbers are what convince: The AI "Private Banker" had over 15 million monthly active users in tests; an automated test environment shrank model test cycles from months to a single day; a backend overhaul cost almost a whole year of project time.[1] The strategic point underneath: Nubank earns roughly $16 per active customer (Q1 2026) versus ~$40 at established banks — the thesis is that a more precise in-house model is exactly what safely supplies credit to customers whom rougher scores would reject.[1]

Our take: This is the most important shift in applied AI right now — and it contrasts sharply with "we're bolting on ChatGPT." Rohan Ramanath, Nubank's General Manager of AI Core, puts it directly: better models "minimize risks and open doors to credit, safely and ethically."[2] Whoever owns the proprietary data and builds a model on it has a moat that no API access replaces. For mid-market, the lesson isn't "build your own foundation model" (too expensive) — it's: your forecasting power lives in your tables, not the next chat model.

Sanofi × Owkin: an AI scientist on license

Health · EU In the first week of June, Sanofi expanded its AI partnership with French company Owkin: a five-year license for Owkin's "K Pro," an AI scientist meant to accelerate drug discovery. Owkin is building custom agentic tools from it for Sanofi.[3]

Agentic AI Scientist: An ordinary AI model answers one question. An "agent" completes a multi-step task — form hypothesis, search datasets, propose experiment, check results, next step. Like a PhD student who doesn't just answer one question but runs a whole investigation. "K Pro" is meant to run exactly such research routines in the pharma pipeline independently.

This fits the pattern: Sanofi and Owkin have worked together since 2021 (a 90 million euro collaboration around oncology targets).[3] The step from "researching together" to "licensing platform and building agents on top" is the same maturity jump as Nubank — away from experiment, toward productive domain systems on proprietary data.

Why this matters: Pharma is a textbook case of expensive, narrowly scoped knowledge work — exactly the profile where a specialist model delivers real value instead of just running demos. And it's European: Owkin (FR), Sanofi (FR), Prior Labs/SAP (DE). The moat trend isn't US-exclusive.

SAP × Prior Labs: AI for tables, not chat [4]

B2B-SaaS · DE As a seven-day context, because it completes the week's pattern: SAP pays over 1 billion euros over four years for Heidelberg-based lab Prior Labs and is building a "Frontier AI Lab" in Europe from it. The goal is Tabular Foundation Models — foundation models not for language, but for structured business data. Close expected Q2/Q3 2026.[4]

Tabular Foundation Model (TFM): Language models (like ChatGPT) are trained on text. But the data that actually runs a company — orders, payments received, delivery times, inventory levels — lives in tables, not prose. A TFM is a model that natively "understands" tables and makes predictions from them: Will this customer churn? Will this invoice pay late? Like an experienced clerk who glances at a spreadsheet and immediately says "I'd be careful with that one."

SAP CTO Philipp Herzig states the thesis unusually directly: the biggest untapped opportunity in enterprise AI is "not the large language model, but AI built for the structured data that runs the world economy."[4] Prior Labs' open tool TabPFN has over 3 million downloads; it predicts business outcomes like payment default, supplier risk, or customer churn.

Our take: Three companies, same bet, in one week — Nubank (financial data), Owkin/Sanofi (biopharma data), SAP/Prior Labs (tabular data). That's no accident, that's the next phase: the general model race becomes commodity, value migrates to the domain model on proprietary data. Important for twiceD: The technology (TabPFN) is open and free — the gap is fitting it to the customer, not the model itself (see idea below).

Gemini 3.5 Flash: the governance detail with consequences

Deploy · EU Google Cloud announced: Gemini 3.5 Flash is now generally available in Global, US, and EU — but the real detail hides in the fine print. As of June 16, the admin toggle that let admins turn the model on or off in Gemini Enterprise disappears: It will then be "enabled by default for all users and cannot be disabled."[5]

Why this matters: Sounds like a footnote, but it's governance. Anyone in the EU who needs to document AI use under the AI Act wants to decide themselves which model runs in the organization — not be overridden by the vendor via a default switch. That's the flip side of last week's governance wave: first the customer wants control over their agents, then the platform vendor takes away a control option. A point you'll want on the compliance checklist.

EU AI Act: the Code of Practice on Transparency is now open for signature

Reg · EU Update to Wednesday's thread: the EU AI Office opened the "Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content" finalized on June 10 for signature. Important — it explicitly opens to both providers and users who fall under Article 50 (2)/(4).[6] Participation is voluntary, but it's the easiest way to credibly claim compliance.

Key date As of August 2, 2026, both providers and users of AI systems within Article 50 (2)/(4) scope must meet transparency obligations — mark AI-generated or AI-altered content in machine-readable form and label deepfakes.[6] The code recommends two mechanisms: digitally signed metadata and invisible watermarking.

Our take: The marking obligation for users remains the underestimated half of the AI Act — it hits every marketing department, every newsletter, every government agency that releases AI content, not just the big model providers. Seven weeks to the deadline.

Skepticism Check: "94% of mid-market without AI"

This number is making the rounds — and it deserves skeptical reading. One report claims 94% of German mid-market firms haven't implemented AI operationally yet, fewer than 6% have operational AI systems.[7]

Methodology The source is a Hamburg AI consulting firm (Dr. Justus & Partner), and it's not original research: the report "synthesizes" peer-reviewed studies from 2020–2025, without its own sample or survey — so a meta-summary of partly multi-year-old numbers, published in January 2026.[7] A consulting firm that sells AI adoption has an incentive for a big "not yet" number. Read this as rough direction, not a measured 2026 baseline.

Our take: The more honest skepticism comes from Gartner: over 40% of agentic AI projects will be shelved by 2027, and per IBM's 2025 CEO study, only 25% of AI initiatives delivered expected ROI.[8] That aligns with the week's pattern: the companies that deliver (Nubank, Sanofi) have stopped bolting on generic models and are building on proprietary data. That exact gap — between "tried AI" and "measurable value" — is the consulting opportunity.

1 · Prediction model for mid-market — the moat from your own tables

ELI5 & Value: Every mid-market firm sits on the tables that run its business — invoices, payments received, delivery times, customer history. That holds usable knowledge: Which invoice will pay late? Which supplier is wobbling? Which customer walks? Today an experienced clerk answers from gut feel — or nobody does. twiceD builds a small prediction system that answers exactly one of those questions on the customer's data, on-prem. Value: concretely averted risk (one early-caught payment default pays for the project) and a prompt/collections decision grounded in data instead of gut feel.
Prototype (V0): (1) one real customer table as training/test set (e.g., two years of invoice history with a "on-time/late" column). (2) Prediction via TabPFN (Prior Labs' open tabular model) — it needs no training, just runs inference in one pass, scales to ~100,000 rows / 2,000 columns. (3) a simple traffic-light frontend ("these 12 open invoices are late-pay risk"). MVP deliberately narrow: one prediction question, one customer, read-only, human decides. Building blocks: TabPFN (Apache-2 derived license), Python/FastAPI, everything on customer hardware.
Market & Gap: Mature platforms exist — DataRobot, H2O.ai, AWS AutoGluon, Google AutoML — for tabular ML.[A] But they're enterprise-priced (DataRobot: median ~$212,000/year)[B] and need a data-science team to wire the workflow. SAP is buying Prior Labs for >1 billion euros — but that lands in the SAP full-stack, not the mid-market firm without SAP.[A] Gap: The technology is now open with TabPFN and needs no training team — what's missing is affordable, on-prem, DE-configured fit into one concrete process. The AutoML market grows from $2.59 billion (2025) to $15.98 billion (2030, CAGR ~44%);[C] on-prem deployment is in strong demand among data-protection-sensitive firms — exactly the mid-market argument.
Buildability (solo + AI): High. The heavy model (TabPFN) is open and needs no GPU training cluster — twiceD builds data hookup + traffic-light frontend + ops. V0 in 3–5 weeks solo. Maintenance low, as long as scope stays to one prediction question per customer (no ongoing model retraining needed — TabPFN is training-free). Scaling: one template per question type, one contractor per mandate. On-prem is the sovereignty lever here: sensitive finance/supplier data never leaves the building. news.sap.com · vendr.com · mordorintelligence.com
