AI Act Omnibus: Agreed, but not yet legally binding

What's been decided — and what's still missing

The EU has agreed on the so-called "Omnibus," a legislative package that streamlines parts of the AI Act and most significantly pushes back the strictest deadline. The European Parliament approved it by 569 to 45 votes (March 26, 2026) — this was approval of the negotiating mandate, not final ratification; the provisional agreement came May 6–7, the Council confirmed May 13.[1][2][3]

The critical caveat: This is a political agreement, not binding law. Legal force takes effect only when published in the Official Journal of the EU — and as of today (June 14) that hasn't happened. Until then the original August 2, 2026 deadline formally remains law. I'm stating this bluntly because the temptation is strong to check off "deadline postponed" as done — it isn't.

Official Journal: The EU's official publication organ. Like a birth certificate lodged with the registrar: a marriage isn't legally valid until it's registered — the ceremony beforehand doesn't count. An EU rule only becomes law once it appears in the Official Journal.

The new deadlines — with asterisks

Once the Omnibus is published, this takes effect: high-risk obligations for standalone Annex III systems (e.g., AI in hiring, lending, education) shift from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027.[1] For AI embedded in regulated products under Annex I (machinery, medical devices), the deadline moves to August 2, 2028.[1] Additionally, the watermarking/NCII grace period for systems released before August 2, 2026 runs until December 2, 2026.[1]

Not postponed Article 50 (transparency) stays at August 2, 2026: AI systems must disclose themselves to users, AI-generated content and deepfakes must be marked. For anyone deploying a chatbot or generative output, that's the deadline that matters now.[1]

My take: the postponement is a trap for the lazy

As of April 2026, 78 % of organizations hadn't taken serious compliance steps.[4] This postponement will reinforce that inaction. That's risky for two reasons: first, the postponement isn't yet legally binding; second, it doesn't touch the transparency layer (Art. 50) at all. If you're running a customer-facing AI assistant, you still have a hard August deadline — except now everyone acts as if pressure's off. That's where a consulting gap opens (see project ideas).

Brazil: Regulation stalled, cloud sovereignty moving

PL 2338 stuck, Bill 762 seeks impact assessment Public

Brazil's AI framework bill PL 2338/2023 passed the Senate in December 2024 and has sat in the Câmara dos Deputados since March 17, 2025; a special committee (33 members) was created in April 2025 — a vote is still undated.[15] Fresher is Bill 762/2026 (Feb. 2026): it mandates an AI impact assessment (AIA) for high-risk/critical systems, with an eye toward public procurement, credit scoring, and contract analysis.[16] ANPD is named as the coordinating authority.

AI Impact Assessment (AIA): Like a safety report before car approval — before a risky AI system goes live, you document what could fail and who gets hurt. Brazil is following the European pattern.

Huawei Cloud MaaS live in Brazil Public

Huawei Cloud rolled out "Model-as-a-Service" on April 13, 2026 across nine additional countries, including Brazil — with models like DeepSeek V3/V3.2, Qwen3-32B, and Zhipu GLM-5.[14] Dataprev, the state IT provider, runs a hybrid architecture on Huawei Cloud to preserve data sovereignty for the national cloud. Pilot graveyard: why 9 out of 10 agent projects die

The numbers — and a methodological caution

The narrative "AI agents go live in 2026" carries a harsh flip side: 86–89 % of agent pilots never reach production scale, according to industry aggregators, and only 13 % of organizations have adequate AI governance.[12] That matches experience — building a pilot is easy; putting it in production is the hard part.

Methodological flag Gartner forecasts that by end of 2026, 40 % of enterprise applications will contain task-specific AI agents (from <5 % in 2025); a second Gartner figure, reported secondhand, claims 80 % of apps shipped in Q1 2026 contained at least one agent.[11] The 40 % comes from Gartner's primary announcement (Aug. 2025); we only have the 80 % through aggregators — treat as directional, not hard fact.
Methodological flag Deloitte's "State of AI in the Enterprise 2026" is cited with an average 171 % ROI from agent deployments (USA: 192 %).[13] We have this number only through a secondary aggregator (OneReach.ai), not from the primary report. Without sample size and ROI definition, it's marketing copy, not evidence — read with caution.

1 · AI Act readiness for German mid-market

2 · Pilot-to-production gate for AI agents

ELI5 & Value: Firms build AI agent pilots that look nice in a demo — then never go live (86–89 % fail). It's almost never the model; it's missing evaluation and governance: nobody can prove the agent is reliable enough to flip the switch. twiceD builds a fitted "Go/No-Go Gate": a test and scoring harness tailored to the actual workflow that automatically checks before each deploy whether the agent meets a defined quality bar. Value: a pilot that actually ships instead of landing in the graveyard.
Prototype (V0): (1) With the customer, define the agent use-case and success criteria (accuracy, hallucination rate, cost/latency). (2) Curate an eval dataset from real cases. (3) Set up a harness that scores each agent run against the criteria and sets a CI-like pass/fail gate. (4) A lightweight governance dashboard (trace, score history, approval button). V0 narrow: one agent, one gate, no full operations monitoring. Building blocks open/self-hostable: Langfuse or Arize Phoenix for tracing/eval, OpenLLMetry as the instrumentation standard.
Market & Gap: The LLM observability market is at roughly $2.7 billion in 2026 with ~36 % CAGR.[C] Players like LangSmith, Braintrust, Galileo, Langfuse, and Arize Phoenix[D] ship generic tracing/eval platforms — the customer has to wire use-case, criteria, and gate themselves. That wiring is exactly where the 86 % fail. Gap for twiceD: the fitted gate for one workflow, on-prem/sovereign on open building blocks — not another platform, but the missing glue.
Viability (solo + AI): Medium to high — the build comes from open source, twiceD adds criteria, wiring, and handoff. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo. Maintenance higher than the audit (the eval dataset needs care), but bounded if scope per customer stays tight; scaling via one contractor per engagement.
