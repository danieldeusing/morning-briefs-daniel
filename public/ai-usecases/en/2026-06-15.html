KPMG shifts 276,000 employees onto Agent 365

Microsoft + KPMG, 06/09/2026 · Lead Story

Microsoft and KPMG announced on June 9 that they will roll out Microsoft Agent 365 across the entire global workforce — 276,000 employees in 138 countries.[1] This is the largest named enterprise-agentic rollout to date and plays in a different league from typical pilot press releases.

Agent 365: Not a single chatbot, but an administrative layer for AI agents — like Active Directory, but for autonomous software workers. Identities, permissions, audit logs, "this agent may access SAP, that one only SharePoint". Generally available since May 2026.[2]

Operationally, that means KPMG will soon sell its customers the exact same governance machinery as a consulting service. Microsoft positions itself as "the hyperscaler for regulated industries with auditable agents"; KPMG gets the argument "we run it ourselves at 276k scale". Quantified ROI numbers did NOT come with it — this is a strategy deal, not a proof of impact.

Methodology flag KPMG has NOT quantified ROI metrics or usage rates — only "adoption and ROI tracking" as a qualitative promise. Read this as a distribution deal, not as proof of impact.

The SIs are the bottleneck — and it's becoming visible

NTT DATA × Google Cloud (06/08) + IBM × ServiceNow (06/11) · Pattern read

On the same three days KPMG announces the Microsoft deal, two more consulting giants do the same in different colors. NTT DATA + Google Cloud plan to co-develop up to 500 agents for banks, insurers, manufacturers, and retailers, announcing 5,000 certified Gemini Enterprise specialists as a target.[3] IBM + ServiceNow expand their alliance two days later: watsonx.data meets Workflow Data Fabric, with joint products in H2 2026.[4]

SI (System Integrator): The firm that does the heavy implementation work at the customer site — wiring software, reworking processes, training staff. Microsoft builds the engine; KPMG/NTT/IBM build the car around it and deliver it. Without them, an agentic product stays on the slide.

The pattern is clear: it's not the models that are the bottleneck, but integration with legacy systems — ERP, CRM, data silos, identity, compliance. ServiceNow calls its platform the anchor for 100+ billion workflows per year;[5] that is the "ground" IBM wants to ignite. Deloitte confirms from the other side: 23% of firms use agentic AI "at least moderately", but only 21% have mature governance.[6] The SIs are filling that gap now.

My reading: The next 12 months are not "which model wins", but "which three SIs manage not to cannibalize their consulting margins by becoming a platform themselves".

Microsoft Build 2026 — the enterprise reading

06/02–06/03/2026, San Francisco · Project Solara + Maia 200

Build was more infrastructure than spectacle — and for applied AI, two things count: a new device concept and proprietary silicon in production. Project Solara is an agent-first device OS built on Android (MDEP, not Windows); instead of apps, agents run and are managed via Intune + Entra ID. Pilot partners: CVS Health, Best Buy, Target, Levi's, AccuWeather.[7]

Agent-first device: Imagine the checkout tablet at a drugstore — today you have five apps on it, each with its own login. Solara flips it: an agent asks "what do you need to do?", and IT operates the apps for you, on your behalf. The device becomes an interface, not an app drawer.

In parallel: Maia 200, Microsoft's own inference accelerator, is already running in Iowa and Arizona, handling inference for Copilot and GPT-5.2 via Microsoft Foundry; Italy, Australia, South Korea follow.[8] Nadella said in April >30% better performance-per-dollar versus prior non-Maia silicon.[9]

Jurisdiction note The coding models from the Build stack (MAI-Code-1, MAI-Thinking-1, GitHub Copilot integration) belong in the ai-dev territory — here only the enterprise levers count: Solara pilots and Maia-200 economics as a case for vertically integrated MS stacks.

EU AI Act Omnibus: the deadline that isn't yet

Political agreement 05/07/2026 · not yet in Official Journal

Brussels agreed in early May on the "AI-Act Omnibus". Content: obligations for standalone high-risk systems under Annex III (HR, education, law enforcement, critical infrastructure) shift from 08/02/2026 to 12/02/2027 — minus 16 months. Annex I (AI embedded in regulated products) shifts to 08/02/2028.[10]

Caution: not legally binding yet The shift is a political agreement. Legal force comes only through formal adoption and publication in the EU Official Journal — expected before 08/02/2026.[11] Until then, the old deadline stands. Anyone stopping compliance programs now is planning on a deadline that legally doesn't yet exist.
Annex III: The "high-risk" categories in the AI Act — where AI can directly affect people: hiring filters, grades, police tools, grid control. Strict rules apply: data quality, logging, human oversight, documentation. Annex I is different: AI embedded in products already regulated by the EU (elevators, medical devices) — AI rules come "on top".

In practice: Article 50 transparency obligations (mark AI as AI, label deepfakes) stay on 08/02/2026 — not deferred. GPAI obligations (general-purpose models) apply as of 08/02/2025; older models must comply by 08/02/2027.[12] Anyone reading "deferred equals pause" misses two obligation packages that roll through.

KfW Focus #533: 20% — the only clean German number

02/11/2026, KfW Research · Primary source for German mid-market

In February, KfW released Focus #533 with the most robust German adoption figure: 20% of mid-market firms use AI (2022–2024) — five times as many as in 2016–18. That's roughly 780,000 firms.[13]

Pro KfW: representative sample (mid-market panel), clear definition of "AI use", development bank without sales interest.
Against "43%": An EY figure in a secondary aggregation measures more broadly ("digital technologies"). Not comparable — KfW is narrower and methodologically transparent.

The distribution says more than the total: large firms (50+ employees) 36%, knowledge-intensive services 28%, R&D-active firms 53% — construction only 8%. If you're selling to mid-market, you're not surveying "mid-market" but the sector (spread 8 vs. 53%, factor of 7).

McKinsey MGI: $486 billion for Germany — with methodology flag

May 2026, "Agents, robots, and us — Europe"

MGI calculates Germany's technical automation potential at 59% (EU median) and sees $486 billion unlockable by 2030 — roughly a quarter of that ($122 billion) in manufacturing. Demand for AI fluency from German employers has sextupled since 2023.[14]

Methodology flag "Technically automatable" is not "will be automated". MGI itself emphasizes: the number doesn't mean mass job loss, but a reallocation of tasks — and value only materializes where workflows are actively rebuilt and skills developed. Read $486 billion as the upper bound of possible, not a forecast.

What I'd watch: manufacturing pairs with $122 billion of industrial leverage and AI weakness (KfW: R&D-active industry only 23%). The real lever sits here — and mid-market manufacturers with IT investment backlogs are the segment the giants serve poorly.

Nubank nuFormer: 70% less risk, +50 bps market share

Q4 2025 results, analysis May 2026 · BR Finance

In its Q4 2025 report, Nubank described how its nuFormer model — a Transformer for credit risk, not a classical score — cuts risk by 70%.[15] In Q4, Nubank gained +50 basis points of BR credit card revenue market share — the largest absolute gain by any single provider in a decade.[15]

Transformer credit scoring: Instead of classical logistic regression on a few dozen features (age, income, debt), a language/sequence model reads the complete transaction history like text — who spent what, where, when — and infers risk from that. The advantage: patterns are captured that no human would define as a feature by hand.

Supporting data: Q4 net interest income $2.8 billion, net interest margin +0.6 pp to 10.5%, 90-day delinquency −0.1 pp to 6.6%.[15] Rollout planned for personal loans BR and credit cards MX/CO.[16]

Source caveat Nubank IR returned a 403; verified via two independent analyses (WhiteSight, Crowdfund Insider) of the Nu Holdings Q4 announcement Feb 2026.

From an applied-AI perspective, the cleanest story of the week: concrete model, concrete population, concrete risk reduction, concrete market effect — all from one source and materialized in the quarterly report.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI-Act readiness audit for German mid-market

ELI5 & value: A mid-market firm often doesn't know if its deployed AI (service chatbot, resume filter, predictive maintenance) falls under Annex III and what obligations attach. Like a GDPR check, but for AI: two days of audit, a report "these three systems are affected, here's your 12-month roadmap". Value: avoided fines (Annex III up to €15 million / 3% group revenue; prohibited practices up to €35 million / 7%[12]) and avoided panic when the Omnibus publishes.
Prototype (V0): (1) Standard question catalog per sector (HR, service, manufacturing, finance) as JSON schema. (2) Structured classification interview with customer (1–2 days), answers mapped against Annex I/III criteria. (3) Report generator (Claude/Llama local) fills a template: affected systems, obligations, maturity gap, 90/180/365-day plan. (4) Live validation with a friendly mid-market contact. MVP narrow: Annex III + Article 50 only; no technical conformity assessment in first engagement — that's follow-on work.
Market & gap: AI-Act consulting exists at PwC, KPMG, Bird & Bird and as platform tooling at Modulos, Trail, Holistic AI. Sector reports show ~78% of organizations had no serious compliance steps as of Q1 2026 — big demand-side, supply-side only at the edges. The Big Four focus on DAX/MDAX (day rates from €2,500); platform tools are SaaS subscriptions in English with US/UK contract logic. Gap for twiceD: the German-speaking mid-market firm (50–500 employees) — too small for PwC, too cautious for self-service SaaS, wants a human writing the result. On-prem variant with local Llama-3 report generator as sovereignty argument.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Audit day rates scale with Daniel's time, not headcount. Maintenance: obligation-mapping upkeep (~2 hours/week), one-time refresh at Omnibus publication. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo, scale via 1–2 certified freelance auditors. Risk: regulatory update frequency — standard-days clause in contract. modulos.ai · holisticai.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu

2 · Agent-governance light for the gap below Agent 365

ELI5 & value: Microsoft Agent 365 is the heavy solution for the KPMGs of the world — license costs, Entra coupling, complexity. An 80-person machinery manufacturer with two Copilot Studio agents and three n8n workflows doesn't need it, but needs something: a list "which agent can do what, what did it do last week, who approved it". Value: before the next audit season, the customer knows their agents match internal policy — and can feed that to the Annex-III auditor (Idea 1) instead of guesswork.
Prototype (V0): (1) Agent inventory: replace Excel maintenance with automatic harvesting from n8n/Copilot Studio/Make APIs. (2) Policy engine as YAML ruleset per customer ("Agent X may only read SharePoint Y"). (3) Audit-log aggregator: append-only storage (SQLite + S3 backup, no custom SIEM). (4) Weekly PDF report showing "what ran, what was blocked, what needs approval". Existing building blocks: OpenTelemetry for tracing, OPA (Open Policy Agent) for rule evaluation, Langfuse as observability layer — nothing built from scratch.
Market & gap: Agent 365 dominates the M365 ecosystem (license-coupled). Salesforce Agentforce and ServiceNow AI Control Tower[4] serve their own platforms. Platform-agnostic: Langfuse, Helicone, Arize — very technical, US-focused, no compliance reporting in audit language. Market size: AI governance directly answers the Deloitte 23/21 gap[6]; IDC sees the market in low single-digit billions USD in 2026. Gap for twiceD: 50–500 employees with mixed tooling need a platform-agnostic reporting layer in German with sovereignty hosting — nobody serves that cleanly yet.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium — honestly. Build solo in 6–8 weeks with AI help (OPA + OpenTelemetry are mature open-source blocks). But: real maintenance load — new agent platforms quarterly, each connector ~1–2 days upkeep. Realistically: solo for 3–5 customers, then 1 contractor per platform connector. Verdict: viable up to ~15 customers. On-prem hosting (Hetzner/Frankfurt) as sovereignty argument versus US SaaS. langfuse.com · openpolicyagent.org · servicenow.com
