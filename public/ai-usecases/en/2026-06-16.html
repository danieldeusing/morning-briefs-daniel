Gartner's "binary" governance — why good agents fail anyway

The week's most important thesis isn't a fresh number, but a finding about how. Gartner says (May 26): companies treat agent governance as an on/off switch — either completely locked down or fully trusted. Senior Director Shiva Varma calls that exactly "the root of failure." The forecast consequence: by 2027, 40% of companies will downgrade or shut down at least one of their autonomous agents because governance gaps only surface after a production incident.[1]

Agent governance: The rules for what an AI agent can do on its own versus where a human must approve — like the difference between an intern who must get email clearance and one who can transfer €50,000 on their own. "Binary" means you have only those two extremes, not gradual approvals based on action risk.

My take: This is the most honest analysis in the entire brief. The hype talks about models; the money burns at the control layer. "Fully locked" renders the agent useless; "fully trusted" makes it a liability bomb. The gap in between — risk level per action, audit log entry, emergency shutoff — no big corporation will build for the midmarket. That's an advisory opportunity, not a platform problem (see twiceD ideas below).

Two Gartner figures that get mixed up constantly

Because the same source issued two "40%" forecasts, here's the clean split — both are right, but they mean opposite things:

Adoption (Aug 2025): 40% of enterprise apps will contain task-specific agents by end of 2026 — up from under 5% in 2025.[2] That's the growth story.
Failure (June 2025): >40% of agentic projects will be canceled by end of 2027 — cost overruns, unclear value, poor risk control.[3] That's the warning.

In short: many firms build agents (adoption climbs steeply), but nearly half of projects don't survive to mature production. Both true at once — and that tension is exactly the story today.

Adoption reality: 57% already in production [4][5]

Against the skeptic's reflex: agents aren't a future slide anymore. A G2 survey (Aug 2025, n=1,035 B2B decision-makers) finds 57% of firms with agents in production, 22% in pilot, 21% before that. Top use case across all sectors: customer service. Median time to measurable value: 6 months.

Microsoft's Work Trend Index 2026 (20,000 knowledge workers, 10 markets) sees active agents in the M365 ecosystem grow 15-fold YoY (18× in large enterprises); 58% of AI users report producing work that was impossible a year ago. Salesforce reports ~$800M Agentforce ARR (standalone, Q4 FY2026) and in their own support over 1M interactions resolved autonomously (~84%, March 2026).

Methodology flag Self-report surveys (G2, Microsoft) and vendor-owned metrics (Salesforce) aren't independently verified ROI data — "in production" doesn't mean "proven to deliver value." Gold standard stays named customer stories with verifiable numbers (see NICE below).

JPMorgan: from 2 minutes to 2 hours agent runtime

The week's most concrete deploy story. Per CNBC (June 9), JPMorgan Chase plans agents this year that run 60–120 minutes straight autonomously — today it's 2–3 minutes. The bank already operates 450+ AI use cases in production and reports +20% gross sales in private banking; the goal for end of 2026 is 1,000 use cases.[6]

Long-running agent: Today's agents do a small job and report back — like an employee you give one task at a time. A long-running agent gets a goal ("fully prep this client meeting") and works through a whole chain of steps on its own before asking again. More leverage, but also more that can go wrong unnoticed.

Why it matters here: Longer runtime is exactly what sharpens Gartner's governance thesis. A 2-minute agent can do limited damage; an agent that runs free for two hours and triggers real actions needs gradual approvals and a complete audit trail. JPMorgan can handle that internally — the midmarket can't.

When surveys wobble, named production deployments help. NICE (contact-center / CX platform) reports Q1 2026 AI ARR of $345M (+66% YoY), AI in 100% of newly closed CXone enterprise deals, and AI backlog +78% YoY. Two real customer cases behind it:

  • Openreach (UK telecom backbone): 15M customer journeys redesigned, one-third fewer missed appointments, Trustpilot from 2.0 to 4.7.
  • Lufthansa: over 7 strike days managed ~2M agent interactions — full rebookings and refunds.

Backed up by the $955M acquisition of Cognigy (2025, ~25× revenue) — the largest consolidation in the CX-AI market to date. Striking is a quote from NICE CEO Scott Russell: "Building automated agents is easy. The real constraints are data quality, security review, guardrail definition, and auditability." Exactly the grunt work, not the models.

Netomi Series C: no-code agent CX, now backed by Accenture

Netomi raised (April 29) $110M Series C — led by Accenture Ventures and Adobe Ventures. The platform is a no-code orchestration layer that coordinates agent networks: proactively sense customer needs, execute actions, across channels. The real lever is the global alliance with Accenture, rolling Netomi out to enterprise customers.[9]

No-code orchestration: Instead of coding each agent workflow, you click it together in a UI — like a flowchart that runs instantly. Lowers the barrier to entry, but shifts the work: wiring to legacy systems, data validation and release still fall to the customer.

Midmarket angle: Accenture rolls this out to DAX corporations, not a 200-person machinery maker in Swabia. The no-code platforms (Netomi, Salesforce, ServiceNow) are horizontal — the customer pieces together workflow, wiring, and guardrails themselves. The "last mile" into a concrete German midmarket process stays open. That's exactly where twiceD sits (idea 2 below).

Standing thread — regulation: the August 2 deadline draws closer [10][11]

For anyone running agents in high-risk EU contexts (finance, HR, critical infrastructure), August 2, 2026 is the hard deadline: from then on, EU AI Act high-risk obligations are in full force. Fines up to €15M or 3% of global revenue. That's in ~7 weeks.

EU AI Act, high-risk: The EU law sorts AI by risk. "High-risk" means applications that make decisions about people (credit, hiring, medical triage). Those carry strict duties — like vehicle safety inspection, only for software decisions.

The agent-specific problem: they "act without a clear record of what they did, when, and why." That's exactly what the AI Act demands — Article 9 mandates ongoing, documented risk management; Article 13 calls for explainable/interpretable systems. Concretely, for agents: audit trail for at least 6 months, shutoff in seconds, human oversight with context.

My take: The deadline plus Gartner's governance finding plus the NICE CEO quote paint the same picture from three angles: technology isn't the bottleneck, controllability is. Whoever helps the midmarket become compliant by Aug 2 isn't selling AI — they're selling peace of mind.

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Agent governance & EU AI Act audit for the midmarket

ELI5 & value: A midmarket shop deploys an AI agent in customer service or accounting — and only after the first incident realizes nobody's logging what the agent does, and there's no kill-switch. twiceD comes in as external auditor: audit the agents, mandate missing audit trails, risk sign-offs, and shutdown mechanics, and make operations AI-Act-compliant. Value: avoided penalty risk (up to €15M / 3% revenue) and an agent that doesn't have to be shut down — exactly what Gartner says kills 40% of projects.
Prototype (V0): (1) Assessment questionnaire along AI Act Articles 9/13 plus Gartner's tiered governance model. (2) A real agent workflow from a pilot customer as test case. (3) Retrofit logging / audit layer (existing components: OpenTelemetry tracing, a policy engine like Open Policy Agent, no custom build). (4) Risk matrix per agent action plus kill-switch script. MVP intentionally tight: one customer, one agent, advisory plus retrofit, no ongoing ops.
Market & gap: Big SIs (Accenture, Capgemini, Deloitte) do AI Act advisory — but for DAX corporations at corporate rates. Only 21% of firms have mature governance per G2; the NICE CEO names exactly this bottleneck ("data quality, security review, guardrails, auditability").[a] Gartner quantifies the risk (>40% of projects at risk, 40% of companies facing at least one agent shutdown).[b] Gap: hands-on audit for a 200-person midmarket shop that can't afford Accenture and has no compliance office — and the Aug 2 deadline makes it urgent.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Advisory / one-shot work has near-zero ongoing ops — the effort is in assessment, not maintenance. V0 as questionnaire plus sample retrofit in 3–4 weeks solo; scaling is "one contractor per engagement." No standing team needed. Sovereignty variant: audit layer fully on-prem, since sensitive agent logs shouldn't leave the building. gartner.com · cmswire.com · cloudsecurityalliance.org

2 · Hands-on agent fit for German midmarket CX

ELI5 & value: No-code platforms (Netomi, Salesforce Agentforce, NICE) ship the agent engine — but the customer has to wire it to their ERP, shop, and data themselves. At a midmarket shop, that stalls because nobody has time. twiceD does the "last mile": take a concrete customer-service workflow (e.g., shipment status + returns) and wire it end-to-end to legacy systems, then ship it live. Value: a working agent in weeks instead of a dead platform subscription.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick an existing platform (Salesforce Agentforce or a European no-code builder like Konverso, SOC2). (2) Model one workflow from a pilot customer. (3) Build adapters to the target system (ERP/shop) plus data release. (4) Human approval before any write action. MVP tight: one channel, one workflow, one customer. Platform supplies model and orchestration — twiceD builds wiring plus fit, not the wheel.
