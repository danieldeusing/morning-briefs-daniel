OpenAI's trillion-dollar IPO—on paper nobody can read

OpenAI filed a confidential S-1 with the US Securities and Exchange Commission on Jun 8–9 and is targeting an IPO in Q4 2026. The target valuation ranges from $852B (the funding round closed in March 2026) to the aspirational $1 trillion mark. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are leading the offering, with a tranche for private investors planned.[1][2]

Confidential S-1: The S-1 is the mandatory document a US company files for an IPO—essentially a prospectus with financials, risks, business model. "Confidential" means only the SEC sees it initially; the public gets a look a few weeks before listing. In short: the filing is real, the contents stay locked down for now.

What it means for us: The juiciest figures aren't out yet. Revenue breakdown, margins, burn rate—all stay confidential until just before debut. Circulating figures (2025 year-end run-rate over $20B, full 2025 revenue ~$13.1B) come from a CFO blog post in January, not the S-1.[1] My take: the $1T headline is a wish, not a market price. For twiceD, nothing to grip here—it gets interesting once the figures are public and show how much of OpenAI's revenue actually comes from enterprise use cases versus chat subscriptions.

Siri now thinks with Gemini—a frontier model in a mass-market product

At WWDC on Jun 8, Apple introduced its new "Siri AI"—and the juicy detail is under the hood: Google's Gemini powers the foundation models that drive the new assistant experience, replacing OpenAI as the primary external model partner. Apple sticks to its privacy approach: tasks run on-device via Apple's "Private Cloud Compute," or on Gemini when needed.[3][4]

Foundation model: the large, pretrained AI model that understands and generates language—the "brain." Apple builds the wrapper (the Siri experience, app integration, privacy framework); Google supplies the thinking. Like a carmaker who installs a third-party engine but owns the chassis and safety.

The new Siri can act across apps, use personal context (messages, email, photos, notes), "see" the screen, and respond conversationally; there's a standalone app. The reported ~$1B/year for the custom Gemini model comes from Bloomberg and MacRumors reports—no official Apple/Google confirmation.[3] Why this matters, not just for consumer tech: it's the strongest evidence yet that even the world's most control-minded hardware giant buys a third-party frontier model instead of building its own. For anyone embedding AI in a product (including twiceD's customers), the lesson is: the model is purchased; value creation happens in integration, privacy, and workflow fit.

AI coding tools from WWDC (Xcode integration) belong in the ai-dev beat—here, just the use case.

Cybersecurity as an AI use case: OpenAI Daybreak [5][6]

Dated context (May 11): OpenAI launched Daybreak, a cybersecurity program that puts Codex Security at the center of vulnerability detection and patch validation. Three model tiers: GPT-5.5 (standard), GPT-5.5 with "Trusted Access for Cyber" (verified defensive workflows), and GPT-5.5-Cyber (red-teaming/penetration testing). Cloudflare, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Oracle, and Zscaler are already using the technology.

Why it matters: This is applied AI with measurable security effect—and a pattern that hits the mid-market once the big players prove it. OpenAI doesn't name prices; access goes through sales. (The coding-model side of this lives in the ai-dev beat.)

The ROI reality check: everyone's building agents, hardly anyone's making money

Methodology Writer survey, fieldwork Dec 17, 2025–Jan 25, 2026, n=2,400 (1,200 C-suite + 1,200 employees) from US/UK/IE/Benelux/FR/DE. Sponsor is Writer itself (an enterprise AI vendor)—so self-interest in "AI is hard but necessary"; the figures, though, align with independent adoption stats.

The numbers are a mirror for everyone selling AI consulting: 97 % of surveyed companies have already rolled out AI agents, 59 % invest over $1M/year—but only 29 % see significant ROI, 79 % hit barriers, and 48 % call AI adoption "a massive disappointment."[7] A second Writer finding weighs on morale: 60 % of companies plan to lay off employees who refuse to adopt AI.[8]

Enterprise AI: Deployment ≠ Payoff (Writer, n=2,400, Jan 2026)

My read: this isn't an AI failure, it's an execution failure. The models work; what's missing is the last mile—clean workflow fit, data, governance, someone who stays. That exact 71-point gap between "rolled out" and "pays off" is twiceD's business (see below).

How far has adoption really come? [9][10]

Cross-section of multiple surveys: EU27 companies were at 20.0 % AI adoption in 2025 (2024: 13.5 %), Denmark leads at 42.0 %, then Finland (37.8 %) and Sweden (35.0 %). In the SME segment, usage jumped from 22 % (2024) to 38 % (2026); 72 % of companies have at least one AI workload in production—but only 31 % actually run agents productively.

A commercial market estimate (Technavio/marketintelo, via tech-insider.org—paywalled, not independently verifiable) sizes the agentic AI market in enterprise automation at $10.86B (2025) → ~$15.2B (2026 forecast), CAGR ~47.4 % through 2030; the Germany segment at ~$0.51B.[9] Take as magnitude, not hard fact. Translate: lots of money in motion, but the gap between "agent is running somewhere" and "agent is making money" is vast—and widest in the mid-market.

Germany's sovereign AI stack takes shape

Two building blocks that together offer something US hyperscalers can't: Telekom × NVIDIA's "Industrial AI Cloud" goes live in Q1 2026 in Munich—1,000+ NVIDIA DGX-B200 systems plus RTX-PRO servers, scalable to up to 10,000 Blackwell GPUs, aimed at German enterprises, SMEs, and startups as sovereign infrastructure.[12] In parallel, SAP is building "OpenAI for Germany" (announced Sep 24, 2025, launch planned 2026): GPT models on SAP's Delos cloud on Azure, 4,000 GPUs planned, target audience German public sector—agencies, administration, research.[13]

Sovereign AI: AI computing that stays entirely under German/European legal and data control—data never leave a US corporate server. Like the difference between keeping your files in your own safe versus storing them overseas, where foreign agencies might theoretically peek.

Why it matters for twiceD: the expensive infrastructure is being built by someone else. What's missing is the integrator who takes a concrete mid-market workflow onto this sovereign stack—see project idea 2.

Standing thread—Regulation: EU AI Act [14][15]

The high-risk deadline for the EU AI Act (originally Aug 2, 2026) is wobbling: On May 7, 2026, the EU reached political agreement to push Annex III obligations by 16 months to Dec 2, 2027; product-safety-related AI (Annex I) shifts by 12 months to Aug 2, 2028—two separate deadlines, not to be mixed up. Critical: this is a political deal; formal adoption is still pending—legally, the old deadline still stands.

Annex III / high-risk AI: the list of AI uses the EU AI Act flags as especially touchy (e.g., AI in hiring, lending, critical infrastructure). Those have the strictest documentation and audit rules. The deferral gives firms breathing room—but only if it's formally enacted.

For twiceD customers, that means: planning certainty only after formal enactment. Anyone building a high-risk system right now should still document to the Aug 2026 standard and treat the deferral as a buffer, not a license.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gaps and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI ROI rescue: use-case audit for mid-market

ELI5 & value: Many mid-market firms have rolled out one or more AI agents but see no measurable payoff—today's Writer figure (97 % rolled out, only 29 % with real ROI, 48 % call it a "massive disappointment") is exactly this picture. A "use-case audit" is a paid, time-boxed diagnostic engagement: twiceD looks at the existing AI projects, measures what they actually deliver, finds the blocked last mile (workflow fit, data, governance), and provides a prioritized roadmap. Value: it turns sunk AI budget into measurable return without the customer starting from scratch.
Prototype (V0): (1) Standardized questionnaire + data-export checklist for the existing AI landscape. (2) A scoring matrix (cost/benefit, data readiness, risk) as a spreadsheet template. (3) Two fixed-price pilot audits with real customers. (4) Output as a compact report + 90-day roadmap. Deliberately out of scope: no building, no implementation in V0—pure advisory, which opens follow-on implementation as an option. Existing building blocks: LangSmith/Langfuse for agent tracing, own scoring templates.
Market & gap: Germany's mid-market is already served by many specialized agencies and consultancies in the agent space—aggregators list over a hundred vendors.[20] A commercial market estimate (Technavio/marketintelo, via tech-insider.org) sizes agentic enterprise AI at $10.86B (2025) → ~$15.2B (2026), CAGR ~47.4 %, Germany segment ~$0.51B—credible as magnitude only, not hard fact.[9] But most of these vendors sell *building* agents—few sell a neutral *audit of what's already built.* twiceD's gap: the vendor-neutral auditor who tells the truth: "these three agents pay for themselves, that fourth one is burning money"—a trust product that implementers won't offer from self-interest.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. An audit is a time-boxed engagement with near-zero maintenance load—no hosting, no ops. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo (templates + two pilots), ~10–15 hrs/week per live audit. Scaling cleanly via contractor per mandate. That's the profile a solo-plus-AI shop can sustain long-term. ensun.io · tech-insider.org · writer.com

2 · Sovereign mid-market agent on Germany's stack

ELI5 & value: German firms with sensitive data (government, health, finance, IP-heavy industry) can't or won't send their data through US cloud models. With Telekom × NVIDIA "Industrial AI Cloud" and SAP "OpenAI for Germany," Germany is getting real sovereign compute capacity for the first time in 2026. twiceD builds a concrete agent for *one* workflow (e.g., file triage in an agency, quote review in manufacturing) where data never leaves the country. Value: AI upside plus legal certainty that a generic ChatGPT connection doesn't offer.
Prototype (V0): (1) A real, data-sensitive workflow from a pilot customer. (2) An open model (Mistral Large 3, Aleph Alpha Luminous, Llama 3.3 70B) as the base—now competitive with US frontier models on German-structure tasks, sufficient for clearly bounded workflows. (3) RAG over customer documents, hosted on sovereign infrastructure (Telekom cloud or OVH SecNumCloud). (4) Human-in-the-loop before every action. Deliberately tight: one workflow, one tenant, on-prem/sovereign-only.
Market & gap: Infrastructure comes from Telekom/NVIDIA (Q1 2026, Munich) and SAP (Delos cloud, public sector).[12][13] What these platforms offer is compute—not the fit into a concrete mid-market process. Hyperscaler agents (Gemini Enterprise, Agentforce) are US-cloud-bound and thus off-limits for sensitive German data. twiceD's gap: the hands-on integrator who actually pours sovereign stack into a work process—German contract language, GDPR reflex, on-prem as default not as a pricey special request.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. Building a V0 is solo-doable (open model + RAG + adapter, 6–8 weeks), but ops on sovereign infra carries real load—monitoring, model updates, maybe GPU costs. Viable as long as scope stays tight (one workflow per customer) and ops is partly offloaded to the platform vendor. Scaling via contractor per mandate; no 10-person team needed as long as twiceD doesn't run its own infra. telekom.com · news.sap.com
