KPMG lifts 276,000 heads onto an agent stack

On June 9, KPMG and Microsoft announced that KPMG is lifting 276,000+ professionals across 138 countries onto Microsoft Agent 365 plus Microsoft 365 Copilot—the largest named rollout where AI agents are not just deployed enterprise-wide but centrally managed.[1] What's novel is not "big firm uses AI"—they all do. It's that oversight ships with it: every agent logged, observable, tied to identity and security policy.

AI agent: an AI program that doesn't just answer, but takes steps on its own—sort emails, compile a report, enter data in another system. Like a digital intern taking on tasks. The problem: when every department quietly hires its own intern, eventually nobody knows who does what and who accesses which data.

My take: this proves that "agent governance" graduated from buzzword to bought product. KPMG sells its clients agent consulting—so it puts itself in the window. For twiceD, the lesson isn't the deal itself but the pattern: the bottleneck is no longer the model, it's control over the fleet. Who built which agent, what can it do, and how do you prove it? That's where the money lands in 2026 (see Project Idea 1).

What Agent 365 actually is—and why the price matters

Microsoft Agent 365 has been generally available since May 1, 2026, at 15 $/user/month (or bundled in Microsoft 365 E7).[2] It follows an "observe–govern–secure" triad: central registry of all agents, observability (who did what), security/identity control. Critical for skeptics: the registry extends beyond Microsoft—as public preview it syncs agents from AWS Bedrock and Google Cloud too.[3]

Cross-cloud agent registry: a central list of all AI agents a firm runs—regardless of which cloud provider hosts them. Like an employee directory that also counts contractors from other firms, not just the home payroll. Without a list like that, you can't audit or shut down what you don't know exists.

Why the price matters: 15 $/user/month is enterprise volume pricing. Microsoft targets the big houses (enterprise services partners: Accenture, Bechtle, Capgemini, Insight, KPMG, Protiviti, Slalom; customer NTT DATA).[3] The German mid-market firm with 80 staff and three homegrown agents falls outside this net—it needs the same thing at small scale, hands-on, sovereign. That's the gap.

The governance gap, by the numbers

The sober flip side of the KPMG showcase. Presented at IBM's Think 2026 conference: by end-2026, most large enterprises will run over 1,600 AI agents—but 70% of executives call their AI governance "not fit for purpose," only 18% maintain a complete, current agent inventory, and just 12% have a central platform against "agent sprawl."[4]

Methodology The IBM figures come from an IBV survey presented at Think 2026; no directly linked source document with sample/methodology is available (secondary citation). Read as large-enterprise sample. Deloitte (below) is the independent cross-check.

Deloitte's "State of AI 2026" (n=3,235, surveyed Aug.–Sep. 2025, 24 countries) confirms the direction: only 21% of agentic-AI users have a mature governance model—the other 79% fly without one. Yet 73% plan to deploy agentic AI within two years.[5] Workforce access to authorized AI tools grew 50% in one year (from <40% to ~60%).[5]

Mature governance model: not a policy sitting in an intranet folder, but lived oversight—a current directory of all agents, clear owners, logs, regular audits, a kill-switch. Like the difference between "we have fire-safety rules in a folder" and "we run fire drills and know every exit."
Adoption races, oversight lags (IBM Think 2026 · Deloitte SoAI 2026)

My take: this is not an AI failure, it's a control gap. Agents are already running—what's missing is sight into what they do. That very gap between "we have agents" and "we know what they do" needs consulting, and soon it'll be legally forced (right).

Meta turns one billion daily chats into sales agents

On June 3, Meta Business Agent launched globally—and the scale is the real story: over 1 million businesses now use the service, handling 1 billion+ B2C chats per day across WhatsApp, Messenger, and Instagram.[6] The addressable market is the 3.5 billion people in Meta's messaging apps.[7] Brazil was among the early test markets—so directly relevant to Daniel's patch.[7]

Business Agent (conversational commerce): an AI chat assistant living right in a firm's WhatsApp/Messenger chat, answering questions, recommending products, and triggering orders—all without a human typing. Like a sales clerk on the floor, except they're in millions of chats at once and never clock out.

The agent can act, not just talk: across hundreds of connected systems like Shopify, Zendesk, and Shopee, it executes actions.[6] Why it matters: applied AI at consumer scale—and for Brazilian merchants where WhatsApp is the sales channel, a concrete lever. Honest caveat: Meta doesn't publish Brazil-specific adoption; "1M+ businesses" is the global sum.

Standing Thread—Regulation: EU AI Act, deadline August 2 [10][11][12]

In just under two months, the governance gap becomes a legal must in the EU. On August 2, 2026, the full high-risk obligations of the EU AI Act take effect—exactly 24 months after the regulation entered force.[10] Concretely: Article 12 makes automated logging mandatory for high-risk systems (keep logs for at least 6 months), Article 73 requires reporting serious incidents within 15 days, and Article 14 demands transparency on decisions.[12] Violations of high-risk obligations cost up to 15M EUR or 3% of global revenue; banned practices up to 35M EUR or 7%.[11]

High-risk AI (Annex III): the AI uses the EU AI Act flags as especially sensitive—AI in hiring, credit decisions, critical infrastructure, medicine. Those get the strictest rules: complete logs, explainable decisions, a human who can step in. Like the difference between a toy-drone license and approval to fly a commercial airliner.

Update—the Omnibus rolls: On June 16, 2026, the EU Parliament passed Digital Omnibus amendments with 423:57; formal adoption by the Council is imminent. Following the agreement, high-risk obligations shift: Annex III stand-alone systems move to December 2, 2027; Annex I embedded systems to August 2, 2028. Not yet finally in force—until publication in the Official Journal, August 2, 2026 remains the legally binding deadline. Advice to twiceD clients: document to the August-2026 state, treat the delay as a very likely buffer—not a free pass.

Market structure: who profits from oversight tools [13][14][15]

When every firm suddenly must watch and log its agents, a market grows: the agent-observability market (tools that monitor AI agents). The tight LLM-Observability-Platform segment: 1.97B USD (2025) → 2.69B USD (2026), CAGR ~36.3%.[13] The broader LLMOps market (incl. versioning, testing, cost tooling) grows from 7.14B USD (2026) to 15.59B USD by 2030, so CAGR ~21.6%.[14]

Observability / LLMOps: "observability" is the ability to see from outside what an AI does inside—every step, every input, every output logged. "LLMOps" is the whole operations toolkit around it (versioning, testing, cost control). Like an aircraft's black box plus the cockpit: one records, the other steers.

The tools are named and partly open: LangSmith (LangChain-adjacent, commercial), Langfuse (open source, MIT, self-hostable—acquired by ClickHouse in January 2026, 2,000+ paying customers), Arize Phoenix, Laminar, MLflow, Datadog LLM Observability, Helicone.[15] Critical for twiceD: these tools exist—but they're horizontal platforms. They log; they don't tell a German mid-market firm whether it meets Art. 12. That exact translation is the consulting product (Project Idea 1).

1 · Agent Inventory & EU AI Act Readiness for Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Many mid-market firms have quietly rolled out AI agents across different departments—one in sales, one in support, a script in accounting—and nobody has a list. Today's numbers prove it: only 18% know their agent inventory, only 21% have mature governance (IBM/Deloitte). twiceD delivers a short paid engagement: find all running agents, enter them into a registry, assign risk and owners, check what falls under the EU AI Act on August 2. Value: transforms "we don't know" into a documented inventory—exactly what Article 12 (logging) legally demands starting August.
Prototype (V0): (1) discovery checklist + interview guide to surface all agents/scripts/Copilot flows. (2) registry template (agent, purpose, data access, owner, risk class, EU AI Act relevance). (3) hook up an open-source observability tool (Langfuse, MIT, self-hostable) for ongoing logging. (4) readiness report + 90-day plan. Deliberately narrow: pure advisory + setup, no ongoing ops. Building blocks: Langfuse/Arize Phoenix for tracing, own templates.
Market & Gap: The tools exist already—LangSmith, Langfuse (acquired by ClickHouse, Jan. 2026, 2,000+ paying customers), Arize Phoenix, Datadog, MLflow.[15] Readiness market is real: mid-market faces 2–5M USD startup cost for high-risk compliance, 5,000–50,000 EUR audit per system, and from mid-2026 qualified auditors are booked solid. The Big Four (Deloitte, PwC) advise on EU AI Act—but for enterprise budgets. Tools are horizontal platforms, advice is enterprise-priced. Gap for twiceD: the vendor-neutral guide who tells an 80-person German firm "these two agents are high-risk, here's your log setup"—on-prem not SaaS sub, hands-on not self-serve.
Viability (solo + AI): High. time-bound engagement, nearly zero ops load—no hosting (the logging tool runs at the customer). V0 solo in 3–4 weeks, ~10–15 hrs/week per engagement. Scale via contractor per customer. The August deadline creates the time pressure that makes sales land—exactly the profile a solo-plus-AI shop can own. laminar.sh · cloudsecurityalliance.org · deloitte.com

2 · Conversational-Commerce Agent for BR Retailers on WhatsApp

ELI5 & Value: In Brazil, sales happen over WhatsApp—customers ask in chat about stock, price, shipping, and a human types painstakingly back. Meta's new Business Agent shows it can be automated (1M+ businesses, 1B+ chats/day), but the off-the-shelf tool is generic. twiceD builds a specific retailer a custom chat agent that knows their real catalog, their real stock, their shipping rules, and files orders directly. Value: no missed chats at night or weekends, fewer staff per sale, faster response—the metrics Brazilian retailers live by.
Prototype (V0): (1) one real pilot retailer with real product catalog. (2) WhatsApp Business Platform (Cloud API) as the channel, not custom infrastructure. (3) a model with RAG over catalog + FAQ so the agent cites real data instead of guessing. (4) hook to one shop/order system (e.g. Shopify or a BR ERP) to file orders; human confirms before close. Deliberately narrow: one retailer, one product category, one order path.
Market & Gap: Meta supplies the platform and the generic Business Agent;[6] Brazilian vendors like Take Blip and Zenvia plus global ones like Twilio build the conversational-commerce infrastructure (WhatsApp commerce GMV in LatAm ~20–25B USD by 2026).[19] What they deliver is the platform—not the fit-to-one workflow of a small retailer with no time or budget for platform implementation. Gap for twiceD: the hands-on integrator who sets the agent up ready-to-run for one specific retailer, instead of selling them a self-serve platform. BR-market closeness (Daniel in PR) is a real advantage here.
Viability (solo + AI): Medium. V0 solo feasible (WhatsApp Cloud API + RAG + one adapter, 4–6 weeks), but operations carry real load—the agent runs live in customer contact, errors are immediate, catalog updates must flow in. Tenable as long as scope stays narrow (one retailer, one path) and monitoring is built in.
