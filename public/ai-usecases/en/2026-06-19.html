OpenAI is now selling implementation—as its own company

On May 4th, OpenAI finalized the Deployment Company ("DeployCo")—a standalone subsidiary whose sole job is rolling out AI at enterprise customers. The vehicle is sized at $10 billion and launches with over $4 billion in private equity capital.[1][2] The investment round is led by TPG, with Brookfield, Advent, Bain Capital, and Goanna on board—19 investors total. OpenAI retains super-voting shares and contributes up to $1.5 billion of its own capital.

The telling part is the structure: OpenAI guarantees PE investors 17.5% annual returns over five years.[1] When you guarantee returns, you're either confident in the business—or you're passing the risk elsewhere. DeployCo is acquiring London-based Applied-AI firm Tomoro with ~150 "Forward Deployed Engineers" and bringing in Bain & Company, Capgemini, and McKinsey as implementation partners.[2]

Forward Deployed Engineer: A developer who doesn't sit in the lab—they're on-site at the customer, rebuilding their concrete workflow. Like a technician who doesn't just ship the machine but sets it up on the factory floor until it runs. That "last mile" is exactly what DeployCo is selling.

My take: This is the most honest statement of the year about the AI market. Models are commodities; the money is in implementation. Axios values the consulting unit at $14 billion—which conflicts with the $10 billion vehicle size and remains unresolved; likely enterprise value vs. raised capital, but only the $10 billion vehicle is cleanly confirmed.[3]

Anthropic overtakes OpenAI in revenue [4][5]

For the first time, Anthropic's revenue run-rate is ahead of OpenAI: $30 billion ARR (as of April 2026) vs. roughly $25 billion at OpenAI.[4] The surge is brutal—from $1 billion in January 2025 to $30 billion in 15 months, $9 billion to $30 billion in just four months. The driver is enterprise: over 1,000 companies each spending more than $1 million/year—double the count from February.

ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue): Revenue scaled to a full year. Take this month's revenue and multiply by twelve—like calculating your annual utility bills from one month's statement. A snapshot, not a booked annual profit.

Backing that up: a 3.5 GW TPU deal with Google and Broadcom (over 1 GW online by end of 2026, over 3 GW in 2027).[5] Claude is also the only frontier model deployed across all three major clouds—AWS Bedrock, Google Vertex AI, and Azure Foundry. Relevant for twiceD: Anyone selling mid-market sovereignty can use exactly this multi-cloud availability as a lock-in-prevention argument.

The maturity gap, finally quantified

On June 17th, Publicis Sapient released the 2026 Global Enterprise AI Report—and the central figure is the business model for the whole industry: 73% of companies regularly use AI across most processes, but only 10% say it's "at the core" of their business.[6] That's the "adoption-vs-readiness gap": nearly everyone has touched AI, almost nobody has embedded it.

In the US: 71% expect major scaling progress in 12–24 months—but only 20% feel fully equipped today.[6] That scissor is exactly what OpenAI with DeployCo and Anthropic with its 1,000 enterprise customers are monetizing.

Methodology n=1,550 AI decision-makers, 6 countries (US, UK, FR, DE, AU, UAE), companies 500+ employees and $100M+ revenue; survey 04/29–05/14/2026, conducted by Protégé. Self-reported—"uses AI" is softer than "measures value contribution." Still a useful benchmark, because the 73/10 scissor aligns with other sources.

Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite: the cost lever is GA

Since May 7th, Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite is generally available—the cheapest, fastest model in the Gemini 3 family. Price: $0.25 per million input tokens, $1.50 per million output tokens, at 1M-token context and roughly half the cost of Gemini 3 Flash.[7][8] Google's customer example (Gladly) shows p95 latency of ~1.8 seconds for a full response and ~60% lower cost vs. comparable "thinking" models.[7]

Why this matters: In volume workflows—classification, routing, bulk extraction—the winner isn't the benchmark peak, it's the price per million tokens. At this price point, a workflow becomes profitable that was uneconomical at premium-model rates. (For coding-model comparisons themselves: see the ai-dev brief.)

Stay honest A circulating claim of "2.5× faster" is not verifiably backed—only price, context window, and GA date are confirmed. Don't cite the speed multiplier.

EU AI Act: the high-risk clock is ticking [9][10][11]

In roughly six weeks—on August 2nd, 2026—obligations for Annex III high-risk systems become enforceable.[9] That covers AI in hiring (candidate screening), credit scoring, education, and law enforcement—exactly the areas mid-market companies are rolling AI into now, often without realizing it's "high-risk."

Annex III (High-Risk): The list in the EU AI Act that names AI applications that decide about people—who gets a job, a loan, a school spot. Like a seatbelt law: not every car is covered, but if yours carries people, seatbelt rules apply (risk management, documentation, human oversight).

Penalties for high-risk violations go up to €15 million or 3% of global revenue (Art. 99 para. 4 — compare GDPR: €20 million / 4%).[10] The higher penalty range of €35 million / 7% applies only to the prohibited practices under Article 5 — not Annex III obligations. The proposed "Digital Omnibus" that would push the deadline to December 2027 is not yet law—companies should treat August 2nd as an operational deadline.[11]

My take: This is the most concrete twiceD opportunity of the week (see below). A specific, dated compliance obligation plus a DE mid-market that mostly doesn't know if they're affected—that's a consulting hook.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · EU AI Act Readiness Check for German mid-market

ELI5 & Value: Starting August 2nd, EU obligations apply to AI that decides about people—candidate screening, credit scoring, education tools. Many mid-market firms have this in play without knowing it's "high-risk." The check is a clearly scoped audit: Are you affected, where, and what do you need to do by when? Value: avoided penalties (up to €15 million / 3% revenue for high-risk violations, Art. 99 para. 4) and planning certainty—purchased for a fraction of that.
Prototype (V0): (1) A structured questionnaire that maps AI deployments against the Annex III list. (2) A risk-classification logic (affected / not affected / gray zone) as a small rule-based app—no bespoke LLM needed, the statute is final. (3) Auto-generated action report (risk management, documentation, human oversight) per finding. MVP deliberately narrow: only Annex III, one report, no ongoing monitoring. Building blocks: consolidated AI Act text (artificialintelligenceact.eu), a template generator, optionally an LLM just to write the report prose.
Market & Gap: Tooling exists—e.g. Credo AI, Holistic AI, and OneTrust AI Governance—and top law firms (Hogan Lovells, Holland & Knight) publish deadline guides.[9] But platforms are enterprise-priced and horizontal: the customer must sort their own processes, and the law firm delivers opinion without technical execution. Gap for twiceD: the small German mid-market player who can't afford a governance platform subscription or a BigLaw retainer—they want one concrete answer and an action list, hands-on, in German, with the August 2nd date looming.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. This is advisory with near-zero maintenance—one closed audit per engagement, no hosted service. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo; the statute changes only on Omnibus passage (then one update). Scale via templates + 1 contractor per wave. Sovereignty variant: the check runs fully on-prem/local, zero customer data leaves the house. hklaw.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu

2 · Cost Audit "Expensive Model Out, Flash-Lite In"

ELI5 & Value: Many firms route every AI call to an expensive premium model—including trivial classification, routing, or bulk extraction that a cheap model handles just as well. The audit finds workflows where switching to a budget model (e.g. Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, $0.25/$1.50 per million tokens) makes sense without quality loss. Value: direct saved inference costs, often 50–70% on the affected volume.[8]
Prototype (V0): (1) Logs/prompts from a real customer workflow as a test set. (2) A "router" prototype that decides per call: trivial task → Flash-Lite, hard → Premium. (3) A/B scoring that measures quality (hit rate) vs. cost in one figure. MVP deliberately narrow: one workflow, two models, human validates routing rules. Building blocks: OpenRouter/Vertex as model access, a lean eval harness, no bespoke model training.
Market & Gap: Model routing as a product exists—OpenRouter, Martian, Not Diamond, LiteLLM (open source).[8] But they deliver infrastructure to route, not the answer of whether a concrete business workflow can switch and how much it saves. That eval and decision step—backed by real customer data and documented quality proof—stays handwork. Gap: a paid, scoped cost audit instead of another routing SDK.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): Medium to high. The audit itself is advisory (low maintenance); if the customer wants the router operated, ongoing load emerges (model prices and versions shift). Honest: V0 in 2–3 weeks solo; as pure advisory, highly sustainable; as a hosted router, only with narrow scope and 1 contractor per customer. openrouter.ai · cloud.google.com
