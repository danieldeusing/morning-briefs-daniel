Google fills the agent marketplace [1][2][3]

Google Cloud has grown the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform (GA on April 22) into a store with 70+ pre-built partner agents — Adobe, Atlassian, Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Workday are among 60+ named partners. Added to this is a $750M partner fund for 120,000 ecosystem members and — the real announcement — $460B in committed enterprise budget that partners can address through the marketplace.[1][2]

Agent marketplace: Think of an app store — except the "apps" aren't programs you click, but AI workers that complete tasks by themselves (review invoices, handle tickets). A company picks a ready-made agent instead of building one from scratch.

The point isn't the number of agents, but the seal of quality: each listed agent carries the "Google Cloud Ready – Gemini Enterprise" badge only after a four-stage review of function, accuracy, autonomous execution, and enterprise standards. That's the lever — Google isn't selling "AI," it's selling vetted agents, and removing the buyer's selection anxiety. Marketplace vendors are already closing 112% larger deals.[1]

My take: This is the moment the platform giants shift competition from "best model" to "best sales ecosystem." For a solo shop like twiceD, the lesson cuts both ways — the marketplace eats the generic agent business, but leaves the last mile (fitting into a concrete DE process) open. More on that in the project ideas.

Physical AI gets its open foundation model [7][8]

NVIDIA Cosmos 3 (launch June 1, GTC Taipei) is, according to NVIDIA, the first fully open "omnimodel" for physical AI — one that unites text, images, video, ambient sound and actions in a single model. The claim: training and test cycles for robots and autonomous vehicles shrink from months to days.[7]

Physical AI / omnimodel: Most AI models work only with text or images on a screen. "Physical AI" controls things in the real world — a robot arm, a car. An "omnimodel" is like a generalist instead of many specialists: one model that sees, hears, and acts at once, rather than wiring three separate systems together. Training begins in simulation — cheaper and safer than on the actual robot.

Cosmos 3 comes in three variants — Super (highest physics accuracy), Nano (fast inference), and Edge (for real-time on-device, "coming soon"). NVIDIA claims top spots on several open leaderboards (including Physics-IQ, RoboArena) and is founding a "Cosmos Coalition" with Agile Robots, Runway, Skild AI, and industry users like LG, Samsung, and Li Auto.[7][8]

Framing The addressable market is small today: physical AI was around $1.5B in 2026, projected to reach $15.24B by 2032 (47% CAGR, a market research forecast — not a hard number).[9] For twiceD, this is watch-and-wait territory for now, not a consulting field: robotics demands hardware depth that a solo shop can't deliver.

ServiceNow & Salesforce: Agents as product catalog [4][5][6]

ServiceNow Autonomous Workforce

At Knowledge 2026 (May 5), ServiceNow extended its Autonomous Workforce to every major business function — AI specialists for IT, CRM, employee service, and security/risk. The headline: the L1 IT service-desk agent resolves cases "99% faster" than a human agent, and employee-service specialists handle 91% of cases without escalation (across 40M cases annually).[4]

Caveat Important for expectations: The L1 service-desk agent is available, but the broader IT AI specialists (Operations, SRE, Asset Lifecycle) were "expected for June 2026" in the May announcement. We haven't found a GA confirmation today — Security/Risk specialists are only in preview as of June anyway, with GA in September.[4]

Salesforce Agentforce

Salesforce released multi-agent orchestration to the public on June 15 (Summer '26 release, out of beta). Commercially, Agentforce delivers: $800M ARR (+169% YoY), 29,000 deals, 2.4B "agentic work units." Customers report $100M+ in annual savings.[5][6]

Multi-agent orchestration: Instead of one all-purpose agent, multiple specialized agents work together — like a small team where one researches, one designs, one reviews. "Orchestration" is the conductor who decides who goes when and brings results together.

My take: Three vendors, one pattern — the agent function moves from "feature" to standalone priced product with ARR visibility. That's the most solid development this week. But: these are vendor numbers (ARR, "99% faster," savings), not independently audited customer audits. This gap is exactly what leads to the next section.

Return reality — where accounting lags marketing [10][11]

Against all the GA announcements stands a sobering figure: only ~29% of organizations see meaningful ROI from generative AI — despite 5× productivity gains for individuals. Forrester adds: only 15% of AI decision-makers report a positive profitability effect.[10]

Methodology "Meaningful ROI" and "productivity gain" are rarely defined in such surveys — mostly self-report, no audited financials. The 5× individual gains are often micro-effects (one task faster) that evaporate at the organizational level. Read the figure as "value creation is real but localized and hard to capture," not "AI doesn't pay."

The contrast to adoption is stark: 72% of companies have at least one AI workload in production (2024: 55%). The question has shifted from "do we use AI?" to "why doesn't it show up on the balance sheet?" — and that gap is exactly where a consultant digs in.[11]

Standing Thread — Regulation & Market [12][13]

EU AI Act, August 2: On this date the transparency obligations (Art. 50) become binding — disclose AI interaction, label AI-generated content and deepfakes; penalties up to €35M or 7% global revenue. The "Digital Omnibus" is no longer a mere proposal: the EU Parliament adopted it on June 16 (423–57), with the Council expected to follow on June 29 — after which it takes effect on publication in the Official Journal. It moves the high-risk obligations for Annex III systems (employment, credit, education, law enforcement) to December 2, 2027 (AI embedded in regulated products: August 2, 2028). So August still bites — on transparency, not high-risk.[12]

Market context: Gartner expects 40% of enterprise apps to carry task-specific AI agents by end of 2026 (from <5% in 2025) — this is an official Gartner forecast and well documented. The circulating figure of $201.9B in agentic AI spending for 2026 comes only from secondary sources citing Gartner — treat as a rough estimate, not a verified number.[13]

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gaps and honest solo feasibility

1 · Agent ROI Audit — from pilot to balance sheet

ELI5 & Value: The return story above is the business case: 72% of companies have AI running, but only ~29% see meaningful ROI. Many mid-market firms have an agent pilot going and simply don't know if it pays — vendors deliver "99% faster," but no number for this operation. An "Agent ROI Audit" is a brief, guided stocktake that measures an active AI deployment against real hours, error rates, and license costs — and gives an honest yes/no/improve answer. Value: the customer stops blindly paying or pulling the plug too soon.
Prototype (V0): (1) Standardized questionnaire + data pull from the affected system (ticket tool, CRM): volume before/after, processing time, escalations. (2) A lean calculation template that sets vendor license + internal hours against measured time savings. (3) Auto-report with status light per use case + prioritized recommendations. MVP intentionally narrow: one existing agent, assessment only — no new deployment, no model swap (that's the follow-on project).
Market & Gap: The vendors themselves provide dashboards — Salesforce shows for Agentforce "$3.50 return per $1 spent" and $100M+ savings,[1] and ServiceNow's AI Control Tower measures agent activity.[2] But both have an obvious interest in a rosy outcome and measure inside their platform. The Big Three consultancies (Accenture, BCG) do it neutrally — but as a six-figure project. The gap in between: an affordable, vendor-neutral, German-language verdict for the mid-market firm that wants one pilot assessed.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High — at core a one-shot audit with reusable questionnaire + calculation template, minimal ongoing upkeep. twiceD handles the heavy lifting (data pull, sanity-check) with AI support; scaling = one contractor per concurrent audit, no standing team. Honest caveat: the value lives in the judgment call (what counts as "gain"?), sharpened across real audits — not the tech. salesforce.com · newsroom.servicenow.com

2 · The last mile — fit marketplace agent into a DE process

ELI5 & Value: Google's Gemini marketplace and Salesforce/ServiceNow ship ready-made, vetted agents off the shelf. What they don't deliver is the fit into the concrete process of a German mid-market firm: its ERP, its document types, its GDPR duties, its edge cases. A "last-mile" offer takes a marketplace agent and makes it work live in the customer's operations — integration, validation against master data, human-in-the-loop sign-off. Value: the customer uses a standard agent without spinning up an in-house dev team.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick a fitting marketplace agent (e.g., for order/receipt processing) and test it on the customer's real doc stack. (2) Hook into one target system for one document type via the platform API. (3) Validation logic against master data + error escalation. (4) Human confirms before posting. MVP intentionally narrow: one agent, one process, one customer — auto-posting only after proven runs.
Market & Gap: The marketplace itself is huge — Google opens $460B in committed budget and names 70+ vetted partner agents.[1] That very scale is the gap: platforms sell horizontal and self-service; the 20-person mid-market firm with a legacy system sits alone. The Big System Integrators (Accenture, Deloitte) do the last mile — but only at large-project scale. Gap for twiceD: hands-on fit in mid-market size, German-language, with on-prem/sovereignty option for sensitive data.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium to high. The heavy model and the agent come from the platform — twiceD builds adapters, validation, and ops. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo per customer; maintenance stays light as long as scope stays tight (one process). Risk stated plainly: platform-API lock-in — if the vendor changes pricing or interface, it hits operations. So keep margin tight and offer the sovereignty variant (open-weight model fallback) to the data-conscious customer. cloud.google.com · gartner.com
