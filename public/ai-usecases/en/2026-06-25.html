"AI authenticity" becomes a product feature, not an add-on

Superhuman — the company that recently went by Grammarly[2] — is acquiring the AI detector GPTZero. The deal value was not disclosed.[1] What matters isn't the price, but what is being bought: GPTZero has over 19M registered users and built roughly $30M in annual recurring revenue (ARR)[1] — as a tool that detects whether a text came from AI. Before this, the startup had only raised $13.5M in venture capital (seed $3.5M + Series A $10M in June 2024).[1]

AI detector: A tool that estimates the likelihood a text (or image) was written by a machine — like a lie detector for written content. GPTZero was built in 2023 by Princeton student Edward Tian as a bachelor's project, originally for teachers wanting to catch AI homework.[1]

The plan: GPTZero becomes an authenticity layer in Superhuman Go, the AI assistant that, according to the vendor, runs in 1M apps and websites.[3] Superhuman counts 40M users, 50,000 organizations, and 3,000 educational institutions.[3] The 30-person GPTZero team, including co-founder Alex Cui, is moving over; the product continues independently.[1]

My take: This is clever, almost cynical. Grammarly/Superhuman spent years helping people generate text — and now buys the tool that detects whether text was generated. The honest reading: in a world where every email is AI-polished, "real vs. machine" becomes a paid question. Whoever sells writing tools now has to sell trust alongside them.

Engram: "learned memory" instead of search layer [4][5]

Engram launched with $98M — at a $600M valuation with only 13 employees, according to Calcalist.[5] (The funding/valuation side — who's investing, what it signals about the market — belongs in the stocks-crypto brief; here we focus on the product.)

RAG (what Engram is replacing): Instead of letting the AI guess, the system searches a document database for relevant passages on each question and feeds them to the model as context. It works, but it's expensive: every single question requires a new search, and the found text gets sent as tokens.

Engram flips it: the model learns an organization's world — documents, processes, institutional knowledge — upfront and compresses it into a reusable "learned memory" layer, instead of searching on every query.[4] This is being tested, the company says, at Microsoft (Microsoft 365), Notion (proprietary agents), and Harvey (legal AI).[4] Investors include General Catalyst, Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia, plus angels like Andrej Karpathy and Wiz co-founder Assaf Rappaport.[4]

Claim check Engram claims to "match or beat frontier models — with up to 100× fewer tokens."[4] This is a vendor claim with no public benchmark. "Up to 100×" means: best case, on an unspecified task. Before piloting, I'd insist on a reproducible comparison, not a press release. The company also doesn't mention Engram's founding year in this source ("founded in October," year unspecified) — so I'm leaving it open.

Why this matters: If the approach holds, the costliest part shifts from "per question" to "once upfront." That's exactly the pattern twiceD customers in mid-market need — and that's where I'm building a project idea below.

Dragos EmberAI: AI for the systems nobody can shut down [6][7]

Dragos has made EmberAI generally available (GA, as of 06-23, directly in the Dragos platform).[6] It's an AI assistant specifically for OT security — grids, factories, water utilities, pipelines, data centers.

OT (Operational Technology): The computers that run physical machines — turbines, conveyors, valves — as opposed to IT, which manages data. Simply: IT handles the factory's email, OT turns the screws. OT attacks are more dangerous, because a mistake doesn't delete a file; it stops a system — or worse.

The sales pitch is data depth no generic model has: EmberAI relies on 5 petabytes of daily OT telemetry, vulnerability research across 600+ OT protocols, and 10+ years of tracking named OT threat groups.[6] Dragos holds the leader position in Gartner's Magic Quadrant for CPS protection platforms for the second year running, per the company.[7]

Sovereignty by design For me, the most important sentence: customer data stays in their environment, every recommendation is transparent and traceable, and analysts keep the final call.[6] That's the countermodel to "ship everything to a cloud API" — and exactly the argument that lands with German mid-market and critical infrastructure.

My take: The real bottleneck in OT security isn't tools; it's people — there aren't enough specialists who understand both a power grid and cybersecurity. EmberAI is essentially packaged senior expertise. That's an honester promise than "autonomous AI security," because humans keep the decision.

Gartner, twice: the hype first, then the hangover [10][11]

Methodology Both are Gartner forecasts (published 2025, not today), not measured actuals. "Forecast" means: model estimate, not a sample of counted deployments. Read with the usual caution around analyst numbers.

Gartner says, on one hand: 40% of enterprise apps will have embedded task-specific AI agents by end of 2026 — up from under 5% in 2025.[10] On the other: >40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027 — due to escalating costs, unclear business value, and insufficient risk controls.[11]

Why both are true at once: The first number is embedding (a feature rolls out), the second is project survival (the feature delivers value or gets axed). This is exactly where twiceD's opportunity sits: not the hundredth generic agent, but the one that measurably cuts a concrete process — with cost and risk controls from day one. The cancellation wave isn't an argument against AI projects; it's an argument against poorly scoped ones.

Regulation: EU delays high-risk — but not transparency [8][9]

Important: not yet law What follows is a provisional political agreement (Omnibus, May 7, 2026, confirmed by member states May 13) — it is not yet formally enacted and not yet published in the EU Official Journal.[8] Until that happens, none of this is binding law. Don't plan based on it as final.

Substantively: obligations for standalone high-risk systems under Annex III are to shift from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027.[8] For AI embedded in regulated products (Annex I), the deadline moves to August 2, 2028.[8]

Annex III / "high-risk": The AI Act tiers AI by harm. "High-risk" = AI that makes decisions about people, where mistakes hurt: hiring screening, credit decisions, medicine, critical infrastructure. These systems must be documented, tested, and monitored — that's the expensive compliance that now gets 16 months more.
What's NOT delayed August 2, 2026 remains an active deadline: the transparency mandate under Article 50 — AI-generated or manipulated content (chatbots, synthetic text/images, deepfakes) must be labeled as such.[8] Anyone deploying AI content should keep planning toward that date, regardless of the Omnibus.

To frame the scale: violations on high-risk systems can run up to €15M or 3% of global annual turnover; banned practices up to €35M or 7%.[9] The delay buys time to build — it doesn't cancel the obligation.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI Authenticity & Transparency Audit for German Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Starting August 2, 2026, AI-generated content in the EU must be labeled as such (AI Act, Art. 50) — and more companies want to know whether an incoming text/application/report came from AI. twiceD doesn't sell a detector product; it sells an audit + workflow: "Where does AI content originate or land in your shop, and how do you make it compliant and auditable?" The value is avoided penalty risk and a documented process — not "we built in AI".
Prototype (V0): (1) One concrete customer workflow as a pilot (e.g., application intake or marketing copy). (2) Plug in existing detector/watermark building blocks rather than training your own — GPTZero API, Originality.AI, or C2PA/Content Credentials for image provenance. (3) A lean labeling and check step in the customer's tool. (4) A compliance folder: what Art. 50 requires, how the process meets it. Deliberately out: your own detector, a SaaS platform.
Market & Gap: The AI detection market is real and growing fast — roughly $0.58B (2025) → $2.06B (2030), ~28.8% CAGR, with named players like Turnitin, Grammarly, GPTZero, Originality.AI, Copyleaks, Pindrop.[A] But: these are tools, not compliance solutions — they output a probability score, not an Art. 50-compliant, documented process for a German GmbH. That exact "last mile" (law + workflow + proof, in German, GDPR-compliant) is a gap the US tool vendors don't fill.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. This is mostly consulting/audit work with low ongoing maintenance — the heavy lifting comes from the detector vendor, twiceD supplies law, fit, and proof. V0 solo in 3–5 weeks, scaling per customer via contractor. Risk: the legal landscape is in flux (Omnibus not yet final) — so pitch it as recurring audit, not a one-time stamp. marketsandmarkets.com · gibsondunn.com

2 · Sovereign Org-Memory Layer (on-prem) for Regulated Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Engram shows the pattern: instead of expensively searching company documents on every AI question (RAG), the system learns firm knowledge once upfront and reuses it. For the customer: cheaper, faster, more consistent AI answers on their own data. twiceD's value: the same principle, but on-prem/sovereign — for law firms, insurers, manufacturers who can't ship their files to a US cloud API.
Prototype (V0): (1) One bounded knowledge corpus from the customer (e.g., a handbook + contracts from one department). (2) Memory layer via an open-source framework — Letta, Zep, or Cognee — on an open model that runs locally/on customer infra. (3) A Q&A/agent surface for exactly one use case. (4) measurement against plain RAG: answer quality, latency, token cost. Deliberately out: reimplementing Engram — twiceD assembles available pieces.
Market & Gap: A whole vendor layer already exists — Letta, Zep, Mem0, LangMem, Cognee — and they compete on benchmarks (Zep 63.8% vs. Mem0 49.0% on LongMemEval).[B] But: these are frameworks/cloud services, not finished on-prem solutions for a German insurer. Engram itself targets Microsoft/Notion/Harvey — not German mid-market under sovereignty rules. The gap: sovereign fit on a concrete workflow, without the customer having to wire up framework, model hosting, and validation themselves.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. The build is solo-doable (pieces are open source), but ops load is real: model hosting, monitoring, re-indexing on new docs. Honest take: this isn't a one-time audit; it's a managed service — only qualifies if scope stays tight (one use case, one customer) and hosting lives on-site. Scaling "one contractor per customer," not "own ops team." agentmarketcap.ai · atlan.com
