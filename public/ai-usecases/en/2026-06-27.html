KPMG deploys 276,000 employees to an agent management layer

The week's biggest story is not a model, but a management layer. KPMG has, together with Microsoft, rolled out Agent 365: over 276,000 partners and employees across 138 countries now get a central control plane for "how AI agents are deployed, managed, monitored, and updated".[1]

Agent management layer: Think of AI agents like employees working around the clock—except every department secretly makes its own. A management layer is the HR department for them: it knows which agents exist, what they're allowed to do, what they're doing, and can shut them down. Without it, you have hundreds of unsupervised "interns" with access to real systems.

Why it matters: The constraint in enterprise is no longer "can the model do the task?"—it's "can we control it?" The fact that an auditor—whose entire business is control—bought management capability first, not raw power, is the signal of the month. My take: this is the most honest roadmap any large corporation has published this year—governance first, then scale.

The execution layer is being consolidated—in four weeks [2][3][4][5]

Four acquisitions in four weeks, all with the same motive: whoever owns a platform is now buying the ability for agents to act across third-party systems.

Execution layer: A chatbot can talk; an execution layer can act—create an order in the ERP, negotiate a contract in procurement, broadcast content across every channel. It's the difference between an assistant that says "call the supplier" and one that actually calls, negotiates, and logs the result.
  • Asana → StackAI ($75M, 28.05): "cross-system execution for human-agent teams"—agents that work across tool boundaries.[2]
  • Coupa → Rossum (12.05): AI document processing for autonomous spend management—Rossum's model trained on dozens of millions of documents.[3]
  • Salesforce → Contentful (01.06): Headless CMS so Agentforce can "dynamically compose and deliver content across every channel"—4,800+ brands, ~30% of Fortune 500.[4]
  • Vertice → Vendr (01.06): "world's largest procurement dataset"—$75B+ in indirect spend, 32,000 suppliers, autonomous AI negotiation.[5]

The pattern is unmistakable: the giants are buying the verbs (create, negotiate, deliver) to go with their nouns (CRM, spend, projects). The AI-in-Procurement market alone is projected to grow from ~$4.25B (2026) to ~$40B (2035).[5] For mid-market companies, this means: the finished execution layer will soon be bundled in enterprise subscriptions—if you're smaller, you get generic platforms and have to wire them yourself. That's exactly where idea 2 comes in below.

The governance gap: everybody deploys, barely anyone manages

The adoption curve is steep. Gartner expects that by end of 2026, roughly 40% of enterprise apps will have embedded task-specific AI agents—up from under 5% in 2025.[6] Deloitte's "State of AI 2026" (survey Aug–Sep 2025, n=3,235 leaders, 24 countries) measures: employee access to approved AI tools grew 50% in one year—from under 40% to roughly 60% of the workforce.[7]

But oversight is visibly lagging: according to the same Deloitte study, only 30% of firms rate their risk and governance strategy as "well prepared" (versus 42% for AI strategy overall).[7]

Methodology flag One widely cited statistic goes: 72% of companies with agentic AI in production, but 60% without formal governance. The source is the Agentic AI Institute (founder Corey Wick); the article is behind a paywall, and sample, methodology, and funding are not publicly verifiable. Treat the 72/60 as plausible but unsubstantiated marketing—the more solid claim is the Deloitte measurement above (30% "well prepared"), which discloses sample size and survey period.

Both numbers point the same direction, and that's the point: agents go to production faster than anyone builds guardrails. This is not hype risk, but concrete liability risk—and for a German regulatory regime (see right) soon statutory risk.

EY: first Big Four complete rollout in audit

EY has rolled out agentic AI across the entire audit function: 130,000 assurance professionals, 160,000 audit mandates, 150+ countries—and a system processing roughly 1.4 trillion journal entry lines annually.[8] Full end-to-end audit is targeted for 2028.

Together with KPMG (above), this shows a pattern: auditors—the professional skeptics—are the earliest full adopters of agentic AI. When the one profession whose job is distrust deploys the tool, that's a stronger adoption signal than any consulting deck.

Germany: the regulatory regime is coming

While corporations deploy, Germany is building guardrails: with the AI Market Surveillance Law (AI-MIG), the Federal Network Agency becomes the central AI regulator; the EU AI Act mandates AI competency requirements for all employees starting Aug 2026 (the Digital Omnibus deferred only the high-risk-AI obligations to Dec 2027 — the competency duty itself still applies from Aug 2026).[9]

AI competency mandate: Companies must prove their people understand AI enough to use it responsibly—like a forklift certification, but for AI tools. If you deploy agents without anyone understanding what they do, you violate law, not just good practice.

For German mid-market, this is the bridge from "nice-to-have" to "must document." That mandate is exactly what makes the audit business below (idea 1) into a market with a hard deadline.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI agent audit for German mid-market

ELI5 & value: Mid-market companies are increasingly deploying AI agents (in CRM, procurement, support), but often don't know what agents are allowed to do, what they're doing, and what they log. An "agent audit" is a TÜV for these agents: an outside expert reviews permissions, data flows, and logging against law and common sense, then delivers a report plus action list. Value: avoided liability risk plus a compliance certificate that will be required anyway starting Aug 2026 (AI competency mandate).
Prototype (V0): (1) A questionnaire framework against EU AI Act + AI-MIG requirements (risk class, logging, human oversight, data origin). (2) An inventory script that catalogs a client's deployed agents/workflows (Microsoft Copilot Studio, n8n, Zapier Agents). (3) An assessment matrix + standard report template. (4) One concrete pilot mandate. MVP is deliberately narrow: baseline audit + gap report only, no ongoing monitoring. Existing building blocks: EU AI Act compliance checklists, agent observability tools (Credo AI, Holistic AI) as reference, GPT for report synthesis.
Market & gap: The AI agent audit & assurance market is projected to grow from ~$0.6B (2026) to ~$23B (2036) (44.0% CAGR); Germany should achieve 43.9% CAGR "because EU AI Act compliance drives demand for documentation and testing".[13] The giants serve themselves: KPMG and EY have rolled out their own agentic AI[1][8]—they audit at corporate scale and corporate rates. twiceD's gap: the mid-size company with five agents that won't pay a Big Four mandate, won't adopt a US SaaS subscription, but wants someone to walk through hands-on in German and leave a report that will hold up to the Federal Network Agency.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Audit/advisory work has almost zero ongoing maintenance—each mandate is a closed piece of work. V0 (framework + report template) in 2–3 weeks solo; scaling is "one contractor per mandate." The framework ages with regulation, not code. Sovereignty lever: audit runs on-premises at the client, no data leaves the house. factmr.com · paperclipped.de

2 · Cross-system agent orchestration—the "last mile" for mid-market

ELI5 & value: Corporations are right now buying the ability for agents to act across their systems (Asana→StackAI, Salesforce→Contentful). A mid-size company with Sevdesk, a shop, and a garden-variety CRM doesn't get that in the box—it gets a generic platform (n8n, Make, Zapier) and has to wire it itself. twiceD builds the finished, customized agent workflow for one concrete business process. Value: headcount savings without a five-figure-per-year enterprise-suite subscription.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick a painful cross-system process at the client (e.g. quote → order → invoice across three tools). (2) Build the orchestration layer on n8n (open source, self-hosted) instead of a SaaS platform. (3) Use a local/European model for decision steps, human confirms before every write action. (4) Audit log for every agent action (connects directly to idea 1). MVP is deliberately narrow: one process, one customer, human-in-the-loop. Existing building blocks: n8n as orchestrator, existing tool APIs, open model on-prem.
Market & gap: The execution layer is being bundled into enterprise suites—Asana paid $75M for StackAI, the AI-in-Procurement market alone grows to ~$40B by 2035.[2][5] The horizontal platforms (n8n, Make, Zapier Agents, Microsoft Copilot Studio) are generic: the customer has to wire workflow, connectors, and oversight themselves. twiceD's gap: the "last mile"—the concrete fit for a German mid-market process, on-premises for sensitive data, with German contract and privacy reflexes, no pro-seat enterprise subscription.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium-high. Harder than an audit because a hosted/operated workflow has ongoing load (monitoring, API breaks on tool updates). But with one process per customer, the load is bounded and solo-sustainable; V0 in 4–6 weeks, deployment with one contractor per customer.
