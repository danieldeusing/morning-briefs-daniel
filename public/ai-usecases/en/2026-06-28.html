The DMA stops a flagship AI feature for the first time [1][2][3]

Apple unveiled the redesigned Siri AI at WWDC26 (06.08) — and in the same breath confirmed it will not ship in the EU. iOS 27, iPadOS 27, and watchOS 27 are affected; macOS 27 and visionOS 27 will get it. For Daniel as a German Apple user, that concretely means: iPhone Siri stays old, MacBook Siri gets smart. Apple names no timeline for an EU solution — this isn't "coming later," it's open-ended.[1][2]

DMA (Digital Markets Act): EU law requiring large platforms ("gatekeepers") to open their services to competitors — as if a mall had to give rival brands equal storefront access. At the core of the dispute: the EU demands that Apple give every third-party AI assistant the same deep device access as Siri — direct access to private data (mail, photos, messages) and control over other apps. Apple says it cannot guarantee that data-compliantly.[1]

Apple's technical workaround — a "Trusted System Agent" to broker access — was rejected by the EU Commission. That means no quick fix, but a genuine stalemate. My take: both sides have a point, and that's exactly what makes it dangerous. Apple weaponizes privacy as a competitive moat; the EU shoots with an interpretation that raises genuine privacy questions. The signal for anyone rolling out AI in the EU is clear: deep device and data integration is now an open regulatory battlefield, not a solved problem. Anyone planning an AI product with OS-level data connectivity needs to factor DMA and AI Act considerations from day one.

Note for the desk: The Siri model architecture (Apple Foundation Models + Gemini, user-selected models) is more platform than use-case territory — details in the software/ai-dev brief.

Hollywood is buying AI tools — Google × A24 [4][5][6]

Google DeepMind is investing 75 million dollars in a multi-year, non-exclusive partnership with indie studio A24 (06.22). Together they'll build AI film tools — starting with AI-generated storyboards and "tools that support artists in authentic storytelling." Key to understanding the deal: Google gets no access to A24's film catalog or data — it's a tools partnership, not a content grab.[4]

Demis Hassabis frames it this way: "The best way to build tools that empower artists is to work directly with them."[4] A24 defends itself against fan backlash with "better at the table than on the sidelines."[6] That's the real story: Google is late to the game. Netflix bought InterPositive (Ben Affleck's AI film firm) in March; Amazon MGM has had its own AI Studio in beta since February.[5] The pattern is clear — platforms are upgrading the production stack, not (yet) the end product. My take: 75 million dollars is pocket change for Google; the value lies in access to creative workflow as a test lab, not profit motive.

Brazil builds the legal AI that fits the market — Enter [7][8][9]

Brazilian legal-AI startup Enter is valued at 1.2 billion dollars (May 2026) — a 3.5x jump in seven months, led by Founders Fund and Sequoia.[7][8] The hook isn't the valuation, but why: Brazil has roughly 80 million active lawsuits — about eight times as many as the US at a smaller population.[9]

Mass litigation (litígio de massa): When a firm is hit with tens of thousands of near-identical lawsuits — say, consumers suing over the same fee. Like a factory instead of a tailor shop: not each case by hand, but a standardized assembly line. That assembly-line character is exactly what makes the field AI-ready — many cases, same structure, clear patterns.

The client roster reads like Brazilian finance's who's-who: Itaú, Santander, Nubank, Mercado Livre, Airbnb, BMG, Vivo. One hard metric, named: Nubank improved its win rate in consumer litigation by 6 percentage points with Enter; 250,000+ cases expected for 2026.[7] That's gold-standard proof — named firm, verifiable metric, no vague "productivity boost."

Contrast with global heavyweights: Harvey (~11 billion dollar valuation, 190 million dollar ARR) and Legora (5.6 billion dollars) target big-law consulting in the US.[10] Enter owns a different niche — mass-litigation handling within its own legal system. That's the lesson for twiceD: don't copy the horizontal US champion; find the local, process-specific slice the big players don't serve. (More below.)

Funding Enter's May 2026 round is a confirmed ~100 million dollar Series B (Law360, Bloomberg) — led by Founders Fund, with Sequoia and Ribbit Capital; the 1.2 billion dollar valuation is also confirmed.

Ad firms are building agentic pipelines [11][12]

Warner Bros. Discovery announced (06.18) an agentic ad platform on AWS — built on Amazon Bedrock AgentCore + SageMaker. Autonomous AI agents handle planning, dynamic forecasting, real-time optimization, and closed-loop performance measurement across linear and digital channels. Rollout: agentic automation for direct response still in 2026, unified media planning Q3 2026, modular order management Q4 2026.[11]

Agentic AI: Instead of just answering questions, the system takes action across multiple steps — it plans, decides, executes, and verifies. Like the difference between a GPS that shows the route and one that actually drives, brakes, and re-plans when traffic hits.

In parallel: Zeta Global is migrating its marketing data cloud to Palantir Foundry (06.23), with Athena AI gaining broader enterprise-data access for real-time decisions. Potential: 100 million dollar+ annual revenue over several years; ZETA stock responded with +5 %.[12] Both moves are the same shift: the ad stack — 2026 market ~1.18 trillion dollars — is moving from dashboards to acting agents.[13] My take: This is the first serious, real-world agentic production use outside the coding space — valuable because advertising is measurable and every optimization point means money directly.

Standing Thread: Regulation [14][15]

The next hard EU AI Act deadline is near: From August 2, 2026 onward, Article 50 transparency obligations take effect — every AI chatbot must disclose itself as AI, and AI-generated content (image, audio, video, text) must be labeled. This applies to all providers operating AI systems in the EU — including small ones.[14]

Good news for high-risk users: In the Omnibus VII compromise (05.07.2026), deadlines for standalone high-risk systems (Annex III) shifted to 12.02.2027 and for product-embedded (Annex I) to 08.02.2028.[15] In practical terms: more breathing room for the heavy stuff, but transparency baseline rules arrive on schedule in August. If you operate a bot or generator in the EU, you have five weeks.

Study: Deloitte State of AI 2026 [16]

Deloitte's new "State of AI" report delivers the usual mix of real progress and hype tempering. Workforce AI access grew 50 % in one year — from under 40 % to about 60 % of employees with released AI tools. But the productivity gap persists: only 25 % of organizations have brought 40 % or more of their AI pilots into production. For agentic AI, they're using only 23 % moderately — expected to reach 74 % within two years.[16]

Methodology flag Survey of 3,235 director-to-C-level leaders in 24 countries — conducted August–September 2025, so roughly ten months old despite "2026" in the report title. "AI access" means released tools available, not measurable productivity gains. Be cautious reading this as a current indicator.

One data point that matters for twiceD: 83 % view sovereign AI as strategically important, 77 % factor country-of-origin into vendor choice. That aligns with the DMA/Siri story above — sovereignty and on-prem are no longer niche arguments.[16]

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gaps and honest solo viability

1 · Demand-Defense Assistant for German Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Brazil's Enter shows the pattern at scale: many near-identical legal cases, AI sorts, prioritizes, and drafts standard responses. In the German mid-market, the same assembly-line problem exists at smaller scale — recurring payment demands, warranty complaints, contract terminations, ToS violations. An AI assistant reads the inbound, classifies it against your contracts/ToS, suggests a response, and provides sourced reasoning. Value: one staffer goes from 20 to 80 cases per day, and win rate climbs because no standard argument gets missed — exactly the +6 percentage-point lift Nubank measured.[7]
Prototype (V0): (1) Real stack of recurring letters from a pilot customer as a test set. (2) Classification + extraction via an existing model (RAG over customer contracts/ToS), not self-trained. (3) Response suggestion with source reference to the matching contract clause. (4) Human approves before send. MVP narrow by design: one case type (e.g., warranty claims), one customer, draft-only — no auto-send. Existing building blocks: a foundation model with German-law competency, an open-source RAG pipeline (LlamaIndex), simple review UI layer.
Market & Gap: Global legal-AI leaders target big-law: Harvey (~11 billion dollar valuation, 190 million dollar ARR), Legora (5.6 billion dollars) — Legora's pricing is undisclosed but enterprise-tier per market reports (~2,400–6,000 dollars+/user/year, 10-seat minimums), unreachable for mid-market.[10][18] Enter proves the local, process-specific cut is the real market.[7] Gap for twiceD: no German vendor serves the non-legal staffer in mid-market with a narrow, on-prem-capable demand workflow tied to German contract law — the big players are too expensive, too generic, too law-firm-centric.
Viability (Solo + AI): Medium-High. The heavy model is licensed, not built; twiceD delivers RAG hookup, contract mapping, review layer, and fit to one process. V0 in 6–8 weeks solo, per-customer rollout with a contractor. Sovereignty is the lever — for legal/contract data, on-prem / open model counts, fitting the 83 % "sovereign AI" vote at Deloitte.[16] Maintenance low as long as scope stays one case type per customer; legal liability stays on the human via required review. briefs.co · haqq.ai · deloitte.com

2 · AI-Act-Article-50-Compliance Kit (Deadline 08.02)

ELI5 & Value: From August 2, 2026 onward, every AI chatbot in the EU must disclose itself as AI, and every AI-generated piece of content must be labeled.[14] Thousands of German mid-market firms have a chatbot or AI text/image generator running somewhere and don't know if they're compliant. A compliance kit is a guided mini-audit plus technical scaffolding (bot-disclosure banner, C2PA content labeling) that fixes exactly that. Value: avoided fine risk and documented compliance — before the deadline hits.
Prototype (V0): (1) A checklist translating Art. 50 obligations into 8–10 yes/no questions. (2) A scanner checking the customer website for visible chatbots / AI generators. (3) Ready snippets: a compliant bot-disclosure banner and C2PA/Content-Credentials integration for AI images. (4) A PDF compliance report as deliverable. MVP narrow by design: Article 50 transparency only, not the high-risk part (whose deadline shifts to Dec. 2027 anyway).[15] Building blocks: C2PA/Content-Credentials open-source SDK, simple crawler, report template.
Market & Gap: AI-governance platforms exist — Credo AI, Holistic AI, OneTrust — but they're enterprise GRC suites with enterprise pricing and year-long contracts, built for corporate compliance teams.[19] For a 30-person machinemaker with a support bot, they're massive overkill. Gap for twiceD: a small, flat, time-limited fixed-price audit for the concrete August deadline, in German, hands-on delivered — no SaaS subscription, no GRC framework nobody in mid-market can operate.
Viability (Solo + AI): High. This is consulting/one-time work with near-zero ongoing maintenance — the ideal solo cut. V0 (checklist + snippets + report template) in 2–3 weeks, each audit after that in days. The deadline creates urgency; post-August you can evolve the kit for the next AI Act waves (high-risk 2027). Scaling via contractor per mandate, no standing team needed. artificialintelligenceact.eu · lw.com
