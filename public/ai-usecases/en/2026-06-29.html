Anthropic vs. Alibaba: Model Theft at Scale

POLICY B2B-SaaS Anthropic charges that Alibaba secretly drained its Claude model to upgrade its own Qwen — and calls it the largest "distillation attack" yet. The numbers tell the real story: roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts generated 28.8M interactions with Claude between April 22 and June 5, 2026.[1] The target was exactly what's commercially most valuable: software engineering and "agentic reasoning" — the ability to plan multi-step tasks independently.

Distillation (model distillation): Imagine a student asking a master a thousand questions, recording each answer, and becoming a master without ever paying for years of training. Technically: An attacker sends a flood of queries to an expensive top model, collects the outputs, and trains a cheaper rival with them. Someone else paid for the expensive training work.

This isn't new: In February 2026, Anthropic caught three Chinese labs (DeepSeek, MiniMax, Moonshot) with 16M queries across 24,000 accounts.[2] This time, the campaign is nearly twice as big — and Anthropic is going political: It's asking the U.S. Senate and White House to clarify antitrust rules (so vendors can share distillation intel), impose export controls on AI chips, and penalize distillation actors.[1]

Policy is already responding. On June 12, the U.S. Commerce Department ordered Anthropic to block foreign nationals from accessing its Mythos 5 and Fable 5 models; Senators Bill Hagerty (R-TN) and Andy Kim (D-NJ) want to amend the defense bill to sanction distillation actors.[3] Alibaba did not comment; ADRs fell over 3% on the news.[4]

My take: What's interesting here is less the espionage thriller than the shift in the security frontier. AI safety long meant "stop the model from saying bad things." Now it also means: read API traffic forensically, spot harvest fingerprints, correlate accounts. For anyone offering a valuable model or database via API, abuse detection becomes a core discipline — not a footnote. That's the twiceD lesson, not the trade war.

OpenAI "Jalapeño": Cheaper Inference, Read With Caution

M&A B2B-SaaS OpenAI unveiled its first proprietary chip with Broadcom — "Jalapeño", made by TSMC at 3nm, built purely for inference.[5]

Inference vs. training: Training teaches a model how to learn — once, extremely expensive. Inference is letting the finished model answer every time a user asks — millions of times, forever. In a service like ChatGPT, inference is the running electricity bill. A chip that cuts answer costs cuts operating costs — not learning costs.

The applied lever is the cost claim: roughly 50% cheaper inference against typical AI GPUs. Key: That's a vendor claim (Broadcom CEO), not an independent benchmark — OpenAI published no technical report, and hard metrics (TOPS, HBM generation) are unconfirmed.[5] What stands out is the pace: nine months from design to tape-out, partly sped up with OpenAI's own models — an AI-builds-chip-builds-AI loop.[6] First shipments end of 2026 at Azure and partner datacenters.

Context: Chip hardware itself is the software brief's domain — here only the applied consequence matters: If inference really does go half the cost, every AI use case's economics shift. Cases that fail today on token costs (bulk document processing, always-on agents) move closer to viability. Until an independent benchmark confirms the 50%, it stays a hope, not a planning number.

Physical AI: Robots Leave the Pilot Phase

DEPLOY Mfg The key sentence from "State of Robotics 2026": Global robotics market is $38B in 2026 — up 34% from last year, the fastest growth in a decade.[7] Twelve humanoid platforms are now buyable or leasable (three in 2024), prices from $28,000 (torso only) to $245,000 (full biped).

What's driving the leap isn't marketing — it's a tech shift:

VLA model (Vision-Language-Action): Old factory robots followed rigid pre-programmed paths — shift one part 2mm and the gripper missed. A VLA model sees the scene via camera, understands a verbal instruction, and converts both into motion. Like an apprentice you tell "put that metal on the pallet" instead of feeding every coordinate.

VLA models now appear in 40% of new deployments — under 5% in 2024, 14% in 2025.[7] In parallel, the biggest cost dropped: teleop data (a human drives the robot) cost $340/hour in early 2024, just $118/hour in March 2026 — down 60%.[7]

From pilot to shift: At BMW's Spartanburg plant, two Figure-02 robots helped produce over 30,000 BMW X3s and load over 90,000 metal parts over eleven months — 1,250 operating hours.[8] Japan Airlines deployed two Unitree humanoids to Haneda baggage handling in May, ~$15,400 each, three-year commitment.[8] Bank of America projects 90,000 humanoids shipped in 2026 and 1.2M by 2030.

EU AI Act: The Deadline Still Lives

REG · EU Critical for anyone with EU customers — and especially confusing now: August 2, 2026 is officially still the deadline when high-risk obligations of the EU AI Act kick in (provider duties Art. 9–17, operator duties Art. 26). Fines: up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover.[10]

"High-risk" AI: The AI Act grades AI by harm potential. "High-risk" systems are those where a failure hits real people — AI in hiring screening, loan approval, medical devices, critical infrastructure. Those get strict obligations: risk management, documentation, human oversight, logging.

The catch: A political agreement from May 7 (the "Digital Omnibus") wants to push this deadline to December 2, 2027 (for standalone Annex III systems; product-embedded Annex I systems to August 2, 2028).[11] But: This postponement is not in force yet — it must be formally published in the EU Official Journal, expected before August 2. Until then, the old deadline applies legally.

My take: Betting on the postponement before it's published would be reckless. If you run a high-risk system, keep doing compliance work as if August 2 were the real deadline — and cheer if the postponement comes through on time. This is exactly where a twiceD opportunity sits (see below): German mid-market is uncertain. Fifty-three percent of German firms cite lack of expertise as their biggest AI hurdle.[12]

Agents in Production: "Pilot" Becomes "Bottom Line"

DEPLOY · Finance Two numbers that show agentic AI has made the leap from demo to ops in major institutions. JPMorgan runs 450+ AI use cases in production and aims for 1,000 by year-end — at roughly $2B in operational savings.[13]

Klarna automates 67% of all customer service chats via its AI agent — the workload of roughly 700 full-time staff, response time down from 11 minutes to under 2. Reported savings range between $40M and $60M depending on source — I cite the range because two sources conflict and neither counts as confirmed year-end actuals.[14]

Methodology caveat "67% automated" does not mean "67% solved." The number measures chats handled, not customer satisfaction — Klarna itself had to bring humans back in 2024 after a too-aggressive AI rollout. An efficiency claim is solid only when escalation and satisfaction rates sit beside it. Treat such percentages as direction, not proof.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gaps and honest solo feasibility

1 · EU AI Act High-Risk Readiness for Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: Many mid-market firms deploy AI in areas the AI Act classifies as "high-risk" (hiring screening, credit review, quality control in medical/manufacturing) but don't know if they're covered or what to do. A "readiness audit" checks the system, classifies it, lists concrete gaps (risk management, docs, human oversight, logging), and delivers a prioritized action plan. Value: avoiding fines up to €35M or 7% revenue — and planning certainty in legal fog (August 2, 2026 deadline vs. possible 2027 postponement).
Prototype (V0): (1) Structured questionnaire mapping a system against Annex III of the AI Act. (2) Classification logic (high-risk yes/no, which articles). (3) Gap-report generator against provider/operator duties (Art. 9–17, 26). (4) One real pilot customer with an AI system. MVP deliberately narrow: one audit run, one report template, no ongoing monitoring. Building blocks: the official AI Act text + existing compliance checklists (artificialintelligenceact.eu), LLM only for report wording — not legal judgment.
Market & Gap: Platforms exist (Credo AI, Holistic AI, Trail) and big auditors (TÜV, DEKRA, Big Four) offer AI governance.[A1] But they're either expensive SaaS for enterprises or enterprise consulting mandates — no hands-on option for a 50-person firm with one high-risk system. Gap: the pragmatic fixed-price audit for DE mid-market, in German contract & compliance language, on-site not self-serve dashboard. Fifty-three percent of DE firms naming lack of expertise as biggest hurdle is the demand side of that gap.[A2]
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Advisory one-time work has nearly zero maintenance — knowledge is the asset, not hosted infra. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo (mapping is legwork, not engineering), scale via extra consultants per engagement. Risk: liability — legal judgment needs a compliance lawyer, twiceD provides technical analysis. credo.ai · holisticai.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu

2 · API Abuse & Distillation Protection as a Service

ELI5 & Value: The Anthropic story shows the pattern: Anyone offering a valuable model or data API gets systematically drained — via thousands of fake accounts that look like real users. An "Abuse Guard" reads API traffic, spots suspicious patterns (odd query distributions, account clusters, systematic harvesting) and alerts before value leaks. Value: protect your most precious asset — model or database — from unpaid mimicry.
Prototype (V0): (1) Log adapter ingesting a customer's API access. (2) Per-account feature extraction (request rate, topic spread, response-storage signals). (3) Anomaly detection (classic ML sufficient — no frontier model needed). (4) Alert dashboard. MVP narrow: one customer, offline log analysis, no inline blocking. Building blocks: open-source anomaly detection (PyOD, scikit-learn), rate-limit gateways like Kong or Tyk as data source.
Market & Gap: Bot management is plentiful — Cloudflare, DataDome, HUMAN Security, Kasada.[A3] But they're tuned to web/login bots and scraping, not the specific pattern of "legit-looking API calls that distill a model in aggregate." Anthropic is building this distillation forensics itself now (behavioral fingerprinting in API traffic).[A4] Gap: lightweight, API-harvest-specific tooling for mid-size vendors without their own security team.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. Detection is solo-buildable, but a hosted always-on service brings real ops load (data pipeline, false-positive tuning, monitoring). Honest: as pure offline audit/consulting ("we analyze your logs for distillation patterns") immediately viable solo; as managed SaaS, the ops mountain grows with each customer — needs contractor support and tight scope. datadome.co · anthropic.com · pyod.readthedocs.io
