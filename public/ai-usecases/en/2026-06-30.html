The Token Bill Arrives: AI Now Has a Visible Price Tag — the Value Doesn't Yet

STUDY B2B-SaaS The biggest story of the week is a billing change. In early 2026, Anthropic and OpenAI switched their large enterprise customers from flat-rate to token-based billing — making visible for the first time what AI actually costs in production.[1] The result is a shock to the books.

Token billing: A "token" is a word fragment — AI models count in tokens rather than letters. Flat-rate billing was like an unlimited plan: one fixed price no matter how much you use. Token billing is like a taxi meter — you pay per mile driven. When the flat rate applied, it didn't matter whether a developer used AI sparingly or ran it continuously. Now every mile counts, and suddenly you can see who's consuming how much.

The numbers are stark: Uber consumed its entire AI coding budget for 2026 in the first four months; R&D spending climbed to $951M in Q1 2026 alone (+17% YoY).[1] Per-developer token consumption rose 18.6× in just nine months, according to Jellyfish.[2] A single engineer ran up $40,000 per month according to Faros AI; one large Anthropic customer accidentally spent $500M in a single month because no spending limits had been set.[3]

But the real bombshell is a single line from Uber COO Andrew Macdonald: it's "very hard to draw a line" from token statistics to "okay, now we're actually producing 25% more useful features" — and on the link between spending and business value, he says plainly: "That connection isn't there yet."[1] One of the biggest enterprise AI users is saying aloud what many quietly fear.

My take: This is the most important shift of recent weeks — more important than any new model. For two years the question was "Does AI work?" Now it's "Does AI pay off at this price?" — and companies are running that calculation for the first time. TechCrunch nails it: the most productive engineers were twice as productive but consumed 10× the tokens.[2] Double the output at ten times the cost is not a free-lunch ROI — it's a FinOps problem. And that's exactly where a concrete twiceD opportunity lies (see below).

Gartner: Spending Explodes — but Layoffs Don't Deliver ROI

STUDY Two Gartner figures provide the macro context for the cost story. First, the scale: worldwide AI spending will reach $2.59T in 2026, according to Gartner — a 47% increase year-over-year.[4] Spending on AI agent software alone is projected to jump from $86.4B (2025) to $206.5B (2026) and then to $376.3B (2027).[4]

Second — and this is the uncomfortable number: in a Gartner survey of 350 executives at companies with more than $1B in revenue, 80% had cut positions where AI is deployed. But there is no correlation between those layoffs and higher ROI.[5] Gartner's Helen Poitevin: "Chasing value purely through headcount reduction will likely put most organizations on a path of limited returns."[6]

Methodology note Both are Gartner forecasts or surveys: the $2.59T figure is a market estimate (model assumptions, not booked revenue); the layoff figure is based on 350 self-reported executive responses. "No correlation" is a strong signal but not causal proof — treat it as a warning against the reflex of "add AI, cut headcount," not as an iron law.

Context: The numbers fit the token story. Money is flowing at record levels, the benefits can't be cleanly attributed, and the obvious savings reflex — cutting heads — isn't delivering the returns. Gartner's read: "people amplification" (enhancing humans with AI) beats headcount reduction — exactly Uber's finding that the most expensive tokens aren't automatically the best lever.

HPE + NVIDIA: The Enterprise "AI Factory" Takes Shape

DEPLOY B2B-SaaS At HPE Discover in Las Vegas (June 16–18), HPE and NVIDIA significantly expanded their joint "AI Factory."[7] This is Daniel's core territory: not a model, but the factory in which enterprises run AI agents.

"AI Factory": A preconfigured, all-in-one stack — servers, GPUs, networking, and software — that an enterprise buys and switches on, rather than assembling it from individual parts. Think turnkey factory rather than a warehouse of machine crates. The sales pitch: run AI in your own data center without having to build a team of infrastructure specialists.

What's concretely new: a server with NVIDIA's Vera CPU (HPE ProLiant Compute DL394 Gen12), the NVIDIA Agent Toolkit (open Nemotron models, "NemoClaw" building blocks, "OpenShell" runtime), and end-to-end Confidential Computing.[7] The server ships in Fall 2026, the fully integrated private-cloud variant in 2027.

Confidential Computing: Data is normally encrypted when stored or transmitted — but while it's being processed, it sits exposed in memory. Confidential Computing encrypts it even then, inside an isolated enclave on the chip. Think of it as a meeting room that even the janitor can't see into. For regulated industries (finance, healthcare), this is often the prerequisite for feeding AI real customer data.

The proof customer: the New York Stock Exchange is piloting the Vera CPU as an early adopter (with Redpanda and HPE).[7] The fact that an exchange — an operation that's extremely sensitive to latency and security — is the reference customer is the real signal.

My take: For twiceD the pattern matters more than the hardware. "AI Factory" means the stack is becoming a commodity; value shifts upward — into fitting the stack to a specific business process. That last mile, which NVIDIA and HPE leave open, is the playing field for smaller providers. Confidential Computing is the keyword that resonates with German mid-market companies: sovereignty is a selling point here, not a nice-to-have.

Anthropic ↔ Microsoft Maia 200: Reported Talks, No Deal

M&A According to The Information, Anthropic is in talks with Microsoft about running Claude inference on Microsoft's in-house AI chip, the Maia 200 — on rented Azure servers.[8] Important: These are early discussions, not a signed contract; reports explicitly note they "may not progress to an agreement."[8]

The applied hook is the cost claim: Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella cited "more than 30% better tokens per dollar versus the latest silicon in our fleet" for the Maia 200.[9] If true, that's another lever against the token-cost problem above — cheaper inference makes more use cases economically viable. The chip hardware details (TSMC 3nm, 10+ PFLOPS FP4) belong in the software brief.

Context: For the applied world, only the direction matters: Anthropic is distributing its inference across more and more chips and clouds (Azure, Google TPUs, AWS Trainium, now possibly Maia). That will push inference costs down over time — and with it the price Daniel's clients pay for AI features. Until the deal exists and a benchmark confirms the 30%, this remains a reported intention, not a planning input.

EU AI Act: Council Formally Adopts Deadline Extension on June 29 — 16-Month Reprieve

REG · EU The most important regulatory news for anyone with EU customers: the "Digital Omnibus" shifts the EU AI Act's high-risk deadline by 16 months — from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027.[10] The EU Parliament approved this on June 16, and the Council formally adopted it on June 29, 2026, as the final step ("final green light").[11] The extension is now decided.

"High-risk" AI & Annex III: The AI Act classifies AI by damage potential. "High-risk" (listed in Annex III) covers systems where a failure affects real people — AI in candidate screening, credit decisions, medical applications, critical infrastructure. These face hard obligations: risk management, documentation, human oversight, logging.

What's now in effect: With the Council's formal adoption on June 29, the shifted deadlines are decided; they take effect upon publication in the EU Official Journal.[11] Two dates are affected: the high-risk deadline (Annex III) moves from August 2026 to Dec. 2, 2027, and the labeling obligation for AI-generated content moves from August 2 to Dec. 2, 2026.[11]

My take: The months-long limbo is over — the extension now stands legally. Anyone building a high-risk system has 16 more months before the Annex III obligations kick in (December 2027). But this is no free pass: the obligations are coming, just later, and the labeling deadline arrives at the end of 2026. For mid-market companies, the message is to use the buffer for proper preparation — not to shelve the topic again. That "what do I need to do by when" planning is an advisory gap, not a footnote.

Shadow AI: Your Workforce Is Already Using Tools Nobody Approved

STUDY · Security While boards debate AI strategy, employees have already decided — quietly. 59–78% of workers use unsanctioned AI tools on the job, according to an aggregated industry report; only 43% of organizations have any AI usage policy at all.[12]

Shadow AI / BYOAI: "Bring Your Own AI" — employees use personal AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude in the browser) for work without IT knowing or approving it. Like a clerk secretly copying customer data onto their personal laptop to work faster. Practical — until sensitive data ends up somewhere nobody controls.

The risk is quantified: a data breach involving shadow AI costs on average $670,000 more — and one in five organizations has already had one.[12] Among HR professionals, over 80% use AI daily; only 14% pay for it.[13] First vendors are responding: Kilo launched "KiloClaw for Organizations" on April 1 for centralized agent governance (SSO, centralized billing, time-limited permissions).[14]

Methodology note The 59–78% range comes from an aggregated report that combines third-party sources (IBM, McKinsey, Gartner, and others) — sample size and methodology are not disclosed. Treat the range as "widespread, plausible order of magnitude," not a precise measurement. The direction is robust (multiple independent studies show the same pattern); the decimal places are not.

My take: This is the flip side of the AI hype — a concrete risk gap. Companies can't ban shadow AI (people use it anyway); they can only make it visible and safe. Not a tool purchase but a governance task: which data may go into which tool, who is liable, how is it logged. That's where the second twiceD idea below sits.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with a market gap and an honest solo-feasibility check

1 · AI Token FinOps Audit for the Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: The token story this week is the demand side: since the billing switch, companies can see that their AI spending is exploding — but they can't say which use case is paying off and which is just burning expensive tokens. A "Token FinOps Audit" wires up usage and billing data, maps spending to teams and use cases, identifies the cost drivers (that one engineer at $40,000/month), and delivers concrete levers: caching, model routing (cheap model for simple tasks), spending limits. Value: Uber burned its annual budget in four months — anyone who saves 30% of token costs without a performance hit recovers the audit fee in weeks.
Prototype (V0): (1) Connector that ingests usage exports from Anthropic/OpenAI/Azure. (2) Attribution logic: tokens → team → use case. (3) Cost-driver report with top consumers and optimization levers. (4) One real pilot customer with a real bill. MVP deliberately narrow: offline analysis of a billing period, one report, no live dashboard. Building blocks: provider billing APIs, OpenTelemetry/LiteLLM logs as data source, a routing proxy (LiteLLM, Portkey) for the recommended measures — not built from scratch.
Market & Gap: AI cost tools exist (Vantage, CloudZero, Datadog LLM Observability), and the FinOps Foundation made token costs a 2026 priority — at FinOps X 2026 it was noted that token bills are just one of nine cost blocks and that tracking is a "trillion-rows-per-month data problem."[A1] But the existing tools are horizontal observability platforms built for large engineering orgs — the customer has to assemble integration, interpretation, and action themselves. Gap: the pragmatic fixed-fee audit plus implementation for a 50-person firm without a FinOps department — hands-on, not a self-service dashboard.
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. Advisory/one-time work has near-zero maintenance burden — the knowledge is the asset. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo (billing formats are documented, the analysis is data work, not an engineering mountain). Scale via additional consultants per engagement; anyone who also manages the routing setup has an optional small recurring component — keep it deliberately narrow or the maintenance load grows. finops.org · litellm · techcrunch.com

2 · Shadow AI Governance Audit for the German Mid-Market

ELI5 & Value: The shadow AI story is the other half: in almost every company, employees are already quietly using personal AI tools with real company and customer data — only 43% have any policy at all. A "Shadow AI Governance Audit" makes visible which tools are actually in use, what data is flowing into them, where the risk sits — and delivers an actionable usage policy plus a list of approved, safe alternatives. Value: a shadow AI data breach costs on average $670,000 more; the audit turns uncontrolled usage into ordered, liability-safe practice — without banning AI, which doesn't work anyway.
Prototype (V0): (1) Discovery: employee survey + analysis of proxy/DNS logs for known AI endpoints. (2) Risk mapping: which data category goes into which tool (GDPR lens). (3) Policy template with task risk zones ("permitted / conditional / prohibited"). (4) List of approved alternatives with German hosting/on-prem options. MVP narrow: one audit run, one report, one policy — no ongoing monitoring. Building blocks: existing governance models as templates (the academic BYOAI-gov framework with Enable/Regulate/Restrict zones), existing AI gateway tools (KiloClaw, Cloudflare AI Gateway) for the enforcement recommendation.
Market & Gap: Enterprise governance platforms exist (KiloClaw for agent governance, Cloudflare/Zscaler for AI traffic control, Microsoft Purview).[A2] But these are hosted products that presuppose an internal security team — no offering for the mid-market company that first needs to know what's actually running and needs a policy in German with GDPR reflexes. Gap: the hands-on audit plus pragmatic policy for firms without a CISO — and the sovereignty variant (on-prem / German-hosted alternatives instead of US SaaS).
Feasibility (Solo + AI): High. Pure advisory work, near-zero maintenance — discovery, analysis, report, policy are well within solo + AI capacity. V0 in 3–5 weeks (survey + log analysis + template). Scale via consultants per engagement; liability discipline required: the legal assessment should be covered by a data-protection attorney; twiceD delivers the technical and organizational analysis. unseensecurity.ai · venturebeat.com · cornerstoneondemand.com
