Fable 5 back—policy had a hand in it

The day's most important event is also the most instructive: today Anthropic re-enabled Fable 5 worldwide, 19 days after the company abruptly shut it down for all customers alongside the cyber model Mythos 5.[1] Not just for foreign users—for the entire customer base globally, to fully comply with a US government directive.[2]

Export control on AI models: Just as the US prevents certain chips or encryption tech from reaching the "wrong" hands, they now increasingly treat a cutting-edge AI model like military equipment. When they invoke "national security authorities," a commercial model can go offline practically overnight—even for paying enterprise customers with contracts.

The trigger was narrow: according to Anthropic, a "narrow, non-universal jailbreak"—essentially a request to read a specific code base and find and fix software vulnerabilities.[3] Anthropic publicly and strongly objected to the proportionality: using a narrow potential jailbreak as grounds to recall a model deployed to hundreds of millions of people is wrong—the same flaw exists in other freely available models anyway.[3]

My take: What's remarkable is not the jailbreak, but the precedent. A vendor had to globally suspend a core commercial capability because a government ordered it—and could only argue publicly against it, not refuse. For anyone embedding a US frontier vendor into a production process, that's a concrete continuity risk, not just news color. The lever for mitigation is multi-model capability and a sovereign fallback.

Fable 5 is now live with a new safety classifier that Anthropic says blocks over 99% of the time.[1] Availability: Pro, Max, Team, and select Enterprise customers; until July 7, up to 50% weekly usage, then via usage credits; cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, Foundry) are still reactivating.[1]

Mythos 5: US only, critical infrastructure only

The second half of the story is the real policy news. Mythos 5, Anthropic's strongest cybersecurity model, does not return globally—the government approved on June 26 only deployment to "a group of US organizations that operate and defend critical infrastructure," under Project Glasswing (defensive cybersecurity).[4] A broader rollout is still under discussion; specific organizations were not named.

Why it matters A frontier model is no longer distributed by willingness to pay, but by customer nationality and sector. For an EU vendor like twiceD, it means: the strongest US cyber capabilities are not reliably available to European customers for now—another argument for European/open alternatives where continuity counts.

GeneBench-Pro: where the real limit lies [5][6][7]

On June 30, OpenAI released GeneBench-Pro—a benchmark for computational biology with 129 tasks across 10 domains and 21 sub-domains (genomics, quantitative biology, translational biomedicine).[5][6] The interesting result is not the winner, but the whole picture: the best models fail at roughly 70% of research-adjacent biology tasks.[7]

GeneBench-Pro—pass rate, as of 06.30.2026 (secondary sources)

The leader is GPT-5.6 Sol Pro at 31.5% on maximum reasoning, followed by GPT-5.6 Sol at 28.7%.[5] The strongest non-GPT model is Claude Opus 4.8 at about 16.0%.[5]

Number caveat The Opus 4.8 figure (≈16.0%) comes from two consistent secondary sources; the OpenAI primary source was blocked on access (403). Treating as plausible, but not primary-confirmed.

The benchmark specifically measures "research taste"—the judgment for what a dataset actually yields. The problems are synthetically generated from known data-production processes, evaluated deterministically, and 82 tasks were reviewed by external experts (estimated 20–40 hours per task).[6]

My take: A 31.5% top score is not "AI solves biology," it's the opposite—a clean measurement of the real boundary. Useful for Daniel's skepticism meter: anyone pitching a model as an autonomous decision-maker in a regulated field (pharma, medical devices) is selling hype today. As an assistant under human control, it makes sense; as a replacement for domain expertise, not yet. (See ai-dev brief for underlying coding/model details.)

NHS: the world's largest healthcare AI rollout [8][9][10]

NHS England is rolling out Microsoft 365 Copilot to 505,000 clinicians and support staff—by its own account, the world's largest AI deployment in healthcare.[8] Announced June 8, Wave 1 starts this month: 100,000 staff in north-west and north-east England.[10]

M365 Copilot: The AI assistant that lives directly in Word, Outlook, Teams, and Excel—summarizes email chains, drafts replies, pulls key points from meetings. Not a specialized clinic system, but the office software the NHS already runs everywhere. The effect comes from saved admin time, not medical diagnosis.

The number that counts: The pilot with 30,000 staff across 90 NHS organizations saved an average of 43 minutes per person per day—scaled up, roughly five weeks per capita per year.[9]

Methodology flag "43 minutes saved" is self-reported from a Microsoft-backed pilot, not independently audited productivity gain. Saved doesn't automatically mean "used for patients." Still: the sheer scale (505,000 people, rolled out through March 2027) makes this the most concrete adoption signal of the day—not pilot theater, but production rollout.

My take: This pattern—existing office software + AI assistant + measurable minutes per capita—is the use case that actually lands in mid-market, not the moonshot story. The rollout is staged: 200,000 users in the first six months, full 505,000 within a year (target: March 2027).[10]

EU AI Act: high-risk deadline shifts 16 months out [11][12][13]

On June 29, the EU Council gave final approval to the "Digital Omnibus"—the AI Act simplification package; Parliament had approved June 16. Publication in the Official Journal is expected any moment; it enters force three days later.[11]

Annex III / high-risk AI: The AI Act categorizes AI by risk. "High-risk" (Annex III) covers systems in sensitive fields—hiring, biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, migration. These face the strictest obligations (risk management, documentation, human oversight). Previously, the hard deadline was August 2, 2026.

Core change: The high-risk obligations (Annex III) shift from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027—16 months delay.[12] New additions include bans: non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and CSAM generated by AI are prohibited starting December 2, 2026.[13]

My take: This is a two-edged signal. Firms with high-risk systems get breathing room—but "16 more months" is not an absolution, it's a planning window. Anyone who feared the Aug 2, 2026 deadline should use the time for clean classification and documentation, not for deferral. That's exactly where an idea below comes from.

Deloitte State of AI 2026: the production gap is the business [14]

The new Deloitte report (3,235 executives, 24 countries, 6 industries; fieldwork Aug–Sep 2025) delivers the numbers backing twiceD's positioning.[14]

Methodology flag Self-reported by executives, fieldwork already Aug–Sep 2025 (so ~9 months old), consulting firm with AI-consulting interest. "Transformative" is self-assessment, not measured value creation. Read as mood and maturity picture, not ROI proof.
  • Only 25% of organizations have moved ≥40% of AI pilots to production; 54% expect to within 3–6 months.
  • 25% report a "transformative" effect—more than double last year (~12%).
  • ~75% plan agentic AI within two years—but only 21% have mature agent governance.
  • 83% see sovereign AI as strategically important; 77% factor the provider's home country into selection.
Agentic AI / agent governance: An AI "agent" is a model that doesn't just reply—it takes steps on its own: fetch emails, query systems, book appointments. "Governance" means: rules, protocols, and kill-switches for what the agent can do. Like delegating tasks to an intern—except most organizations haven't yet written the handbook.

My take: The gap between "75% want agents" and "21% can govern them" is not a footnote—it's a concrete service demand. And the 83% sovereignty number is twiceD's structural opening: a German vendor with an on-prem/open-model reflex hits exactly the nerve that US hyperscalers structurally can't serve.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI Act readiness audit for German mid-market

ELI5 & value: The EU AI Act pushed its hardest deadline (high-risk obligations) to December 2027—not a free pass, but a 16-month planning window. Many mid-market firms don't even know whether their deployed AI (candidate screening, credit scoring, biometric access) counts as "high-risk." A readiness audit tells them in plain language: What AI do you have, which category is it, what documentation/oversight is missing, and what's due by when. Value: avoided fine risk and planning certainty instead of panic in fall 2027.
Prototype (V0): (1) Real AI inventory of a pilot customer as test case. (2) Classification checklist per Annex III (hiring, biometrics, critical infra, education, migration)—using the official AI Act texts + the EU compliance checker as template, not homespun. (3) Gap report against the obligations (risk management, technical documentation, human oversight). (4) Prioritized roadmap with deadlines through 12.02.2027. MVP deliberately narrow: one customer, one report, no ongoing platform. Building blocks: the official AI Act compliance checker (artificialintelligenceact.eu), the Official Journal texts, an LLM for drafting documentation.
Market & gap: The AI Act compliance market was estimated at ≈$609M in 2026 (CAGR 37.3% to $10.5B by 2035).[a] Mature vendors exist—OneTrust, Credo AI, Holistic AI, IBM AI Governance (software) and SGS (conformity assessment).[a] But they're either expensive enterprise GRC platforms or pure audit shops without engineering depth. The gap: the mid-market player doesn't want a six-figure platform or a lawyer without ML chops—they want someone who gets both the tech and the rule, in plain English contract language, hands-on, for a real inventory.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. This is advisory/one-shot work with ~zero maintenance after delivery—the ideal solo fit. V0 in 3–4 weeks solo, each customer a closed report; scale through a contractor per parallel engagement. 2 · Copilot rollout that actually sticks in mid-market

ELI5 & value: The NHS rollout shows the pattern that truly lands: existing office software + AI assistant + measurable minutes per capita (43 min/day in pilot). The mid-market problem isn't usually the license—it's that bought Copilot seats gather dust because no one rewires the workflows. twiceD doesn't sell the license, it sells the rollout that sticks: concrete use cases per department, custom agents for the two or three processes that actually drain time, and change management. Value: dead license cost becomes measured time saved.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick two or three time-draining workflows from a pilot customer (offer drafts, email triage, meeting notes). (2) Measure before: minutes per task. (3) M365 Copilot + one or two tailored Copilot Agents (Copilot Studio) on exactly those workflows. (4) Measure after + brief training. MVP tight: one department, three use cases, hard minute numbers, not vibes. Building blocks: M365 Copilot ($30/user/month add-on), Copilot Studio for agents—twiceD builds fit + measurement + adoption, not the model.
Market & gap: Copilot is at scale now (>20M paid seats as of April 2026), but SMB penetration (<500 head count) is only ~12%.[b] And vendors who just provision seats and bank on "organic use" don't get it—only firms with workflow integration and change management see meaningfully higher active use.[b] This hands-on piece is missing: large systems integrators do enterprise rollouts (hundreds of seats), not the 40-person shop with three concrete pain points.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High to medium. The V0 is solo-doable in weeks; the heavy lifting comes from Microsoft, twiceD delivers fit, measurement, and training. Maintenance is light and bounded (adjust agents when workflows shift), scale is clean through one contractor per customer. Honest take: more competitive territory than idea 1—the wedge has to be German mid-market focus + hard minute metrics, not "we do Copilot too." aibusinessweekly.net · adoption.microsoft.com
