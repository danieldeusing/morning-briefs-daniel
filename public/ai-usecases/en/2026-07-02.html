Gartner: $234B in SaaS spending at risk from agents

Study Gartner released one of the sharpest figures of the year for software yesterday (01.07.2026): $234 billion in enterprise application software spending "at risk" through 2030 — roughly 20% of all enterprise app SaaS spending. The reason has a name: agentic arbitrage.

Agentic Arbitrage: "Arbitrage" normally means getting the same value cheaper at one place than another. Here: Until now, for a single task—say, creating an order—you open CRM, ERP, and a reporting tool one after another, clicking through three interfaces. An AI agent handles exactly this chain on its own across all systems and delivers the result directly. The expensive, click-heavy program interfaces in between become redundant—and so do the license fees for them.

Gartner's managing VP George Brocklehurst puts it sharply: "Agentic AI is changing the economics of software"—systems will deliver "outcomes directly and bypass traditional, UX-heavy applications." Translation: if you make money today because humans click through pretty interfaces, you have a problem once those interfaces aren't needed anymore.

Methodology / Heads up Gartner's own press release was access-restricted (HTTP 403) on direct retrieval. The figures ($234B, ~20%, target year 2030) and the Brocklehurst quote are cross-checked against two independent trade sources reporting the same numbers—so verified, but not primary-sourced. "At risk" is Gartner's framing, not "will be lost": it quantifies the endangered volume, not a realized loss. A forecast remains a forecast.

My take: The $234B is a marketing number built on a real observation. The real observation: value shifts from the interface (the UI that SaaS vendors charge per seat for) to execution (the agent that completes the task). For twiceD, that's not a threat signal—it's an opportunity: whoever builds agents that handle concrete work steps across existing systems sits on the side where money flows to, not away from.

Microsoft makes Copilot permanently expensive—AI becomes a fixed cost

Deploy As of 01.07.2026, Microsoft has locked in Copilot pricing permanently. Business Standard with Copilot costs a fixed $23.50 per user/month, Business Premium with Copilot $32.00. The standalone Copilot add-on ran as a promo at $18 through 06.30—as of 07.01 it's fixed at $21 per user/month.

For the use-case lens, the cent-per-month detail doesn't matter—what matters is the transition: the promo phase was the "try it out" invitation. It's over. AI in the office is now shifting from a discount experiment to a fixed budget line—and from now on, every Copilot license purchased has to show measurable value, or it gets cut at the next license review.

Territory note The actual licensing and pricing mechanics of Microsoft 365 base plans (E3/E5 increases, bundle restructuring) belong in the Software Brief and are covered there. Here, only the AI/Copilot angle counts. The reported "E7" bundle number ($99 including Copilot + Agent 365) is so far only verified via search synthesis and not primary-confirmed—so deliberately left out of hard numbers here.

My take: This fits exactly with the Gartner thesis above. Microsoft today still sells Copilot as an add-on on top of the old interface. The next step—Agent 365, agents that act on their own—is exactly the "agentic arbitrage" Gartner means. Microsoft is eating its own UI before someone else does. Smart, but it makes proving AI ROI more urgent for customers, not easier.

Bitkom: AI adoption in Germany doubled—disillusionment too

Study The best German anchor today is the Bitkom study "Artificial Intelligence in Germany" (research report, April 2026): 41% of German firms actively use AI—just 17% in 2024. Another 48% plan to deploy it. More than a doubling—that's real.

But the study also delivers the fine print that gets buried in the hype: 33% report that AI turned out more expensive than expected, and 19% have already cut jobs because of it. Large enterprises (500+ employees) sit at over 60% adoption; mid-market is catching up, but slower, more often through SaaS platforms than homegrown systems.

Methodology / Heads up Bitkom research report, published April 2026, n = 604 companies, method CATI (computer-assisted telephone interviews). "Actively uses AI" is self-reported and elastic—everything from a thought-through agent workflow to "someone occasionally pastes something into ChatGPT" counts. The figures here come from a secondary source; the Bitkom primary page was not directly retrieved.

My take: The number I hold onto isn't the 41%—it's the 33%. If a third of users say "it cost more than I thought," that's the market gap for honest consulting—not another tool, but someone who tells you what an application case really costs before you buy. That's twiceD territory (see project ideas below). And the 19% job cuts are the uncomfortable truth behind every productivity chart: for the people affected, "efficiency gain" sometimes just means the job is gone.

Legal AI: Harvey valued at $11B—bigger than its market?

M&A Legal AI startup Harvey raised $200M in fresh capital on 25.03.2026 at a valuation of $11B, led by Singapore's GIC and Sequoia; altogether Harvey has now raised over $1B.

Vertical AI: AI that doesn't do "everything a little," but is tailored to one industry—here: law. Like a specialist tradesperson instead of a hardware-store generalist. Harvey reads contracts, drafts pleadings, and searches legal precedent, trained on legal language instead of the whole internet.

The provocative framing circulating in reports: Harvey alone is worth nearly twice as much as the entire legal AI software market it serves. That's rhetorically catchy—and worth treating with caution.

Methodology / Heads up The valuation ($11B) is cleanly documented via CNBC/Bloomberg. The market sizes set against it (~$5.6B legal AI market in 2026; or $3.11 → $10.82B through 2030 at 28.3% CAGR) come from search synthesis across Research-and-Markets/MarketsandMarkets, not from a directly reviewed primary report. I name them as a rough order of magnitude, not a hard figure—and the CAGR certainly not as fact. The core claim holds: one vendor's valuation is far ahead of market reality.

My take: If a startup is worth more than its market, the capital market is betting the market itself grows by many multiples—not that Harvey eats today's pie. For twiceD, the lesson: in narrow, industry-specific AI niches with high willingness to pay (law, compliance, insurance), there's money. Don't rebuild Harvey—but occupy the German mid-market version of a tightly-scoped specialist workflow.

Rossum → Coupa: an IDP market leader loses independence

M&A Coupa announced the acquisition of Rossum (12.05.2026, at Coupa Inspire 2026)—"to accelerate end-to-end autonomous spend management." Rossum had been counted as one of ten market leaders in the IDP field (Everest Group PEAK Matrix 2026, alongside ABBYY, Hyperscience, Nanonets, UiPath, Microsoft, and others).

IDP (Intelligent Document Processing): Software that reads unstructured documents—PDF invoices, scanned shipping slips, email orders—understands them, and turns them into clean datasets that an ERP can process. Like a digital back-office worker who opens the mail and types it into the system, except without coffee breaks or typos.

Why this belongs here and not just in the business section: with the acquisition, Rossum vanishes as an independent vendor and becomes part of a spend-management suite. For a mid-market customer who wanted to use Rossum as a standalone building block, that means: either live with the whole Coupa platform or look elsewhere. Exactly these consolidations open gaps at the lower end of the market—which is where the twiceD idea below sits.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals—with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Document-order agent for mid-market

Eli5 & value: Many mid-market firms receive purchase orders, complaints, and delivery notices as PDFs, emails, or faxes and hand-type them into their ERP. A "document agent" reads these unstructured inbound records, checks them against master data, and files them as clean records. Value: saved expensive back-office time and fewer data-entry errors—the error cost from mis-entered orders is the real pain point, not the typing minutes.
Prototype (v0): (1) Real document stack from one customer as test set. (2) Extraction via an existing IDP model (layout + fields)—e.g., Azure Document Intelligence, Google Document AI, or LlamaParse—not home-built. (3) Validation logic against master data. (4) Write adapter into the target ERP for one record type. MVP deliberately narrow: one document type, one customer, human approves before posting.
Market & gap: The IDP market has mature, named players—ABBYY, Hyperscience, Nanonets, UiPath, Microsoft, Google Document AI, and until the Coupa acquisition, Rossum as a standalone mid-market player. That very acquisition (12.05.2026) is the current lever: Rossum vanishes as an independent mid-market building block into a large spend suite. The remaining giants are horizontal platforms—the customer assembles workflow, integration, and validation themselves and pays per page/per seat. Gap for twiceD: the last mile—fitting into a concrete German mid-market process, on-prem for sensitive data, no SaaS per-page sub. (Market size floats: one vendor blog claims ~$2B 2025 → ~$14B 2026—a 7× jump in one year, so explicitly not used as fact here, only flagged as caution.)
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. The heavy model comes from the platform; twiceD builds the adapter + validation + ops. v0 in 4–6 weeks solo, rollout with 1 external dev per customer. Maintenance low, as long as scope stays tight (one document type per phase). On-prem as a sovereignty wedge possible via an open layout model where data cannot leave the building. coupa.com · idp-software.com

2 · "AI costs & AI Act" readiness check for mid-market

Eli5 & value: One-third of German AI users say per Bitkom: costs more than I thought. And most have no clear sense which of their AI applications fall under the EU AI Act. A "readiness check" is a tight, paid audit: where does AI actually pay (with real before-and-after math), what does operation honestly cost, and which use cases need documentation or risk-rating because of the AI Act? Value: customer avoids an expensive mis-investment and a compliance trap—both with real price tags.
Prototype (v0): (1) Questionnaire + interview guide that captures existing and planned AI use cases. (2) A risk-class matrix against AI Act categories (banned / high / limited / minimal)—the official regulation provides the criteria, nothing invented. (3) Cost model per use case (license + ops + maintenance) that makes the "costs more than I thought" trap visible. (4) 5-page report with traffic-light prioritization. MVP deliberately advisory, not a product: one customer, one report.
Market & gap: The consulting field is crowded by the big houses (Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, Capgemini) and pure legal boutiques on AI Act. Their offers are either big-company expensive or purely legal (the AI Act side) without the technical cost/ROI side. Gap for twiceD: the German mid-market firm that needs both together—honest technical economics and compliance rating—from someone who builds the tech themselves and can credibly say what an application case really costs. Exactly what Bitkom's findings target: 41% use it, but 33% pay more than planned.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Very high. Pure advisory/one-time work has nearly zero ongoing maintenance—no hosting, no GPU, no monitoring. One report per customer, scalable via one external dev per parallel engagement. The risk is content: the AI Act must stay current. Exactly the kind of engagement that fits twiceD's size. mybusinessfuture.com · artificialintelligenceact.eu
