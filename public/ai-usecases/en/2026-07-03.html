The deployment company becomes standard playbook

Microsoft announced the "Frontier Company" on Thursday — a new firm with a $2.5 billion investment and 6,000 "industry and engineering experts" whose sole purpose is to actually bring AI to productive use at large customers. First customers named: London Stock Exchange Group, Unilever, Land O'Lakes, and Accenture.[1]

This is not an isolated case, but the fourth such announcement in eight weeks. AWS moved two days earlier with an internal $1 billion deployment venture; OpenAI and Anthropic launched comparable units in May. All four are copying a model Palantir has been running for years.[1]

Forward-deployed engineering: Instead of just selling the customer a tool and leaving them alone, the vendor sends its own engineers on-site to fit the software into the customer's actual processes. Like a furniture store that doesn't just deliver the cabinet but builds it into your slanted attic wall — because the cabinet itself was never the problem, the wall was.

My take: This is the most important story of the week, precisely because it sounds unsexy. The hyperscalers are officially admitting what every practitioner already knows: the bottleneck in enterprise AI is not the model, it's the last mile — data integration, process fit, change management, operations. And that last mile is labor-intensive, customer-specific, and hard to automate. For twiceD, this is the best news of the year: if even Microsoft needs 6,000 people for it, the market for small, hands-on implementers is huge — the corporate squads only serve Fortune-500 customers, not the 200-person German machine-tool builder.

Bitkom 2026: 36% — not 41%, and why it matters

Bitkom has published its annual "Artificial Intelligence in Germany" study. The solid core number from the primary report: 36% of German companies (20+ employees) are already deploying AI — a doubling from 20% in 2024. 29% are planning higher AI investments than the prior year.[2]

Number check Secondary aggregators are circulating a 41% adoption rate for the same study. But the Bitkom primary report explicitly states 36% ("more than one in three companies, a doubling from 20% in 2024"). We're citing the primary source. Basis: two representative surveys (companies 20+ employees + individuals 16+), Bitkom Research 2025.

Where does AI sit in the firm? According to the primary report, overwhelmingly in customer contact (88% of AI-using firms), then marketing/communications (57%), a distant third is R&D (21%), production (20%), controlling (17%). In IT (2%), sales, legal, and management (each 5%) barely at all. Only 12% embed AI in their own products.[2]

My take: The doubling is real and impressive, but the distribution exposes the hype: in Germany, AI is almost everywhere a marketing/support tool, not part of value creation. The jump from "writes our emails" to "runs our production" is exactly the gap where consulting makes money.

EU AI Act: Deadlines shifted — but not all

The EU Council approved the "Digital Omnibus" simplification package on June 29 (the EU Parliament had approved it on June 16). Core: the compliance deadline for high-risk AI systems slips from Aug 2, 2026 to Dec 2, 2027 — over a year's reprieve. For AI embedded in products under EU product-safety law (elevators, toys), the deadline is even later: Aug 2, 2028.[4][5]

High-risk AI (AI Act): AI that has a say in things about people, where mistakes hurt — resume screening, loan approval, medical devices, critical infrastructure. Like an elevator: it needs TÜV inspection, a desk chair doesn't. For these systems, the AI Act requires risk management, documentation, human oversight, and conformity proofs.
Read closely Only the high-risk deadline shifted. The Art. 50 transparency mandate — users must be able to tell they're talking to an AI or that content is AI-generated — takes effect unchanged on Aug 2, 2026. Only the watermarking of GenAI systems that were already on the market before that date has been pushed to Dec 2, 2026 (grandfathering). If you run a chatbot, you have four weeks — not yet 13 months.

The penalty framework stays: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.[5] On point, the Bitkom number above: 93% of firms affected by the AI Act expect "somewhat" or "very" high implementation effort, and 56% think the effort is bigger than the benefit.[2]

My take: The reprieve is a gift to everyone building high-risk systems — but a trap for everyone who thinks "the AI Act got pushed." The transparency mandate hits practically every firm running a customer chatbot, and it stays in August 2026. Miss that one now, and you wake up in fall with a €35 million threat.

Stanford HAI: 88% use AI — <10% have scaled it

The Stanford HAI AI Index 2026 (Economy chapter) reports: 88% of surveyed organizations have deployed AI in some form, 70% use generative AI in at least one business function — but fewer than 10% have fully scaled AI in any function. Agent deployment stays "single digit" across nearly all functions.[6]

Methodology flag The retrieved chapter page doesn't disclose sample size or survey method (probably in the full PDF appendix). "Productivity gain" is defined differently across studies. Reported deltas: customer support +14–15%, software development +26%, marketing output +50% — largest in structured, measurable work. Read as order-of-magnitude, not as verified individual values.

My take: The gap between "88% adopted" and "<10% scaled" is the whole story of applied AI in 2026. Pilot is easy, production is hard — and that explains why four hyperscalers are simultaneously founding deployment companies.

  1. hai.stanford.edu — AI Index 2026, Economy chapter.

Salesforce: Agents billed per resolved case

Salesforce is bringing its Agentforce Help Agent and Customer Service Portal to general availability in July — with a striking pricing model: $2 per resolved case, not per seat or month.[7]

Outcome pricing: You don't pay for access to the tool; you pay only when it delivers the result — here, a genuinely resolved support ticket. Like a plumber who only gets paid if the faucet doesn't leak afterward. Shifts risk from buyer to vendor.

Per Salesforce's own "State of Service" survey, agent adoption in customer service rose from 39% (2025) to 66% (2026) — a 1.7x jump — and 70% of adopters reported measurable value within 60 days.[7]

Conflict of interest This is a Salesforce-commissioned survey of its own customer/market ecosystem — vendor-interested, not an independent source. Read adoption and value rates with that caveat.

My take: The pricing model is the real story. If Salesforce dares bill per solution, it's a strong signal that the agents are reliable enough in narrow, well-defined support domains. In open-ended domains, nobody bills that way.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI-Act readiness check for mid-market

ELI5 & value: Starting Aug 2, 2026, any firm running a customer chatbot must disclose that a person is talking to an AI (Art. 50) — and anyone building high-risk AI has a long compliance checklist through Dec 2027. A "readiness check" is a structured audit: we map which AI systems a firm uses, classify each by AI-Act risk tier, and deliver a prioritized to-do list with deadlines. Value: avoided penalties (up to €35M / 7% revenue) and clarity instead of panic. Bitkom says: 93% of affected firms expect high implementation effort — the pain is already felt.[9]
Prototype (V0): (1) Questionnaire + interview guide along AI-Act risk categories. (2) A classification tree (chatbot → Art. 50; resume screening → high-risk; internal text summary → minimal). (3) An LLM-powered report generator that pulls action items and deadlines from the responses. (4) A fixed-price package for a first pilot customer. MVP intentionally narrow: consulting + report, no ongoing compliance platform. Building blocks: the AI Act text itself, a standard LLM for report generation.
Market & gap: They already exist — governance platforms like Credo AI, Holistic AI, and OneTrust offer AI governance software.[9] But they're enterprise software with enterprise pricing and enterprise onboarding — built for corporations with dedicated compliance teams, not the 150-person machine-tool maker with one chatbot and a CV-screening tool who just wants to know what to do by August. twiceD's gap: the hands-on audit in German, with DE contract and GDPR reflex, as an affordable fixed-price project instead of a software subscription.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. It's advisory work with nearly zero ongoing maintenance — each audit is a closed project. V0 in 2–3 weeks solo (the hard knowledge is the statute, no code). Scale via extra contractors per customer, not a standing team. The Aug 2026 deadline creates a real, dated demand spike. credo.ai · holisticai.com · gibsondunn.com

2 · The "last-mile" fitter for a support agent

ELI5 & value: When Microsoft, AWS, OpenAI, and Anthropic all spin up firms whose only job is fitting AI, they're saying: the value lives not in the model, but in the integration. twiceD sells exactly that last mile at small scale — a ready-made customer-service agent (off-the-shelf model), but properly wired to one concrete mid-market firm's ERP/ticket system, tested on its real processes. Value: the support-time leaps Salesforce/Anthropic advertise (Lyft: −87%), but for firms too small for corporate deployment squads.
Prototype (V0): (1) A real ticket backlog from the customer as test and training data. (2) A standard agent framework (e.g., an LLM + RAG over customer knowledge base). (3) Wiring to one target system (the ticketing tool) for one ticket type. (4) Escalation rule: uncertain cases go to a human. MVP tight: one ticket category, one customer, human confirms before send.
Market & gap: Platforms exist — Salesforce Agentforce ($2/resolved case), Intercom Fin, Zendesk AI.[10] But they're self-service: the customer has to wire knowledge base, integration, and testing themselves — and Stanford HAI shows that's exactly where >90% fail (adopted, not scaled). And all three run on US cloud; there's no on-prem option for sensitive data or sovereignty concerns. twiceD's gap: hands-on fitting for the German mid-market, including an on-prem variant with an open model for firms where the cloud is off the table.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. The build is solo-doable (the model is bought, twiceD builds adapter + validation), V0 in 4–6 weeks. But: a live agent has real operational load — monitoring, hallucination control, model updates. Honest truth: only viable if scope stays narrow (one ticket type, one system) and each customer has a contractor for operations. Too broad a promise tips into team-scale needs. salesforce.com · intercom.com · hai.stanford.edu
