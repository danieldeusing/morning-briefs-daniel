Microsoft and AWS are building deployment troops — that's the real story

Why four companies are betting on the same model within eight weeks

Deploy Microsoft "Frontier Company": $2.5B, 6,000 people at customer sites

On Thursday (July 2), Microsoft unveiled a $2.5 billion initiative called Frontier Company: roughly 6,000 industry and engineering experts working directly on-site at the customer, instead of selling licenses and leaving the rest to the IT department.[1] It's led by Rodrigo Kede Lima (most recently President of Microsoft Asia); according to GeekWire, it is not a separate legal entity but an internal unit.[2] Launch customers: the London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG), Land O'Lakes, Unilever, and Novo Nordisk — finance, agriculture, consumer goods, pharma, deliberately spread wide.

Forward Deployed Engineering (FDE): Instead of selling the customer a tool and hoping they get it running themselves, the vendor sends its own engineers into the customer's operation — like a furniture store that doesn't just deliver the cabinet but sends someone to fit and assemble it in your apartment. Microsoft explicitly stresses "going beyond what's been called FDE" — marketing speak, but the underlying model is exactly that.

Deploy AWS: $1B for "Forward Deployed Engineering"

Two days earlier, on Tuesday (June 30), AWS had announced its own Forward Deployed Engineering unit with $1B: teams ("pods") of ~5–6 engineers per customer, with the stated goal of getting AI projects live "in weeks instead of quarters."[3] AWS VP Francessca Vasquez put the demand in a nutshell: "The currency customers are talking about right now is speed."[3] Launch customers named in Amazon's own announcement: Allen Institute, Cox Automotive, NBA, NFL, Ricoh, Southwest Airlines.[4]

Source caveat The Amazon blog post carried no visible date at time of retrieval; the CNBC primary source was blocked for the crawler (403). The June 30, 2026 date rests on the CNBC URL path plus an independent MarketScale rendition — solid, but not read firsthand. A circulating "45-day engagement" detail couldn't be confirmed and is omitted here.

M&A Four bets, one pattern

AWS and Microsoft aren't the first. According to Amazon's own announcement, Anthropic launched an AI services vehicle with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs in May; shortly after came OpenAI Deployment Co. with TPG, Advent, Bain Capital, and Brookfield.[4] Four "forward-deployed" constructs in eight weeks — from two hyperscalers and two model labs, some backed by PE money. My take: this is the collective admission that the bottleneck is no longer the model itself, but the last mile — the concrete fit into a real company process. Whoever solves that gets paid; whoever just sells models becomes a commodity.

Why now, exactly: the pilot-to-production gap

Two studies explain the demand side — both with an asterisk

Study 73% use AI, 10% have it at the core

The Global Enterprise AI Report 2026 from Publicis Sapient delivers the sharpest number of the week: 73% of companies say AI is used "regularly or across most processes" — but only 10% say it's "core" to their business.[5] That's the gap the FDE billions are meant to close: rolling it out is easy, embedding it at the core is the work.

Methodology n=1,550 AI decision-makers, companies with ≥500 employees and ≥$100M revenue, countries US/UK/FR/DE/AU/UAE, collected April 29–May 14, 2026, by Protégé for Publicis Sapient. Caveat: Publicis Sapient is itself an AI/digital consultancy — the "deployment isn't enough" thesis directly supports its own offering. Number plausible, framing interest-driven.

Study Governance is falling behind ambition

Deloitte's State of AI in the Enterprise (2026, "The Untapped Edge") makes the counterpoint: ~75% of companies want to roll out agentic AI within two years, but only 21% report having a mature model for governing it.[6]

Agentic AI: AI that doesn't just answer, but independently carries out multi-step tasks — for example, checking an order, approving it, and posting it to the ERP without a human triggering every step. It's the difference between a GPS that shows the route (an answer) and a self-driving car that drives it (an action). "Governance" here means: who's liable if the agent posts something wrong — and how do you pull it back?
Methodology n=3,235 business/IT leaders, surveyed Aug.–Sep. 2025, published 2026. A widely circulated "23% already use agentic AI at least moderately" claim could not be confirmed as a current figure on Deloitte's own site — there it's a projection for 2027, not today's status. Deliberately not cited here as a current adoption rate.

The gold standard: a deployment with numbers

Snowflake + Anthropic — concrete, but with a vendor benchmark

Deploy Snowflake deepens the Anthropic partnership

At Snowflake Summit 26 (June 1), Snowflake expanded the $200 million partnership with Anthropic signed in December 2025.[7][8] The adoption figures cited: Cortex Code — Snowflake's coding assistant — reportedly has "more than 7,100 users" (Snowflake calls it the fastest-growing product in its history), and Claude reaches "over 13,900 Snowflake customers" worldwide (up from 12,600 in December).[8]

Text-to-SQL: An employee types "show me revenue by region for last quarter" in plain English, and the AI translates that into the database query language SQL and returns the table. Like an interpreter between a "plain question" and a "machine query" — skipping the detour through the data department.
Methodology The ">90% accuracy on complex text-to-SQL" figure comes from Snowflake's own, internal benchmark (Dec. 2025 announcement), not from an independent audit — read with caution. The user/customer numbers are Snowflake's own figures; for the named customers (Basis, Block, Carvana, Deloitte, eSentire, Indeed, Notion) no quantified outcome metrics are disclosed.

Why this is still the best story type nonetheless: a named company, a named product, a named user count with a date — the contrast to the anonymous "73%" of the surveys. The reflex still holds: read vendor numbers skeptically, but they beat an unnamed percentage estimate.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Last-Mile AI Deployment for Mid-Market Companies

ELI5 & value: Four corporations bet more than $4B combined this week that the bottleneck isn't the AI model, but fitting it into a real company process — they're sending their own engineers to the customer. That's exactly twiceD's format, just a size smaller: a German mid-market company has a specific, annoying process (quote generation, invoice review, support triage), wants AI for it, but can't afford either a 6,000-person conglomerate or a 5-engineer AWS pod. Value: a working agent fitted to its process, instead of a generic license gathering dust in a drawer.
Prototype (V0): (1) Take a single, tightly scoped workflow of a real customer (e.g., incoming order emails → review → ERP draft). (2) An existing model + framework as the engine — Claude/GPT via API, orchestration with LangGraph or the Vercel AI SDK, no in-house build. (3) Connect to one target system (the customer's ERP/CRM) via its API. (4) Human-confirms-before-posting as a safety net. MVP deliberately narrow: one process, one customer, no multi-tenancy. Existing building blocks: model APIs, LangGraph/AI SDK, n8n for plumbing.
Market & gap: At the top sit the hyperscaler FDE units (Microsoft Frontier $2.5B, AWS FDE $1B)[1][3] and the big SIs (Accenture, Deloitte, Capgemini). But those are calibrated to corporate budgets — an AWS pod of 5–6 engineers "in weeks" simply isn't addressable for a 150-person machine shop, and the generic consultants don't bring an on-prem/sovereignty offer for companies unwilling to put their order data in a US cloud. Gap for twiceD: German mid-market companies, a concrete process instead of a platform, German contract/GDPR reflexes, optionally via open models (Llama/Mistral) on-prem.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High — this is the native format. The heavy model comes from the API; twiceD builds the fit + integration + operations. V0 in 4–6 weeks solo, rollout with 1 contractor per customer (scales exactly like a hyperscaler pod, just smaller). Maintenance manageable as long as scope per customer stays narrow; the sovereignty variant (open model on-prem) is the price wedge against the US vendors. blogs.microsoft.com · marketscale.com

2 · Agentic Governance Audit as a One-Shot Consulting Engagement

ELI5 & value: Deloitte says: ~75% want to roll out agentic AI, only 21% have a mature governance model for it.[6] Translated: masses of companies are letting AI independently do things (post, approve, email), without a clean answer to "who's liable if it goes wrong, and how do we pull the agent back?" Here twiceD sells no software, but a paid audit + playbook: which agents are running, what are they allowed to do, where's the kill switch, what does the EU AI Act require. Value: avoided liability/compliance risk — the cheapest mistake is the one that never happens.
Prototype (V0): (1) Build a structured checklist, anchored to the EU AI Act (GPAI/high-risk obligations) + NIST AI RMF as a recognized framework. (2) A two-hour assessment interview + review of the actually running agents/prompts. (3) Output: a risk register + prioritized action plan as a PDF. (4) Optional follow-up: actually closing the top-3 gaps. MVP narrow: just the audit + report, no ongoing monitoring. Existing building blocks: EU AI Act text, NIST AI RMF, own model only for report formatting.
Market & gap: Governance platforms already exist (Credo AI, Holistic AI, IBM watsonx.governance) as do the Big 4 consultancies with expensive programs. The platforms are horizontal tools the customer must configure and maintain themselves — software, not an outcome; the Big 4 are too expensive and too slow for mid-market companies. Gap: a lean, fixed-price audit for the 500–5,000-employee zone, with a German legal/GDPR lens, that delivers an outcome (register + plan) instead of a subscription.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Pure advisory/one-shot work — practically zero ongoing maintenance, the most attractive part of the model. Scales via reusable checklist IP + 1 contractor at capacity. Risk: requires genuine regulatory fluency — twiceD has to build and maintain that, or the report is show instead of substance. deloitte.com · credo.ai
