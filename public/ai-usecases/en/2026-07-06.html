The AI bill is being squeezed from two sides

Two movements, one cause: AI has gotten too expensive, and now the bill is being squeezed from both sides. On the supply side, the labs are building their own chips to make inference cheaper — OpenAI unveiled "Jalapeño" with Broadcom (deployment by late 2026, ~50% cheaper per token, per Broadcom), and Anthropic is exploring a custom 2nm chip with Samsung. On the demand side, corporations are cranking up spending discipline: Uber capped per-employee spend, Microsoft pulled licenses, and a Bain survey shows the promised savings aren't materializing. In between, generative AI itself is becoming a commodity — Google's Nano Banana 2 Lite generates an image for 3.4 cents per thousand. And in Geneva, starting Wednesday, a 44-member UN commission convenes to try to rein it all in.

The labs are now building their own chips — to make inference cheaper

OpenAI's "Jalapeño" is near-live, Anthropic's Samsung talks are still raw

Deploy OpenAI + Broadcom: "Jalapeño," the first custom inference chip

On June 24, OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled Jalapeño — OpenAI's first self-designed AI chip, manufactured by Broadcom.[1] Broadcom CEO Hock Tan told Bloomberg it delivers roughly 50% lower cost per inference token compared to current GPUs.[2] The chip is a pure inference accelerator (not a training chip), is slated to be deployed in ChatGPT, Codex, and the API by end of 2026, and went from design to tape-out in just nine months — the fastest ASIC cycle in this performance class, according to those involved.[3]

Inference vs. training: "Training" is teaching the model to read, once — extremely compute-intensive, and it happens rarely. "Inference" is every single answer after that — millions of times a day. It's like the difference between building a factory (one-time, expensive) and every product that rolls off the line afterward (frequent, and every cent counts). A chip built only for inference can make that ongoing operation cheaper than a general-purpose GPU that can also train.

M&A Anthropic explores a deal with Samsung — very early, very vague

On July 2, The Information, Bloomberg, and TechCrunch reported that Anthropic is in early talks with Samsung about a custom AI chip — reportedly on Samsung's 2nm process.[4][5] Important context: this is an "early exploratory phase." Anthropic itself stresses that a "diversified hardware stack with chips from Google, Amazon, and Nvidia" remains central — and that it has "not yet decided what the chip is for, how it fits into the server, or how powerful it will be."[4] My take: this isn't a product, it's a signal. Together with Jalapeño, it shows both frontier labs are running the same math — the cost of their own inference has grown too high to keep paying Nvidia for it indefinitely.

The other side of the ledger: "tokenmaxxing" is over

Why corporations are suddenly capping AI spend hard

Study Uber caps spend, Microsoft pulls licenses

The cheaper chip is solving a real problem: AI has gotten more expensive inside companies than budgeted. Uber capped spending on agentic coding tools at $1,500 per employee per month — after the company burned through its entire 2026 annual AI budget in about four months.[6][7] Uber COO Andrew Macdonald admits it's "very hard to draw a line" between AI usage and building new consumer features. Meanwhile, Microsoft reportedly pulled most employees' direct Claude Code licenses as of June 30, and Amazon shut down an internal AI-usage leaderboard after employees were running pointless bots just to climb the rankings.[8]

Tokenmaxxing: The habit of reflexively reaching for the biggest, most expensive model for every task — even when a small one would do. Like driving a sports car to the corner bakery: it works, but the fuel bill is absurd. The countermove is called model routing: a front-end system sends simple requests to a cheap model and only the hard ones to the expensive one.

Study Bain: the promised savings aren't showing up

A Bain & Company survey of 951 large companies, reported via secondary sources, provides the number behind the mood shift: 37% had expected savings of 10-20%, yet 40% report actual savings of ≤10%, and only 4% exceeded 30%.[8] In other words: per-token prices have fallen massively over the years, yet companies' total bill still rose — because usage grew bigger, more frequent, and more widespread. That very gap is why silicon (supply) and spending caps (demand) are tightening at the same time.

Generative media is becoming a commodity

Google DeepMind pushes image and video down to cent-level pricing

Deploy Nano Banana 2 Lite: an image for 3.4 cents per thousand

On June 30, Google DeepMind made Nano Banana 2 Lite (officially Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite Image) generally available — by its own account the cheapest and fastest image model to date: $0.034 per 1,000 images, output in under 4 seconds.[9] It's immediately available in Google AI Studio, the Gemini API, and the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, and is recommended as a drop-in replacement for the first Nano Banana version. Alongside it came Gemini Omni Flash — a conversational video model — in public preview, at $0.10 per second of generated video (the same price as Veo 3.1 Fast), with clips up to ten seconds.[9]

Conversational video: Instead of typing a perfect prompt and hoping, you describe what you want in a dialogue, see the result, and say "make the sky darker, slow the camera down" — the model adjusts. It's like talking to an editor instead of sitting alone at the editing software. The per-second, cents-level pricing makes this kind of iteration affordable for a small business for the first time.

Why this belongs in the use-case beat rather than the dev-tools beat: the point isn't the model architecture, it's the economics. When a product photo or a 10-second clip costs cents instead of euros, the math flips for marketing teams, e-commerce catalogs, and social content at small and mid-sized businesses. That's exactly what the second twiceD idea below builds on.

Governance: 44 names, one commission

Standing thread — Regulation & Public AI

Policy AI for Good Global Commission — kickoff on Wednesday

The ITU (the UN's telecommunications agency) launched the AI for Good Global Commission on July 2: 44 founding members, co-chaired by Rwandan President Paul Kagame and Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff, with ITU Secretary-General Doreen Bogdan-Martin as vice-chair.[10] Among the tech members: Jensen Huang (Nvidia), Andy Jassy (Amazon), Brad Smith (Microsoft), and Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark.[11] The kickoff session is on July 8 in Geneva during the AI for Good Global Summit (07/07-10). Stated goal: responsible AI across sectors and borders, with participation from developing countries — the reference point being the 2.2 billion people still offline.[10]

My take: commissions like this produce communiqués first, not rules. For twiceD and mid-market companies, the concrete lever remains the EU AI Act — whose next GPAI deadline (08/02) is closer on the calendar than any Geneva declaration of intent. But the roster is notable: the same companies building their own chips and capping their own spend are now sitting at the governance table.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · LLM cost gateway for mid-market companies

ELI5 & value: The exact problem Uber and Microsoft solved this week with a sledgehammer (cap spend, cut licenses) will soon hit every mid-market company too — just without a dedicated platform team. An LLM gateway is a front-end layer between a company's apps and the AI providers: it routes simple requests to a cheap model and only the hard ones to the expensive one (model routing), sets per-team budgets, caches repeats, and shows who's using how much. Value: an AI bill you understand and control, instead of a surprise at month's end — at Uber it took four months to empty the budget.
Prototype (V0): (1) Use LiteLLM (open-source proxy, 100+ providers behind one OpenAI-compatible API) as the base — don't build it yourself. (2) Configure virtual API keys with per-team budgets and hard spend caps. (3) One simple routing rule: default requests → cheap model, escalate only when needed. (4) A per-department cost dashboard. Keep the MVP deliberately narrow: one client, self-hosted, no multi-tenant SaaS. Existing building blocks: LiteLLM, optionally Cloudflare AI Gateway for caching/logging, Grafana for the dashboard.
Market & gap: The market is real, but sizing varies wildly depending on definition: the broader estimate puts the LLM gateway market at $2.76B in 2026, projected to reach $7.21B by 2030.[a] A narrower, ~150x smaller definition (pure "LLM middleware gateway") puts it at just $18.9M (2026) → $189M by 2034, but with ~49.6% CAGR and ~42% enterprise adoption.[b] The spread itself is the story: no settled market picture, lots of movement. Established providers: LiteLLM, OpenRouter, Cloudflare AI Gateway, Kong AI Gateway,[c] plus TrueFoundry (listed by Gartner as a Representative Vendor in the "Market Guide for AI Gateways") and Portkey.[d] But these are horizontal tools: the customer still has to assemble routing logic, budgets, and operations themselves — software, not an outcome. The gap for twiceD: the 200-2,000-person mid-market company with no platform team; a gateway that's set up and operated rather than a SaaS subscription, self-hosted for companies that don't want their prompts routed through a US cloud.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. The heavy lifting (the proxy) is open source; twiceD supplies the setup, routing rules, GDPR-compliant hosting, and operations. V0 buildable solo in 3-4 weeks. Honest note on maintenance: a hosted gateway carries ongoing load (updates, monitoring, onboarding new models) — manageable for one person, but not zero. Scales at roughly 1 contractor per handful of clients; the sovereignty angle (self-hosted, open-model fallback) is the wedge against the SaaS providers. elinext.com · intelmarketresearch.com · getmaxim.ai · truefoundry.com

2 · Brand-consistent image pipeline built for the cent economy

ELI5 & value: Google's Nano Banana 2 Lite drops an AI image to 3.4 cents per thousand, and a video clip costs cents per second.[9] That flips the math for any business that constantly needs visual content — e-commerce catalogs, social posts, product variants. The problem: raw models produce generic images that don't match the brand (wrong colors, wrong look, occasionally a sixth finger). twiceD's value: a configured pipeline that delivers on-brand, vetted images at the push of a button — the client skips the photoshoots and freelancer rounds, without the catalog looking like stock AI.
Prototype (V0): (1) Turn the client's brand guide into reusable prompt templates plus reference images. (2) Generate via the Gemini API (Nano Banana 2 Lite) — not self-trained. (3) An automated QA stage: check brand colors/logo, filter out obvious artifacts (hands, text), route the rest to human approval. (4) Export to the target system (shop/PIM/social scheduler). Keep the MVP narrow: one image type (e.g., product-on-background), one client, human sign-off. Existing building blocks: Gemini API, a small vision model for QA, n8n for the plumbing.
Market & gap: There's no shortage of image generators (Adobe Firefly, Canva, Midjourney) or enterprise content platforms. The generators are self-service tools — the client still has to ensure consistency, brand fidelity, and QA on their own, and that's exactly where SMBs fall short (the output looks like generic AI). The enterprise suites are too expensive and too broad for mid-market companies. The gap: not a tool, but a fitted, vetted workflow for a specific catalog/content need — with an eye on GDPR (no customer photos going into third-party training pipelines) and German copyright-law reflexes around image provenance.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium to high. The V0 is buildable solo in 4-6 weeks; generation comes from Google, twiceD builds templates, QA, and integration. Honest caveat: the QA stage (reliably checking brand fidelity) is the hard, maintenance-heavy part — without it, it's just an expensive Midjourney wrapper. Scales project by project (one brand setup per client); the risk lies in the model dependency on Google (pricing/availability) — mitigated with a swappable generation layer. blog.google · getmaxim.ai

It's Monday, July sixth. The news day itself is quiet, but the week before it tells a clear story: the bill for artificial intelligence is suddenly being squeezed from two sides.

On the supply side, the big labs are now building their own chips. OpenAI teamed up with Broadcom to unveil a chip called Jalapeño — its first custom-built component made purely for answering questions, not for training. Broadcom's CEO says it's roughly fifty percent cheaper per request than today's graphics cards, and it's expected to be deployed by the end of the year. One thing to keep in mind: that's Broadcom's own claim, with no independent verification, and OpenAI's own announcement page was blocked for us.

Shortly after, it emerged that Anthropic is also talking to Samsung about a custom chip, reportedly on a very advanced two-nanometer process. But this isn't a product yet — it's an early exploration. Anthropic itself says it hasn't even decided what the chip would be used for. Both labs are running the same math: their own inference has gotten too expensive to keep paying Nvidia for it forever.

On the demand side, corporations are tightening spending discipline. Uber capped its AI spending at fifteen hundred dollars per employee per month, after burning through its entire annual budget in just four months. Microsoft reportedly cut most employees' direct licenses for Claude Code, and Amazon shut down an internal AI ranking. A Bain survey shows why: the promised savings aren't showing up — most companies are seeing under ten percent instead of the hoped-for ten to twenty. Those figures come with a caveat, since they trace back to a single secondary source.

Between these two movements, generative AI itself is becoming a commodity. Google made its image model, Nano Banana 2 Lite, generally available — an image now costs just three point four cents per thousand, generated in under four seconds. There's also a video model called Gemini Omni Flash in preview, priced at ten cents a second. When a product photo or a short clip costs cents instead of dollars, that changes the math for marketing and retail.

In Geneva, starting Wednesday, a new UN commission with forty-four members is convening — including the CEOs of Nvidia, Amazon, and Microsoft, along with an Anthropic co-founder. My take: gatherings like this produce statements of intent first. The concrete lever for mid-market companies remains the EU AI Act, whose next deadline is coming up in early August.
