Microsoft Sends 6,000 People Into Customer Offices

Applied AI · B2B · The largest entry in a six-week series

On July 2, Microsoft announced the Frontier Company: $2.5 billion, with which 6,000 industry and engineering experts will be embedded directly with customers — literally, to "co-design, roll out, and continuously improve AI systems at scale, measured against provable business outcomes."[1] The president is Rodrigo Kede Lima; early customers include the London Stock Exchange Group, Land O'Lakes, Unilever, and Novo Nordisk. TechCrunch independently confirms the sum and headcount.[2]

Forward-Deployed Engineering (FDE): Imagine the maker of an expensive machine sends its installation crew straight into your factory floor — and leaves them there until the machine is actually producing. That's exactly what AI providers are doing now: instead of just selling a model, they place their own engineering teams inside the customer's operations. At AWS, that's roughly five to six people for about 45 days.[3]

The most notable sentence isn't in the number, but in the fine print: delivery runs through the major systems integrators — Accenture, Capgemini, EY, KPMG, and PwC — who are meant to carry Microsoft's "Forward Deployed Engineering" practice into every market.[1] Microsoft isn't building a software feature. Microsoft is building a consultancy and outsourcing its scaling to consultancies.

My take: This is the industry's most honest admission in two years. Four providers, all claiming their model can do the work, are spending over eight billion dollars within six weeks to put people next to it. The bottleneck isn't intelligence, it's fit: data access, domain process, approvals, operations. And notice who the customer lists describe — LSEG, Unilever, Novo Nordisk, the NBA, Southwest Airlines. A 180-person machine-tool maker in Swabia will never be visited by these 6,000 people. That's not a complaint — it's a market gap with billion-dollar validation.

HP Brings OpenAI In-House

Applied AI · Consumer/B2B · Confusingly, also called "Frontier"

Four days before Microsoft, on June 28, HP Inc. announced a strategic partnership with OpenAI — same name, "Frontier," but a different thing in substance: no engineering squad, but a corporation deploying OpenAI models across four areas. HP's own announcement names them explicitly: customer- and partner-facing solutions, telemetry analysis via HP's WXP platform, employee productivity, and accelerated software development.[4] It followed a pilot running from February through June 2026.

Not primarily sourced Two catchy pilot figures are circulating in coverage: one developer reportedly moved "122 pull requests across 43 projects" in a few weeks, and a security team fixed bugs in one day instead of an estimated month. These figures appear neither in HP's announcement, nor were they available on openai.com today (HTTP 403). They come from secondary coverage. Until either company publishes them itself: a marketing anecdote, not a metric.

My take: What's interesting isn't the productivity, it's the direction. HP is a hardware corporation handing its customer interface — store, chat, voice, support telemetry — to an outside model. The developer piece belongs in the ai-dev brief; what matters for us: this is a sovereignty decision being sold as an efficiency decision.

ifo: AI Is Mainstream — Just Not at Mid-Sized Firms

Applied AI · Study · Germany

The ifo Institute published its May survey on June 5: 54.5% of German companies use AI in business processes — up from 40.9% a year earlier.[5] Klaus Wohlrabe, head of ifo surveys: "Artificial intelligence has definitively arrived in the mainstream of the German economy. The rollout is happening at a rapid pace."[5]

What matters is in the breakdown: large companies at 67.2%, mid-sized ones at 47.2% — a twenty-point gap. Small firms (51.2%) curiously rank ahead of mid-sized ones. Construction jumped from 7.1% three years ago to 39.8%.[5]

AI adoption among German companies — ifo Business Survey, May 2026
Methodology This is based on the monthly ifo Business Survey, a self-reported panel. The source gives no sample size and doesn't define what "AI in business processes" means — a team using ChatGPT for email presumably counts the same as a production line with image inspection. So the jump from 40.9% to 54.5% partly measures how normal it has become to check "yes." The relative gap between large and mid-sized firms is still solid, though: same questionnaire, same definition.

My take: This 20-point gap isn't a technology problem. Large firms have a person whose job is "roll out AI." The 200-person mid-sized firm has an IT lead already fighting four other fires. What's missing isn't the model — it's someone who sits next to the process for three weeks. That's exactly what corporations are now buying for billions.

Profound Aim: The Fight for Visibility in AI Answers

Applied AI · B2B SaaS · Marketing

On July 2, Profound launched Aim: a background agent that continuously translates signals from AI searches — which prompts users ask, which sources get cited, how a brand is talked about — into a weekly re-ranked list of "projects," then hands them off to sub-agents to work through.[6] A customer quote from Plaid: "The projects Aim suggests are literally gold."[6]

GEO / AEO (Generative Engine Optimization): It used to be about ranking first on Google. Today people ask ChatGPT "which CRM should I use?" and get three names back, never seeing a results list at all. GEO is the attempt to be one of those names: shaping content and data trails so models cite you — and measuring whether they do.

The relevance isn't a guess: eMarketer expects 31.3% of the US population to use generative AI search in 2026; Google AI Overviews appear in at least 16% of all searches.[7]

Market sizes: caution For "the GEO market in 2026," three research firms give three numbers: $365 million (US only), $1.09 billion, and $1.48 billion (global) — a spread of roughly a factor of 4 for a supposedly single figure.[8] None of the three discloses its methodology; every CAGR figure (40–50% through 2034) sits on that base. Exactly one statement is usable: the market is small today and growing fast. Anyone who drops one of these numbers into a pitch deck is pretending to know something.

My take: Profound itself is the more interesting story: a $96 million Series C at roughly a $1 billion valuation in February, ~$155 million total raised.[6] A market no one can measure cleanly is being flooded with capital — meaning capital has decided the category exists. My thesis: it exists, but not as a dashboard. It exists as work. More on that in idea 1.

Briefly Noted

Applied AI · 7-day context

Standing thread: EU AI Act — August 2 gets serious REG · EU

On 08/02/2026, per Article 113, "the rest of the regulation" takes effect — with a single exception, Article 6(1) (the high-risk classification under product-safety law), which doesn't apply until 08/02/2027.[9] The sharper point is elsewhere: Article 101, the fine provision against providers of GPAI models, was explicitly excluded back in August 2025 — and now becomes applicable.[9]

GPAI (General-Purpose AI): A model not built for one task, but for many — GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama. Like a Swiss Army knife versus a screwdriver: the legislator regulates the knife's maker, not everyone who uses it to turn a screw.

Roughly two dozen providers have signed the voluntary GPAI Code of Practice, including Anthropic, Google, IBM, Microsoft, Mistral, OpenAI, and Cohere. Meta has not signed, and xAI signed only the safety chapter.[10] The obligations hit model providers, not users. But anyone building models into customer products will, starting in August, get asked during procurement: "Which model — and did its provider sign?"

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Sovereign AI Visibility for German Mid-Sized Firms

ELI5 & value: If a buyer today asks "who builds custom machinery for food packaging?", ChatGPT answers with three company names — not a results list. Whoever isn't among those three doesn't exist for that buyer. The lever is different from the old Google game: not keywords, but whether the models can find reliable, citable facts about you. twiceD sells a recurring engagement for this: measure where the brand stands in AI answers, close gaps, prove it monthly.
Prototype (V0): (1) A prompt set for one niche (30–50 realistic buyer questions in German). (2) Run the prompts against the relevant models on a schedule, logging mentions, position, and cited sources — a cron job plus API calls, not a research project. (3) A diff report: who gets named instead of you, which sources the models draw from. (4) Close gaps — place structured company data and evidence exactly where the models demonstrably look. Deliberately not in the MVP: real-time dashboard, multi-tenancy, agency white-label. Building blocks: model APIs, a scheduler, Postgres — optionally fully on-prem against an open model.
Market & gap: The market is real and funded: Profound raised a $96 million Series C at roughly a $1 billion valuation in February (total ≈ $155 million) and moved from measuring to executing with "Aim" on 07/02.[6] Peec AI — the uncomfortable truth for any "no German provider" thesis — is a Berlin GmbH with over 2,000 marketing teams. Otterly.ai is Austrian, a Gartner Cool Vendor 2025, priced at $29–489/month. AthenaHQ comes out of the YC ecosystem. So the real gap isn't origin, it's the product shape: all four are English-language self-service SaaS for marketing teams, none lists an on-prem or self-hosted option (neither Otterly's pricing page nor Peec's product page) — and none does the actual work, they just display it. A 180-person machine-tool maker has no marketing team for a dashboard and can't dump its quote data into a US SaaS. As for market sizes: ignore them, factor-4 spread, no methodology.[8]
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium to high. The measurement piece is a weekend prototype; maintenance is real but bounded (models change answers, prompt sets go stale — one to two hours per customer per month). The value piece, actually closing the gaps, is manual work and precisely for that reason defensible: a $189/month SaaS can't deliver it. Scale via contractors per niche. Honest risk: if Peec announces a German on-prem variant, only the manual-work part remains — which still holds up, but as a consultancy, not a product. tryprofound.com · fortune.com · peec.ai · otterly.ai · emarketer.com

2 · The Mid-Market FDE: A Fixed-Price Deployment Sprint

ELI5 & value: Microsoft, AWS, OpenAI, and Anthropic have committed over eight billion dollars in six weeks to put their own engineers in customer offices — because AI projects don't fail on the model, they fail on fit. These squads go to LSEG, Unilever, the NFL. The mid-sized firm gets a subscription and a tutorial. The offer: a three-week fixed-price sprint at the end of which one real process runs productively with AI — not a pilot, not a workshop write-up. Value: a calculable amount, then measurably one fewer thing eating up staff hours.
Prototype (V0): (1) A pre-screening questionnaire filters for processes with a clear before/after (quote generation, complaint triage, tender review). (2) Week 1: observe and measure — minutes per task, where errors arise. (3) Week 2: build against existing building blocks — a model API or local model, a vector index (pgvector), an adapter into the existing system. (4) Week 3: run in parallel with real operations, a human confirms every case, deviations are counted. (5) Handover with a runbook and a kill switch. Deliberately out of scope: operations, SLA, a second process — those are follow-on engagements.
Market & gap: Who's already doing this? Microsoft explicitly delivers the Frontier Company through Accenture, Capgemini, EY, KPMG, and PwC.[1] AWS places teams of five to six engineers for roughly 45 days with customers like Southwest Airlines, Cox Automotive, Ricoh, the NBA, and the NFL.[3] That's the target audience — and it isn't mid-sized firms. The gap is quantified: per ifo, 67.2% of large German companies use AI in business processes, but only 47.2% of mid-sized ones — a twenty-point gap, same questionnaire, same definition.[5] No major integrator will write a three-week proposal for €25,000; its sales costs alone exceed that. That's exactly where the pricing room sits for a solo provider with German contract law, GDPR instincts, and an on-prem option.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium — and honesty is part of it. Building and maintaining is uncritical: after handover, the process belongs to the customer, twiceD carries zero ongoing load (that's what the kill switch in the runbook is for). The catch is the business model: revenue is tied to weeks, not licenses — body-shop risk. The fix is productization: the same three weeks, the same runbook template, the same adapter kit; from the third customer in an industry onward, effort drops noticeably. Two sprints per quarter solo, more only with a contractor. If no reusable pattern emerges after four customers, the idea is disproven — drop it rather than scale it into an agency. blogs.microsoft.com · aboutamazon.com · ifo.de
