Brussels gives itself more time — except on transparency

Regulation · EU · Standing Thread

On June 29, the Council of the EU gave final approval to the AI Act Omnibus — the part of the Digital Omnibus package presented on 11/19/2025 that simplifies the AI regulation; Parliament had formally approved it on June 16.[1] The core: the high-risk obligations that would have taken effect on 08/02/2026 are being postponed — for standalone Annex III systems to 12/02/2027, for Annex I AI embedded in products to 08/02/2028. Political agreement was reached on 05/06, confirmed by member states on 05/13.[2]

High-risk system / Annex III: The AI Act sorts AI into risk classes. "High-risk" is the tightly regulated tier — AI that helps decide over people: applicant screening, credit approval, exam grading, border control (that's "Annex III"). Think of it as a TÜV inspection for software: before such a system goes live, it needs risk management, documentation, and human oversight. That exact inspection date has now slipped by 16 months.

For applied AI, though, the other half of the news matters more: the transparency obligations under Article 50 were not postponed. They stay at 08/02/2026; only the watermark detail from Art. 50 para. 2 gets a four-month grace period until 12/02/2026.[2]

Article 50 (transparency): The rule that hits the ordinary user, not the high-risk special case. Anyone running a talking chatbot, publishing AI-generated text, images, or video, or producing deepfakes must disclose it — "you're talking to a machine," "this image is AI-generated." No TÜV procedure, just a notice — but mandatory, and first in line.

My take: The headline reads "Brussels backs off," and for corporations with recruiting or scoring AI that's true — they get 16 more months. For applied AI, as most Mittelstand companies use it (a support chatbot, AI-written marketing copy, generated product images), nothing changes: the labeling obligation lands in three and a half weeks. So the postponed deadline isn't a reprieve — it's a redirection of attention away from the obligation that actually kicks in first. Incidentally, the Omnibus also newly bans the creation of non-consensual intimate content and CSAM, and pushes the national regulatory sandboxes to 08/02/2027.[1]

What a verifiable case looks like: General Mills

Applied AI · Case · Mfg/Logistics

Against the adoption percentages that no one defines cleanly, here's the counter-type — a named company with an audited number. General Mills CFO Kofi Bruce told an investor conference on 02/18/2025: AI models that evaluate over 5,000 deliveries a day from plants to warehouses have saved more than $20M since fiscal year 2024; for ongoing manufacturing data, he forecasts roughly another $50M in scrap reduction.[6]

My take: The value of this item is its verifiability. No "up to" range, no anonymized "one food company" case study (the kind consulting decks are padded with) — a CFO stating a figure to investors and standing behind it. That's the gold standard this brief measures adoption claims against. And the use case itself is unspectacular: no AGI, no agents, just a model that prioritizes freight data. It's exactly these "boring" cases that deliver verifiable numbers. (Context: statement from February 2025 — included here as a reference example, not as today's news.)

Does 41% or 54.5% use AI? Both numbers are right

Applied AI · Study · Germany

Two German surveys, two numbers. The Bitkom AI Study 2026 (published 04/11, n=604, telephone CATI survey): 41% of German companies actively use AI — up from 17% a year earlier — with another 48% planning to; among firms with 500+ employees, over 60%.[3] The ifo Institute measures 54.5% for May (prior year 40.9%), with large companies at 67.2% and mid-sized ones at 47.2%.[4]

Methodology The 13-point gap (41 vs. 54.5%) isn't a contradiction — it's a measurement artifact. Bitkom surveys 604 companies with 20+ employees by phone and counts "actively in use"; ifo draws its panel from the monthly business-cycle survey (no published sample size) and asks about "AI in the business process." Different population, different definition, different method — the two figures cannot be directly compared. What holds up across both studies: the roughly 20-point gap between large firms and the Mittelstand.

My take: The interesting finding isn't the percentage — it's its fuzziness. "AI usage" today ranges from "one team drafts emails with ChatGPT" to "a production line with visual inspection" — both check "yes." Anyone who puts one of these figures into a pitch should say who was asked. What's reliable is the structure, not the point estimate: the big players have arrived, the Mittelstand is twenty points behind — and that exact gap is the addressable market.

Brazil's central bank eyes the black box REG · BR

The Financial Stability Report of the Banco Central do Brasil (Vol. 24 No. 2, November 2025) flags the "black box" risk of AI in the national financial system and announces it will review AI-specific supervisory rules for the 2025/26 agenda.[7] In parallel, BR fintechs are rolling out agentic assistants that carry out whole tasks — closing products, renegotiating debt, proactively managing finances — rather than just answering.[7]

Agentic AI: A chatbot answers; an agent acts — it's allowed to carry out steps itself (clicking, booking, negotiating), not just output text. Like the difference between an information desk and a case worker with power of attorney. That's exactly where the central bank's supervisors now want to look.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI Act compliance toolkit for the German Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: From 08/02/2026, anyone operating AI chatbots or publishing AI content must label it (Art. 50); the heavy high-risk obligations follow from late 2027. A corporation has a legal department for this and buys a governance platform for a six-figure sum. The 150-person Mittelstand firm has neither — but the same law. Value: twiceD sells a lean "AI Act readiness package" — a registry of all deployed AI systems, a risk classification per system, the required transparency notices ready to install, and ongoing monitoring that flags when a new tool or a new deadline changes the picture. Concretely: avoided fine risk and documented diligence, without an enterprise price tag.
Prototype (V0): (1) A discovery questionnaire that captures all deployed AI systems (chatbot, marketing generator, scoring, HR tool). (2) A classification logic along the AI Act risk classes (prohibited / high / transparency / minimal) — rule-based, fed from the regulation text, not reinvented. (3) A generated registry plus, per system, the concrete obligations and ready-made labeling components. (4) A simple monitor (cron plus change feed) that adds new systems and deadlines. Deliberately out of the MVP: fully automated model audits, bias testing, multi-tenancy. Building blocks: the open AI Act Explorer (artificialintelligenceact.eu), a model API for classification assistance, Postgres — fully on-prem against an open model is possible.
Market & gap: The market is real and fast-growing (per a single estimate from Dimension Market Research): "EU AI Act compliance solutions" from $609.4M (2026) to $10,534.9M by 2035, 37.3% CAGR (dimensionmarketresearch.com). Two camps offer this today: GRC incumbents extending their platform with AI governance (IBM watsonx.governance, ServiceNow AI Control Tower, OneTrust, Collibra), and AI-native platforms (Credo AI — publicly reported to have raised over $42M, customers including Autodesk and Mastercard —, Holistic AI, Modulos) (guptadeepak.com). All of them target the corporation: horizontal platforms, English-language, self-service, with annual prices roughly in the €30k–75k range for a dedicated tool up to six figures enterprise-wide. The gap: no one does the last mile for a German 150-person company — they don't know the company's own systems, the GDPR/copyright reflex is missing, and a tool the IT lead is supposed to run "on the side" doesn't help him; he needs the finished registry and the built-in notices, not another dashboard. None of the players listed offer on-prem/sovereign as standard.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium to high. The heavy research (regulation text, risk classes) is a one-time, public effort; the classification is rule-based plus AI assistance, not an ML training project. V0 in four to six weeks, solo. Maintenance is real but bounded: the AI Act changes in bursts (exactly like this week) — one to two days per change wave to update rules and deadlines; that's also the recurring revenue (monitoring subscription). Scale via contractors per industry/language. Honest risk: if OneTrust sells a cheap, German-localized Mittelstand package, the hands-on part remains the moat — but as a service, not a product. dimensionmarketresearch.com · guptadeepak.com · credo.ai · artificialintelligenceact.eu

2 · The transparency sprint: Article 50 by August 2nd

ELI5 & value: Idea 1 is the big, permanent solution. Idea 2 is the emergency exit with a date. In three and a half weeks, Art. 50 takes effect: every chatbot must disclose it's a machine; every AI-generated marketing text and every generated product image needs a label. Many Mittelstand companies built exactly these features last year without thinking about the law. Value: a fixed-price sprint (one week), at the end of which a customer's genAI touchpoints are verifiably Art. 50 compliant — a disclosure notice in chat, labeling on generated content, and a short proof sheet for the file.
Prototype (V0): (1) Quick audit: where does the customer touch generative AI externally? (2) At each finding, install the required notice (chat disclosure, content label). (3) A proof sheet: what was labeled, referencing Art. 50. Deliberately out: high-risk topics and watermark depth (the grace period until 12/02/2026 applies there anyway). Building blocks: the regulation text, small UI snippets, no new system.
Market & gap: The governance platforms from Idea 1 do not solve this — they inventory and document, they don't install a disclosure notice into an actual product. And the Big Four consultancies won't write a week-long proposal for a 150-person company — their sales cost exceeds the deal. The gap is the timing plus the format: concrete, small, with a deadline. The basis is the hard deadline — Art. 50 was explicitly not postponed in the Omnibus (gibsondunn.com).
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. Pure implementation and consulting work, zero ongoing load after handover. The catch is the shelf life: a deadline product that loses its train in August — after that, useful only for latecomers and as a door-opener into Idea 1. Which is exactly why: now or never. artificialintelligenceact.eu · gibsondunn.com
