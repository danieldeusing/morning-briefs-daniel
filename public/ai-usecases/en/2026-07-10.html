Two AI Colleagues in One Week

Applied AI · Deploy · US · Headline

On July 9, OpenAI launched ChatGPT Work — an agentic workplace assistant built on the new GPT-5.6 model. It pulls context from connected apps and files and turns it into finished artifacts: reports, spreadsheets, presentations, whole websites. The rollout runs across web, mobile, and desktop, first for Pro, Enterprise, and Edu users, then for Plus and Business.[1] Two days earlier, on July 7, Anthropic had brought Claude Cowork to mobile and web (beta first for Max users). Cowork already existed; what's new is the reach across phone and browser — and the fact that the agent keeps working in the cloud when no device is online, and checks in as soon as it needs approval.[2][3]

AI agent (vs. chatbot): A chatbot answers — you ask, it types back. An agent acts: it's allowed to carry out several steps itself (open files, build a spreadsheet, send a draft), not just talk. Think of the difference between an information desk and an assistant with power of attorney who actually gets the task done and reports back when it's finished.

My take: What's remarkable isn't the individual product, but the simultaneous move. Both labs have stopped selling a model and started selling a colleague — the product is no longer intelligence, it's getting things done. The models underneath (GPT-5.6 with its Sol/Luna/Terra tiers) are developer territory: details in the ai-dev brief. OpenAI's marketing claim that Sol is "54% more token-efficient" at agentic coding than Anthropic's model is exactly that — an unsubstantiated vendor figure relayed by CNBC from Sam Altman; only the applied side matters here. For applied AI, the move means the bottleneck is shifting away from "can the model do it?" toward "is the agent allowed at my data, and does it keep its promises?"

What the AI Colleague Is Actually Used For

Applied AI · Study · Global

This week, Anthropic analyzed what Cowork is actually used for: 1.2 million anonymized sessions from more than 600,000 organizations, sampled May 11–31, 2026. The result disproves the obvious expectation that an AI "coding agent" would mainly be used for coding: business processes and operations make up the largest segment at 33.4%, followed by content and text at 16.4% — software development sits at just 8.7%.[4] Anthropic's own framing: roughly half of usage is "the work around the work" — tasks that belong to many jobs but are rarely anyone's core task.

Claude Cowork — usage by task type (top 8 of 20), sample May 11–31, 2026
Methodology These are Anthropic's own numbers — the company has an interest in showing Cowork as broader than "just dev." It's a usage sample from a single product (not "the AI market"), categorized by classification, not by interview. What's solid is the structure, not the second decimal place: coding is a niche topic here, the bulk is office work. That's exactly this brief's territory.

My take: This is the most important number of the week, because it corrects a narrative. The headlines are about "coding agents spilling into the office" — but the office was already there. The value of these agents isn't created for developers (they already have their tools), but for case workers, sales, marketing, the back office. And that's exactly where Germany's Mittelstand sits, with neither a data-science team nor a coding culture. The colleague is built for them — they just don't know it yet.

The $700 Billion Foundation

Economy & Labor · Standing Thread · US

Both launches stand on the same foundation: a historic compute investment. Combined, the four big hyperscalers — Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta — plan roughly $700–725 billion in capital expenditure for 2026, versus about $410 billion the year before; that's a rise of around +77%. From the Q1 2026 numbers: Alphabet $35.67 billion in quarterly capex (more than doubled), Amazon $44.2 billion (AWS grew 28%), Microsoft $30.88 billion (+84% year over year), Meta raised its full-year guidance to $125–145 billion.[5]

Hyperscaler / capex: Hyperscalers are the handful of corporations that run the world's big cloud data centers. "Capex" (capital expenditure) is the money they pour into concrete, chips, and power instead of day-to-day operations. Like a bakery that doesn't buy flour but builds a second baking hall instead — a bet on future demand.

My take: This number is the quiet half of the AI-colleague story. An agent working in the background for hours burns through many times the tokens of a chat prompt — the $700 billion is the precondition for Cowork and ChatGPT Work being able to "keep running when the laptop is closed" at all. The flip side: this bet has to pay off. If the impact scorecard (next column) stays weak, this is the most expensive infrastructure in tech history in search of its use case — and that won't be decided at the labs, but at the companies that make it productive.

And Is It Working? The Honest Scorecard

Applied AI · Study · Skepticism layer

Against two product launches and $700 billion in capex, it helps to look at impact. The Stanford HAI AI Index 2026 is the solid side: generative AI reached 53% population adoption in just three years — faster than the PC or the internet — and the estimated consumer value for US consumers stands at $172 billion per year.[6] The uncomfortable side comes from McKinsey's "State of AI": 88% of companies use AI in at least one function, but only about a third scale it company-wide — and fewer than 6% attribute a noticeable profit effect (EBIT) to their AI.[7]

Methodology The Stanford numbers (adoption, consumer value) come from a primary source, but it's a 400-page compilation of many studies — solid, but coarse-grained. We could not confirm the McKinsey numbers (1/3, <6%) primarily today (only via secondary reports), and it's a self-reported manager survey — carried as skeptical context, not as a headline.

My take: Both truths hold at once. Adoption is real and fast — but "adopted" too often means "a few people type with the colleague," not "embedded in the process and visible in profit." The <6% isn't an argument against AI, but against the belief that the model alone delivers the return. The return sits in the last mile: data, process, operations. And a gold-standard case — a named company with an audited number for ChatGPT Work or Cowork — simply doesn't exist yet today. Only anonymous blog anecdotes, which this brief doesn't count as evidence.

Access Rules Tighten

Regulation · Standing Thread · EU/US

While the labs roll out AI colleagues, corporations and lawmakers are tightening who gets to give which colleague access to which data. Two signals from recent weeks: Microsoft advised its employees against Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 on June 10 — a day after its launch — over a new, mandatory 30-day retention of prompts and outputs for Anthropic's Mythos class.[8] And the EU AI Act Omnibus (covered yesterday), with the Council's final agreement on June 29, uniformly defers all high-risk applications under Annex III — employment and recruiting included — to December 2, 2027; there is no earlier August cutoff specifically for HR uses.[9]

Employment high risk: The AI Act classifies AI that helps decide over people in an employment relationship — sorting applicants, performance evaluation, task allocation, monitoring, termination — as "high risk." That's exactly where the AI colleague in an HR or team context falls. Under the Council's final agreement of June 29, these employment uses too are covered by the uniformly deferred deadline — December 2, 2027, with no earlier August cutoff.

My take: This is the third side of the AI colleague. Once an agent gets access to company documents, HR data, and customer emails, "which tool is allowed to do that?" becomes a governance question — and Microsoft's Fable 5 block shows that even tech giants say no on data-protection grounds. For the mid-sized company whose employees have long been using ChatGPT and Claude, this is no abstract risk: it's shadow AI that nobody has inventoried.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Sovereign AI Colleague: On-Prem Knowledge-Work Agent for Regulated SMEs

ELI5 & value: ChatGPT Work and Claude Cowork do "the work around the work" — reports, spreadsheets, quotes, back office. But both run in the US cloud and send documents there. A regulated Mittelstand company, a law firm, a municipal utility, or a clinic can't just dump contracts, HR data, or patient records into a US cloud. Value: the same capability — delegating a knowledge-work task to an agent — but on the company's own infrastructure against an open model, so the data never leaves the building. twiceD doesn't sell "a model," it sells a ready-fitted agent for one concrete workflow, plus operations.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pick exactly one high-frequency workflow with the customer (e.g., quote generation from a template + product database, or contract-clause review). (2) Set up an open agent orchestrator — the n8n agent layer or Haystack/deepset — no building a framework from scratch. (3) Serve an open model on-prem or in an EU cloud, wired up to the customer's documents/mail/spreadsheets. (4) Human-in-the-loop: the agent drafts, a human approves before anything goes out. Deliberately out: a generalist across all tasks, multi-tenancy, autonomy without approval.
Market & gap: The agent market is real and growing fast — $9.89 billion (2026) → $57.42 billion (2031), 42.14% CAGR (Mordor Intelligence).[A] The generalists (ChatGPT Work, Cowork) are cloud-only, with no on-prem option. The sovereign EU providers are either platforms the customer has to assemble themselves — Aleph Alpha PhariaAI (on-prem agents, data residency at the customer, over $500 million raised — though as of April 24, 2026 no longer an independent sovereign provider: Cohere is acquiring/merging Aleph Alpha into a combined ~$20 billion entity, with a ~$600 million commitment from the Schwarz Group) and n8n (Berlin, $180 million Series C in Oct 2025, total funding ~$240 million, $2.5 billion valuation, self-hostable) — or vertical and narrow: Parloa (contact center), Taktile (financial decisions), Flank (legal).[B] Nobody does hands-on "general knowledge-work delegation" for a 150-person company on-prem. Exactly this combination — generalist capability, but sovereign and ready-fitted — is served by no one.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium to high. The heavy model and the orchestration come as open source; twiceD builds the integration, approval logic, and operations — assembly, not primitives. V0 per workflow in four to six weeks solo, rollout with one contractor per industry. Honest burden: on-prem operations (model serving, monitoring) carries real but bounded maintenance — as long as the scope per customer stays tight at one or two workflows. Sovereignty is the moat that the US-cloud generalists structurally cannot close. mordorintelligence.com · knowlee.ai · n8n.io

2 · AI Gateway for SMEs: Approved Agents, Protected Data

ELI5 & value: As soon as employees turn ChatGPT Work, Cowork & co. loose on company documents, shadow AI emerges: nobody knows which tool has seen which data. That's exactly why Microsoft blocked Claude Fable 5 internally. An "AI gateway" is the checkpoint in between: it logs which agent makes which request, blocks it when sensitive data (contracts, personnel files, customer data) would leave the building, and enforces a list of approved tools. Value: the boss can allow AI instead of banning it out of fear — with proof of which rule fired when.
Prototype (V0): (1) Set up an open gateway building block — LiteLLM or Cloudflare AI Gateway — that routes all AI calls through it. (2) A lean policy: approved models/agents, plus DLP rules that check for sensitive patterns (IBAN numbers, personnel IDs, contract markers). (3) An audit log plus a simple dashboard: who used what. Deliberately out of the MVP: fully automatic intent classification, agent behavior analysis. Building blocks: open-source gateway + rule engine, fully operable on-prem.
Market & gap: The AI governance market is young — roughly $418 million (2026), ~36% CAGR through 2033 (Grand View Research); the broader AI trust & security market stood at $4.9 billion in 2025.[C] It's served by enterprise-priced cloud providers: Zscaler (zero-trust proxy, AI Guard/DLP, MCP gateway), WitnessAI (shadow-AI detection, $58 million in its latest round, total funding ~$85 million), Palo Alto (Protect AI).[D] All of them target large corporations with a SOC team, delivered as SaaS in their cloud. The gap: the 150-person company without a security department that needs a lean, on-prem-operable checkpoint — set up hands-on, not as one more subscription dashboard.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. The heavy building blocks are open source; twiceD delivers setup, the rule set, and operations — an integration and advisory service, not product development. V0 in three to five weeks solo. Honest risk: security is a field with well-funded incumbents and an ongoing threat landscape — rule maintenance is a recurring burden (and recurring revenue). Viable as a service with a tight scope, not as an attempt to beat Zscaler at the product level. grandviewresearch.com · bankinfosecurity.com · aurascape.ai
