EU pushes the high-risk deadline to late 2027 [1][2]

On June 16, the European Parliament gave final approval to the "Digital Omnibus" (423 in favor, 57 against, 174 abstentions). The core takeaway for anyone rolling out AI in Germany: obligations for high-risk AI no longer kick in on August 2, 2026, but only on December 2, 2027 for standalone systems — and on August 2, 2028, if the AI is embedded as a safety component in a regulated product.

High-risk AI: AI systems that make decisions about people where a lot is at stake — credit scoring, hiring, access to education, medical triage. Think of it like a car's roadworthiness inspection: before such a system is unleashed on real people, it has to be checked, documented, and continuously monitored. That exact compliance paperwork is now due 16 months later.

My take: This is a breather, not a free pass. Two things bite sooner: the watermarking requirement for AI content (marking generated images/text as machine-readable) moves up to December 2, 2026, and the transparency obligations under Article 50 (users must know they're talking to AI) remain relevant. Anyone reading the delay as "so, later" is giving up exactly the time needed to become compliant cleanly instead of in a panic. For SMEs this is more of a project window than a reason to lean back — that's the first twiceD idea below.

Interactions API: the new standard gateway to Gemini [3][4]

In the week around June 22, Google took its Interactions API to general availability — it's becoming the standard interface through which applications talk to Gemini models and agents. What's interesting for use cases is a feature called Managed Agents: a single API call provisions an isolated Linux sandbox where an agent can think, run code, research the web, and manage files. On top of that comes background execution (background=True) for long-running tasks.

Context: For business teams, this means less plumbing — you no longer have to build a secure execution environment yourself just to let an agent "do something." The purely developer/CLI side of this launch (SDKs, tool-calling, coding workflows) belongs in the ai-dev brief; what matters here is that agents with real capability to act are becoming a standard order, not a special project.

Nano Banana 2 Lite: images for a fraction of a cent [5][6]

On June 30, Google made Nano Banana 2 Lite (model ID gemini-3.1-flash-lite-image) generally available: text-to-image in under 4 seconds for $0.034 per 1,000 images — good three cents per thousand images, well below a cent apiece. Available in AI Studio, the Gemini API, and the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, with rollout to Search (AI Mode), the Gemini app, NotebookLM, Google Photos, Stitch, Flow, and Google Ads. The original Nano Banana becomes a legacy model.

The same post introduced Gemini Omni Flash for video — $0.10 per second of output (on par with Veo 3.1 Fast), currently capped at 10-second clips.

Why it matters: The news isn't quality — it's price. At three cents per thousand images, the math flips for anyone who needs images at scale — product catalogs, variant rendering, marketing assets. What was a photo shoot or a per-image SaaS subscription last year becomes a rounding error on the cloud bill. That's the concrete basis for the second twiceD idea below.

Microsoft builds a company for deployment alone [7]

On July 2, Microsoft launched the "Frontier Company": a $2.5 billion commitment and roughly 6,000 industry and engineering experts whose sole job is to actually get enterprise AI running at the customer. Cited references include London Stock Exchange Group, Unilever, Land O'Lakes, and Accenture. Commercial chief Judson Althoff calls it "the industry's largest, most capable, outcomes-focused engineering organization" — moving past the term "forward-deployed engineering."

My take: This is the real signal of the week. The big providers are implicitly admitting the model was never the problem — the problem is the last mile at the customer. ctx According to web-search summaries (not primarily confirmed), Microsoft, OpenAI, and Anthropic together plan to pour roughly $8 billion into such "deployment companies" in 2026, Amazon committed $1 billion two days earlier, and OpenAI's deployment arm reportedly acquired Northslope. Only the Microsoft figure is solidly sourced; I'm treating the rest as context. The bottom line stands: deployment is the new battlefield — and that's exactly where a solo shop with a hands-on focus plays.

Two studies, one pattern: broad adoption, shallow transformation [8][9]

The German figure first: according to the Bitkom AI Study 2026, 41% of firms with 20+ employees actively use AI — up from 17% in 2024. Another 48% are planning or discussing it; large companies (500+) sit above 60%. Honest about the downside: 33% find AI more expensive than expected, 19% say they've already cut jobs because of AI.

Methodology flag Bitkom: CATI phone interviews with 604 companies, published Q1/Q2 2026 (publication date is inconsistent across sources). "Active use" is self-reported — it says nothing about depth. Deloitte: 3,235 executives, 24 countries, fieldwork Aug–Sep 2025 (2026 report) — meaning the figures are already three quarters old.

Deloitte's "State of AI in the Enterprise" 2026 sets up the contrast: 66% report productivity/efficiency gains, but only 30% are actually redesigning core processes around AI, and of the 23% using agents at least moderately, only 21% have mature agent governance.

My take: The gap between "we report gains" and "we redesigned our processes" is the whole story. The vast majority of companies are bolting AI onto the edge of existing workflows instead of rethinking them. That's not a criticism — it's the market opportunity. Whoever delivers the last mile (process redesign, governance) addresses exactly what 70% haven't done yet.

Project ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · AI Act readiness package for SMEs

ELI5 & value: Any SME using AI will soon have to prove it operates it in compliance — which systems carry how much risk, who oversees them, how AI content gets marked. The deadline for the toughest obligations was just pushed to Dec 2027 (story above), but watermarking (Dec 2026) and transparency bite sooner. Value: a fixed "readiness package" — an inventory of AI usage, risk classification, a lean evidence folder, and a watermarking checklist — takes the fear of an audit off the customer's shoulders without requiring them to buy an enterprise suite. Classic consulting work with a clear trigger.
Prototype (V0): (1) A questionnaire + workshop that lists a customer's actually-used AI systems. (2) Classification per system by AI Act risk class. (3) A ready-made German evidence template (purpose, data sources, oversight, logging obligations) — filled in per system. (4) A watermarking/transparency check for generative use. MVP deliberately narrow: consulting + templates, no hosted compliance platform. Building blocks: the official AI Act texts as the basis, an EU-hosted LLM (e.g. via Langdock/DeutschlandGPT) for drafting documents — none of it built in-house.
Market & gap: Mature enterprise GRC vendors already exist — OneTrust, Holistic AI, Credo AI — but according to market overviews, they run "€30k–100k+/year and a sales call."[10] At the SME end, German EU-hosting offerings like CompanyGPT, Langdock, and DeutschlandGPT compete for the same customers.[10] ctx One report-mill estimate (single source, no visible methodology — treat with caution) puts the "EU AI Act Compliance" market at $609.4M in 2026 with a 37.3% CAGR.[11] The gap: the GRC suites are horizontal platforms the customer has to piece together themselves, and they're too expensive for SMEs. Nobody delivers the last mile — the concrete fit into one German business, hands-on, in German contract/document language. GRC = "Governance, Risk & Compliance": the software category where companies prove at the push of a button that they follow the rules.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. This is advisory + templates, not a hosted platform — the maintenance load is essentially zero, each customer is a self-contained project. V0 solo in 3–4 weeks (questionnaire + template set), scaling: one contractor per additional customer. Sovereignty lever: an EU/on-prem model instead of US SaaS is the selling point for many German customers. kla.digital · dimensionmarketresearch.com

2 · Product-photo pipeline for e-commerce SMBs at sub-cent image costs

ELI5 & value: Small online shops need clean, brand-consistent photos for every product — cutouts, variants, lifestyle scenes. Traditionally that means a photo shoot or a per-image SaaS subscription. With Nano Banana 2 Lite (story above), a generated image costs a fraction of a cent. Value: a shop with 500 items gets a complete, consistent catalog for a few euros in compute cost instead of a four-figure shoot budget — and can churn out variants at the push of a button.
Prototype (V0): (1) A real shop's product feed (Shopware/Shopify export) as a test set. (2) Cutouts + on-brand rendering via Nano Banana 2 Lite / the Gemini API as the backend. (3) Consistency rules (background, lighting, aspect ratio) as a reusable prompt/style template. (4) Writing the finished images back into the shop. MVP deliberately narrow: one shop, one product type, human approval before upload. Building blocks: the Gemini API (image backend), a cutout model, the shop's import API — none of it built in-house.
Market & gap: The space is crowded: Claid is considered the strongest platform for large catalogs (500+ SKUs, batch, on-model mode), Photoroom dominates mobile cutouts, Pebblely and Flair are entry-level (overviews note they show "limits beyond 50 SKUs"), Nightjar focuses on catalog consistency.[12][13] Concrete backend costs are demonstrably low today: $0.034 per 1,000 images with Nano Banana 2 Lite.[5] The gap: all of them are horizontal self-service SaaS with a per-image/monthly subscription. What's missing for one specific German/EU shop is the last mile — the fit into its feed pipeline, EU hosting, no per-image lock-in when model costs are already sub-cent anyway.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. Honestly: a hosted pipeline carries real ongoing load (model calls, monitoring, error images) — unlike idea 1, this isn't a one-off project. But the load is bounded and billable per customer; a solo shop plus an occasional contractor can carry a handful of clients. V0 in 4–6 weeks. It doesn't scale to Shopify App Store size — but it doesn't need to: a small number of well-tended German shops is a viable offering on its own. claid.ai · nightjar.so
