The rationing wave: big companies turn off the AI tap [1]

Within a few weeks, four major companies independently made the same move — they restricted internal AI access. Citi temporarily blocked access to newer models from Anthropic and OpenAI and now recommends instead which model to use for which task (Citi disputes this to 404 Media). Adobe ended unlimited internal use at the end of June — employees were told to "get done whatever you can" beforehand. Amazon introduced token limits shortly after shutting down an internal usage leaderboard: "Crazy, we're going from 'no more leaderboard' to actual usage limits within two weeks," 404 Media quotes from an internal Slack. At Atlassian sits the number that explains everything: after the caps came off, the internal AI bill climbed to $120 million per year — up from a baseline of $5 million.

Source contradiction heise's summary of the 404 Media investigation says costs "tripled — to $120 million annually, from a $5 million baseline." That doesn't add up: 5 → 120 is a factor of 24, not 3. Either the tripling refers to a different time span than the baseline, or the summary is over-compressed. I'm reporting the figures as published — the direction is solid, the multiplier is not.

The most interesting finding is in the fine print: according to an Accenture observation cited in the same report, it isn't technical applications driving the costs, but non-technical staff burning tokens on everyday tasks — like turning PDFs into slide decks. The world's most expensive model, put to work on formatting.

Why the bill keeps rising even as the price falls [2][3]

The rationing seems illogical. Isn't AI constantly getting cheaper? It is — dramatically. Gartner forecasts (Mar 25, 2026) that by 2030, running inference on a model with one trillion parameters will cost over 90% less than in 2025. And in the same breath, Gartner delivers the rest: "Because token consumption is rising faster than token costs are falling, total inference costs are expected to increase." Then the second uncomfortable line: the lower token costs are not being fully passed on to enterprise customers.

The Jevons paradox: When LEDs replaced light bulbs, an hour of light suddenly cost a fraction of what it used to. Result: people didn't spend less on light — they turned it on everywhere. The bill didn't drop, it rose. The exact same thing is happening with AI: when a query costs almost nothing, nobody stops to ask whether it's necessary. Unit price down, total bill up. Atlassian's jump isn't an outlier — it's the textbook case.
Token price falls → Caps come off, everyone can use everything → Consumption grows faster than the price falls → Bill rises anyway → Company rations by hand

How much cheaper would the right model be? A Forbes analysis (Jun 25, 2026) puts task-specific small models, for narrowly scoped tasks, at 161x cheaper than Claude, 89x than GPT, and 29x than Gemini — concretely: 10,000 summaries a day for $7.20 instead of $58 with GPT-4.1 Mini.

Methodology flag These figures come from benchmarks by ScaleDown AI — the very vendor whose business model is task-specific small models. A vendor benchmark showing that the vendor's own products win isn't neutral evidence: there's no independent reproduction, and we don't see the task selection or the tuning effort hiding behind the "161x." Useful as a directional signal, not as a basis for a proposal.

My take: Rationing by decree — "you're no longer allowed to use the good model" — is the crudest answer imaginable. It punishes the productive users just as hard as the PDF-to-slides crowd. The grown-up answer isn't less AI, it's allocated AI: the cheapest model that still gets the job done, per task. But somebody has to measure what "still gets the job done" means — against the actual workload, not the small-model vendor's brochure. That measurement is exactly where the consulting product lives.

Germany: the Bundesnetzagentur becomes AI oversight [4][5]

Germany finished its AI oversight structure this week. The Bundesrat approved the AI Market Surveillance and Innovation Promotion Act (KI-MIG) on July 10, 2026; according to the federal government, "the law can now be executed and promulgated. It takes effect the day after promulgation." From August 2, 2026, the Bundesnetzagentur is thus Germany's central market-surveillance authority for AI.

Provider vs. deployer: The key distinction in EU law. A provider is whoever builds an AI system and puts it on the market. A deployer is whoever uses it in their own operation. If you just buy someone else's model and use it in your own process, you're a deployer — and you still have obligations of your own. That's the role almost every Mittelstand company is in. And a market-surveillance authority is the body that checks all this and can pull a product from the market if needed: the inspector checks your car, but the authority behind it can recall an entire model.

The BNetzA coordinates, but it doesn't consolidate: BaFin (financial services), BSI (critical-infrastructure cybersecurity), BfDI (data protection/biometrics), and BfArM (medical devices) keep their own turf — companies still have to deal directly with the relevant sector regulator. For deployers, a compliance review lists, among other things, reporting serious incidents within 15 days (Art. 73 AI Act), fundamental-rights impact assessments for certain critical services, and registering high-risk systems where applicable.

My take: This didn't become the single point of contact many hoped for — it's a map with a switchboard in the middle. For an 80-person company running an AI assistant in customer service, the real question isn't "which authority?" but "am I a deployer, and what do I owe as one?" No piece of legislation spells out that answer neatly — and that's exactly why it's advisable business.

EU: the delay that isn't binding yet [6][7]

In parallel, Europe's regulatory relief is playing out — and it's being thoroughly misread right now. The Digital Omnibus (political agreement in early May 2026, Council confirmation May 13) pushes the high-risk deadlines back significantly: standalone Annex III systems — recruiting, credit scoring, law enforcement, education, border control — now only need to comply by December 2, 2027 instead of August 2, 2026. Product-embedded Annex I systems slip to August 2, 2028.

Annex III / high-risk: The AI Act sorts systems by potential for harm, not by technology. Annex III lists the use cases where a mistake seriously affects a person — who gets the job, who gets the loan, who gets across the border. Analogy: it's not the knife that's regulated, it's whether you're using it to cut bread or perform surgery.

What is not being delayed is the part that affects most companies: the Art. 50 transparency duties for AI-generated content still kick in on August 2, 2026 as planned (with a proposed four-month grace period for existing systems), and from that same date the Commission and the AI Office get full enforcement power over GPAI models — with fines of up to €15 million or 3% of worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.

Important caveat The delay is not yet binding. Law firm Gibson Dunn's analysis (May 27, 2026) is unambiguous: the new dates only take effect once the Omnibus is published in the Official Journal. According to later reporting, Parliament approved it on June 16 and the Council gave final green light on June 29; it takes effect on the third day after publication, expected before August 2, 2026. I could not confirm today that this last step has actually happened. Consequence: anyone locking in December 2, 2027 today is planning against a legal status that is formally still pending.

My take: Everywhere the headline read "high-risk rules delayed," and it was read as a general all-clear. It's the opposite. What got delayed is the obligation that hits few companies. What stays is the one that hits almost everyone: disclosing that something came from an AI. Anyone running a customer-facing AI chatbot or AI-written text has a labeling duty in 16 days, not a delay — the least spectacular, most overlooked deadline of the summer.

Adoption: 88 percent use it, almost nobody with agents [10][11][12]

The research landscape is strikingly contradictory — and the contradiction is the real finding. Stanford HAI reports in the AI Index 2026: 88% of surveyed organizations use AI in at least one business function, 70% use generative AI — but the use of AI agents stays "in the single digits across nearly all business functions." Almost everyone has AI. Almost nobody lets it actually work.

The German view, from SAP/Oxford Economics (2,600 executives across 13 countries, 200 of them German decision-makers): German firms expect 24% ROI for 2026, up from 17% the year before. Only 4% consider themselves "fully prepared" for AI agents, and 57% "still have no process in place that builds human oversight into agentic workflows." The KI-Mittelstandsindex 2026 (Salesforce + Deutscher Mittelstands-Bund, Mar 9, 2026) fits the picture: agent deployment sits at 16.6% — doubled year-over-year, but off a small base.

What tips the scale? An Info-Tech Research Group survey (n=551 senior strategy leaders, June 2026): organizations with a dedicated, governed AI strategy report measurable impact three times as often — 60% vs. 20%. A side finding for twiceD: 96% of IT leaders expect rising AI budgets — and 80% would rather buy than build.

Methodology flag Stanford HAI discloses no sample size and no methodology on the page as retrieved — 88% and 70% are HAI's own metrics, not independently reproducible survey internals. SAP/Oxford Economics is a study commissioned by the software vendor: finding that "returns rise, oversight lags" happens to align with an interest in selling governance software; the KI-Mittelstandsindex is co-sponsored by Salesforce — the same pattern. In all three, "measurable impact" is self-reported, not an audited business-figure effect.

My take: These numbers tell exactly the same story as the cost piece above. 88% adoption against single-digit agent deployment means, in plain terms: most enterprise AI is a chat window with a human sitting there typing. Exactly the kind of consumption Accenture flagged as the cost driver — lots of people, lots of ad-hoc requests, no process. And 24% expected ROI against 4% preparedness isn't a maturity number, it's a hope number.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · Best-fit model router for the Mittelstand

ELI5 & value: Nearly every company today sends every AI request to the same expensive flagship model — the email summary just like the contract analysis. That's like sending every letter by courier because the courier can also move pianos. A router is the mailroom in front of it: it routes each task to the cheapest model that still handles it cleanly. Value: Atlassian's bill jumped to $120 million/year once the usage caps came off,[1] and Gartner says the total bill keeps rising even as the token price falls by over 90%.[2] For a Mittelstand company with a €3,000–8,000 monthly AI bill, 30–50% savings is real money — and the application doesn't change, because the router sits in front of it.
Prototype (V0): (1) Pull four weeks of real LLM traffic from the logs and sort it into 3–4 task classes (summarizing, classifying, extracting, free text). (2) Put an OpenAI-compatible proxy in front — LiteLLM as the open-source gateway, nothing built from scratch. (3) Build a golden set of real cases per class, plus a quality threshold. (4) Shadow mode: send traffic to the flagship and the candidate model in parallel, measure quality and cost — only switch live afterward. (5) Report: savings per class, quality gap, residual risk. Keep the MVP narrow: one customer, three classes, a fixed routing table instead of a learning router. Out of scope: auto-optimization, multi-tenancy, SLA.
Market & gap: The infrastructure is long since there, and it's good. OpenRouter claims on its own site 400+ models, 70+ providers, 100 trillion tokens a month, and 10 million+ users.[15] Add LiteLLM, Portkey, Not Diamond, Unify, TrueFoundry, and Cloudflare AI Gateway. The market for AI gateways is put by one market researcher at $4.31 billion (2025), projected to $11.86 billion by 2032 at 15.53% CAGR (vendor estimate, read accordingly).[14] What none of these vendors do: make the decision. They deliver the pipe and the dashboard — which model is good enough for which task, and the proof that quality doesn't slip, stays homework for the customer. That's not infrastructure work, it's domain and measurement work. Second gap: these are mostly US-hosted SaaS services that every prompt passes through — a tough sell to a Mittelstand company that, from Aug 2, has BNetzA oversight looking over its shoulder.[4] And the small-model numbers (161x cheaper) come from the small-model vendor itself[3] — nobody measures that against the customer's actual workload. That's exactly the product: not the router, but the proof.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High as an audit, medium as an operated service — the difference is what matters. The audit version ("model cost assessment": analyze the logs, shadow measurement, report with quantified savings, routing table for the customer to run themselves) is a 3–4-week solo project; the gateway is open source, the work is in the golden set — maintenance load is practically zero, with a light retainer whenever the next model switch comes around. That's the right entry point. The hosted version is honestly medium: the router sits on the critical path — router down means AI down for the customer, so on-call duty and provider outages become your problem. Workable solo, but only with a small number of customers and a limited SLA. On-prem is the sovereignty lever: self-hosted LiteLLM plus open models for the cheap classes — no prompt ever leaves the building, something the US SaaS competitors structurally can't offer. Scaling: one contractor per customer, no standing team. openrouter.ai · 360iresearch.com · heise.de
