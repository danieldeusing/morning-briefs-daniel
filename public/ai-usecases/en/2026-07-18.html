Bonsai 27B: the re-measurement that corrects the sales pitch [1][2][3]

On 2026-07-14, PrismML released Bonsai 27B — a 27-billion-parameter model built on Qwen3.6, compressed so hard that the 1-bit variant fits an iPhone 17 Pro at 3.9 GB, and the ternary variant fits a regular laptop at a nominal 5.9 GB. Apache 2.0 license, 262,144-token context, weights open on Hugging Face and GitHub.[1] Remarkable enough on its own: a multimodal model of this size with no cloud, no API bill, no data leaving the device.

Quantization (1-bit / ternary): An AI model consists of millions of numbers, normally stored at 16 bits each — very precise, very large. Quantization rounds them more coarsely: ternary means "only −1, 0, or +1," 1-bit means "only two states." Like a photo going from RAW to JPEG: same picture, but details get lost — and which ones only becomes clear on close inspection.

Someone who looked closely was wavect.io. The vendor advertises: the ternary variant retains 95%, the 1-bit variant 90%, of the uncompressed model's performance.[1] Recalculated, it's 94.6% and 89.5% — not a scandal, but not what's on the box either. The more important line from the review: it retains 89.5% of a vendor-reported benchmark average, not 90% of every capability.[2]

Bonsai 27B (1-bit): performance retained per category versus the uncompressed model — vendor claim, blanket "90%". Own calculation from wavect.io's measurements, as of 2026-07-16.

This is where an average becomes dangerous. Math barely loses ground (95.3 → 91.7 points), coding holds up decently (88.7 → 81.9). But tool use falls from 80.0 to 66.0 and image understanding from 72.6 to 59.6.[2] Tool use is the ability to call a function in a structured way, read the result, and plan the next step — exactly what every agentic workflow rests on. Use Bonsai locally for writing or summarizing, and you get almost the full model. Use it as the engine of an automation chain, and you get a noticeably weaker one.

There's also a number missing from every launch announcement: the actual ternary deployment occupies roughly 7.2 GB, not the 5.9 GB shown in the idealized figure.[2] On a 16 GB laptop, that's the difference between running smoothly and swapping to disk. Speed isn't the bottleneck (up to 163 tok/s on an RTX 5090, 11 tok/s on an iPhone 17 Pro Max[1][2]); Decrypt got a working small game out of it after two iteration rounds, though single prompts didn't come close to cloud models.[3]

My take This isn't an anti-Bonsai story — the model is impressive, and the open license makes it interesting for sovereign deployments. It's an anti-average story. "Retains 90% of performance" is the new version of "up to 100 Mbit/s": technically defensible, practically misleading. The only number that matters is the hit rate on your own tasks — and that's exactly what the reviewers recommend: pin the stack, measure peak memory, test real tasks, compare cost per successful outcome.[2]

The coding-model side of this story — local toolchains, agent harnesses, benchmarks — runs in the ai-dev brief; here the use case is what counts.

  2. wavect.io — independent re-measurement: 89.5% / 94.6%; category values math 95.33→91.66, coding 88.74→81.88, knowledge 83.15→73.39, tool use 80.00→66.03, vision 72.61→59.57; "the actual deployment comes in at about 7.2 GB"; 11 tok/s on iPhone 17 Pro Max.

ChatGPT Work: nine days old, zero independent tests [4][5][6]

On 2026-07-09 OpenAI launched the GPT-5.6 model family and, on top of it, ChatGPT Work — not a chatbot, but an agent for longer tasks: researching, analyzing, working across connected apps and files, and delivering finished documents, spreadsheets, presentations, and websites. It connects to Slack, Microsoft Teams, Gmail, Google Drive, SharePoint, and Salesforce; a "picture-in-picture" view lets you watch the agent click, type, and move files locally.[4]

The rollout is staggered: web and mobile first for Pro, Enterprise, and Edu, then Plus and Business; in the desktop app, Chat, Work, and Codex run worldwide on every plan, including the free one.[4] API prices per million tokens: Sol $5 / $30, Terra $2.50 / $15, Luna $1 / $6.[5]

Important for admins Enterprise and Edu workspaces run a two-week preview during which Work is off by default. At the end of that preview, it switches on automatically — administrators have to actively object beforehand.[4] Started on 07/09, that means by the calendar: around 07/23, every affected organization will have an agent active with access to mail, Drive, SharePoint, and CRM, without anyone having said "yes." That's a governance decision made by the calendar.

And now the caveat that puts all of this in context: no reviewed outlet has so far published its own hands-on results for ChatGPT Work. Coverage in TechCrunch, Forbes, InfoWorld, 9to5Mac, and testingcatalog relays OpenAI's own account; the only older "hands-on" pieces found date from July 2025 and cover the identically named but different product, "ChatGPT Agent." Everything above is announced functionality, not verified. Even the performance claim of "54% more token-efficient at agentic coding" comes from the OpenAI CEO speaking to CNBC.[6] One widely cited score comparison against a competing model could not be traced to any openable primary source — it deliberately does not appear in this brief.

My take Bonsai and ChatGPT Work are the same story from two sides. With Bonsai, someone re-measured, and the pitch lost three percentage points and a gigabyte and a half. With ChatGPT Work, nobody has re-measured — and it's the product that, in a few days, gets let into corporate mailboxes without being asked. The preview isn't a marketing window, it's a testing window: whoever tests nothing before 07/23 tests it in production afterward.

EU AI Act: what was not delayed [7][8]

Update since yesterday The postponement itself was in yesterday's brief; today the addition that was missing there. Confirmed: the European Parliament finally approved the amendments on 2026-06-16; obligations for stand-alone high-risk systems under Annex III move from 2026-08-02 to 2027-12-02 — 16 months of breathing room.[7]

What was not postponed, however, are two new bans: using AI systems to generate or manipulate non-consensual intimate material and depictions of child sexual abuse becomes prohibited as of 2026-12-02.[8] The Council gave final approval to the simplification package on 2026-06-29; only publication in the Official Journal remains outstanding, expected by 2026-08-02.[8]

Source trap The page everyone links to as the "official" deadlines overview — artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline — carries a last-updated date of 2024-08-01 and simply doesn't show the 2026-06-16 change. Anyone reading compliance dates off it today is planning on a two-year-old legal state. For current deadlines, only the law-firm trackers are currently reliable.

Document AI consolidates: Coupa swallows Rossum [9][10][11]

On 2026-05-12, Coupa — the platform for "autonomous spend management" — acquired document-AI vendor Rossum; the purchase price was not disclosed in any of the sources reviewed.[9] Rossum was the best-known independent vendor in the field, and attractive to mid-market companies precisely in that role: a tool you could buy without also buying the corporate suite behind it. That role is now gone.

IDP (Intelligent Document Processing): Software that reads unstructured inputs — PDF invoices, order emails, delivery notes, scans — extracts the relevant fields, and writes them cleanly into the ERP. Like a very patient clerk typing up stacks of paper: the value is that nobody has to type anymore.

The market behind this is growing fast: from $3.22B (2025) to $4.31B (2026), with a projected compound annual growth rate of 33.68% through 2034.[10] What's left sorts into two camps: enterprise platforms like Hyperscience, aimed at large corporations and public administration, and sovereignty-focused vendors like Germany's Konfuzio (Helm & Nagel GmbH, founded 2016), which runs on-premise via Kubernetes/Helm with German data hosting.[11]

My take: consolidation amid 33% growth doesn't mean saturation — it means buyers want bundles. For small vendors, that's a gap, not a drawback: every mid-market company that specifically did not want Rossum as part of a procurement suite is now looking for an alternative. See project idea 2.

McKinsey: maturity is rising, the bar stays low [12][13]

McKinsey's "State of AI Trust 2026" reports an average responsible-AI maturity score of 2.3 — up from 2.0 the year before. Only about one-third of organizations reach a 3 or higher across strategy, governance, and agent governance.[12]

Methodology Roughly 500 organizations, surveyed between December 2025 and January 2026 — the figures are a good half-year old. Respondents were people with direct responsibility for AI governance, risk, or investment; it's self-reported, not an audited maturity score. Whoever grades their own governance grades generously — and the consultancy that asks the question sells the fix.[12]

Set against that, the expectation driving everyone: Gartner projects that by the end of 2026, 40% of enterprise applications will feature task-specific AI agents — up from under 5% in 2025.[13] The gap is the interesting part: agents arrive by product update, while control over them grows by 0.3 points a year. That gap is exactly where ChatGPT Work's auto-activation on 07/23 sits.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and an honest solo-feasibility read

1 · Local-model fitness audit ("does this thing hold up on my task?")

ELI5 & value: A customer reads "runs on the laptop and retains 90% of performance," pencils in the saved cloud costs and full data sovereignty — and three months later discovers that exactly the steps they wanted to automate are the ones that fail. twiceD sells the measurement beforehand: a two-week audit on the customer's real documents and workflows that determines which compression tier still holds their hit rate and what the operation actually needs in memory. Value: an avoided bad investment plus a solid number for the make-or-buy decision.
Prototype (V0): (1) 30–50 real customer tasks as a test set with a clear success criterion — not benchmark questions, but "was the invoice number recognized correctly." (2) Run the same tasks against three tiers: cloud model as the ceiling, ternary and 1-bit local model. (3) Log peak memory and latency on the target hardware, not the developer machine. (4) Calculate cost per successful outcome, not per token. (5) Report with a clear recommendation. Deliberately out of scope: operations, hosting, training. Building blocks: Bonsai 27B (Apache 2.0, via Hugging Face or Together.ai), local runners, a custom evaluation script.
Market & gap: Public benchmarks measure the wrong thing — Bonsai's vendor claim of 90% just barely holds up on re-measurement on average (89.5%), but that hides the collapse in tool use to 66.0 and vision to 59.6 points, and the real deployment size comes in at 7.2 instead of 5.9 GB.[14] The very reviewers who found this write, as a recommendation, exactly what nobody sells as a service today: pin the stack, measure peak memory, test real tasks, compare cost per success.[14] The governance vendors nearby — Credo AI with AI Act rule packs, Holistic AI with fairness and robustness tests — check compliance, not fitness for task X.[15] The gap for twiceD: the small, vendor-neutral pre-purchase test. Demand is there: only about a third of organizations reach governance maturity 3+, while Gartner expects 40% agent-equipped enterprise apps by the end of 2026.[16][17]
Feasibility (solo + AI): High. A pure consulting product: brief in, report out, zero ongoing maintenance afterward — no hosting, no on-call, no customer data in continuous operation. V0 solo in 2–3 weeks, then 1–2 weeks of effort per customer. The test-set scaffolding becomes reusable from customer to customer, improving margin with every engagement. Scaling comes from a second reviewer, not a department. The honest catch: it's a one-time engagement per customer — it works as a door-opener for idea 2, not as a sole revenue base.

2 · Sovereign document agent for mid-market firms (after the Rossum exit)

ELI5 & value: At many mid-market companies, orders, invoices, and delivery notices arrive as PDFs or email and get typed into the ERP by hand. A document agent reads them, checks them against master data, and files them as a clean record — on the company's own server, without a single sheet leaving the building. Value: saved clerical time, fewer typos, and, for customers with sensitive data, a data setup they can explain in an audit.
Prototype (V0): (1) One document type for a single customer, 200 real documents as a test set. (2) Extraction via an existing local multimodal model rather than building from scratch — at the tier idea 1 has measured as viable. (3) Validation against master data: anything not unambiguous goes into a review queue instead of the ERP. (4) A write adapter for a target system. (5) A human confirms before posting. Deliberately out of scope: multi-tenancy, additional document types, fully automatic posting. Building blocks: open model weights (Apache 2.0), Kubernetes/Helm for on-prem, the customer's ERP interface.
Market & gap: The market is large and growing fast — $4.31B in 2026, a projected 33.68% a year through 2034.[18] What matters is the movement inside it: Rossum, the best-known independent vendor, has been part of Coupa's spend-management suite since 2026-05-12.[19] Anyone who wanted a tool and not a platform now has one fewer option. What's left is Hyperscience, focused on large enterprises and the public sector,[20] and, as Germany's sovereignty-minded answer, Konfuzio with Helm/Kubernetes on-prem and German data hosting.[21] Konfuzio is a good product — and precisely because of that, the gap isn't "one more platform" but the last mile: fitting a concrete process, the ERP connection, the customer's own test set, operation without a per-seat subscription. Platforms sell capabilities; mid-market companies buy a solved problem.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. Honestly: this is operations, not a report. An on-prem agent needs model updates, monitoring, and a point of contact for when the queue is full in the morning — realistically 4–8 hours per week per customer in the first quarter, then 2–4, as long as the scope stays to one document type. Only viable with discipline: identical delivery for every customer, extensions sold as a paid project, at most three or four customers in parallel solo, then a contractor per additional customer. The hard part — the model — is bought, not built; that's what makes the idea viable at all. Idea 1 is the entry point: measure first, then build.

It's Saturday, July 18th. Today's brief comes down to a single question: who actually checked the numbers? Two of this week's big AI announcements are still known to us only through their vendors' own press releases. For one of them, somebody did check — and the result is instructive.

PrismML released a model called Bonsai 27B on July 14th. It's compressed so heavily it fits on a phone, and it ships under an open license. The vendor advertises that the more heavily compressed version retains ninety percent of performance. An independent review by wavect puts it at eighty-nine point five percent. That's not a scandal.

The real story is hiding in the average. Those ninety points are a mean across many capabilities. Break it down by category, and the model holds over ninety-six percent on math — but only eighty-two point five percent on tool use, and eighty-two percent flat on image understanding. Tool use is exactly the capability every automation depends on. Use the model for writing, and you get almost everything. Use it as the engine of a process chain, and you get noticeably less.

Then there's a number that appears in no announcement at all. The actual installation takes up about seven point two gigabytes, not the five point nine everyone's been quoting. On a laptop with sixteen gigabytes of memory, that difference decides whether the thing runs smoothly or not.

The second story is ChatGPT Work. OpenAI launched it on July ninth, alongside the GPT five point six model family. It's not a chatbot — it's an agent that works across Slack, Teams, Gmail, Google Drive, SharePoint, and Salesforce, and delivers finished documents, spreadsheets, and presentations. That sounds like a lot. Here's the catch: nine days after launch, no reviewed outlet has published its own hands-on results yet. What's out there is either OpenAI's own account relayed by the press, or older reports from July twenty twenty-five about a different product with the same name, ChatGPT Agent.

One detail here matters for every administrator. Business and education accounts are running a two-week preview during which the feature is switched off. At the end of that preview, it turns itself on automatically unless someone actively objects. Launched on July ninth, that means around July twenty-third, every affected organization will have an agent active with access to mailboxes, file storage, and customer databases — without anyone having agreed to it.

From Europe, there's a follow-up to yesterday's postponement story. Confirmed: Parliament approved it on June sixteenth, and the high-risk obligations now move to December second, twenty twenty-seven. Two new bans were not delayed — using AI to generate non-consensual intimate images and depictions of abuse becomes illegal as of December second of this year. And a warning: the page everyone links to as the official deadline overview still carries an August twenty twenty-four timestamp and doesn't show the change at all.

In the document-AI market, there was also a move that matters for twiceD. Coupa acquired the vendor Rossum on May twelfth; nobody disclosed the purchase price. That means the market just lost its best-known independent vendor to a large procurement platform, even as it keeps growing at more than thirty percent a year.

Out of that come today's two project ideas. First, a fitness audit for local models: measure whether a compressed model really holds up on a customer's actual tasks before they bet on it. Pure consulting work, no maintenance burden, high feasibility. Second, a sovereign document agent for mid-market companies, aimed squarely at the customers who wanted a tool, not a corporate suite. That's operations rather than a report, so feasibility is medium — viable only with a tight scope.

Two things sit on the calendar for the coming days. Around July twenty-third, the silent activation at OpenAI's enterprise customers wraps up, and the first independent hands-on reports should start to appear.
