The Number That Shifts the Beat [1][2]

On July 7, 2026, Anthropic opened Cowork on web and mobile and, in doing so, published something vendors rarely release: an analysis of what their tool is actually used for. The result, in the original wording: "More than 90% of Cowork usage involves non-software development tasks, the largest categories are business operations and content creation — together about half of total usage."[1]

Agentic tool: A normal chatbot answers — an agent does. It can open files, plan steps, check intermediate results, and deliver a finished artifact at the end instead of just producing text. It's like the difference between a colleague who explains how to build a spreadsheet and one who puts the finished spreadsheet on your desk. For the user, that means it's no longer "ask the right question," but "hand off the right task."

The sample is unusually solid: 1.2 million anonymized sessions from more than 600,000 organizations, collected over the last two weeks of May 2026. The breakdown that TechCrunch cites from the same data is the real story: business processes 33.4%, content creation 16.4% — software development 8.7%.[2]

Cowork usage by category — the three categories named by Anthropic (the rest isn't broken out and is deliberately absent here). Sample: 1.2M sessions, 600,000+ organizations, last two weeks of May 2026.

My take: The percentages are interesting, but the sales pitch is in the denominator. These aren't survey intentions or pilot projects, but measured sessions from real accounts. When development slides to third place in a tool that was born as a coding assistant, SMEs are no longer buying "AI for the IT department" — they're buying a workforce for administration. The honest caveat: Anthropic has an incentive to show exactly this shift — the category definitions come from the vendor and aren't publicly verifiable.

The Example Anthropic Is Giving Away [1]

For "ordinary knowledge work," Anthropic's announcement page names exactly one image: "turning a folder of contracts into a renewal tracker, with flagged risks".[1] That sounds casual and it isn't. It's the use case with an entire, well-paid software category built around it — one that almost every SME today solves with Excel and calendar reminders.

Notice period / automatic renewal: Many B2B contracts silently renew for another year if nobody cancels in time — typically 60 to 90 days' notice. It's like a magazine subscription that keeps running because you missed the notice in January, just with four more zeros. The risk isn't legally complicated, it's purely organizational: the deadline sits on page 14 of a PDF nobody opens anymore.

Reports on Cowork usage describe the more advanced version: a command that sorts the renewal register by "last possible cancellation date" and outputs it as an Excel workbook, flagging notice periods, change-of-control clauses and time-limited audit rights along the way.[1] Anyone selling contract software today is no longer competing against a product, but against a folder and a prompt. Why that's still an opportunity is explained below — in short: the SME that needs this won't drag its contract folder into a chat window hosted abroad.

ChatGPT Work: Built for People Without a Dev Environment [3]

OpenAI launched ChatGPT Work on July 9, 2026 — per reports, an agent that "carries out tasks across different applications and files" and, in doing so, produces "finished documents, spreadsheets, presentations, reports and websites."[3] The rollout went first to Pro, Enterprise and Edu accounts, then staged to Plus and Business.[3]

From this beat's vantage point, the technology matters less than the target audience. "Finished spreadsheet" and "finished presentation" aren't developer promises; they're aimed at the purchasing clerk and the sales manager, neither of whom wants to operate a toolchain. It's the same movement as Cowork, just from the other side: both vendors are now targeting the same desk, and it's not IT's desk.

What a Named, Measured Deployment Is Worth [4]

The gold standard for this brief is customer stories with names and numbers. Google's enterprise agent platform delivers a whole list of those — and a single hard metric: expense-management vendor Payhawk "reduced expense submission time by over 50%" by equipping agents with persistent memory.[4] Other named deployments: PayPal, L'Oréal (beauty-tech agent platform), Comcast (Xfinity assistant), Color Health (breast-cancer screening assistant), Geotab, Burns & McDonnell.[4] As a scale indicator, Google states that more than six trillion tokens run through its agent toolkit every month.[4]

Methodology All of these figures are vendor-published, in a marketing post, with no stated baseline, no measurement period, and no independent verification. "−50% submission time" doesn't answer whether it took five minutes or fifty minutes before, let alone whether staffing costs ultimately fell. As evidence that "this is running in production at named companies," it holds up. As ROI evidence, it does not.

My take: Even so, this list is worth more than any adoption survey — a survey measures intentions, a named reference costs reputation if it's wrong. Anyone looking for patterns for their own offering shouldn't read it for the percentage, but for the process: at Payhawk, a highly repetitive approval workflow with clear rules — exactly the profile an 80-person company also has.

Regulatory Clock: What Actually Happens on August 2 [5]

Because a lot of half-knowledge will circulate over the next two weeks, here's the clean separation. On August 2, 2026, the transparency obligations under Article 50 (disclosing when people are talking with AI or seeing AI-generated content) and the sanctioning and market-surveillance powers take effect — as scheduled, they were not postponed.[5] At the same time, in Germany the Bundesnetzagentur becomes central market-surveillance authority (details ran in the July 17 brief).

What does not happen on August 2: the substantive obligations for high-risk systems under Annex III don't take effect until December 2, 2027 — postponed by 16 months through the Digital Omnibus package (political agreement May 6, 2026, confirmed in Council on May 13, 2026).[5]

Don't confuse these "An authority becomes responsible starting in August" does not mean "the high-risk rules apply starting in August." Jurisdiction and substantive obligation are two different clocks. An operator who panics in August and launches a compliance project is racing against a deadline that's actually 16 months out — and may well miss the transparency labeling that's genuinely due immediately.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · Contract Radar: The Deadline Watchdog for SMEs

ELI5 & value: Almost every company has 80 to 400 active contracts — software subscriptions, maintenance, leasing, cleaning, insurance — scattered across drives, mailboxes, and a filing cabinet. Nearly all of them renew silently if nobody cancels in time. Contract Radar reads that inventory in once and then delivers a single list: By when, at the latest, do I need to act on which contract, and for how much money? Plus a reminder before the deadline passes. The value is unromantic: a single overlooked maintenance contract worth €18,000 a year that renews unintentionally costs more than the whole project. Even bigger is the second benefit — whoever knows 90 days before expiration can negotiate; whoever finds out afterward just pays.
Prototype (V0): (1) One real contract folder from a single customer, 50–150 PDFs, as a test set — with a hand-maintained ground-truth table for term, notice period, and annual value, otherwise nothing is measurable. (2) Extraction via an existing multimodal model on exactly five fields: counterparty, start date, term, notice period, annual value — no full-text analysis, no clause evaluation. (3) Derivation of the one decisive field that appears in no contract: the last possible cancellation date. (4) Output as a sorted table plus a calendar file — that's deliberately the entire product in V0, no portal, no login, no permissions management. (5) A verification step where every data point is backed by a page number and anything uncertain goes into a query queue instead of silently being wrong. Deliberately out of scope: contract drafting, approval workflows, e-signature, negotiation assistance — everything that makes up an actual CLM system. Building blocks: open model weights for the on-prem variant, standard PDF processing, iCal for reminders.
Market & gap: The market exists and is growing steadily, but not explosively: contract lifecycle management software is projected by Fortune Business Insights to go from $2.07B (2026) to $5.09B (2034), 11.9% annually[6] — by comparison, the broader agent market is growing at 43.6% annually[9], so this is not a hype segment but a tough, established market. That's exactly what's good about it: tough markets are poorly served by the big players. The vendor list is long — Ironclad, Icertis, Sirion, DocuSign CLM, LinkSquares, Conga, Evisort, Agiloft, ContractPodAi (renamed "Leah" since January 2026) — and one competitor comparison describes them all in the same way: Ironclad "priced for enterprise, plus implementation, support and integration costs," Icertis "at a premium that makes it inaccessible," Evisort "may not fit smaller budgets."[7] In DACH, there's the sovereignty answer: otris offers its contract management either as cloud with a data center in Germany or on-premises[8], alongside d.velop, ELO, ContractHero. But these are grown document-management systems — you buy a platform and an implementation project, not an answer to a question. The gap: between "an Excel list nobody maintains" and "a CLM rollout for five-figure sums," there's nothing. And the third option now emerging — dragging a contract folder into a cloud agent tool, as Anthropic itself demonstrates[1] — fails with exactly the customers who matter here: the contract portfolio is a company's nervous system, supplier pricing and cancellation rights included. The SME that hesitates over data protection won't upload it. twiceD's offer, therefore, isn't "better CLM," it's the same output Anthropic demonstrates, just on the customer's premises and tailored to their portfolio — as a fixed-price project, not a per-user subscription.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High to medium — and the grading depends entirely on the delivery model. Framed as a project (read it in once, hand over the table plus calendar, an annual refresh engagement), it's highly feasible: V0 solo in 3–4 weeks, then 3–5 days per customer, virtually no ongoing maintenance load because nothing runs permanently. Framed as a service (a permanently monitoring on-prem agent), it slips to medium: model updates, availability, data access into the portfolio — realistically 2–4 hours per week per customer. Recommendation: start with the project variant, offer the service only where the customer explicitly asks for it. The risk that shouldn't be argued away: the extraction quality is bought, not built — if it becomes a free feature of every office suite in two years, the only remaining value is the fit and the customer's trust. Hence fixed price, and no subscription valuation in the business plan. Scale via one contractor per additional customer, never via a department.
