OpenAI Presence: the Agent Comes With an Installation Crew [1][2][3]

On July 22, 2026, OpenAI unveiled Presence — a platform that lets enterprises run production voice and chat agents for support, sales, and internal IT.[1] The real break isn't in the technology but in the sales channel: Presence is not a self-service product. It ships as a limited GA, rolled out through OpenAI's own "Forward Deployed Engineers" and select systems integrators, who identify each workflow individually, connect knowledge and systems, set permissions and policies, test, and bring it into production.[2]

AI agent: A regular chatbot answers. An agent acts — it checks the account balance itself, cancels the booking, closes the ticket. The difference is like an information desk versus a caseworker with access to the files.
Forward Deployed Engineer: Not a download, but a team that shows up and connects the agent to your systems — like a kitchen fitter who doesn't just deliver the cabinets but installs them on-site.

OpenAI is also touting a Codex-driven improvement loop: OpenAI's coding agent reviews the real conversations and suggests changes to the agent's behavior — but staff must test and approve them before they go live.[3] BBVA, SoftBank, and IAG are named as reference customers.

A number to take with caution The widely cited 75% without human intervention is OpenAI's own figure for its own English-language support hotline — a vendor metric on its own, well-mapped use case, not an independent benchmark.[3] The specific customer mapping (BBVA = banking support in Mexico, SoftBank = Japanese-language CX, IAG = demand spikes during severe weather) comes from secondary reporting; OpenAI's own pages blocked direct access (403), so this carries somewhat lower confidence than the rest.
My take The fact that the world's most expensive model provider doesn't sell its agent as a product but delivers it as a project is the most honest signal of the week: the last mile — connecting systems, setting permissions, testing, going live — is a service, not software. That exact mile, which OpenAI only walks for large enterprises via its Forward Deployed model, remains open for the Mittelstand. That becomes Idea 1 below.

Washington Calls China's Catch-Up Effort Theft — the Evidence Is Thinner [4][5][6][7]

OSTP Director Michael Kratsios (the White House's technology office) stated on X on July 23, 2026, that Moonshot AI had distilled Anthropic's model Fable to build its model Kimi K3, and had run "a sophisticated internal platform to conduct large-scale distillation against US models" to do so.[4] Moonshot is also alleged to have used banned Nvidia GB300 chips via intermediaries in Thailand.[5] Kimi K3 (released Jul 17) is a mixture-of-experts model with 2.8 trillion parameters that activates 16 of 896 experts per token.[5][6]

Distillation: A smaller or cheaper model learns by imitating the answers of a large, expensive model — like a student copying the class star's homework instead of working the problem out themselves. You save the expensive original training, but you need access to the reference model's answers.
Allegation, not fact The pushback carries real weight. Anthropic's Sarah Heck backs Kratsios and calls distillation "IP theft and industrial espionage."[4] The Chinese embassy called the allegations "entirely unfounded" in comments to Reuters.[4] And independent experts doubt the causality: fewer than three weeks separate Fable's release (early July) and K3 — "you can't distill that much data, train a model, and ship it in two weeks"; K3's own tech blog attributes the leap to architectural innovations (KDA, AttnRes, Stable LatentMoE, MXFP4/8 mixed precision).[7] Even OpenAI president Greg Brockman called K3 "pretty good" and said it's "too early" to judge whether any distillation happened at all.[6]
My take For Daniel's practice, what matters less is who's right than the operational question it raises: where does the model I'm putting into a client's process come from — and am I allowed to use it commercially? When model provenance becomes a geopolitical dispute, the origin and licensing question becomes part of every serious proposal.

Two Studies, One Gap: Heavy Adoption, Little Value Creation [10][11]

Germany, solid ground: Bitkom (a phone survey of 604 companies with 20+ employees, early 2026) reports that active AI usage has doubled to 41% within a year (2025: 17%). The uncomfortable numbers in the same dataset: 19% of AI-using firms have already cut jobs, 33% call AI more expensive than expected — while 77% report an improved competitive position and two-thirds plan to expand their use.[10]

Global, with caution: McKinsey's "State of AI" shows the same gap at scale — 88% use AI in at least one function, but only 39% report any EBIT impact and only 23% scale agents (62% are experimenting). Roughly 6% are "AI high performers" with over 5% EBIT from AI.[11]

EBIT: operating profit — what's left after costs but before interest and taxes. "EBIT impact from AI" means the AI shows up in the profit-and-loss statement, not just in a pilot project.

Project Ideas for twiceD

Researched proposals — with market gap and honest solo feasibility

1 · EU-Hosted, Hands-On Voice Agent for the Mittelstand (the Presence Gap)

ELI5 & value: Today, OpenAI delivers production support agents only to large enterprises — with its own engineering crew connecting everything. Nobody does that exact installation work for a 40–200-person business: it wants to hook its phone and chat support (appointment scheduling, status inquiries, simple complaints) up to AI, but has neither an enterprise budget nor any wish to put customer data on a US server. Value: saved case-handler time on the hotline and availability outside business hours — without a cloud-sovereignty conflict.
Prototype (V0): (1) One high-frequency call/chat type from the pilot customer as a test set (e.g., "Where's my delivery?"). (2) A voice stack built from existing components instead of from scratch: a voice-agent API (Retell/Vapi) for the pilot, and mid-term an EU-/self-hosted variant (open STT/TTS + open language model). (3) Connection to one target system (ERP/ticketing) for real answers. (4) Human handoff on uncertainty, a log for every interaction. Out of scope: outbound sales, multilingual support, complex case decisions.
Market & gap: The market is real and growing fast — enterprise agentic AI $5.3B → $24.5B by 2030 (46.2% CAGR, Grand View; search-snippet only, so order of magnitude)[12], the broader agent market $11.55B (2026) with ~43% CAGR (Precedence).[13] At the top sits OpenAI Presence — hands-on, but only for large enterprises and via Forward Deployed Engineers.[2] Below that, the self-serve platforms: Retell AI (~$0.07–0.15/min, no platform fee — the cheapest for pilots), Vapi (~$0.05/min fee, actual 10–13 T$/month for the full stack), Bland AI ($299–499/month + ~$0.11/min) — all developer-facing, US-hosted, with no EU/on-prem offering and no hands-on service.[14] The gap is the middle: Presence-level, hands-on service at Mittelstand scale, EU-hosted, with someone who installs it instead of handing over API docs.
Feasibility (solo + AI): Medium. In favor: the heavy lifting (model, voice engine) is bought in, twiceD builds the integration + handoff logic + operations. V0 per customer in 4–6 weeks solo. Against it — and this needs to be said honestly: this is an operated service, not a one-time project — latency, outages, model drift, and changing target systems create ongoing maintenance (realistically 4–8 hours/week per active agent). Scaling via one contractor per parallel customer. Sustainable as long as the scope stays "one call type, one target system"; the on-prem conversion is the sales lever, but also the effort driver. retellai.com · precedenceresearch.com · pymnts.com

2 · Agent Control Tower for the Mittelstand — Registry, Guardrails, Audit Log (On-Prem)

ELI5 & value: Once a company is running not one but five agents (support bot, invoicing agent, IT helper …), the question arises: which one is allowed to do what, who authorized it, and what did it actually do last week? A "control tower" is the registry plus the guardrails plus the unbroken log — like an access and change log for employees, just for AI agents. Value: you can put an agent into production without giving up control — and already have the evidence ready that the AI Act will require anyway starting 2027.
Prototype (V0): (1) Inventory of the pilot customer's running/planned agents (purpose, data access, action permissions). (2) A registry with an approval status per agent. (3) Guardrails as a policy layer in front of tool calls (which action needs human confirmation, which data may leave) — built on existing open-source guardrail libraries, not built from scratch. (4) An audit log of every agent action, stored tamper-proof. All on-prem/EU. Out of scope: automatic risk classification under Annex IV — that's the paperwork side (covered by yesterday's Idea 1).
Market & gap: The category exists among governance vendors: Credo AI offers exactly this — agent discovery, registration, risk gates, and runtime monitoring plus policy packs for AI Act/NIST/ISO 42001[15]; Holistic AI covers bias testing and audit trails and is considered more Mittelstand-friendly.[16] Both, however, are tailored to large enterprises, SaaS-first, and sales-gated. The gap is the fit: a lean, on-prem-installable control-tower set for a company with a handful of agents that doesn't want to put its logs in someone else's cloud — delivered by someone who installs it rather than licenses it.
Feasibility (solo + AI): High to medium. In favor: clearly scoped, runs on-prem, builds on open guardrail/logging components; a V0 toolkit takes one to two weeks solo, 2–4 weeks per customer. The registry/audit part is project work with low maintenance; the guardrail policies need upkeep per customer as new agents get added (a small operating contract). Honestly: the real pull only arrives with the 2027 AI Act deadline — today it's an early, well-prepared entry point, not a mass market. credo.ai · modulos.ai
