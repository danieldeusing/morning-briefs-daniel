What's happened since Monday — mechanism

Monday/Tuesday were still dominated by BRL weakness (USD/BRL up to 5.04). Today the vector has flipped, and the trigger is not Brazil but oil:

graph LR

  B --> C[Inflation fear drops]
  C --> D[US-yield pressure eases]
  C --> E[DXY softer 99.08]
  E --> F[EM carry bid stronger]
  F --> G[USD/BRL -0.6..1.1% to ~5.007]
  G --> H[Cross-check EUR/BRL ~5.808]

The key point: the real's move is imported. Brazil delivered no catalyst of its own today — it's the global risk-on reaction to cheaper oil and a softer dollar. That makes the move fragile: if the Iran narrative flips again (Trump recently threatened "another big hit"), the risk premium returns and the real gives it back.

Risk premium (in oil): The markup the market adds to the oil price purely for the fear that a supply could be cut — not because less oil is actually flowing today. Picture paying more for bread at the bakery because a strike that might halt tomorrow's delivery is looming. If the strike is called off, the surcharge drops instantly — exactly what's happening to Brent now.

FOMC minutes: Three weeks after each rate meeting, the Fed publishes a detailed transcript of the debate — who made which argument, without names on every statement. Picture the logbook after a heated faculty meeting: the grade is already set, but now you read up on how loudly they argued. For the market, that tone is exactly what counts: if the majority sounds more worried about inflation than expected, rate expectations jump immediately.

US rates & tape [USA]: The 2Y Treasury has jumped from 3.40% (end of February) to above 4.10%, the 10Y stands at 4.67% (05-19). The market has nearly priced out the cut path: fewer than 50% of economists still see a reduction by December (two-thirds a month ago). S&P 500 7,353.61 (−0.67%), third straight down day — the yield fear weighed. Today the oil crash could ease that fear. cnbc, 05-18

2-year Treasury yield: The interest on a US government bond with a two-year maturity — the market's mirror of what investors expect from the Fed over the next two years. When it rises (as now, from 3.4 to above 4.1%), the market is saying: "We no longer believe in near-term rate cuts." The short maturity reacts far more directly to the Fed than the 10-year paper.

It's Wednesday, May twentieth — and sentiment in the markets flipped overnight. The decisive trigger is oil. Brent has crashed five point four percent, to one hundred five dollars and twenty-four cents. The reason: talks between the US and Iran are showing progress, and President Trump called off a planned military strike at the request of the Gulf states. With that, the risk premium the market had recently piled onto every barrel is melting away.

And cheaper oil works like a chain link: less oil means less inflation fear, that takes pressure off the dollar, and a softer dollar gives emerging-market currencies like the Brazilian real room to breathe. That's exactly what we're seeing today.
