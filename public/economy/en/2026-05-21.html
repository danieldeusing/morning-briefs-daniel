What happened since yesterday — mechanism

The real lesson of the day: a hawkish central-bank signal need not weaken the real if a bigger driver is pulling at the same time. That's exactly what happened.

Hawk (hawkish): A central banker who sees inflation as the greatest danger and prefers to keep rates high or raise them — even if that brakes growth. The opposite is the "dove," who leans toward cutting to support the economy. Picture a guard dog that barks at the faintest sound of inflation: that's the hawk. Yesterday's minutes were full of it.

My read: The real's move is borrowed, not earned. Brazil delivered no catalyst of its own — it's the global risk-on reaction to cheap oil. That makes it fragile: the Iran relief is a letter of intent, not a signed deal, and Trump has walked back optimistic announcements before. If the narrative flips, the oil risk premium returns and the real gives way. That's why I don't yet treat the break below 5.00 as a new trend.

Risk premium (in oil): The markup the market puts on the oil price purely for the fear that a shipment could fail — not because less oil is flowing today. Like a higher bread price because a strike might halt delivery tomorrow. If the strike is called off, the markup disappears immediately.

CDS (credit default swap): An insurance against a borrower's default — here the Brazilian government. The price in basis points says how expensive that protection is: the higher, the riskier the market judges the country. Like a car-insurance premium that rises when you count as a riskier driver. 124 bp means roughly: ~$124,000 per year to insure $10M of Brazilian government bonds — historically low for Brazil.

It's Thursday, May 21st. Yesterday brought a clear test event: the minutes from the Federal Reserve's last rate meeting. And those minutes were unmistakably hawkish — most policymakers worry that inflation will stay elevated longer, and some even wanted to strike the hint of future rate cuts. By the textbook, that should have strengthened the dollar and weakened the real.

But the opposite happened, and the reason is oil. Brent's plunge of five point four percent from the day before held, leaving the price near a hundred and five dollars. The easing of tensions between the United States and Iran continues — President Trump talks of final negotiating phases, and tankers are passing through the Strait of Hormuz again. Cheap oil means less inflation fear, and that gave markets room to breathe: US stocks climbed to a record high, and bond yields actually fell. On this day, oil mattered more than the central bank.

For Daniel's exchange question, that means the real recovered further and broke below the mark of five reais per dollar — about four point nine nine eight. The last clean euro-to-real fix from the European Central Bank stands at five point eight three four seven, from yesterday; today's live value sits below it at about five point eight one, because the real firmed. My working figure for exchange decisions is five point eight one — so the euro is buying rather few reais right now.

But a word of caution: this lift in the real is borrowed, not earned. Brazil itself supplied no trigger of its own. The Iran easing is a letter of intent, not a signed contract — if the narrative flips, the oil risk premium comes back, and the real gives way. That's why I don't yet read the break below five as a new trend.

Two things I have to correct from my earlier briefs, and both move the forecast. First: the next European Central Bank meeting is on June eleventh, not the fourth — I had that wrong. Second, and this is the bigger one: euro-area inflation jumped to three percent in April, the highest in more than two years. The market now expects the central bank to raise rates, not cut them. So Europe is leaning hawkish too.

And here's why that matters for Daniel: if the European Central Bank raises rates, the euro grows stronger — and a stronger euro lifts the euro-to-real rate. Daniel would then get more reais per euro invoice. This is the rare situation where a rate hike in Europe improves his exchange math, even if the real stays firm against the dollar. So anyone who can wait to exchange has, from June eleventh, a solid reason for patience on the euro side.
