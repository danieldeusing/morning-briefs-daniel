What turned this week — the mechanism

Today's lesson: a central bank can't hike freely into a shrinking economy. That's exactly the tension the euro PMI opened up today.

  B --> C[Expectation: euro firmer, EUR/BRL up]
  D[Euro PMI 47.5: economy shrinks] --> E[Doubt over the hike path]
  E --> F[Bund yields + EUR/USD ease today]

Stagflation: When the economy shrinks and prices rise at the same time — the most uncomfortable combination for a central bank. Imagine the baker selling fewer and fewer rolls but having to make them more expensive anyway, because flour and electricity are going up. If the central bank cuts rates, it fuels inflation; if it hikes, it chokes the already-weak economy. That's exactly the bind today's euro PMI shows: output 47.5 (below 50 = contraction), but input prices at a three-year high.

My read: Yesterday I sold the June 11 ECB hike as a clean upside lever for EUR/BRL — "hike → stronger euro → more reais per invoice." I have to qualify that today. The euro PMI shows a shrinking economy, and the market reacts at once: Bund yields (10Y 3.06%, from 3.08%) and EUR/USD (1.1607, −0.1%) are easing — the opposite of what rising hike expectations would do. A hike into a contraction simply doesn't lift a currency reliably; sometimes it even weakens it, by amplifying recession fear. The euro side has thus become more two-sided — the upside lever for EUR/BRL is real, but no longer as clear as I claimed yesterday.

Flash PMI: A monthly sentiment index for which S&P Global asks hundreds of purchasing managers whether orders, output and prices are rising or falling. "Flash" = the quick preliminary estimate mid-month. Above 50 means growth, below means contraction. Like a snap poll of store managers: is business running better or worse than last month? Today: US 51.7 (growing), euro area 47.5 (shrinking).

Hawk vs. dove: a "hawk" sees inflation as the biggest danger and prefers to keep rates high; a "dove" prefers to cut, to support growth and jobs. Picture two doctors at the same patient: the hawk prescribes the bitter medicine (high rates against the inflation fever), the dove doesn't want to overtax the weakened patient. If the new chief physician (Warsh) is a dove but the medical team is hawkish, there's a fight over the dose — and the market guesses along.

CDS (credit default swap): insurance against a borrower's default — here the Brazilian state. The price in basis points says how expensive that protection is: the higher, the riskier the market judges the country. It's Friday, May twenty-second, and the week closes on a turn. Today was the day of the purchasing-manager indices — the first quick read on the economy for May. And the picture is a scissors: American manufacturing is booming, hitting its highest level in four years, but with rising prices. Europe's economy, by contrast, is shrinking at the sharpest pace since October twenty twenty-three — and prices there are climbing anyway.

This is called stagflation: the economy weakens but prices climb. For the European Central Bank, it's the most uncomfortable of all situations. And it's exactly what changes my forecast from yesterday. I had said the central bank's rate hike on June eleventh would be a reliable reason for a stronger euro, and thus a higher euro-real rate. I have to walk that back today. The market reacted immediately: German bond yields fell, and the euro eased against the dollar. The message: the central bank can't hike that aggressively if the economy is shrinking.

For Daniel's exchange question, that means the upside reason on the euro side has gotten weaker. The fresh euro-real fix from the European Central Bank, dated May twenty-first, sits at five point eight one three four — a touch lower than the day before, because the real strengthened this week. Today's live value is around five point eight one. On the week, that's essentially unchanged. My working value for exchange decisions is five point eight one — so the euro still buys rather few reais.

The second big factor is oil. Brent turns back up by a good three percent to about one hundred six dollars. The de-escalation between the US and Iran that calmed markets this week is fraying, and the risk premium is returning. That's a headwind for the real, because its strength this week was borrowed — it came from cheap oil and global risk appetite, not from Brazil itself. If oil turns, that prop falls away, and the real hands back its brief push below five reais per dollar.

In America there's a historic moment today: Kevin Warsh is sworn in at the White House as the new head of the US central bank. The intriguing part is that Warsh himself is likely to be a dove — he sees the inflation spike as temporary and artificial intelligence as price-lowering. So a rather gentle chair could be meeting a strict, hawkish committee. That sets up conflict for the months ahead. Rates don't change in the short term: the market expects an almost ninety-nine percent hold at the June meeting. A cut is effectively priced out, and a hike remains just a talking point, not a base case.

In Brazil itself, the good mood is cooling. The Ibovespa stock index added only a little, after its strong jump on Wednesday. The benchmark rate sits at fourteen and a half percent, which means high interest income on real assets. In the Banco Master scandal, the prosecutor-general's office keeps negotiating a cooperating-witness deal, even though the federal police walked away.

One correction on the election: yesterday I cited a single poll that showed Lula clearly ahead of Flávio Bolsonaro. That was an outlier. For the exchange decision today: five point eight one is in the lower part of the window — the euro buys few reais.
