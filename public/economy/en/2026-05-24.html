The weekend turn — mechanism

Yesterday, Monday's risk was called escalation: a hardened Iran driving oil up. Overnight the headline flipped to the opposite. On May 23 Trump said a deal was "largely negotiated"; per Axios (May 24) it includes a 60-day ceasefire extension, a toll-free reopening of the Strait of Hormuz (Iran clears its mines), and in exchange the lifting of the US port blockade plus sanctions waivers, so Iran can sell oil freely again. Oil risk premium: the surcharge an oil price carries only because the market fears a supply disruption — not because less oil flows today. Like an umbrella seller already raising the price because dark clouds are gathering, even though it's still dry. If the Iran deal lands and the Strait of Hormuz (through which a fifth of the world's oil travels) stays open, the "clouds" clear — and exactly that surcharge falls back out.

The Brazil twist — reversed: Brazil exports oil, so falling oil prices are actually unfavorable for its trade figures. Yet a de-escalation-driven oil decline is usually good for the real short-term — because global risk appetite returns, money flows back into emerging markets, and the carry (14.50% Selic) pulls again. Short-term, the risk-on wave beats the exporter logic. Exactly mirror-image to yesterday: then the Iran risk was an upside risk for EUR/BRL, today the Iran de-escalation is a downside risk.

My read: the weekend flipped the sign but did not remove the uncertainty. The deal is not signed — every source stresses it "could still fall apart," and the announcement was only floated for Sunday. If it confirms by Monday, I see a small risk-on gap to the downside for EUR/BRL (real stronger, Brent lower). If it falls through, yesterday's upside scenario snaps back. For Daniel that means: Monday is a headline-driven day — the first gauge isn't the Focus survey, but whether a signature arrived overnight.

Iran deal "largely negotiated" — the weekend turn [Global]: on May 23 Trump said an agreement with Iran was "largely negotiated"; "final details" would be announced "shortly." For markets that means: the headline that carried yesterday's upside gap risk has reversed — bearish oil and risk-on if confirmed. axios · cbs · wapo

Strait of Hormuz: a narrow strait between Iran and the Arabian Peninsula through which roughly a fifth of the world's traded oil moves by tanker — the most important chokepoint in global oil logistics. Like a single highway bridge most of a region's traffic must cross: close it (mines, threats) and everything backs up and prices jump; reopen it and the flow normalizes and the surcharge falls away.

Oil: Friday close with a weekend overhang to the downside [Global]: Brent closed May 22 at ~$103.94, WTI at ~$97.00. The futures market doesn't trade over the weekend, but the news bias flipped: if the Hormuz deal lands, Iranian barrels and an open tanker corridor return — the risk premium carrying oil falls out. But as long as no signature exists, Monday's open stays open in both directions. One standing counter-factor: Russia halted gasoline exports through July 31 after drones hit several central-Russian refineries (most recently Syzran, May 21). te brent · moscow times

The ECB remains the central heavyweight question [EU]: unchanged, but central to the 30-day view: euro inflation (April 3.0%) carries a 25bp deposit-rate hike from 2.00% to 2.25% on June 11 as base case (~75–86% priced via swaps/prediction markets), yet the May PMI with Composite 47.5 (steepest contraction since Oct 2023) makes it two-sided. Bund yields eased on Friday (10Y 3.04%, 2Y 2.64%) — the market voice doubting whether the ECB can hike into a contraction. If it hikes, EUR/BRL rises (good for Daniel's reais); if it scraps the hike, the euro falls with it. ecb calendar · te bund

Stagflation: when the economy shrinks and prices rise at the same time — the most uncomfortable spot for a central bank. Imagine the baker selling fewer and fewer rolls but having to make them pricier anyway, because flour and electricity climb. Cut rates and you fuel inflation; hike and you choke the weak economy. That's exactly the bind the euro PMI shows — and it makes the June 11 ECB meeting a genuine nail-biter.

And risk appetite is usually good for the Brazilian real, because money flows back into emerging markets and Brazil's high interest rate pulls again.

Here is the reverse twist from yesterday: Brazil itself exports oil, so a falling oil price is actually unfavorable for its trade figures. Yet short-term the risk appetite wins out — the real tends stronger, and the euro-real rate leans down on Monday, not up.
