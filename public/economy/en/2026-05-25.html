Why EUR/BRL rises even as oil crashes — the mechanism

Yesterday I built Monday as a down-gap: Iran de-escalation → oil down → risk-on → stronger real → lower EUR/BRL. The first two links happened — Brent fell ~4.9% to ~$98. But the chain snapped after that. The real barely reacted (USD/BRL only ~5.02 rather than jumping below 5.00), and at the same time a second driver showed up: Lagarde firmed up the euro over the weekend. The euro leg of the cross outweighs the real leg.

Cross rate (EUR/BRL): EUR/BRL isn't a market of its own — it's the product of two others: how many dollars a euro costs (EUR/USD) times how many reais a dollar costs (USD/BRL). Like an exchange across two counters — euro→dollar first, then dollar→real. If one leg rises (firmer euro) while the other stays flat (real motionless), the product rises. That's exactly today: the euro is pulling, the real is standing still — so EUR/BRL climbs, even though the headline ("oil crashes, risk-on") sounded like a stronger real.

My read: Monday's lesson is a humility lesson about single narratives. I had the oil/Iran channel right, but it wasn't the only open channel — and the smaller, quieter euro signal overruled it. For Daniel, practically: the swap rate is marginally better than Friday, but the real lever of the next three weeks isn't Tehran, it's Frankfurt. If the ECB hikes on June 11, EUR/BRL keeps climbing (good for the invoices); if it scraps the hike, the euro turns and the rate with it. Today is also thin US-holiday tape — moves can be exaggerated and low-signal.

Macro & economy

Iran deal: agreement in principle, but no signature [Global]: over the weekend the state of play matured from "largely negotiated" to an agreement "in principle" / a memorandum of understanding (a roadmap, not a signed treaty). A senior Trump official said the Iranians had "in principle agreed to a deal," but Secretary of State Rubio called it a "work in progress" that "could materialize," and Trump told negotiators "not to rush — time is on our side." Iran's foreign ministry said it had "reached a conclusion on a large portion of the issues" but that agreement was not imminent. Crucial for markets: per CNN, Trump said the port blockade would "remain in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified, and signed." The Strait of Hormuz stays under Iranian supervision; shipping is to return to pre-war levels over ~30 days (on Sunday ~33 ships passed with Iran's permission, ~240 still waiting). cnn · cbs

Risk premium (oil): the markup the oil price carries only because the market fears a supply disruption — not because less oil flows today. Like an umbrella seller raising the price because dark clouds are gathering, even though it's still dry. With the prospect of an open Strait of Hormuz (a fifth of the world's oil passes through it) the "clouds" partly clear — and that markup dropped out of Brent by ~5% on Monday.

Oil crashes — but Russia pushes back [Global]: Brent fell to ~$98.49 (−4.88%) on 2026-05-25, WTI to ~$91.77 (−5.00%); both broke the round levels. The market is front-running the Hormuz reopening even though the deal isn't signed — that's the priced-in de-escalation. Counter-force: in Russia, drone strikes over two weeks have knocked out or throttled refineries with roughly a quarter of the country's capacity; the gasoline export ban runs to July 31. OPEC+ is discussing output. Net: crude bearish, refined products firmer — a split picture. te brent · moscow times

The ECB is now the central driver [EU]: Lagarde said on 2026-05-24 on Italian TV that the ECB would "probably" revise its inflation forecast up in June (March projection: 2.6% for 2026) — "the situation has evolved" — but deliberately avoided confirming a June hike. EUR/USD rose ~0.35%. The day before (Eurogroup, Nicosia) she warned that excessive fiscal spending could intensify the pressure to hike. The market still prices a 25 bp deposit-rate hike from 2.00% to 2.25% on June 11 as the base case (~77% for a move), but the May PMI (Composite 47.5, the steepest contraction since Oct 2023) keeps the dovish counter-scenario open. Bund 10Y eased to 2.96% (−8 bp vs. Friday's 3.04% close), 2Y last 2.64%. fxstreet · te bund

Stagflation: when the economy shrinks and prices rise at the same time — the most uncomfortable spot for a central bank. Picture the baker selling fewer and fewer rolls but still having to raise prices because flour and electricity are climbing. Cut rates and you fuel inflation; raise them and you choke the weak economy. That's exactly the bind the euro-area PMI shows — and it makes the ECB meeting on June 11 a genuine nail-biter.
