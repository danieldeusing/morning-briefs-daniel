Why everything is flat today — the mechanism

Yesterday oil crashed and the firmer euro nudged EUR/BRL up. Today two things turn: first, the Iran de-escalation stalls — Tehran says an agreement is "not imminent". So oil stops falling and settles around $100. Second, the euro turns back minimally intraday (EUR/USD 1.1634 instead of 1.1643). Against both stands a new, quiet driver: Brazil's fundamentals are firming. On net the forces cancel each other out — EUR/BRL stays flat.

COT net-long (positioning): The CFTC publishes weekly how the big speculators are positioned on the US futures markets. "Net-long the real" means: far more bets for a stronger real than against (currently +71,012 contracts net). Like a betting shop where almost everyone backs the same team — as long as nothing big intervenes, that lopsidedness supports the price, because there's hardly anyone left to sell. That's why the real doesn't give ground even with a firm dollar.

Both keep the real stable — and the euro gives no signal today. For Daniel, practically: no pressure to act, the window stays in the friendly lower third. Macro & economy

Iran deal: the tone cools [Global]: Overnight the situation pulled back from weekend euphoria ("agreement in principle"). Iran's foreign-ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baqaei said on 25.05 that progress had been made, but an agreement was "not imminent". US Secretary of State Rubio did say a deal "could materialize today", but conceded that the nuclear and Hormuz questions remain open. The proposed (unsigned) roadmap: phase 1 = reopen the Strait of Hormuz to pre-war levels + a no-nukes assurance; phase 2 = 30–60 days of nuclear talks, partial release of Iranian assets. The sticking point remains sovereignty over Hormuz (possible tolls) vs the US demand for full freedom of navigation. And it didn't stop at words: overnight CENTCOM reported "self-defense" strikes in southern Iran (25.05, "to protect our troops"), Iran claimed it downed a US MQ-9 drone and fired on other aircraft, and on 26.05 the Supreme Leader said the region would "no longer serve as shields" for US bases. The ceasefire "holds" nominally per CENTCOM, but both sides keep firing, and the blockade is active. It's precisely this overnight escalation that gives the oil risk premium a fresh reason — and explains why Brent, after Monday's crash, isn't falling further but ticking up. cbs · al jazeera

Risk premium (oil): the surcharge an oil price carries only because the market fears a supply disruption — not because less oil flows today. Like an umbrella seller who raises the price because dark clouds are gathering, even though it's still dry. On Monday this surcharge fell out of the Brent price by ~5 % as the deal neared. That it isn't falling further today is precisely because the clouds aren't fully clearing: as long as there's no signature, a residual surcharge stays in the price.

Oil: stabilized, with a real counterweight [Global]: Brent trades around ~$99.8 on 26.05, WTI around ~$93.3 — after Monday's 5 % crash (Brent ~$98.5), no further collapse but a small gain on the overnight escalation (CNBC: Brent ~+2 %). The exact daily delta is inconsistent across sources (TradingEconomics shows a negative daily change, OilPrice/CNBC a positive one); the level around $100 is robust. JPMorgan expects the Hormuz reopening "toward early June" and oil to average ~$97 for the year. A real counterweight from the supply side: in Russia, drone strikes over two weeks have knocked out ~25 % of refining capacity (processing down to 4.69 m b/d, the lowest since 2009), with the gasoline export ban running to 31.07. On net: crude capped on the downside, refined products firmer. te brent · moscow times

The ECB remains the central driver [EU]: there's been little fresh since Monday — Lagarde's weekend comment (24.05, Italian TV) holds the tone: the ECB will "probably" revise its inflation forecast up in June ("the situation has evolved"), without confirming a June hike; GC member Demarco doubled down, saying the March projection may have been too optimistic. The market still prices the 25 bp deposit-rate hike from 2.00 % to 2.25 % on Thu 11.06 as the base case (~77 %), but the May contraction (composite PMI 47.5; services PMI 46.4) keeps the dovish counter-scenario open. Bund 10Y last ~2.96 %. bloomberg · te eu-pmi

Stagflation: when the economy shrinks and prices rise at the same time — the most uncomfortable spot for a central bank. Imagine the baker selling fewer and fewer rolls but having to make them pricier because flour and electricity are going up. If the central bank cuts rates, it fuels inflation; if it hikes, it chokes the weak economy. That exact bind is what the euro PMI shows — and it makes the ECB meeting on Thu 11.06 a real cliffhanger.

CDS (credit default swap): insurance against a debtor's default — here, the Brazilian state. The price in basis points says how expensive that protection is: the higher, the riskier the market judges the country. Like a car-insurance premium that rises when you're rated a riskier driver. ~120 bp roughly means: ~$120,000 a year to insure $10 m of Brazilian government bonds — historically low for Brazil, and still falling.
