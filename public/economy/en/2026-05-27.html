Why EUR/BRL rises even though everything is "risk-on" — the mechanism

This is the twist of the day. Globally everything smells of relief: the Iran talks moved to Doha, oil cracks a second time (Brent ~$96), US stocks close at records, yields fall. By the textbook, this risk-on should pull money into emerging markets and the real — so EUR/BRL should fall. The opposite happens, for two reasons. First: Brazil is a net oil exporter. Falling oil drags Petrobras, the Bovespa (−0.69%) and with it the real — so the oil crash doesn't help Brazil, it hurts slightly. Second: the euro is firm into the ECB (Villeroy hawkish, hike ~76% priced). Softer real plus firmer euro = EUR/BRL higher.

Oil-exporter effect (petro-currency): Countries that sell a lot of oil have a currency that breathes with the oil price. When oil rises, more dollars flow into the country → the currency strengthens; when oil falls, that reverses. Like a baker whose income hangs on the price of bread: if bread gets cheaper, he earns less. Brazil sells a lot of crude through Petrobras — which is why the oil crash hurts the real, even though cheap oil is good for importers (like the eurozone). That's why the global risk-on, for once, does not lift the real today.

My read: The last three days have been a lesson that "risk-on lifts EM currencies" is a rule of thumb, not a law of nature. For Brazil the oil channel flips the sign: the relief that gives equity bulls wings worldwide takes a support pillar away from the petro-currency real. At the same time the euro leg is reasserting — Villeroy's "do what is necessary" and stubborn euro inflation (PMI report: "nearing 4%") keep the hike trade alive. Net, that pushes EUR/BRL higher, slowly but steadily. For Daniel that's good: anyone wanting to convert EUR→BRL gets more at the top of the band. The pacesetter stays Frankfurt (Thu 11 Jun); this week the 28/29 May double day (US PCE + BR IPCA-15) is the next date with teeth.

Frozen assets: When a country is sanctioned, its balances in foreign accounts can be "frozen" — the owner can't touch them, but the money stays their property. Like a blocked bank account where the bank holds the key, not you. That Iran is demanding the release of these billions while the US wants to see the enriched uranium destroyed first is the last big knot — and exactly why no signature sits under the Hormuz deal yet.

The ECB remains the central driver [EU]: Fresh is Villeroy's tone: the Banque de France governor said on 26 May the ECB would "do what is necessary" to tame inflation — a hawkish signal so close to the meeting (the original source, CNBC, returned only the headline on fetch, no full text; so I quote only the core statement and attribution). The market keeps the 25 bp deposit-rate rise from 2.00% to 2.25% on Thu 11 Jun as the base case (~76% via market bets), backed by stubborn inflation — the S&P Global PMI report warned that price growth is "nearing 4%" while output contracts at its fastest since late 2023 (Composite PMI 47.5; Services PMI 46.4). Bund 10Y eased to ~2.95% (−3 bp), off last week's 15-year high of ~3.2%, as the Iran de-escalation pushed risk yields down. polymarket · te bund

Stagflation: When the economy shrinks and prices rise at the same time — the most uncomfortable spot for a central bank. Picture a baker selling fewer and fewer rolls but having to charge more because flour and electricity are going up. Cut rates and you fuel inflation; raise them and you choke the weak economy. That's exactly the bind the euro PMI shows (contraction with inflation "nearing 4%") — and it makes the ECB meeting on Thu 11 Jun a genuine nail-biter.

US tape at records, yields down [USA]: Wall Street celebrated on Tuesday: the S&P 500 7,519.12 (+0.61%) and Nasdaq 26,656.18 (+1.19%) both closed at records, driven by a Micron tech rally; the Dow eased slightly (50,461.68, −0.23%). CDS (credit default swap): Insurance against the default of a borrower — here the Brazilian state. The price in basis points says how expensive that protection is: the higher, the riskier the market judges the country. Like a car-insurance premium that rises when you're seen as a riskier driver. ~120 bp roughly means ~$120,000 a year to insure $10m of Brazilian government bonds — historically low for Brazil. That the tightening pauses today fits the mild real headwind.

It's Wednesday, the twenty-seventh of May. All over the world it smells like relief — only this time it doesn't help Daniel. The Iran talks have moved on to Doha, and according to U.S. Secretary of State Rubio the disagreement is down to a single word, a single sentence. Oil is cracking a second time: Brent has fallen to around ninety-six dollars, more than three percent down. And Wall Street is celebrating — the S&P five hundred and the Nasdaq both closed at records yesterday, and U.S. yields have dropped to a two-week low. Classic risk-on.

You'd think that kind of backdrop would strengthen the Brazilian real — money usually flows into emerging markets when fear eases. Today the opposite happens, and the reason is oil. Brazil is a net oil exporter. When the oil price falls, Petrobras suffers, the São Paulo stock market gave up nearly a percent yesterday, and the real softens — it sits just above five-oh-three to the dollar. For once, the global risk-on doesn't lift the petro-currency real; it actually weighs on it a little.

On the other side stands the euro, and the euro is firm. The head of the French central bank, Villeroy, said yesterday the European Central Bank would do what is necessary to tame inflation — a hawkish tone right before the meeting. The market still puts the odds at around seventy-six percent that the ECB hikes rates on the eleventh of June. A softer real plus a firmer euro lifts the euro-real rate higher.

And that is the move of the day: the rate is creeping up. The European Central Bank's reference rates trace a clear line through the week — from five-eighty-one on Friday to five-eighty-four on Tuesday, and live today around five-eighty-six; yesterday it even touched five-eighty-seven. For Daniel's euro-to-real invoices that's the friendlier news: more reais per euro, closer to the top of the band — even though we're still well below last year's average of six. No need to act, but the window is getting more attractive.

Beneath the surface Brazil's fundamentals stay solid: the insurance premium against a default holds steady around a hundred and twenty basis points, and the big speculators are still heavily positioned for a stronger real. But today that's only enough to cushion, not to support — the oil channel and the firm euro win the tug-of-war.
