Why EUR/BRL keeps rising—the mechanism

Today's move is euro-driven, not real-driven. Three layers: (1) Euro leg pulls hard—the ECB rate hike bet for June 11 jumped from ~76% (Wed) to ~91% (Thu) after Lagarde's inflation-revision hint (May 23) and Schnabel's open hike call. Higher hike odds → higher EUR yields → EUR/USD holds above 1.16. (2) Real leg differentiates—IPCA-15 breached the BCB ceiling, making BCB cuts pricier, which props up the real against the dollar (USD/BRL −0.28%). Against the euro, that's not enough. (3) Oil channel keeps headwind—WTI Wed −5.55% to 88.68 $, Brent today mild correction (+1.94% to 96.12 $); Hormuz reopening is aggressively priced, Petrobras suffers, Bovespa stayed green yesterday just barely at +0.02% thanks to bank strength.

Target ceiling (meta de inflação): The BCB doesn't get to land inflation anywhere it likes—it has an official target (for 2026: 3.0% ± 1.5 pp, so floor 1.5%, ceiling 4.5%). Like a school grade that can't fall below satisfactory: if the student breaks the limit, the teacher must explain to the principal why. The BCB then formally writes an open letter to the Finance Ministry. At 4.64% Y/Y, the limit is broken for the first time in 2026—the cut path (from 14.50% Selic) becomes much harder to justify, and hike pressure builds.
PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures): The inflation measure the Fed chair loves to watch—because it weights prices by actual consumption habits, not a fixed basket like the CPI. Like a shopping list that adjusts monthly: if ham gets too expensive, you switch to chicken. "Core PCE" (excluding energy and food) is the steering measure: Fed target is 2.0%, last print (March) was 3.2%. Today 09:30 BRT the April print—above 3.2% means: Fed must stay higher longer → dollar stronger → EUR/BRL even higher.

My read: Fourth day of upside drift, the driver switches—early week weak real, Wed onward strong euro, today the real reasserts against the dollar (IPCA-15 forces BCB), but the euro surge is stronger. The US PCE in two hours can flip the picture: hot (> 3.3% core) halts the drift, cool (≤ 3.1%) sends EUR/BRL toward 5.90+. Whoever wants to trade EUR→BRL: around 5.88 is the best window in days—before the PCE print.

It's Thursday, May twenty-eighth. Today is actually the double day of the week, and the Brazilian half has already delivered: yesterday evening came the IPCA fifteen, Brazil's official inflation preview. It was zero point six two percent for the month, but four point six four percent year-on-year, and for the first time this year above the Brazilian central bank's upper target of four point five percent. That's a marked shift. The Brazilian central bank, which just recently cut its key rate to fourteen point five percent, suddenly finds itself forced to hold there for a while, possibly pause rate cuts entirely.

Yet the euro-to-real exchange rate keeps climbing this morning. Live, just after nine o'clock UTC, it sits at around five point eight eight, and yesterday it even touched five point eight nine. The European Central Bank's reference rates trace a clear staircase across the week: Friday five point eight one, Monday five point eight two, Tuesday five point eight four, Wednesday now five point eight seven. Four days of steady upside in a row, and the driver's shift is clear today.

Early week the weak real pulled the move; from Wednesday onward the strong euro; today the real reasserts against the dollar. But the euro wins the tug-of-war clearly. The reason: the market's bet that the European Central Bank will raise rates on June eleventh jumped from around seventy-six percent on Wednesday to around ninety-one percent. Lagarde hinted at inflation revisions on Friday, board member Schnabel openly called for a hike, and eurozone inflation hit three percent in April through the Iran-driven energy wave. The repricing is sharp, and the euro holds firm above one point one six against the dollar.

This morning at nine-thirty Brasília time comes the other heavyweight: the US PCE for April. That's the inflation measure the Federal Reserve watches most closely, because it tracks actual consumer habits. Last print was three point five percent headline, three point two core, both well above the Fed's two percent target. A hot print supports the dollar further, which softens the euro-real rate slightly; a cool print can send it toward five point nine.

Below the surface much stays as it was. Brazil's credit-default swap premium holds steady around one hundred twenty basis points, the Bovespa slipped about half a percent yesterday—Petrobras weakness outweighed bank support, because West Texas Crude collapsed more than five percent Wednesday to nearly eighty-nine dollars. Petrobras suffers, the real should too, but today the IPCA-fifteen surge overlays the picture and makes the real stronger against the dollar.

What counts: the IPCA-fifteen shock should force the curve today to price in a later rate-cut path for the Brazilian central bank.
