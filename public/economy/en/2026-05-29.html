PCE juggling act (YoY vs MoM): Inflation can be measured two ways — month-on-month (MoM, what actually happened in April) or year-over-year (YoY, where we stand vs. April a year ago). Like two speedometers in a car: one shows your current speed, the other your average speed over the last twelve months. When your current speed is down but your annual average is still high, you know: you're driving slower now, but the trip was fast on average. That's exactly what PCE delivered — the April month was cooler (0.2 %), but because earlier months were so hot, the annual rate still climbed to a three-year high (3.3 % Core). Market reading: the trajectory is turning, the Fed doesn't have to get even more hawkish.

Core PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures, Core): The inflation measure the Fed Chair watches most closely — prices weighted by consumption patterns, excluding the volatile energy and food pieces. Like a shopping list that adjusts monthly if ham gets expensive and you switch to chicken — and without the gas bill, because it swings too wildly. Fed target is 2.0 %, current reading 3.3 % on an annual basis. As long as that climbs to a three-year high, the Fed can't aggressively cut — even if the month reading was mild.

It's Friday, May twenty-ninth. The trading week closes today with a clearly defined picture — yesterday was the double-header of the week, and both halves delivered. The Brazilian side came in Wednesday night with the IPCA-fifteen boost that broke the central bank's ceiling. The US side came in yesterday morning with the April PCE — and it was a juggling act.

Year over year, the PCE climbed to three-year highs — three point eight percent in the headline, three point three in core. Month over month, though, it was cooler than expected — zero point two percent instead of zero point three. Wall Street read the monthly measure as a turning point and shifted to risk-on. The S&P five hundred closed at a new all-time record at seven thousand five hundred sixty-three — despite the hottest PCE in three years.

For the euro-real cross, that had a clear effect. The ECB daily fix on Thursday jumped to five point eight nine twenty-eight — the week's high after a staircase of Friday five point eighty-one, Monday five point eighty-two, Tuesday five point eighty-four, Wednesday five point eighty-seven. Four consecutive days of upward drift, plus one point three percent over the week. This morning, just after nine o'clock coordinated universal time, the cross retraced to five point eight six eighty-six — about zero point three percent down.

The mechanism is clean: the ECB hike repricing for June eleventh stays firm at ninety-one percent — Lagarde's inflation revisions and Schnabel's open hike call continue to work. But the Dollar softened yesterday after the PCE juggling, because the monthly portion let the market think less hawkishly about the Fed. That gives the Real room against the Dollar — and through that, the euro-real cross a small retrace.

For Daniel's operational question, that matters. The week's upper band was acknowledged yesterday. Today the markets are quieter, without big US data triggers, without a Brazilian print — movement comes from positioning and weekend hedging. Anyone wanting to swap euros into reals has a friendlier mark of five point eight seven this morning than five point eighty-nine yesterday afternoon. No urgency, but the window is clearly open.

Below the surface, the rest is sorting itself out. The Brazilian labor market sent a double signal yesterday.
