FX in detail — where we stand & why

The EUR/BRL rose in May from 5.77 (12-month low 5.7632 on 05.05) to 5.8747 at the last ECB fix on 29.05 — just under +2%.[1] This morning it's trading a touch lower intraday, around ~5.84.[2] Practically the entire move is euro-driven, not real-driven: the dollar index DXY sits at 98.91, below 100, and the real is rather firm against the dollar. What lifts the euro is a single expectation — the ECB rate hike.

Rate hike & exchange rate: Higher rates are like a better savings interest at a bank — money flows to where it earns more. If the market expects the ECB to raise euro rates, investors buy euros beforehand. That lifts EUR/USD and thus also EUR/BRL, even before any rate actually rises.

The cross-check is clean: from USD/BRL 5.0453 × EUR/USD 1.1644 you get exactly 5.8747 — divergence 0.001%, so well below the 0.5% threshold. The three anchors are consistent.[3]

This is exactly where today's tension sits: The ECB hikes (with ~90% probability) because of inflation whose driver — oil price — is running out from under it. Brent fell nearly 20% in May to $92.56, after the US and Iran agreed "broadly" to a 60-day memorandum that eases shipping through the Strait of Hormuz again.[4] The 3.0% inflation from April was almost pure energy effect (energy +10.8%), and core rate even fell to 2.2%.[5] My dissent from consensus: The hike probably goes ahead (too near-term to flip forward guidance), but the path afterward is far more dovish than the euro rate currently prices in.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Bonds

The Brazilian side is surprisingly unified: The Focus Survey of the BCB (as of 25.05) raised the IPCA forecast 2026 for the 11th time in a row, to 5.04% — above the 4.5% target ceiling. At the same time, the USD/BRL year-end forecast was lowered to 5.17 (firmer real) and the Selic year-end forecast sits at 13.25%.[6]

That's an interesting contradiction: higher inflation expectation, yet stronger currency. Resolution: The market expects the high inflation to come from imported oil prices (supply-driven, not demand-driven) — and the high Selic rate makes the real attractive enough for carry investors to justify the firmer rate.

IPCA: Brazil's official consumer price index — the measure the central bank uses to target inflation. When it exceeds the target ceiling (4.5%), the BCB comes under pressure not to cut rates too fast.

That aligns with the hawkish tone of the COPOM minutes from 05.05: The central bank explicitly warned that the Iran–US conflict might force it to pause the easing cycle.[7] With oil since fallen, that worry is at least partially defused — clearing the way for the next Selic cut on 16–17.06.

It's Monday, June first, and today's Economy Brief focuses on a single tension in the euro-real rate. The euro currently costs about five point eighty-four reals; at the European Central Bank's last reference rate from May twenty-ninth, it was five point eight seven.

The entire May rise comes from the euro, not the real. The reason: the market expects with about ninety percent probability that the European Central Bank will raise rates on June eleventh, to two point two five percent. Higher rates attract capital and strengthen the euro, even before the rate actually rises.

What makes this interesting is the contradiction underneath. The central bank is raising rates because of inflation, which was three percent in April. But that figure came almost entirely from expensive energy. And that very energy is just getting cheaper: the Brent oil price fell nearly twenty percent in May because the United States and Iran agreed to a sixty-day ceasefire. So the rationale for the rate hike is slipping away from the central bank right now.

My assessment: the rate move probably still happens, it's too near-term to change course. But the path afterward is much more dovish than the euro rate currently prices in. If oil stays calm, the euro loses its rate bonus over the coming months.

In Brazil the picture is surprisingly clear. The central bank's market survey from May twenty-fifth sees inflation for this year at five point zero four percent, above target — but at the same time a stronger real, with a dollar rate of five point seventeen by year-end. The high Brazilian policy rate of fourteen point five percent attracts foreign capital and supports the real.

For the next two weeks, that means: tomorrow comes European May inflation, the first figure that could show oil relief. On June eleventh the European Central Bank decides. And on June sixteenth and seventeenth the US Federal Reserve and the Brazilian Central Bank meet the same week — the trickiest week of the month.

Anyone converting euro invoices to reals has rare clarity this week and full uncertainty in two weeks. Tomorrow we watch the European inflation figure and whether it takes wind from the euro's sails.
