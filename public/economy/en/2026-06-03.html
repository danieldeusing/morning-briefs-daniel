Currency in detail — where we stand & why

The real didn't suddenly get strong — the euro got weaker. EUR/BRL fell from 5.89 (05-28) to the 5.8310 fix on 06-02, a drop of just over one percent across the week. USD/BRL fell in parallel to 5.0056. Both legs tell the same story: it's a real-strength story against both major currencies.[1]

The causal path over the last few days:

Brent falls $114 → $95→ BR inflation fear cools→ BR 10Y yield 14.5% → 14.13%→ Real firms

The high Selic rate of 14.50% makes the real attractive as a "carry" target (more on that in the ELI5 below), and the oil decline takes out the biggest inflation risk — expensive diesel and expensive gasoline — to some degree. Both support the real.[2][3]

BRL Carry Trade: Borrow cheap money (e.g., euros at 2%), lend it in Brazil at 14.5%. Imagine borrowing a bike for almost nothing and renting it out expensive — as long as nobody steals the bike. The "theft" here is a real crash: as long as the rate doesn't collapse, the interest spread is your profit. That's exactly the trade holding the real firm right now.

Positioning & BR bonds

This week's sentiment anchor is the BR 10-year yield: jumped to 14.5% mid-May (highest in over a year, driven by fiscal and inflation worries), then retreated to 14.13% at month-end as oil fell and stagflation fears eased.[2]

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against a sovereign default — the pricier it is, the more jittery the market. Like a travel cancellation policy that gets more expensive when a storm rolls in. Brazil's 5-year CDS is not cleanly verifiable across the usual sources today (conflicting values/date stamps), so deliberately omitted here — the 10Y yield is today's more reliable mood gauge.

Positioning itself is real-friendly: the dollar lost about 10.5% against the real this year, driven by the carry — the high Selic rate pulls in foreign money. USD/BRL fell to around 4.91 in early May (lowest since early 2024), since then a modest retracement to ~5.00. As long as the Selic path doesn't drop abruptly and oil stays tame, this trade has no obvious trigger to flip.[2]

What it means for Daniel's timing

The operational question is always the same: convert euro bill to real now or wait? This week the setup leans toward sooner rather than later, if a conversion is planned — because the risk through 06-11 points upward: if the ECB hike confirms, the euro buys more reais, which for a euro-income recipient sounds good at first — but Daniel's chain is euro in, then real out. If EUR/BRL rises before a planned real payout, he gets more reais per euro; the ±1-week wiggle can be used to land the day after a possible euro appreciation.

The other side of the bet: if the hike doesn't happen (8% residual probability) or the tone disappoints, the euro drops — then waiting would have been more expensive. No verdict, just the mechanics: the upside risk in EUR/BRL is real this week and euro-driven, culminating Thursday.

It's Wednesday, June 3rd. The Brazilian real is holding firm right now — but the interesting move this week is coming from the other side, from Europe.

First the numbers. The euro costs an average five point eight three reals, based on the European Central Bank's reference rate from yesterday. That's a third of a percent less than the day before. The dollar sits at five point zero zero against the real, also slightly weaker. Important: it's not a sudden real surge, but rather a weaker euro and dollar. The real holds its advantage because the policy rate in Brazil is very high at fourteen point five percent, and oil has gotten cheaper again.

The real story of the week is the European Central Bank. On June eleventh it decides — and the market estimates a ninety-two percent chance it will raise rates to two point two five percent. That would be unusual, because people had recently expected cuts instead. The reason is the Iran conflict and the stubborn inflation risk it creates. For Daniel's euro invoices, this means: a stronger euro lifts the exchange rate, no matter what the real does. The pressure is coming for the first time in a long while not from Brasília, but from Frankfurt.

In Brazil itself, the picture stays mixed. Economists' inflation expectations rose for the ninth consecutive time, to four point nine one percent for this year — well above target. The drivers are oil, higher diesel taxes, and a thirty-billion-real stimulus package from President Lula. The central bank in Brazil might therefore pause its rate cuts on June sixteenth and seventeenth — which would support the real.

Oil, the third big lever, has fallen from around one hundred fourteen dollars in April to about ninety-five dollars. The market is weighing mixed signals over a possible peace deal between the US and Iran. A breakthrough would ease Brazil's inflation further; a setback would strengthen the dollar as a safe haven.

My forecast for the band: this week sideways-firm between five point eighty and five point ninety, with the new upside risk coming from the euro side. Most likely is five point eighty-six. These probabilities are my own confidence estimate, not the market's implied values.
