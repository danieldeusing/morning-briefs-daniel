FX in detail — where we stand & why

The EUR/BRL anchor itself barely moved: 5.8951 on June 4, 5.8897 on June 5 — a decline of 0.09%, essentially flat. But beneath the calm surface, Friday's entire assumption flipped, the one the last briefing rested on. Expected was a weak US jobs report (~80k), which would soften the dollar. What arrived was the opposite: 172,000 new jobs, more than double the consensus, plus March and April revised up together by +93k.[1]

The causal path of this flip — left to right:

NFP 172k vs. 80k→ Fed hike priced in (~65%)→ DXY +0.66% above 100→ Real pressure downward

Until Friday midday, the market bet the Fed would cut next. After the jobs report, it flipped: Fed funds futures now price a roughly 65% probability that the policy rate ends up above the current 3.50–3.75% corridor by December — meaning a hike. Mid-May, the same bet stood at just ~25%.[1][2] That is the real move of the last 72 hours: not the rate, but the direction of expectations.

Priced in: The market doesn't wait for the event to bet; it bets before. When everyone suddenly believes the central bank will tighten instead of ease, they buy the dollar today — not in December. That's exactly what happened Friday: a single data point (the jobs report) flipped the bet from "cut" to "hike," and the dollar moved immediately, long before the Fed even meets.

Why this matters for Daniel: A stronger dollar tends to press the real (money flows to US assets), and a weaker real lifts EUR/BRL — without any euro move at all. This week that combines with the nearly certain ECB hike on Thursday. Two central banks, both hawkish, both supporting EUR/BRL. The only real counterweight is Brazil's own high rates (Selic 14.50%), which keep the real attractive via carry.

Positioning & Brazil bonds

Positioning remains fundamentally real-friendly — but the new dollar regime is the risk to watch. The dollar lost roughly 10% against the real in 2026, carried by the carry trade: the Selic rate of 14.50% attracts foreign money. But this very carry advantage melts if the Fed now hikes instead of cuts — then the US rate rises, and the yield gap to Brazil narrows.[4]

BRL carry trade: Borrow where it's cheap (say, in euros or dollars) and lend it in Brazil at 14.5%. Like renting a bicycle for almost nothing and subletting it at a premium — the profit is the rate spread. But when the "cheap" side gets pricier (Fed hikes), the margin shrinks, and some money looks for better returns elsewhere. Still, the gap is massive (14.5% vs. ~3.6%); the trade is not yet at acute risk, but the direction has turned.

The Focus survey (BCB economists) sees USD/BRL at year-end 2026 still around ~R$ 5.16, with inflation expectations for 2026/2027 at 4.9% / 4.0% — both above target, explaining Copom caution.[5]

Brazil's 5-year CDS today not cleanly verifiable via usual sources (last reading ~135–139 bps, end of March); the dated Focus consensus serves as sentiment proxy. CDS moves slowly.

What it means for Daniel's timing

The operative question remains: convert euro invoice to real now or wait? Daniel's flow is euros in → real spending; a higher EUR/BRL is good for him (more reals per euro). The setup is this week as euro-friendly as it gets: two hawkish central banks, a dollar above 100, expensive oil. Everything pulls the rate upward.

The mechanics for the next few days: the ECB hike Thursday is ~99% priced — surprise lies only in the tone. If Lagarde hints at further steps (September hike), the euro breaks above 5.96; if she confirms just what's expected, the euro can pull back lightly on "sell the news" — meaning the day before the meeting would have been better. The counterweight is the real: if Copom pauses next week (likely) and stays high-rate, it supports the real and dampens EUR/BRL upside. No verdict, only mechanics — this week the expensive side comes from both Frankfurt and Washington.
