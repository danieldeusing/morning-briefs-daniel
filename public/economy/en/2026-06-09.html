FX in Detail — where we stand & why

Monday's briefing said, "the tension is underneath" — the anchor was flat, but sentiment had shifted. By Tuesday, tension has broken through. The EUR/BRL fixing jumped from 5.8897 (06/05) to 5.9353 (06/08) — +0.77% in a single session, the sharpest single-day jump in weeks, intraday even to 5.964.[1] The euro has stopped waiting.

The driver is exactly the dual hawk that the last briefing flagged — but it is now working forward-looking, ahead of the actual event. Both legs have tightened since Friday: The dollar is holding above 100 in the DXY (Fed hike bet now ~70%), and the euro itself is moving into the nearly certain ECB hike on Thursday. The causal chain — left to right:

Fed hike bet ~70%→ DXY holding above 100→ Real −1.6% (USD/BRL 5.14)→ EUR/BRL +0.77% to 5.94

Note: This time it was mainly the real that gave way (USD/BRL from ~5.06 to 5.1432), not the euro that rose — the EUR/USD cross actually fell slightly (1.164 → 1.154). The strong dollar is the heavyweight this week, and it is pressing the real, which lifts the EUR/BRL — even though the euro itself is not gaining against the dollar.[1]

Cross Rate: EUR/BRL is not a directly traded price, but calculated via two bridges: EUR→USD and USD→BRL. Like a trip with a connecting flight — the final price depends on both legs. This week the second leg got pricier (the real weakening against the dollar), even though the first actually got cheaper (the euro giving up a bit against the dollar). Net: The euro costs more in reais because the real is the weaker currency in the triangle.

The new trigger this week is not Thursday, but Wednesday: The US May CPI comes tomorrow (06/10) at 09:30 BRT — one day before the ECB. In April US inflation was 3.8% (core 2.8%), driven by an energy spike (+17.9% y/y) from the Iran oil shock.[2] If May comes in hot, it locks in the Fed hike bet and drives the dollar — EUR/BRL higher. If it comes in mild (the fallen oil could help), the dollar eases, and the euro would have to deliver its rise from the ECB hike alone.

Positioning & BR Yields

Positioning remains fundamentally real-friendly — the 14.50% Selic rate continues to attract foreign capital, and the dollar lost around 8% against the real in 2026 (yearly low in spring). But this carry advantage is now what we need to watch: If the Fed hikes instead of cuts, the US rate rises and the yield spread to Brazil shrinks. This week, carry is only partially cushioning the real — the USD/BRL rose 1.6% despite the high rate.[3]

BRL Carry Trade: Borrow where it is cheap (euro or dollar), lend in Brazil at 14.5%. Like renting a bike almost free and subletting it expensively — the profit is the rate spread. When the "cheap" side gets dearer (the Fed hiking), the margin shrinks. Still, the spread is huge (14.5% vs. ~3.6%); the trade is not acutely threatened, but the real giving way despite the high rate this week shows: The strong dollar is overriding carry for now.

The Focus Survey (BCB economist poll) sees USD/BRL at year-end 2026 at ~R$ 5.16 — today's 5.14 rate is nearly already at consensus. Inflation expectations for 2026/2027 are 4.9% / 4.0%, both above target, explaining Copom caution.[4]

Brazil's 5-year CDS today not cleanly verifiable through normal channels (last clear reading ~135–139 bps, late March); the dated Focus consensus serves as mood proxy. CDS moves slowly.

What it means for Daniel's timing

The operational question remains: Convert euro bills to real now or wait? Daniel's chain is euro in → real out; a higher EUR/BRL is good for him (more reais per euro). And the rate has moved exactly that way since Monday — 5.94 instead of 5.89, a 0.77% gift if one had not yet converted on Monday.

The mechanics for the next few days have shifted: The critical day is now Wednesday, not Thursday. The ECB hike is ~99% priced — surprise only in tone. But the US CPI coming tomorrow is not priced and can flip the dollar both ways. If it comes hot, EUR/BRL runs toward 6.00 and today's rate was cheap. If it comes mild, the dollar eases and the euro could even pull back slightly before the ECB — then waiting past the CPI would be better. No verdict, just mechanics: The rate is high, the next impulse comes tomorrow morning from Washington, not Thursday from Frankfurt.

That takes some inflation pressure off. What is striking, though: The bet on a rate hike by the US Federal Reserve still rose slightly, to around seventy percent. In April it was at three point eight percent, driven by an energy spike from the Iran oil shock. If the May read comes in hot, that locks in the hike bet and drives the dollar; if it comes in mild, the dollar eases.
