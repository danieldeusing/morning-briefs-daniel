FX in detail — where we stand and why

The euro has simply steamrolled the real this week. The ECB Fix climbed from 5.8284 on 06/03 to 5.9751 on 06/09 — nearly 2.5% in seven trading days, the year-high.[1] Important for context: This is not a strong dollar pushing down all emerging currencies — the DXY fell in the same week.[2] These are two separate forces that happen to be stacking right now.

Force 1 — the euro is rising because the ECB raises rates the day after tomorrow. This goes against intuition: central banks are mostly cutting in 2026. The ECB does the opposite because the oil shock from the Hormuz crisis (Brent hit ~$118 in late April) kept eurozone inflation elevated. The market prices in ~99% odds of a 25 basis-point hike on Thursday.[3]

Basis point (bps): one-hundredth of a percentage point. "+25 bps" means the rate rises by 0.25 percentage points — from 2.00% to 2.25%. Think of a speedometer ticking in 0.25 increments; 25 bps is exactly one click.

Higher rates make the euro more attractive to hold — more yield for money sitting in euros. Money flows in, the euro rises. That's happening right now, in anticipation.

Force 2 — the real carries its own penalty. On 06/01, the US trade office (USTR) proposed a 25% tariff on Brazilian imports — via Section 301, the same lever as past trade disputes.[4] It's just a proposal (hearing not until 07/06), but markets price risk ahead of time. The real weakened during exactly this window — while other risk assets even gained on the Iran-Israel ceasefire signal. This decoupling is the tell: BRL weakness is Brazil-specific, not global.

The ECB held steady on 04/30 (deposit rate 2.00%, main refi 2.15%).[5] Now the market prices in ~99% odds of a move to 2.25%.[3] The driver is imported oil-price shock, not hot growth — a "defensive" hike. COPOM 06/17: Selic at 14.50%, pause likely

Brazil cut on 04/29 for the second straight time by 25 bps to 14.50%.[6] For 06/17, the market is split — the hawkish tone (2026 inflation view raised to 4.6%) points more to a pause than further cuts. Focus survey sees year-end at 13.50% (previously 13.25%), so only slow easing ahead.[7] A high rate is the real's best defense: it attracts carry-trade capital.

Carry trade: Borrow money where it's cheap (e.g., eurozone at 2%), lend it in Brazil at 14.5%. As long as the real doesn't crash, the interest gap is your profit. If the real tanks hard, the party's over. This wager is what supports the real as long as Selic stays high.

It's Wednesday, June 10th. The euro is stronger against the Brazilian real than at any point this year, and that's two days before a rate decision from the European Central Bank.

The ECB's reference rate is five point nine seven five one real per euro, as of June 9th. In just one week, the euro has gained roughly two and a half percent. Here's what's remarkable: the dollar was weak in that same week. So it's not a strong dollar pushing down all emerging currencies — it's two separate forces that happen to be adding up right now.

The first force is the central bank in Frankfurt. On Thursday, it's widely expected to raise rates by a quarter percent, lifting the deposit rate from two to two point two five percent. This goes against the trend, because most central banks are cutting right now. The ECB is doing the opposite because the oil-price shock from the Persian Gulf crisis has kept inflation in the eurozone elevated. Higher rates make the euro more attractive, so money flows in and the euro rises — even before the decision.

The second force is a penalty specifically for the real. In early June, the US trade office proposed a twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian imports. It's just a proposal; the hearing is in July. But markets price in the risk ahead of time. That's exactly when the real weakened, even though other risky assets were actually gaining on the Iran-Israel ceasefire signal. That disconnect tells us: this real weakness is Brazil's own problem, not a global thing.

But be careful — this high rests on an expectation. If the rate hike arrives on Thursday and everyone already priced it in, the euro could actually slip back a bit.
