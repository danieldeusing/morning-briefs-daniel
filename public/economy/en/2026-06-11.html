Foreign Exchange in detail — where we stand & why

It's ECB day, and the euro heads in with tailwind. The ECB Fix climbed from 5.9751 on June 9 to 5.9896 on June 10 — the yearly high, a hair's breadth under the round 6.00.[1] The cross-check is clean: USD/BRL 5.1907 × EUR/USD 1.1539 equals 5.9895 against the published fix of 5.9896 — a deviation of 0.001%, so full consistency.[2]

What has changed since Monday — the diff. Monday, the story was "euro strong, dollar weak": the DXY fell, so the euro boost came only from the euro side and homegrown real weakness. This week the dollar has turned. The DXY has climbed from ~99.7 to over 100.[3]

DXY (Dollar Index): a basket that measures the dollar against six major currencies. When it rises, the dollar is globally more expensive. Think of the dollar as a team captain: when he gets stronger, typically all emerging currencies — including the real — take a hit. That's exactly what's now amplifying the EUR/BRL move from the dollar side.

That means: The real is now being pressed from two sides — a firmer global dollar and its own risk premium from the US tariff dispute. This makes the yearly high more robust than on Monday, when only one force was at play.

But the main driver remains the ECB. The market is pricing with ~100% a move to 2.25% (deposit rate) — the first hike of this cycle, driven by the oil-price boost from the Hormuz crisis, which kept euro inflation at ~3%.[4] Money is flowing into the euro in advance because higher rates make it more attractive to hold.

Today the market is pricing with ~100% the move to 2.25% — the first hike since the start of the easing cycle.[4] The driver is imported oil-price boost, not hot growth. COPOM June 16–17: consensus shifts to cut

Brazil last cut on April 29 to 14.50% (second cut in a row, prior 14.75% in March).[5] New: The market now largely expects June 17 to see another cut to 14.25% — XP, Itaú, and Goldman; only Santander sees a pause.[6] This is a shift from the hawkish April minutes. The key is tomorrow's IPCA: a hot number would flip the cut.

Carry trade: Borrow where it's cheap (Eurozone ~2%) and lend in Brazil at ~14.5%. The rate spread is your gain, as long as the real doesn't crash. If COPOM cuts, that spread shrinks — the real loses a piece of its main shield.

It's Thursday, June 11th — and it's ECB day. Today at 09:15 Brazilian time, the European Central Bank decides on interest rates, and the euro heads into this day with tailwind.

The ECB reference rate stands at 5.9896 reals per euro, as of June 10. That's the yearly high, just a hair's breadth under the round 6.00 mark. The market expects with nearly 100 percent certainty that the ECB will hike rates today by a quarter percentage point. The deposit rate rises from 2 to 2.25 percent. This is the first increase of this cycle, triggered by the oil-price shock from the Persian Gulf crisis, which has kept inflation in the eurozone high.

What has shifted since Monday is the dollar. Early in the week it was weak; the story was simply euro strong and real weak. Now the dollar index has climbed over 100. That means the real now faces pressure from two directions: a firmer global dollar and euro strength. That makes the yearly high more robust than it was on Monday.

For Daniel, that means concretely: The exchange window is still the best of the year today, nearly 6 reals per euro. But be careful. The rate hike is already priced in. If it materializes as expected at 09:15, the euro could even pull back slightly afterward. Traders call that selling the fact. Those who are flexible can wait for Christine Lagarde's tone at midday: if she keeps the door open for a second summer hike, the euro stays on top. If she sounds reassuring, it can turn.

On the Brazilian side, there are two important dates. Tomorrow morning at 8 o'clock, the statistics office releases the May inflation figure. The expectation is an increase between 0.5 and 0.6 percent compared to the previous month. This is the most important data point because it determines what the central bank does next week.

And here is a real shift: The market now largely expects the central bank to cut its benchmark rate on June 17, from 14.5 to 14.25 percent. Only Banco Santander is betting on a pause. A cut would be bad for the real because it shrinks the high rate that has been attracting capital to the country. But a hot inflation number tomorrow could still flip that cut.

My outlook for today is two-fold: Before the decision it stays calm, then comes movement. For the day I'm looking at a range of 5.93 to 6.03, with confidence of roughly 65 percent. For the whole week I see a strong euro between 5.95 and 6.05. These probabilities are my assessment, not the market price.

Tomorrow the brief will revolve around two things: how the ECB decision from today actually plays out, and the Brazilian inflation figure for May, which comes just before the central bank's rate decision next week.
