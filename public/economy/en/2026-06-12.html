FX in detail — where we stand & why

The ECB delivered, and it played out exactly as I flagged yesterday as the main risk. The ECB fix fell from 5.9896 on 06/10 to 5.9536 on 06/11 — a retreat of 0.60% away from the year high, away from the round 6.00 level.[1] The cross-check is clean: USD/BRL 5.1604 × EUR/USD 1.1537 gives 5.9536 against the published fix of 5.9536 — deviation 0.001%, full consistency.[2]

The "sell the fact" mechanism, spelled out. The hike was ~100% priced in. Markets trade expectations, not events: money flowed into the euro before the announcement because everyone expected the hike. When it then landed, the good news was already in the price — so traders took profits, and the euro fell. That's not contradictory to hawkish Lagarde commentary; it's the textbook case.

"Sell the fact": Like a long-awaited concert — anticipation drives ticket prices up, on the night itself the excitement flattens. When everyone has expected the hike, by the time it arrives it's already baked in; afterward it gets sold. That's exactly what pulled the euro from its year high, even though the rate decision was "positive."

Lagarde was hawkish anyway. She explicitly rejected the "insurance hike" label — meaning not a one-off precautionary step but a shift in the base case. ING expects a second hike in July or September, but warns against aggressive pace (weak growth, only 0.8% expected for 2026).[3] For the euro that means: supported over the medium term, but digesting "buy the rumour, sell the fact" in the near term.

Central banks & macro

ECB: first hike since 2023 — and it continues [3]

On 06/11 the ECB raised the deposit rate to 2.25% (main refinancing 2.40%) — the first step up in nearly three years.[4] The driver is imported oil-price surge from the Hormuz/Iran crisis, which pushed eurozone inflation above 3% — not hot growth. Lagarde called the decision "robust across scenarios" and rejected the "insurance hike" label: a hawkish signal. ING expects the next step in July or September, but warns against pace (growth only 0.8% expected for 2026).[3]

US PPI jumps to 6.5% — Fed hike back in play [5]

New as of yesterday: US Producer Prices (PPI) rose in May to 6.5% YoY (April 5.7%) — the highest level since November 2022.[5] The market now prices 43% odds for a Fed hike in December (a month ago 14%). That supports the dollar and is relevant for Daniel: a firmer dollar typically weakens the real and lifts EUR/BRL.

PPI (Producer Price Index): measures what manufacturers pay upstream for their inputs before the shelf. Like the wholesale price before the supermarket adds its markup. When PPI rises steeply, it often reaches the consumer months later — an early warning for inflation that makes the Fed nervous.

The deposit rate rose from two to two point two five percent. And Christine Lagarde stayed stern: she explicitly rejected the idea that this was just a cautious insurance step, and left the door open for a second hike this summer. But the euro fell. The reference rate dropped from five point nine eight nine six real on June tenth to five point nine five three six on June eleventh, a drop of zero point six percent.

That's exactly the risk I called the main danger yesterday: traders call it selling the fact. The hike was already priced in. Money flowed into the euro ahead of time because everyone expected it. When it then happened, the good part was already in the price, and traders took profits. That's not a contradiction to Lagarde's stern tone; it's the textbook case.

That supports the dollar, and a stronger dollar typically weakens the real.
