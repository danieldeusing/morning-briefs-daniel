FX in Detail — Where We Stand & Why

The ECB-fix fell for the second straight day: from 5.9536 on 11.06. to 5.9076 on 12.06. — a drop of 0.77%, cumulative −1.37% from the year high on 10.06. (5.9896).[1] The cross-check aligns exactly: USD/BRL 5.1073 × EUR/USD 1.1567 = 5.9076 against the published fix 5.9076 — deviation 0.0%, full consistency.[2]

Two forces pushed the real stronger — both I flagged yesterday. First, the hot IPCA: May inflation came in at +0.58% above consensus (expected 0.52–0.55%). Just as I wrote here Friday, "hot" paradoxically helps the real — stubborn inflation keeps rates elevated, and high rates attract foreign capital. Second, the Iran turn: the deal prospect flipped to "risk-on," and in those phases money flows back into emerging-market currencies like the real.

"Risk-on / Risk-off": The market's mood. On "risk-off" (crisis, war), everyone hunkers down — into dollars, gold. On "risk-on" (easing), they venture back out and buy riskier, higher-yielding assets — like Brazilian ones. The Iran deal hope flipped the weather Friday from "off" to "on".

But the real blockbuster is oil. Brent tanked over 4% to ~$86.71 — a two-month low.[3] The trigger: a 14-point draft to reopen Hormuz in 30 days, with possible signature this weekend. This is explosive because the ECB hiked on Thurday for this very reason. If oil falls for good, the inflation boost loses steam — and the July/September follow-up hike analysts expect becomes harder to justify. Caveat: Trump forcefully denies Iran's leaked conditions ("have NOTHING to do with what we agreed") — the deal is not yet done.

It's Saturday, June thirteenth. The big story of the day is a reversal: the reasoning the European Central Bank gave when it raised rates Thursday was already crumbling by Friday.

The ECB hiked precisely because of the oil shock from the Iran crisis and the blocked Strait of Hormuz. And Friday, Donald Trump said an agreement with Iran could be signed this very weekend in Europe. The draft says Hormuz reopens within thirty days. Oil prices crashed as a result, down more than four percent to around eighty-eight dollars a barrel — the lowest level in two months.

An important caveat: Trump has flatly disputed the terms Iran put out. So the deal is not yet done, and if it breaks, oil prices will shoot right back up.

From Brazil came May inflation at the same time. It came in hotter than expected at zero point five eight percent, and the year-on-year rate climbed to four point seven two percent, above the ceiling. But here's the key: the core inflation cooled. Exactly as I predicted yesterday, that mix supported the real, because high rates pull in foreign money.

The reference rate fell for the second straight day as a result, to five point nine zero seven six real per euro. That's more than one percent below the year high and further from the six-mark.

For Daniel concretely: the exchange window stays good, above five point nine real per euro. But the trend is turning. Both big drivers — falling oil and appetite for risk — are pointing toward a weaker euro. Anyone with flexibility should watch the weekend: if the Iran deal holds, the euro could slip further Monday.

My forecast for this week: a range of five point eighty-four to five point ninety-six, with a confidence of roughly fifty-five percent. Most likely I see five point eighty-eight. These probabilities are my own judgment, not the market price.

The next big switch is the Brazilian central bank Tuesday and Wednesday. Despite the hot headline inflation, one final small rate cut to fourteen point two five percent remains most likely — precisely because the core inflation is easing. That's the story for the next edition.
