FX in detail — where we stand

Recap of the three moves that shifted EUR/BRL this week

1 · ECB raises — and the euro doesn't flinch [1][2]

On June 11, the European Central Bank raised its three key rates by 25 basis points; the deposit rate now stands at 2.25% — the first hike in roughly three years. Rationale: the Iran war has driven energy prices higher (energy inflation in May +10.9%), the ECB raised its 2026 HICP forecast from 2.6% to 3.0% and calls the risks "tilted to the upside".

Basis point (bps): One hundredth of a percentage point — like a cent to the euro. 25 bps = 0.25%. When a central bank raises rates, its currency becomes more attractive to savers because it yields more interest. Normally this strengthens the currency.

"Normally" — but that's exactly what didn't happen this week. EUR/USD stayed flat around 1.155, near a two-month low. Why? The market had already priced in the hike, and at the same time US inflation (CPI ~4.2% y/y) keeps the dollar bid firm. For you, that means: the ECB hike didn't lever EUR/BRL higher — the euro leg is neutral, the move comes from the dollar leg.

2 · Iran deal presses Brent, lifts the dollar [3][4]

Brent crude fell ~3.4% to ~$87.3/barrel (as of June 12) because markets expect a US-Iran deal with reopening of the Strait of Hormuz — over the month, Brent is down ~17%. Trump announced signing for today; Iran's foreign minister pushed back on June 13 ("unlikely today").

Iran-deal hope→ Brent −3.4%→ risk-on, US growth supported→ dollar firm→ USD/BRL ~5.19

For EUR/BRL, headwind: lower oil eases Europe's inflation (good for the euro), but the dollar strength from the risk-on surge weighs more in the cross. Net: EUR/BRL stays anchored around 5.91.

Positioning & BR rates [6][7][8]

Foreign sentiment on Brazil is split. The EWZ (the largest US-listed Brazil equity ETF — basically "a basket of Brazil for dollar investors") saw −$450M outflows over 1 month, −$148M over 5 days — but +$1.23B inflows over 3 months. Nervous near-term, still constructive mid-term.

Bank of America downgraded Brazil to neutral this week — reasoning: Selic stays higher longer due to fiscal pressure. Brazil's 5Y CDS last at 125.42 bps (as of Dec 2025, stale) — the source showed a "December 6" date, value not confirmed as current as of June 14.

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against sovereign default — the price (in basis points) shows how risky the market thinks a country is. Like car insurance: the higher the premium, the riskier the driver.

Selic stands at 14.50% (Copom cut 25 bps on April 29), real rate (Selic minus inflation) at ~10.9% — tops globally and the main reason the real doesn't collapse despite fiscal noise.

It's Sunday, June fourteenth. Equity markets are closed, the real rate sleeping — but the week behind us has shifted the picture for your euro payments. The EUR/BRL anchor sits around five point nine one, the last ECB fix from June twelfth.

The big news comes from Frankfurt: the European Central Bank has raised rates for the first time in roughly three years, by a quarter percent to two point two five. The reason is energy inflation from the Iran war. And yet the euro barely moved — it's still around one point one five five against the dollar. Why? Because the market expected the hike, and because the dollar is globally stronger right now. For you, that means: the ECB hike doesn't lift EUR/BRL — the move comes from the dollar.

And the dollar comes from two directions. First: a possible US-Iran peace deal. Trump says it will be signed today, Iran's foreign minister pushes back. The oil price fell on the hope by just over three percent, to around eighty-seven dollars — and that risk appetite strengthens the dollar and pressures the real. Second, and that's the more important date: the Federal Reserve meeting on Tuesday and Wednesday. A rate change is ruled out nearly completely, but the tone counts. If new Fed Chair Warsh turns hawkish, the dollar rallies.

In Brazil, fiscal policy is noisy. In the Senate this week sits a spending package of three hundred eighty-six billion reals plus a pension add-on of ninety-nine billion. Bank of America downgraded Brazil to neutral as a result. Against that, the real interest rate of almost eleven percent holds the real up — that's tops globally and a magnet for foreign money.

My takeaway for the five horizons: today sideways around five point nine one; this week steady, between five point eight five and five point ninety-five, with the Fed statement as the trigger; this month a mild upside risk for the rate through the fiscal issue. At five point nine one, as a euro seller you're in the friendlier half of the year's range — but before the Fed meeting, the risk is two-sided. The probabilities are my estimate, not the market.

Tomorrow the markets reopen, and the week start will be driven by the Iran headline and Fed expectations. That's where I look next.
