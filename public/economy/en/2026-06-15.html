FX in detail — where we stand & why

Monday full analysis · anchors live + dated, forecast confidence is my assessment

EUR/BRL trades today at 5.85 (implied spot) — at the low end of June's range (low 5.80992 on 06/03, high 5.96418 on 06/08[1]). Two forces are pulling, and they're nearly canceling each other out.

The euro side is surprisingly weak. The ECB raised on Thursday for the first time since 2023 — all three policy rates +25 bp, deposit rate now 2.25%.[2] A rate hike normally makes a currency more attractive (higher savings rates draw capital). Yet the euro didn't rise; EUR/USD is stuck at ~1.1567 — its lowest level since early April.[3]

"Priced in": imagine everyone knows weeks ahead that a store will cut prices on Friday — on Friday itself, there's no rush because everyone already planned. Same here: the market had fully expected the ECB hike, so the confirmation barely moved the euro. Once it was done, eyes immediately shifted back to the US.

And in the US, inflation is hot. May CPI came in at 4.2% — the highest since April 2023.[4] That keeps the dollar firm, because hot inflation stops the Fed from cutting rates. High US rates = strong dollar = pressure on the real. Bottom line: the euro isn't strengthening against the dollar, so EUR/BRL stays capped from above.

ECB +25 bp (priced in)→ EUR/USD stays ~1.157→ US CPI 4.2% supports USD→ EUR/BRL capped

The real side gets tailwinds from oil. Brent has fallen −5.09% to $82.89[5] — after Trump signaled that oil shipments from the Persian Gulf could resume soon and the US blockade of Iranian ports would be lifted.[6] From the ~$120 war peak, that's nearly −26% this month. Cheaper oil means less import inflation for Brazil and a firmer real — that pushes EUR/BRL down.

It's Monday, June fifteenth, and this is a twin interest-rate week. On Wednesday, two central banks decide on the same day — Brazil's central bank on the Selic and the US Federal Reserve on its policy rate. Both affect Daniel's EUR to BRL calculation, in opposite directions.

The euro-to-real trades at around five point eight five in the live spot rate. The European Central Bank's official reference rate from Friday sits at five point nine zero. This gap of nearly one percent is not an error — the two rates are two trading days apart, and the real has strengthened since Friday. For your exchange timing, today's spot is what matters.

The big news from last week: the European Central Bank raised rates for the first time since twenty twenty-three — all three policy rates up by a quarter percent, the deposit rate to two point two five percent. Yet the euro did not climb. The market had fully expected the move, so the confirmation barely changed anything. Immediately after, attention turned back to the United States.

And there, inflation is running hot. The consumer price index in May came in at four point two percent, the highest level since April twenty twenty-three. That keeps the dollar firm and caps the euro-to-real from above.

On the Brazil side, there is tailwind from oil. Brent crude fell by more than five percent to just under eighty-three dollars per barrel, after Trump signaled that oil shipments from the Persian Gulf could soon resume. Over the month, that is almost twenty-six percent lower. Cheap oil means less import inflation for Brazil and a stronger real.

Wednesday is the hinge of the week. Brazil's central bank might cut its benchmark rate from fourteen point five to fourteen point two five percent, as XP, Itaú, and Goldman expect. But inflation is stuck at four point seven two percent, above the tolerance ceiling — a hold would not be a surprise, and I think the hold case is underappreciated.

The biggest risk to the real is homegrown. The Senate is pushing what they call a Pauta-Bomba — populist spending bills worth three hundred eighty-six billion reais, which tear deep holes in the budget. Bank of America downgraded Brazilian financial stocks to neutral because of it.

On balance: disinflation from cheap oil pulls the euro-to-real down, fiscal risk and threatened US tariffs pull it up. The standoff keeps the rate in the five-eighty corridor for now. Tomorrow will be quiet — the real moves come Wednesday with the twin rate decision.
