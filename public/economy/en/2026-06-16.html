FX in Detail — where we stand & why

The spot is calm (5.8613, ECB fix from yesterday), but beneath the surface two forces pull EUR/BRL in opposite directions. This exact stalemate holds the rate flat — until Wednesday (FOMC afternoon, Copom evening, 18:30 BRT) when the central banks break it.

Carry / Rate differential: Brazil pays 14.5% interest, the eurozone 2.25%. Picture two savings accounts — one pays next to nothing, the other pays a lot. Money flows to high yield, as long as the currency doesn't crater there. This rate advantage is why the real stays so firm despite Brazilian debt worries.

Force 1 — euro leg (up): The ECB has hiked for the first time since 2023. Higher euro rates make the euro more attractive, pushing EUR/BRL structurally upward.

Force 2 — real leg (down): Brent falls to around $81 (TradingEconomics $80.94, as of 06-16), the DXY is soft (99.56), and Brazil's carry remains huge. All three support the real and push EUR/BRL down.

Net: sideways around 5.86. Until Warsh or Copom pulls one of the two levers harder.

Central Banks — the week that matters

Fed: Warsh's trial by fire USA

Kevin Warsh — sworn in 05-22 (Senate 54–45) — chairs his first meeting on 06-17. The rate hold at 3.50–3.75% is 97.4% certain. What the market pays for is the tone: if Warsh shifts the bias from "easing" to "neutral," the dollar rises — and the real faces headwinds.

My take: Warsh is known as a hawk. A hawkish first message would not break his reputation but confirm it — and the market is not fully priced for that. That is the week's bigger surprise risk.

Copom: cut or pause? BR

Selic stands at 14.50% (last cut April, −25 bp). Consensus for 06-17 (two-day meeting 06-16–06-17, decision 06-17 evening 18:30 BRT) expects another −25 bp to 14.25%. The problem: IPCA rose to 4.72% in May — above the 4.5% ceiling of the target band. A central bank that cuts while inflation sits above its ceiling sends a muddled signal.

Inflation target band: Brazil aims for 3% inflation, tolerates ±1.5 points — so the ceiling is 4.5%. If measured inflation exceeds that (4.72%), it's like a speedometer already in the red: pushing harder (= cutting) feels risky.

My view: a pause would be the clean answer to the data, but Copom has signaled an easing path. Cut anyway and it costs credibility — and that may weaken the real near-term.

ECB: first hike since 2023 ECB

The ECB raised the key rate (deposit rate) by 25 bp to 2.25% — the first hike since 2023, driven by energy inflation (eurozone inflation 3.2% in May, energy costs +10.9% y/y). The ECB expects 3.0% inflation average for 2026, 2.3% for 2027.

For you: the euro has structural tailwind. Higher euro rates draw capital into the eurozone, the euro appreciates — and EUR/BRL sits higher from the euro side, regardless of what the real does.

It's Tuesday, June sixteenth. The spot market is calm — the euro is at five point eight six one three reals, the European Central Bank's reference rate from yesterday. But this is the quiet before an important week, not after. Two central banks decide in the next day, and both tug on your exchange rate.

On Wednesday, Kevin Warsh chairs his first meeting as head of the Federal Reserve. That interest rates stay at three point five to three point seven five percent is all but certain — nearly ninety-eight percent according to the FedWatch tool. What matters is the tone of his first press conference. Warsh is known as a hawk. If he sounds tough, the dollar strengthens, and the Brazilian real faces headwinds.

That same Wednesday evening, around half past six Brasília time, Brazil's central bank, Copom, announces its decision. The policy rate stands at fourteen point five percent. The market expects a cut of one quarter point to fourteen point two five. The problem: inflation rose in May to four point seven two percent — above the ceiling of the target band. A cut despite inflation that is too high would be a muddled signal. A pause would be the cleaner answer.

The European Central Bank has already acted: it raised the rate for the first time since twenty twenty-three, one quarter point to two point two five percent, because of high energy prices. That gives the euro tailwind and tends to push the euro-real rate higher.

On the other side, cheap oil supports the real. Brent has fallen to around eighty-one dollars because a deal between the US and Iran is closer — a Trump representative speaks of eighty percent probability. In April oil was above one hundred eight dollars. Cheaper oil means less inflation pressure in Brazil.

On balance these forces pull each other flat — the euro lever up, the oil and carry lever down. That's why the rate is at five point eight six. Only Warsh and Copom break the stalemate. My forecast: this week a wider range between five point eighty and five point ninety-two, most likely five point eighty-seven.
