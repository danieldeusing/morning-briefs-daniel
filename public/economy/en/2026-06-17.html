Currencies in Detail · Diff from Monday

Where we stand, why — and the five forecast stages.

Update since Mon The real has firmed slightly since week start, driven by falling oil. EUR/BRL hovering around 5.90, USD/BRL dipped below 5.09. This week's dominant driver isn't inflation but oil + the dual rate decision.

Carry trade: Investors borrow money where rates are low (USA, eurozone) and park it where they're high (Brazil, Selic 14.50%). Like a savings account earning 14% instead of 3%. As long as the real stays stable, it's self-sustaining — and precisely this rate advantage props up the real, even with shaky fiscal position.

Top move in the last 72h — oil down, real up: The US-Iran peace MOU signed on 06-15 at the G7 opens the Strait of Hormuz; first tankers depart today. Brent fell from above $100 (blockade high) toward $79, down 4.8% Monday alone. For Brazil as a net energy exporter and global risk appetite, cheap, stable oil is tailwind:

Headwind: The ECB hike from 06-11 (+25 bp to 2.25%, first increase since 2023) makes the euro fundamentally firmer — that pulls EUR/BRL higher and fights the oil tailwind. On balance: standoff, hence sideways move around 5.90.

Copom · 18:30 BRT — open

Selic stands at 14.50%. Market leans toward a −25 bp cut to 14.25% (~84.5% betting odds), but other forecast markets see the cut chance closer to ~67% and a hold at ~33% — hikes ruled out. Hot inflation (IPCA 4.72%, Focus forecast 5.11% for 2026) makes a hold real possible. Not yet announced. A cut weakens the real on average (less carry); a surprise hold props it up.

Heads up Both figures above are expectations, not announced results. At 06:15 BRT deadline, no decision was published. Check the actual outcome after 15:00 or 18:30 BRT.
Dot Plot: The anonymous dot cloud where each Fed member marks where they see the policy rate in coming years. Like a secret class survey "Where's the rate at end 2026?" — the median reveals the committee's mood. Warsh has little use for it, hence the suspense over whether he'll even submit dots.

It's Wednesday, June 17th — the loudest central bank day of the month. Two major rate decisions are happening today, and both are still pending as I'm speaking. At three p.m. Brazilian time, the US Federal Reserve decides. At six-thirty p.m., the Brazilian central bank follows with the Selic decision.

At the Fed, a hold is expected — the policy rate will likely stay between three and a half and three point seven-five percent. What's interesting isn't the outcome but the first public appearance by new Chair Kevin Warsh and whether the central bank will remove its willingness to cut rates from the statement. U.S. inflation ran at four point two percent in May — too high to cut.

In Brazil, the market is leaning toward a small quarter-point cut, down to fourteen point two-five percent Selic. But inflation is running hot — four point seven-two percent in May, above target. So some analysts also see a hold as possible. Important: both are expectations, not announced results. The real outcome comes this afternoon and evening.

On the euro-real rate, the live market today sits around five point nine. Yesterday's official ECB reference was five point eight six six nine — that looks like a gap, but it's just timing, because the reference rate is one day old. If you work out the cross cleanly using today's numbers, you get five point nine. Everything checks out.

The biggest tailwind for the real comes from oil. The U.S.-Iran peace deal opens the Strait of Hormuz, the first tankers sail today, and Brent crude has dropped to around seventy-nine dollars a barrel — down almost five percent just Monday. Cheap, stable oil supports the real. Working against it is a firmer euro, because the European Central Bank raised rates for the first time since 2023 last week.

For your exchange timing: no rush today. A sharp tone from the Fed or a rate cut in Brazil could briefly push the rate toward five point nine-four — then better to wait. A gentle Fed tone plus a surprise hold in Brazil would firm the real and open a cheaper window. The clearer picture comes only after six-thirty p.m.
