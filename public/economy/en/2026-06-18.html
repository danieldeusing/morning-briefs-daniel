DXY — the Dollar Index, explained
Why this one number matters for your EUR/BRL forecast.
DXY (Dollar Index): a basket measuring the US dollar against six major currencies — by far the largest weight is the euro. Imagine a scale: on one side the dollar, on the other all the other major currencies combined. When DXY rises, the dollar is globally stronger.

For you, this is an early warning signal: A rising DXY typically means (1) a weaker euro against the dollar and (2) headwinds for emerging-market currencies like the real. Both paths end at EUR/BRL — the first pulls it down (weak euro), the second up (weak real). Which wins determines the short-term direction. Yesterday (+0.50 %) the real path won: BRL weakened, USD/BRL firmed.

Currencies in detail — where we stand & why

The dual hawk and what it means for EUR/BRL.

Yesterday was central bank day on both sides of the equator — and both institutions turned harder. This is the decisive move for your calculation, because the two effects on the real partly neutralize each other.

Brazil [BR]: The COPOM (Brazil's central bank rate-setting committee) cut the Selic benchmark rate by 25 basis points to 14.25 % — the third 25 bp step in the easing cycle that began at 15.00 % in March.[1] Sounds real-negative (lower rates = less incentive to hold reals). But the real story is in the statement: The committee dropped its pledge to cut further, writing instead that "future steps depend on new data."[3] At the same time, it raised its own inflation forecast for 2026 to 5.2 % — well above the 4.5 % target.[2] Translation: We're cutting slower and inflation still needs more work. That is real-positive — higher rates for longer.

Hawkish: central bank tone pointing to higher or longer-sustained rates, to fight inflation. Like a hawk hunting (= inflation). The opposite is "dovish" — softer, leaning toward rate cuts.

USA [USA]: The Fed held its benchmark rate at 3.50–3.75 % — Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair, decision unanimous 12:0.[4] Here too, the fine print matters: The "Dot-Plot" (the anonymous dot cloud where each member enters their rate forecast) turned hawkish — 9 of 18 now see a hike sometime in 2026, the median endpoint rose from 3.4 % to 3.8 %.[5] Wall Street hated it: S&P −1.21 %, Nasdaq −1.34 %, Dow −507 points — per market commentary, the sharpest reaction to a Fed chair debut since 1994.[5]

Net effect on you: COPOM hardness supports the real, Fed hardness supports the dollar — on USD/BRL the two largely offset (the real weakened only slightly after FOMC). For EUR/BRL, the euro decides: A hawkish Fed Dot-Plot pressures EUR/USD, and a weaker euro pulls EUR/BRL lower — even if the real stays flat. That's precisely the path that could slightly cheapen your exchange today.

Carry trade (real): borrow where it's cheap (e.g., dollars at low rates) and lend in Brazil at high rates. As long as the real doesn't collapse, the rate spread is your profit. This exact carry is what keeps the real steady despite the cut — the COPOM signals "rates stay high." If the real falls, the party ends: that's why the fiscal/election tail risk is this trade's Achilles heel.

Why EUR/BRL doesn't fall sharply despite the strong dollar: Brazil's fiscal tail (see right column) sustains a risk premium on the real. The carry advantage and debt risk pull against each other — the result is more of a tight sideways band around the current fix than a clear direction. For your exchange, that means: no rush to act, but a slightly favorable day if the strong dollar keeps pushing EUR/USD lower.

It's Thursday, June eighteenth. Yesterday was the most important day for your exchange rate in weeks — because two central banks made decisions, and both turned harder.

In Brazil, the central bank cut its benchmark rate by a quarter-point to fourteen point two five percent. But the statement was tough: the promise of further cuts was dropped, and the bank's own inflation forecast for this year rose to five point two percent — well above target. That supports the real.

In the United States, the Fed under new chair Kevin Warsh held rates steady, but the members' dot-plot forecast also turned hawkish: nine of eighteen now see a rate hike this year. Wall Street hated it — the Dow fell over five hundred points, the sharpest reaction to a Fed chair debut since nineteen ninety-four.

For you, the two effects partly cancel: Brazilian hardness supports the real, American hardness supports the dollar. So the dollar-real rate doesn't move much. For euro-real, the euro decides — and a strong dollar weakens the euro, which makes your exchange slightly cheaper today.

The euro-real reference rate yesterday was five point eight nine two nine. The cross-check against the dollar rates matches to three decimal places — the number is solid. My bias for this week: sideways to slightly firm, ranging from five point eighty-four to five point ninety-two.

In the background, good news for inflation: after the US-Iran deal, the Strait of Hormuz is open again, and oil prices are falling — Brent under seventy-eight dollars. That takes some pressure off global interest rates.

The brake remains Brazil's budget: gross debt is over eighty percent of GDP, and twenty twenty-six is an election year. That's why the real can only partly gain from high rates.

What to watch next week: Monday brings the first market survey after yesterday's decision. Then we'll see if economists share the central bank's inflation concern — and whether my sideways bias holds.
