DXY — the Dollar Index, explained
Why this single number matters for your EUR/BRL forecast.
DXY (Dollar Index): a basket measuring the U.S. dollar against six major currencies — by far most heavily weighted toward the euro. Think of a seesaw: the dollar on one side, all other major currencies on the other. When DXY rises, the dollar is globally stronger.

For you, it's an early warning signal: A rising DXY usually means (1) a weaker euro against the dollar and (2) headwinds for emerging-market currencies like the real. Both paths feed into EUR/BRL — the first pulls it down (weaker euro), the second pushes it up (weaker real). Which one wins determines near-term direction. Currently at multi-month highs — the hawkish FOMC dot-plot has strengthened the dollar globally.

FX in Detail — Where We Stand & Why

After the hawkish double: the new dated real risk is tariffs.

Market tape is quiet today — EUR/BRL virtually flat at 5.8989, USD/BRL intraday slightly firmer for the real, DXY at multi-month highs.[1][2] This fits with digesting Wednesday's central bank double-punch: COPOM hawkish despite a cut (Selic to 14.25%, forward guidance dropped, 2026 inflation guidance raised to 5.2%)[3] and Fed hawkish despite holding (dot-plot median 2026 up from 3.4% to 3.8%, PCE forecast up from 2.7% to 3.6%, 9 of 18 members expect ≥1 hike by year-end).[4][5]

But the real new element for your horizon is the trade conflict: On June 2, U.S. Trade Representative Jamieson Greer announced a 25% special tariff on Brazilian imports under Section 301 — as punishment for "illegal deforestation, ethanol market access, and anti-corruption deficits." The USTR hearing is scheduled for July 6.[6] This is no longer a vague "might happen" risk, but a concrete date within your 30-day forecast window.

Section 301: A provision in U.S. trade law allowing the president to unilaterally impose tariffs if USTR finds "unfair trade practices" by a partner — no WTO process involved. Like having a citation power without a court. The 25% surcharge would stack on top of the 40% tariffs in effect since August 2025 on footwear and other non-ag goods; meat and coffee were exempted by Executive Order in November 2025.[7]

Why this matters for your exchange: An extra 25% surcharge pushes Brazilian export goods further out of the U.S. market. Brazil's 2025 response (record export growth +$11.6B despite U.S. shipments down −$2.6B)[8] shows diversification capability — but the near-term path to a new buyer pipeline runs through a weaker real. The market hasn't fully priced this risk yet because hearing dates often collide with last-minute deals. That's exactly why July 6 is a hard waypoint: if no deal, the rule takes effect — the real reacts.

Against it, three tails: the fiscal tail (2026 election year), the tariff tail (July 6), and the global dollar path (DXY at multi-month highs). For your FX positioning, that means: no rush to act in summer, but a concrete watchlist with a hard date.
