Forex in detail — where we stand & why

The whole week turns on one theme for your EUR/BRL exposure: the dollar has become globally stronger. The dollar index (DXY) climbed to 100.79 — the highest level since May 2025. The driver is not Brazil and not Europe, but the U.S. central bank, the Fed.

Dot plot: once a quarter, each Fed member draws a dot on a board — at what level they see the policy rate at year-end. Imagine 18 weathermen each placing a flag on a temperature scale. This time the flags have shifted higher: 9 of 18 expect a rate hike in 2026 — three months ago, almost nobody expected one.

This reversal comes from the new Fed chair Warsh and surprisingly high U.S. inflation (4.2% in May). Higher U.S. rates pull capital into the dollar — that's the mechanism putting the real under pressure.[3][4]

Fed dots shift higher→ DXY heading toward one-year high→ USD globally stronger→ BRL under carry pressure→ EUR/BRL held/firmer
Carry trade (BRL): borrow where it's cheap (e.g., U.S.-adjacent), lend in Brazil at high rates — you pocket the interest gap, as long as the real doesn't crash. When U.S. rates rise, the cheap side gets pricier and the incentive to stay in the real shrinks. That's exactly what's happening now: the carry becomes less attractive, capital flows back to the dollar.

The euro itself is not a driver this week. True, the ECB raised all three policy rates by 25 basis points on 06-11 (deposit rate now 2.25%) — but EUR/USD fell anyway, because the Fed sounds even more hawkish. For you, that means: this week the EUR/BRL exchange rate hinges more on the dollar-vs.-real leg than the euro leg. As long as both central banks lean toward "tighter," the cross stays high.

Copom: cut with brakes on [1][2]

On the surface, an easing: the Copom — Brazil's central bank monetary policy council — unanimously cut the Selic on 06-17 by 25 basis points to 14.25%. But the accompanying text reads restrictively (in the jargon: "hawkish").

Forward guidance: when the central bank signals in its text what it plans to do next — a kind of weather report for rates. This time, the Copom dropped the sentence in which it had previously signaled it would "continue the cutting cycle." Translation: the next cut is no longer promised.

On top of that, the bank raised its own inflation forecast for 2026 from 4.6% to 5.2% — well above the 4.5% ceiling of its inflation target. It pushed back the date when inflation would return to target to early 2028 (previously end of 2027). That's the honest message behind the cut: We're cutting, but we're far from target and could pause soon.

In the background is election year: President Lula is running stimulus measures ahead of the October vote, and the Copom warns itself that this stimulus "weakens some of the usual transmission channels" of monetary policy. BCB chief Galipolo also flagged El Niño as a risk to food prices. Capital Economics still expects 50 basis points of cuts over the next four meetings (Selic 13.75% by year-end) — the Focus market sees 13.50%.

It's Saturday, June twentieth. Today is a trading holiday, but the week before was rare: three major central banks moved in the same direction, and all three sound more restrictive than their numbers suggest. For your euro-to-real exposure, that means one thing above all — the dollar is gaining strength.

The main driver comes from the United States. The Fed kept its policy rate at three point five to three point seven five percent, but the internal rate projection shifted higher: nine of eighteen members expect another hike this year. That pushed the dollar index to around one hundred point eight, the highest level in over a year. A strong dollar tends to weaken the real — and that puts pressure on the euro-real cross.

In Brazil, the central bank cut its policy rate by a quarter point to fourteen point two five percent. Sounds like easing, but barely: the Copom raised its own inflation forecast to five point two percent, well above target, and dropped the sentence promising further cuts. Translation: the next cut is no longer promised, the cycle could pause soon.

The European Central Bank, meanwhile, hiked — all three policy rates by a quarter point, the deposit rate now at two point two five percent. Still, the euro fell against the dollar, because the Fed sounds even more determined. For you, that means: this week the dollar-to-real leg decides, not the euro.
