FX in detail — where we stand & why

This week is the textbook example of "diverging monetary policy" — three major central banks moving simultaneously in three different directions, and that hits your EUR/BRL directly.

Diverging monetary policy: Picture three drivers at an intersection — one hits the gas, one hits the brakes, one coasts. When central banks move rates in opposite directions, capital flows to where it earns the most interest, and exchange rates shift accordingly.

The ECB surprised [DE/EUR]. On June 11, it raised for the first time since September 2023 — +25 basis points to 2.25% deposit rate, effective June 17. This reverses eight consecutive rate cuts (Jun 2024–Jun 2025). The reason: the Iran war is pushing energy prices up, and eurozone inflation (HICP) jumped to 3.2% year-over-year in May — the highest since September 2023.[1][2]

Basis point (bp): One hundredth of a percent. "+25 bp" = the rate rises by 0.25 percentage points. Central bankers count in basis points because even tiny moves trigger large capital flows.

The Fed holds — but bares its teeth [USA]. On June 17, Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Fed chair, the policy rate stayed unanimous at 3.50–3.75%. But the key wasn't the hold — it was the "dot plot": the median expectation of the Fed's members for end-2026 rose from 3.4% to ~3.8%. Nine of eighteen members see at least one hike this year — the market is already pricing in a move as early as October.[3][4]

Dot plot: A scatter of dots where each Fed member anonymously marks where they see the rate by year-end. Like a secret betting round in the office — you see the trend, but not who placed which dot. When the median dot moves up, the dollar gets stronger.

Brazil cuts against the current [BR]. The BCB cut Selic on June 17 by 25 bp to 14.25% — the third consecutive cut in a loosening cycle running since March (15.00 → 14.75 → 14.50 → 14.25%). The kicker: it's cutting even though Brazilian inflation (IPCA) rose to 4.72% in May — the highest since September 2025, driven by the energy component at +8.90% (Hormuz effect).[5][6]

The causal chain [1][3][5]

Here's how it all flows into your exchange rate — two of three moves pull EUR/BRL higher:

ECB +25 bp→ EUR stronger→ EUR/BRL ↑
Fed hawkish→ DXY high→ BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL ↑
BCB Selic −25 bp→ BRL yield advantage shrinks→ BRL slightly weaker

Bottom line: all three central bank moves are currently working against a strong real. The fact that EUR/BRL sits at only 5.92 and not higher owes to Brazil's still-massive absolute interest-rate level (14.25% vs. 2.25% in the eurozone) — the carry trade is propping up the real.

Carry trade (BRL): Borrow where it's cheap (eurozone, 2.25%), lend in Brazil at high rates (14.25%). As long as the real doesn't collapse, the interest differential is your profit. If the real plunges hard, the party ends. That differential is exactly what's holding the real up now — even as it shrinks from both sides.

It is Sunday, June twenty-first. The foreign exchange markets are closed for the weekend, so all the rates today are from the close on Friday, the nineteenth. But the week before was anything but quiet — three major central banks moved in three different directions, and that directly affects your euro-to-real rate.

The most important move came from the European Central Bank. On June eleventh, for the first time in three years, it raised interest rates by a quarter of a percent to two point two five percent. The reason: the Iran war is pushing energy prices up, and inflation in the eurozone jumped to three point two percent in May. That makes the euro stronger and pushes your euro-to-real rate up.

The US Federal Reserve, by contrast, held steady at three point five to three point seven five percent. But the tone was decidedly more hawkish. The Fed members now expect rates at around three point eight percent by year-end, and the market is pricing in a hike as early as October. That makes the dollar strong — the dollar index is around one hundred point eight, the strongest level since May twenty twenty-five.

And Brazil? The central bank there is cutting against the tide. On June seventeenth, it lowered its key rate for the third time in a row, to fourteen point two five percent — even though Brazilian inflation rose to four point seven two percent in May, mainly from expensive energy. Bottom line: all three central bank moves are currently working against a stronger real.

The fact that your euro-to-real rate sits at only five point nine one shows the power of the so-called carry trade. Brazil still pays massive interest — fourteen point two five percent compared to two point two five percent in the eurozone. As long as that interest gap stays this wide, it makes sense to move money into Brazil, and that props up the real. But that gap is shrinking now from both sides.

Looming over everything is a fragile peace. On June seventeenth, a ceasefire framework between the US and Iran was signed electronically, with the Strait of Hormuz reopening. But follow-up talks in Switzerland were postponed on June nineteenth, and Iran threatened to close the strait again the next day. The price of oil, Brent, rose to eighty point six dollars. That is the biggest macro risk facing us this week.

For you, this means: this week I see the euro-to-real trading sideways-to-firm, somewhere between five point eighty-six and five point ninety-five, most likely around five point nine. The key date is Thursday, the twenty-fifth — that is when US inflation data for May comes out. If it runs hot, the dollar goes higher and euro-to-real likely moves toward the top of that range.
