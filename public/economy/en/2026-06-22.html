FX detail — where we stand & why

The central bank picture from last week stands: three big central banks diverging (ECB hiking, Fed hawkishly holding, BCB cutting). New today is the oil axis — and it pulls the rate in favor of the real for once.

Oil falls — and it helps the real [Global]. Brent slipped from ~80.6 $ Friday to ~79.2 $ Monday (−1.7 %) because the market expects partial Hormuz reopening after 27.06. following the US-Iran deal. Cheaper oil is disinflationary — it takes pressure off energy prices in both blocs. For Brazil, an oil importer, it also lowers the import bill. Both speak toward a firmer real.[7]

Disinflationary: Not "prices fall" (that would be deflation), but "prices rise slower than before." Picture a car easing off the gas: still moving forward, but slowing down. When oil drops, the inflationary push from fuel, power, and transport slows — the inflation engine loses momentum.

The BCB cuts against its own model [BR]. The Copom cut Selic unanimously on 17.06. by 25 bp to 14.25% — but simultaneously raised its own 2026 inflation forecast sharply: to 5.2% (from 4.6%), and for the policy horizon Q4/2027 to 3.7% — above the 3% target. BCB Chief Galípolo explicitly warned of impending El Niño weather that could drive food prices.[5][6]

Selic: Brazil's key rate, steered by the Copom committee of the central bank. Like the master dial of a floor-heating system for the whole country: the BCB turns it up, credit gets expensive and brakes the economy (and inflation); it turns it down, money gets cheaper. 14.25% is extreme by world standards — exactly what props up the real.

The kicker: a central bank cutting while forecasting more inflation signals to the market that political pressure in an election year might be at play. That's real-negative long-term — but for now the high absolute rate still masks it.

The causal path [5][7]

How the forces feed into your rate today — this time they pull against each other:

Hormuz reopening expected→ Brent −1.8%→ Inflation pressure falls→ BRL firmer→ EUR/BRL ↓
BR fiscal blowout→ Debt risk ↑→ Risk premium BRL ↑→ EUR/BRL ↑
Fed hawkish→ DXY ~101→ BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL ↑

Bottom line: oil relief pulls down, fiscal risk and strong dollar pull up. That EUR/BRL sits nearly unchanged at ~5.91 is exactly this stalemate — plus Brazil's huge carry advantage (14.25% vs. 2.25% eurozone) still propping the real.

Carry trade (BRL): Borrow cheap where money is cheap (eurozone, 2.25%), lend high in Brazil (14.25%). As long as the real doesn't crash, the rate gap is your profit. If the real tanks hard, the party ends. This gap props the real now — even as both sides erode it.

Brazil's fiscal mountain — the real risk

The biggest BRL risk isn't monetary policy, it's the budget in election year [BR]:

  • Over 700 billion R$ stimulus 2026 — a record. Only ~9 billion R$ of it (barely 4%) falls inside the debt ceiling; ~96% runs outside.[8]
  • Net public debt 67% of GDP (April 2026) — highest level since the series began in 2001.[9]
  • 24,000 new federal posts by law (5.3 billion R$/year); plus a proposal for two extra weekly rest days — both raise inflation risks per Copom on an already-tight labor market.[10]
Debt ceiling (fiscal cap): A legal cap on how fast government spending can grow per year — like a spending limit on a credit card. When a spending package runs "outside" the cap, it officially doesn't count against the limit, but gets paid for with debt anyway. That's exactly what makes the market nervous: 96% outside means the limit loses its teeth.

My read: as long as the carry advantage holds and oil stays cheap, the market forgives fiscal sins. But any escalation — another stimulus tranche, a rating warning — hits a real already sitting on 67% debt-to-GDP. That's the threshold above which EUR/BRL could break higher.

It's Monday, June twenty-second. Markets are open again, and the picture tilts slightly in your favor. The shaky peace between the US and Iran is pushing oil down — and cheap oil props up the Brazilian real. The euro-real rate stays remarkably stable just under five point ninety-one.

Here's what's happening. Brent oil fell from around eighty point six dollars Friday to about seventy-nine point two dollars today. The reason: the market expects the Strait of Hormuz to at least partially reopen after June twenty-seventh. Cheap oil slows inflation in both economic blocs — that's called disinflationary. For Brazil, which imports fuel, it also lowers the bill. Both of these support a stronger real.

But Brazil has a bigger problem: the state budget in an election year. The government is planning over seven hundred billion reals in stimulus for twenty twenty-six — a record — and about ninety-six percent of it bypasses the debt ceiling. Net public debt is at sixty-seven percent of GDP, the highest level in twenty-five years. That's the biggest risk for the real.

The Brazilian central bank cut the key rate by a quarter point last week to fourteen point two five percent — interestingly, even though it raised its own inflation forecast for this year to five point two percent. A central bank that cuts while expecting more inflation, that makes the market nervous long-term.

Pressure from Europe and the US is still pushing up: the European Central Bank raised rates for the first time in three years, to two point two five percent, making the euro stronger. And the US Federal Reserve is holding at three point five to three point seven five percent with a hawkish tone — the dollar index sits at one hundred point ninety-four. Both of these tend to push the euro-real rate up.

That the rate stays almost unchanged at about five point ninety, that's exactly this stalemate: oil pulls down, fiscal risk and a strong dollar pull up. And above it all is Brazil's rate advantage holding the real steady — fourteen point two five percent against two point two five in the eurozone, that's the carry trade.

For you, that means: this week I see the euro-real moving sideways, somewhere between five point eighty-four and five point ninety-five, most likely around five point ninety. The falling oil gives you a small tailwind down. If you're planning a larger exchange, the lower end of this band is a sensible window. The key date is Thursday, June twenty-fifth — that's when the US inflation gauge PCE for May comes out.
