Foreign Exchange in detail — where we stand & why

The central bank picture from last week still holds (ECB hikes, Fed holds, BCB cuts). New since Monday is the magnitude of the oil collapse — and the paradoxical Real reaction.

Oil didn't just fall, it crashed [Global]. Brent is at ~$77.5 — −19.9% month-to-date. The driver is a concrete US measure: a 60-day license allowing global buyers (including US refineries) to purchase Iranian crude. This is more than yesterday's Hormuz hope — it's real supply flowing back into the market.[1][2]

Disinflationary: Not "prices fall" (that would be deflation), but "prices rise slower than before". Think of a car easing off the gas: it still moves forward, but decelerates. A one-fifth drop in oil loses the inflation engine notable momentum — across the eurozone and Brazil alike.

Yet the Real loses — that's the real story. By the textbook, cheaper oil should support the Real. Instead, USD/BRL has climbed above 5.15 and EUR/BRL clings to just under 5.90. Two forces override the oil effect: the firm dollar (DXY at 14-month high) and Brazil's own rate cut, which quietly erodes the carry advantage from the Real side.[3][4]

Terms of Trade: The ratio of what a country exports to what it imports. Brazil is a net oil exporter — when oil prices fall, export revenues from oil shrink. The oil collapse is not just "cheaper fuel", but also a modest drag on Brazil's trade receipts. That tempers the Real tailwind from disinflation.

The BCB cuts against its own model [BR]. Copom cut the Selic unanimously on 17.06. to 14.25% — the third cut in a row. Yet IPCA at 4.72% (May) sits above the 4.50% target ceiling and has risen from 4.39% in April. A central bank easing while inflation climbs above target signals market accommodation — real-negative.[5][6]

Selic: Brazil's benchmark rate, set by the Copom committee. Like the master dial on a nation-wide floor heating system: turn it up and credit gets expensive, the economy cools, inflation slows; turn it down and money gets cheap. 14.25% is extreme by global standards — that's exactly what still supports the Real. Every cut erodes that shield.

The causal chain [1][3][5]

How the forces flow into your rate today — and why cheap oil isn't winning:

60-day Iran oil license→ Brent −19.9% MTD→ Disinflation→ BRL support (weak)→ EUR/BRL slightly ↓
Fed holds hawkishly→ DXY 101.26 (14-M high)→ BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL ↑
Selic −25 bp to 14.25%→ Carry advantage shrinks→ BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL ↑

Bottom line: oil easing pulls weakly downward, but a strong dollar and shrinking carry both pull upward. Monday's standoff has tilted slightly against the Real — so a higher USD/BRL with only a minimal lower EUR/BRL.

Carry Trade (BRL): Borrow where it's cheap (eurozone, 2.25%), invest in Brazil at high rates (14.25%). As long as the Real doesn't crater, the rate spread is your profit. That spread supports the Real — but it's shrinking now from both sides: the ECB hikes, the BCB cuts.

What structurally brakes the Real

Cheap oil is cyclical tailwind — the headwind is structural [BR]:

  • October 2026 election: BNP Paribas explicitly names fiscal sustainability and worsening debt dynamics as BRL headwind in the election year.[7]
  • IPCA above target and rising: 4.72% in May against a 4.50% ceiling; energy/fuel alone at 8.90%. The BCB cuts anyway — undermining inflation credibility.[6]
  • Focus turns hawkish: IPCA-2026 expectations climbed to 5.11% — the 13th weekly rise in a row. The market doesn't trust disinflation.[8]

My reading: the oil collapse should have given the Real a clear boost — that it instead retreats shows how heavy dollar strength and fiscal worry weigh. As long as the absolute rate level stays at 14.25%, the Real is supported; but the buffer thins with every cut.

It's Tuesday, June twenty-third. We have a small economic puzzle today: the oil price has collapsed — nearly twenty percent since month-start — and cheap oil should normally support the Brazilian Real. Yet the Real is weaker today than yesterday. Your euro-real rate is holding just under five point nine.

First, the oil. Brent is at roughly seventy-seven point five dollars — down nearly twenty percent in one month. The reason is a concrete American action: a sixty-day license that lets buyers worldwide, including US refineries, purchase Iranian oil. This is real supply flowing back to the market, not just hope. Cheap oil slows inflation in both the eurozone and Brazil — we call that disinflationary.

So why does the Real lose? Because two stronger forces pull the other way. First, the US dollar: the dollar index stands at one hundred point twenty-six, the highest level in over a year. The Federal Reserve is holding its rate and the market expects almost no change through December — Goldman Sachs doesn't see a cut until twenty twenty-seven. A strong dollar weakens the Real.

Second, Brazil itself. The central bank cut its benchmark rate to fourteen point twenty-five percent, the third cut in a row — and this while inflation at four point seventy-two percent sits above the target and has even risen. Every cut erodes the interest advantage that brings foreign capital to Brazil. The carry trade gets thinner.

Bottom line: cheap oil is a weak tailwind, and a strong dollar plus shrinking carry is a stronger headwind. That's why the dollar-real has climbed above five point one five, while the euro-real barely moved.

For you this means: this week I see the euro-real moving sideways, somewhere between five point eighty-four and five point ninety-five, most likely around five point nine. At this level you're a bit better off than the average from last year. If a bigger exchange is coming, the lower end of the range is a decent window — but the risk points upward.

The key date is Thursday, the twenty-fifth: the US inflation gauge PCE comes out for May. If it's hot, the dollar rises further and pushes the euro-real toward five point ninety-five. In the next letter we'll see how PCE landed and whether cheap oil finally helps the Real. Until then.
