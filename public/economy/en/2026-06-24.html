Foreign exchange in detail — where we stand & why

The big move this week is not the real, but what's happening above it: for the first time in years, both major central banks are tightening at the same time. The ECB raised all three key rates by 25 basis points on June 11 (deposit rate 2.25%, effective June 17) — the first hike in roughly three years, triggered by war-related inflation in the Middle East.[1] The Fed held on June 17 (3.50–3.75%), but its "dot plot" — the anonymous collection of dots where the 18 officials place their year-end rate view — jumped from 3.4% to 3.8%; the market now prices roughly 68% odds of a September hike, up from 29% a week ago.[2][3]

Dot plot: Imagine each member of the rate-setting committee anonymously places a dot on a board: "here's where I see the rate at year-end." If the dot cloud shifts upward, the committee is telling the market without words: "we're leaning toward hikes." That's exactly what happened — and the dollar reacted.

Together, this pushes the dollar index to a 13-month high (~101.4, June 23).[4] For you, that's scissors with two blades: the stronger euro (your income currency) is tailwind, the stronger dollar is headwind for the real. Net, EUR/BRL has actually moved slightly in favor of the real versus Monday (5.9020) — down to 5.8925 — because the real is holding up relatively well in an oil-friendly world this week.

Positioning, sentiment & BR debt

Speculators remain net long the real: CFTC data (as of June 17, reported June 22) show +41.0K contracts, slightly unwound from 43.8K the prior week.[7] That means: major speculators are still betting, on balance, for a firmer real — but the cushion is thinning cautiously.

CFTC net position: The US regulator counts weekly how many betting contracts speculators have placed on a currency — minus their offsetting bets. "Net long" means: on balance, they're betting on a rising real. If the number swings toward zero or negative, that's an early warning that sentiment is flipping.

The ETF tells the same mixed story: the EWZ (the big US-listed Brazil equity basket) sits at $34.27 (June 22), 3-month flows are strongly positive at +$1.23 billion — but the last 5 days saw $148 million flowing out, and the past month even -$450 million.[8] Short-term money is exiting, but structurally 2026 is a good year for Brazil stocks (NAV +7.0% YTD).

On credit risk it's quiet: Brazil's 5-year CDS was last around 123 basis points (week 23 data, early June — today's live rate was not available).[9] NTN-B real yields (inflation-protected sovereigns) I couldn't pull today — the Tesouro Direto page returned 403; no value is guessed.

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against sovereign default. The price (in basis points) is the annual premium: 123 bp means insuring €100 of Brazilian state debt costs roughly €1.23 per year. If it rises, the market trusts the state less — if it falls, the situation is relaxed.

The ECB raised all its key rates by a quarter percent in mid-June — the first rate increase in roughly three years, triggered by war-related inflation in the Middle East. The Fed held steady, but its own projections now point upward. The market now estimates about sixty-eight percent odds of an increase in September. The dollar index has hit a thirteen-month high.

For you, that's scissors with two blades. The stronger euro is tailwind for your invoices. The stronger dollar is headwind for the real. On balance, EUR/BRL on the twenty-third of June closed at five point eight nine two five — even a tick firmer for the real than on Monday. The cross-check is clean, no discrepancy.

Brazil is caught between two forces. On one side, the central bank cut its key rate for the third time, to fourteen point twenty-five percent — still a high rate that draws money. On the other side, a record-breaking election year: over seven hundred billion real in stimulus, the debt ratio climbing toward ninety-five percent of economic output. My impression: some banks' relatively upbeat real outlook underestimates how quickly this fiscal picture can tip into a risk premium.
