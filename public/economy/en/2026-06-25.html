Oil: −25% in one month, because Hormuz is open again

The biggest move this month is not a central bank decision, but geopolitics. Brent stands at 72.32 $/Bbl (6/25, −1.92% vs yesterday) and has fallen ~25% in four weeks.[1] The reason: On 6/17, the presidents of the US and Iran signed a ceasefire at Versailles (on the sidelines of the G7 summit), and the Strait of Hormuz is open again.[2]

Risk premium in oil prices: Imagine the only bridge to an island with a bakery is threatened with closure — everyone hoards bread and pays sky-high prices. That's exactly what happens to oil during war fears: a markup "in case supply gets cut off". One-fifth of global oil and gas trade flows through Hormuz; when the threat was the fear, reopening is the all-clear — and the markup vanishes.

That it's real and not just headlines: On 6/18, 25 merchant ships crossed the strait — the highest count since mid-April.[3] Year-over-year, Brent is still +8.45% higher; the crash is the unwinding of the war premium, not a demand collapse.[1]

What that means for you: Cheap oil is disinflationary for importer economies — for Brazil the effect is mixed because the country exports oil (Petrobras) but imports fuel at world-parity prices. Net: the energy boost to BR inflation eases, letting COPOM cut further — and a falling Selic is exactly what strips the Real of its rate support (see next section).

Three central banks, three directions [4][5][6]

This is the rare moment when your three key central banks are pulling apart at once — and it's exactly this divergence that's driving EUR/USD and the Real.

ECB +25 bp→ EUR firm→ Fed holds hard, USD firm→ Selic −25 bp, carry shrinks→ BRL weak, EUR/BRL ~5.90

ECB [DE/Global]: +25 bp to 2.25% deposit rate on 6/11 (effective 6/17) — the first hike since 2023. Rationale: The Middle East war created inflation pressure; the ECB sees headline inflation at 3.0% for 2026 (core 2.5%).[4] A central bank that hikes supports its currency — the euro thus has tailwind.

Fed [USA]: held at 3.50–3.75% on 6/17 (12:0), but sounded hawkish: the "Dot Plot" — each member's anonymous rate forecast — was raised for end-2026 from 3.4% to 3.8%; 9 of 18 see at least one more hike, the market prices it in from October onward.[5] If the Fed holds high or even hikes, the dollar stays globally demanded — bad for the Real.

COPOM [BR]: cut Selic unanimously by −25 bp to 14.25% on 6/17 — the third straight cut (Mar, May, Jun). The Focus consensus sees Selic at ~13.0% by end-2026.[6] Next meeting: 8/4–5.

Carry cushion (BRL): Brazil pays one of the world's highest real rates at 14.25% — that attracts traders who borrow cheap in dollars and invest in Real, and supports the rate. If Selic falls and the Fed stays high, the rate spread narrows: the cushion thins, and some money drifts away. That's exactly what we're seeing now.
