FX in Detail — where we stand & why

EUR/BRL stands at the ECB fix of 06/25 at 5.9000 — at the top end of the recent range. For you that simply means: exchanging now is expensive. The cross-check is clean — from the two building blocks USD/BRL 5.2019 and EUR/USD 1.1342, the math yields exactly 5.9000, no discrepancy (0.0 %).[1]

Cross-check (cross rate): EUR/BRL can be read directly or calculated indirectly — via the dollar detour: "How many dollars does a euro cost?" times "How many reals does a dollar cost?". Imagine you price a trip from euros to reals once directly and once via the intermediate stop of dollars — if both ways land on the same amount, the rate is right. If they don't (over 0.5 % apart), a source is stale. Today: consistent.

The interesting bit: the upward pressure on EUR/BRL this week comes almost entirely from the euro side, not the real. The real is actually firm. The driver is the surprising ECB rate hike — the causal chain:

ECB +25 bp (Iran energy inflation)→ euro rates rise→ EUR/USD firmer (~1.138)→ real vs. USD ~flat→ EUR/BRL higher

Counterforce for the real: falling oil (good for Brazil's trade balance and inflation) and a massive interest rate advantage — Selic 14.25 % vs. ECB 2.25 %. As long as this advantage holds, the real stays attractive. The open flank is the fiscal risk, now visible in the bond market (right column).

Brent plummets — and the Iran spark jumps back [2][3]

Brent fell on 06/26 by 3.64 % to 72.52 $ per barrel — down roughly 21 % in the month. The market had settled on a quick supply recovery: Saudi tankers exporting again through the gulf, Qatar issued its first post-war oil tender, Hormuz transits running as fast as ever since war started.

Then, on 06/25, a drone hit the Singapore-flagged freighter "Ever Lovely" leaving the Strait of Hormuz — despite the Islamabad Memorandum signed on 06/17 (ceasefire framework Trump–Pezeshkian). So the situation is: ceasefire on paper, sparks in practice.

Why it matters for you Cheap oil is a double win for Brazil: underpins inflation (good for the real) and eases import costs. But the range is huge — BloombergNEF reckons even 91 $ by end-2026 if Iran flares up again. A Hormuz shock would drive oil, dollar, and risk aversion all together — bad for the real, bad for EUR/BRL. That's the biggest tail risk in this brief.

Three Central Banks, Three Directions [4][5][6]

ECB [EU] — surprise +25 bp (06/11): Deposit rate to 2.25 %, first increase since September 2023. Reason: the Iran war drove energy inflation to +10.9 %, overall inflation to 3.2 % — highest since 2023. ECB Director Schnabel warned, "the risk of de-anchoring inflation expectations is rising". The market prices ~50 % odds on another move in September.

Stagflation hike: Normally a central bank raises rates because the economy is overheating — a good sign for the currency. Here it's different: the ECB raises despite a shrinking economy (EZ GDP Q1 −0.2 %), just to brake energy inflation. Like a driver hitting the brakes even though the road goes uphill — because the engine is overheating. For the euro mixed: the rate effect pulls it up, the growth effect pushes it down.

Fed [USA] — held, but hawkish (06/17): Policy rate stays 3.50–3.75 %. But the Dot Plot (FOMC members' rate expectations) jumped to a median of 3.8 % for end-2026, 9 of 18 see at least a hike. That supports the dollar — and a strong dollar tends to weaken the real.

BCB [BR] — Selic cut to 14.25 % (06/17): third 25 bp cut in a row. Copom stresses, "the overall magnitude of adjustment depends on upcoming data" — so data-dependent, no autopilot. Inflation-target return: Q1 2028.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Bonds [7][8][9]

The crack in the bond market: Brazil's real bond yields have broken above the 8 % mark — the NTN-B (Treasury IPCA+) maturing 2032 traded on 06/22 at 8.44 % real. The Treasury cancelled its 06/23 NTN-B auction, rather than sell at even higher yields — a clear stress signal.

NTN-B Real Yield: A Brazilian bond that strips out inflation — you get a guaranteed IPCA + X % on top, no matter how high inflation goes. That X (the real yield) shows how much real value growth the market demands from the state. 8.44 % real is high — the market wants a fat risk premium. Like a landlord suddenly wanting much bigger collateral because they trust the tenant less.

But the real itself holds: CFTC data (06/16) shows the asset-manager/institutional category strongly net-long real — 57,275 long vs. only 1,298 short contracts. Speculators are plainly betting on a firm real, carried by the rate advantage. The EWZ (Brazil ETF) was at NAV 34.25 $ on 06/25, +1.1 % on the day, YTD +7.4 %. Brazilian risk assets have recovered far from the war shock.

The tension: Bond market screams fiscal risk, currency market stays calm. That works only as long as the rate advantage carries the carry. My read: the market slightly underprices the fiscal tail — the cancelled auction is a warning shot, not noise.

It's Friday, the twenty-sixth of June. The euro is stuck at five point nine zero reals today — at the top of the recent range, and not a good time to swap euros for reals. The unusual thing: this week the pressure isn't coming from Brazil, it's coming from Europe.

The reason is the European Central Bank. For the first time since twenty twenty-three, it raised interest rates, by twenty-five basis points. The trigger was the Iran war, which drove energy prices and with them inflation in Europe higher. Higher rates support the euro — and that's exactly what makes your exchanges more expensive. The tricky part: the central bank is raising even though the European economy is shrinking. That's called a stagflation hike, and for the euro it's a mixed signal.

In Brazil, it's running the opposite way. The central bank cut the Selic for the third time in a row, to fourteen point two five percent. The interest rate gap between Brazil and Europe is enormous — fourteen against just over two percent. That gap is exactly what's keeping the real firm, even though there are worries.

The oil price is falling sharply — Brent lost almost four percent today and over twenty percent in the month. That's good for Brazil because it dampens inflation. But watch out: a drone hit a freighter yesterday as it left the Strait of Hormuz, despite the ceasefire. The Iran conflict is still smoldering, and a new shock would drive oil and the dollar up together — that would be bad for the real.

The real crack is showing in Brazil's bond market. Real yields have climbed above eight percent, and the Treasury cancelled an auction on Monday rather than sell at even higher rates. That's a stress signal — the bond market is worried about Brazil's fiscal picture, while the currency market stays relaxed. My reading: the market is slightly underpricing this risk.

For next week I'm watching two dates. Monday brings the European inflation flash estimate and the next Focus report from Brazil. And the big date on the horizon is the central bank meeting on the twenty-fourth of July — a second rate move would cement euro strength and make your exchanges pricier. Until then: better to wait than to swap at five point nine zero.
