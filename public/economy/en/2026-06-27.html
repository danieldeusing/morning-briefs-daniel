FX in Detail

Two weeks, one complete central bank reversal. On June 11, the ECB raised rates for the first time since 2023 — deposit rate +25 bp to 2.25% (effective June 17), main refinance to 2.40%.[1] Rationale: energy and Middle East inflation. The ECB sees 2026 inflation at 3.0%, back to target (2.0%) only in 2028.

Central bank hike & your EUR/BRL: Think of interest rates as the price a currency pays to be borrowed. When the euro suddenly pays more (ECB hikes), investors want to hold more euros → the euro gets more expensive against everything, including the real. Since you earn in euros and spend in reals, a stronger euro is good for you: the same hour's work buys more reais.

Across the Atlantic: the Fed held rates on June 17 at 3.50–3.75% — but hawkish.[2] The dot plot median (where members see the rate by year-end) was raised from 3.4% to 3.8%; 9 of 18 see at least one more hike in 2026, only one sees a cut. New Fed Chair Warsh submitted no personal projection.[3] Reason for the hard line: US PCE inflation climbed to 4.1% in May (from 3.8%).[4]

Dot plot: A scatter plot where each Fed member anonymously marks where they see the benchmark rate at year-end — like 18 weather experts each plotting a temperature on a scale. The median (the middle dot) is the committee's best guess. When that dot moves up, the Fed is indirectly saying: "We're cutting later and less than you thought." That supports the dollar.

Bottom line: Both large central banks are pushing the euro and the dollar higher, the real stays relatively firm in between. The EUR/BRL move to 5.90 came mainly from the euro leg, not the real. The cross-rate check is clean (see dashboard), the spot is trustworthy.

Brazil: Selic down, inflation high, spending explodes [5][6][7]

The Copom cut Selic on June 17 by 25 bp to 14.25% — the third cut in a row, down from 15.00% (since June 2025).[5] BBVA expects more steps down to 13.0% by year-end. The problem: inflation is not cooperating. IPCA in May was 4.72% (from 4.39% in April), market expectation for 2026 at 5.3% — both above target.[6] Drivers: energy/gasoline at 8.90% YoY (Middle East effect), health at 6.04%.

BRL Risk #1 Brazil has announced a R$143.7 billion election-year package with 11 new programs; total 2026 stimulus is heading toward R$700 billion — twice 2025, a record for an election year.[7] The official primary surplus target (+R$34.3 billion) is a paper surplus: backing out off-target items leaves an effective primary deficit of R$23.3 billion. More spending + more debt + election uncertainty = downward pressure on the real.

Why Selic cuts weaken the real: High Brazilian rates are the magnet pulling in foreign capital (the carry trade: borrow cheap abroad, invest high in Brazil). When Copom cuts, that magnet shrinks — less capital inflow, typically a weaker real. Paired with rising spending, the effect amplifies.

My take: The market is pricing the easing story too smoothly. As long as the election-year package rolls and inflation sticks above 4.7%, the "Selic down to 13.0%" expectation is optimistic. That's the channel through which BRL weakness could return — pushing EUR/BRL even higher.
