Currencies in detail — where we are & why

The short answer: your euro has grown stronger over the past few weeks, and that's good for you — you earn in euros, spend in reais. At 5.90 you get roughly 5,900 reais for 1,000 euros instead of the ~5,770 reais from spring. The driver sits almost entirely on the euro side, not the real side.

The trigger was the ECB meeting on June 11: first rate hike in three years, +25 basis points to 2.25% deposit rate. Higher rates make a currency more attractive to investors — more demand for euros, euro strengthens. The causal path to your rate:

ECB +25 bp → 2.25%→ Euro rates relatively higher→ EUR/USD firmer→ BRL ~flat vs. USD→ EUR/BRL high at 5.90
Basis point (bp): One hundredth of a percent. 25 bp = 0.25%. Central bankers count in bp so "a quarter point" doesn't get confused with "25 percent" — like millimeters instead of centimeters when precision matters.

Why did the ECB hike at all, when it's been cutting for years? The Iran war energy shock pushed euro-zone inflation to 3.2% in May — well above the 2% target.[1] The ECB responds classically: rates up to brake prices. For you, the side effect is the important part — a stronger euro.

And the real side? It's been remarkably quiet. The dollar index (DXY) held almost flat on Friday at 101.37 — the dollar barely moves the real, so the whole EUR/BRL move came from the euro leg. That's the clean read: not the real is weak, the euro is strong.

DXY (Dollar Index): A basket that measures the US dollar against six major currencies — chiefly the euro. When DXY rises, the dollar is globally stronger, which tends to weaken the real and push EUR/BRL higher via the USD/BRL leg. When it holds still like now, the EUR/BRL move comes from elsewhere — here from the euro.

Three central banks, three directions

This is the most interesting part of the FX picture right now: the three central banks relevant to you are moving in different directions — and that's exactly what moves EUR/BRL.

ECB — up
2.25%, +25 bp (June 11). First hike since 2023. Supports the euro → EUR/BRL higher.
BCB — down
Selic 14.25%, third cut at 25 bp each. Lowers real returns → tends to weaken the real.

Fed — sideways, but hawkish (June 17, 3.50–3.75% held): Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Fed chief, the cut bias was removed. 9 of 18 members project a hike before year-end, the market prices October.[2]

Dot plot: A scatter chart where each Fed member anonymously plots where they see the policy rate going. Like a classroom survey "where will rates be in a year?" — the distribution of dots reveals the mood, here: hawkish (tilted higher).

Why this matters for your rate: If the Fed stops cutting (or even hikes) while the BCB cuts, the rate spread between the US and Brazil shrinks. That spread is exactly what attracts the money that props up the real.

Carry trade / rate spread: Borrow where it's cheap (USA), invest at high rates in Brazil, pocket the difference. As long as the real doesn't crash, the gap is your profit. When the spread shrinks, the trade pays less — money flows out, the real becomes vulnerable. BBVA expects exactly this: a structurally weaker real ahead.[3]

Brazil — inflation, Selic & oil tailwind

The BCB has cut the Selic for the third time running, down to 14.25% — from 15.0% since March.[4] The room comes from two sources: softer inflation data and the collapsed oil price.

The advance IPCA-15 for June came in at +0.41% mom below the expected +0.44% — a tick softer.[5] But caution: 12-month inflation sits at ~4.6%, which is above the BCB's 4.5% target ceiling. June's driver was mainly electricity (+2.04% for the month, tariff adjustment plus rate changes in several cities). That's why an August cut is debated, but not certain.

IPCA-15: Brazil's "advance inflation" — measured mid-to-mid month rather than month-end. Like a weather forecast two days before the weekend: an early look at the full IPCA value, good enough to see direction.

Brent fell −21.96% in June, to $71.99 (−4.34% on Friday alone), because shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is normalizing and a US-Iran deal is making progress.[6] Cheap oil means cheaper fuel and transport → less inflation pressure → more room for the BCB to cut. That's the overlooked reason why Selic can fall despite inflation above target.

It's Sunday, the twenty-eighth of June. Markets are closed, so we look back at a week that ended well if you earn in euros. All rates are from Friday's close, the twenty-sixth.

The big story: your euro is buying as much real as it has in ages. The European Central Bank's reference rate on Friday was five point nine zero. That means for one thousand euros you get roughly five thousand nine hundred reais, instead of around five thousand seven hundred seventy from spring. If an exchange comes up, that's a good window.

The reason sits almost entirely on the euro side. The European Central Bank raised rates on June eleventh for the first time in three years — a quarter point to two point two five percent. Higher rates make the euro more attractive to investors, the euro strengthens, and your rate against the real goes up with it. The trigger was an energy shock from the Iran war, which pushed inflation across the euro zone to three point two percent.

What's interesting right now is that the three central banks relevant to you are all moving in different directions. The European bank is hiking. The Federal Reserve is holding steady but removed the bias toward cuts — nine of eighteen members see a hike before year-end. The Brazilian Central Bank is cutting for the third time, down to fourteen point two five percent. This shrinking gap between US and Brazil rates is structural headwind for the real.

In Brazil there are two reasons for the central bank to keep room to cut: softer inflation data and cheap oil. The advance inflation number came in at zero point four one percent, slightly softer than expected. But caution: twelve-month inflation sits around four point six percent, above the four point five percent target. Oil prices have fallen nearly twenty-two percent in June because shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is normalizing. Cheap oil dampens inflation and gives the central bank space to keep cutting.
