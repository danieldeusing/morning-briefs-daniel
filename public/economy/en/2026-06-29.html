FX in detail — where we stand & why

Short answer: your euro has grown stronger in recent weeks, and that's good for you — you earn in euros, spend in reals. At ~5.88 to 5.90 you get around 5,880–5,900 R$ per 1,000 € instead of the ~5,770 R$ from spring. Today, though, the near-term currents shift slightly against you: not the euro falling, but the real firming.

Today's move comes from the real side. The dollar index weakens (101.33, second weakness session), and a globally soft dollar lets risk currencies like the real appreciate. Through the carry — Brazil's rate at 14.25 % far exceeds the US rate of 3.50–3.75 % — money flows into the real, USD/BRL falls to 5.1718. The causal path to your rate:

DXY −0.03 %→ Dollar globally soft→ Carry draws money into BRL→ USD/BRL −0.28 %→ EUR/BRL spot ~5.88
DXY (Dollar Index): A basket measuring the US dollar against six major currencies — mainly the euro. Rising DXY means the dollar is globally stronger → typically weaker real → EUR/BRL high through the USD leg. Falling today, the dollar is soft → real can appreciate → EUR/BRL spot slides.

The medium-term driver sits on the euro side: the ECB meeting on June 11 delivered the first rate hike in three years, +25 basis points to 2.25 % deposit rate.[1] Higher rates make a currency more attractive to investors — more euro demand, firmer euro. That's why EUR/BRL sits at ~5.9 rather than 5.77.

Basis point (bp): One hundredth of a percent. 25 bp = 0.25 %. Central bankers count in bp so "a quarter point" isn't confused with "25 percent" — like millimeters instead of centimeters when precision matters.

Why did the ECB hike when it cut for years? The Iran-war energy shock drove euro inflation high — the ECB sees 2026 at 3.0 % average, clearly above the 2 % target, back to 2.0 % only in 2028.[1] It responds classically: rates up to brake prices. For you, the firm euro is the relevant side effect.

Three central banks, three directions

This is the most interesting part of the FX picture right now: the three central banks relevant to you are moving in different directions — and that's exactly what's moving EUR/BRL.

ECB — up
2.25 %, +25 bp (June 11). First hike in three years. Supports the euro → EUR/BRL high.
BCB — down
Selic 14.25 %, 3rd cut of 25 bp each (June 17). Lowers real yield → tends to weaken the real.

Fed — sidelined (June 17, 3.50–3.75 % held): the US central bank stays put, no cut or hike.[2] The real benefits today regardless — as long as the Fed doesn't cut, the carry advantage for Brazil stays attractive, and a soft dollar lets the real gain.

Carry trade / rate spread: Borrow cheap (US ~3.6 %), invest at high Brazilian rates (14.25 %), pocket the spread. As long as the real doesn't crash, the difference is your profit. That's exactly what's supporting the real today. But if the BCB cuts further, the spread shrinks — and the bet pays less.

The tension for your rate: Near-term, carry helps the real (pushes EUR/BRL down). Medium-term, the BCB is cutting the Selic toward ~13.0 % (BBVA), narrowing the rate spread — and October's election brings fiscal risk. So the near-term real strength isn't the whole story.[3]

Brazil — oil tailwind & the election-year fiscal risk

The BCB has cut the Selic for a third consecutive time by 25 bp, to 14.25 %.[4] The room comes from one side, the risk from another.

The tailwind: oil. Brent recently fell to ~72 $ (down 4 % alone on Friday), because shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is normalizing and a US-Iran interim deal holds.[5] At the April peak, Brent sat over 60 % above pre-conflict levels — the decline since has taken pressure off inflation. Yet the energy component in Brazil's IPCA still runs at +8.90 % year-on-year (May), and overall inflation hit 4.72 % — above the 4.5 % target ceiling.[6] Cheap oil is why the BCB can cut despite this.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow waterway at the Persian Gulf through which much of the world's oil ships. If blockaded (as in the Iran war), oil prices spike; if it reopens, they fall — and global inflation pressure eases with them.

The risk: fiscal expansion in an election year. The government has rolled out a package of 11 new measures totaling R$ 143.7 bn — ahead of the October election.[7] Officially, 2026's budget targets a primary surplus of +0.25 % of GDP, but credibility is questioned: the consolidated sector ran 12 months through April with a −R$ 126.6 bn deficit (−0.97 % of GDP), and gross debt sits at ~80.4 % of GDP.[8] Election-year spending is the key medium-term risk for the real — and why the near-term carry strength can reverse from Q3 onward.

Primary balance: Government revenues minus spending — without interest payments. Like your household budget minus the credit card bill: shows whether ongoing operations pay for themselves. A primary deficit means the state spends more than it takes in before interest even comes due — and must borrow anew.
