What is the DXY — and why does it matter for you?
The leading indicator ahead of the real.
DXY (Dollar Index): a basket that measures the US dollar against six major currencies — mainly the euro, then yen, pound, and three smaller ones. Picture a scale: on one side the dollar, on the other world currencies. If the scale tips in favor of the dollar (DXY rises), the dollar is globally stronger. For you, that means: a stronger dollar usually pulls the Brazilian real down with it — capital flows from emerging markets into US assets. Weaker real → more reais per euro → typically higher EUR/BRL. That's why the DXY at 101.37 sits front and center as a warning light: it often moves before the real reacts.

The oil shock is turning — and inflation with it

Brent back to $73, WTI below $70 Global

The most important number of the half-year is one that's falling. Brent crude oil stands at $73.48/barrel (−0.59% day-on-day, −22.6% over the month), and the US contract WTI has for the first time since war's start on Feb 27 again closed below $70.[1][2] For comparison: at the peak of the Iran war in March, Brent was at nearly $120 — a gain of over 55% from pre-war levels.[3]

The trigger for the crash is the peace agreement in Switzerland: after the USA and Iran ended their war, the Strait of Hormuz is being reopened.[4] One-fifth of the world's oil flows through this waterway; its closure had in March squeezed global supply by over 10 million barrels per day.[5]

Strait of Hormuz: a narrow waterway in the Persian Gulf through which a huge portion of all oil tankers must pass. Think of it like the only gate of a full underground parking garage: if it's closed, nobody gets out, no matter how many cars are inside. That's exactly what happened with oil — supply was there, but blocked, so prices went up. Gate opens again → congestion clears → prices come down.

Why the oil crash affects you directly

Energy was the engine of the global inflation wave in 2026 — and thus the reason why central banks kept rates high or even raised them. If oil falls sustainably, inflation pressure falls with a lag, and room for rate cuts grows again. That's medium-term euro-negative and dollar-unclear — but for Brazil a double-edged sword: cheaper oil eases IPCA inflation, but the real hinges on overall risk appetite, not just oil.

Hormuz open→ Brent −22% over the month→ Energy inflation falls with a lag→ more rate-cut room 2027

Three central banks, three directions

ECB: first hike since 2023 — euro fell anyway DE

The ECB raised its deposit rate on June 11 by 25 basis points to 2.25% — the first rate hike in nearly three years, a direct response to war-driven inflation.[6][7] The projection sees headline inflation in 2026 at an average 3.0%, but growth at only a meager 0.8%.[6][8]

The paradox: the euro fell after the hike — EUR/USD slipped on June 23 below 1.1400 and toward 1.1350.[9] The market reads the hike not as a rate bonus, but as a sign that the ECB has to tighten into a weaker economy.

Basis point (bp): one hundredth of a percent. 25 bp = 0.25%. Central banks count in these tiny steps because even small rate changes move big money — like a referee measuring in cents instead of euros.

Fed: Hold — but the Dot-Plot points up USA

The Fed left its benchmark rate at 3.50–3.75% on June 17.[10] What matters is not the hold, but the projection: the median "dot" for year-end 2026 moved up to 3.8% (from 3.4% in March) — the committee is signaling at least one hike still this year.[11] Traders are already pricing in a hike as early as October.[12]

Dot-Plot: a scatter of dots where each Fed member anonymously places one — "where do I see the rate by year-end?". Like a classroom survey with sticky dots on a board: if the cloud moves up, the majority expects higher rates. That's exactly what happened — and a higher US rate pulls capital into the dollar, hence headwinds for the real.
federalreserve.gov · cnbc.com

BCB: third cut to 14.25% — but guidance is gone BR

Brazil's central bank cut the Selic unanimously on June 17 by 25 bp to 14.25% — the third cut in a row.[13] But the tone was hawkish: the committee removed its commitment to keep cutting and said instead "the overall size of the cycle will be set in light of new data" — August is thus open.[14]

The reason for caution: the BCB raised its 2026 inflation projection to 5.2% (from 4.6%) and pushed the target achievement to early 2028.[15]

Forward Guidance: when the central bank promises upfront what it will do next ("we'll keep cutting"). That calms markets — like a bus driver announcing the next stop. If he removes the announcement, you don't know where he'll stop: more uncertainty, and that's exactly what makes the real jittery before each meeting.

Currencies in detail: where EUR/BRL stands — and why

The level: pinned at 5.89

EUR/BRL is at the ECB reference fix of 5.8899 (29.06.), with the intraday spot around 5.8962 (30.06.).[16][17] The cross-rate check is clean: USD/BRL 5.1639 × EUR/USD 1.1406 = 5.8899 — deviation 0.001%, well below the 0.5% warning threshold.[18] Over the past seven days, EUR/BRL moved tightly between 5.8859 and 5.9120.[17]

Cross-rate check: EUR/BRL can be read directly or calculated — via the dollar detour (USD/BRL × EUR/USD). Like two routes to the same destination: if both agree, the number is reliable. If they diverge, one source is stale — today they don't.
ecb.europa.eu · alanchand.com

The forces at play right now

Three forces keep EUR/BRL in a tight band: (1) the firm dollar (DXY +2.19% over the month) pushes the real down, supporting EUR/BRL; (2) the weak euro after the misunderstood ECB hike pulls the other way; (3) the high Selic rate (14.25%) makes the real attractive for carry traders and brakes the real's weakness.

Carry Trade: borrow where it's cheap (eurozone, 2.25%) and lend where yields are high (Brazil, 14.25%). The rate gap is your profit — as long as the real doesn't collapse. If the real falls sharply, the rate advantage vanishes. This trade is why the real isn't falling harder despite dollar strength.

BR bonds & fiscal — the long-term anchor

Brazil's IPCA inflation rose in May to 4.72% year-on-year (from 4.39%); energy was the main driver at 8.90% — directly from Hormuz supply squeeze.[25] Cheaper oil will ease this with a lag. On real yields: the last verified level of the NTN-B 2035 was 7.66% (19.05.2026); a more current June figure was not available today.[26]

NTN-B Real Yield: a Brazilian government bond that strips out inflation — you get guaranteed IPCA + X%, no matter how high inflation jumps. The X value (7.66%) is the real real return the market demands from the state. High = the market doesn't quite trust the fiscal situation.

Structurally: BR debt ratio running from 87.3% (2024) to ~95% of GDP in 2026, GDP growth projected at only ~1.6%, primary surplus target 0.25% of GDP ± 0.25 pp (achievement uncertain).[27]

It's Tuesday, the thirtieth of June — the last trading day of the first half-year. And the news of the day is a relief: the oil shock that has shadowed us since February is over.

For half a year, the Iran war drove oil prices to nearly one hundred twenty dollars. The Strait of Hormuz was closed, one-fifth of the world's oil was stuck. Now the USA and Iran have made peace in Switzerland, the waterway opens again — and Brent has crashed, back to seventy-three dollars. A monthly loss of just over twenty-two percent. For you, that matters because energy was the engine of the entire inflation wave. As oil falls, inflation pressure falls with it, with a lag.

Behind the scenes, though, the three major central banks are pulling apart. The European Central Bank raised rates for the first time in three years in early June, to two point two five percent — against war-driven inflation. Oddly, the euro then fell, because the market reads the hike as a sign of a weaker economy.

The American Federal Reserve, by contrast, held steady, but its projection points upward: the majority now expects at least one more rate hike this year, perhaps as soon as October. That strengthens the dollar — and a strong dollar traditionally weighs on the Brazilian real.

Brazil's central bank cut for a third time, to fourteen point two five percent, but removed the promise of further cuts. August is thus open. The reason: Brazilian inflation stands at four point seven two percent in May, driven by energy from the Hormuz squeeze.

For your euro-to-real rate, that all means: pinned at five point eighty-nine. The cross-rate check holds, the number is reliable. Three forces balance each other — the firm dollar pushes the real down, the weak euro pulls the other way, and Brazil's high interest rate makes the real attractive to investors.

My outlook: in the short term, sideways, somewhere between five point eighty-five and five point ninety-three. The test this week is the US jobs report on Friday. Medium-term, Brazil's election on October fourth becomes the main risk for the real — and I think the fiscal risk is underestimated.

Remember: the probabilities are my assessment, not measured market. The rates themselves are live and dated. Tomorrow we'll see if the quarter close has moved the rate — and whether the oil crash keeps running.
