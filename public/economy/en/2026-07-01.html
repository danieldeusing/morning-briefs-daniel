FX in Detail — where we stand & why

EUR/BRL stands at 5.9003 (ECB fix, 30.06) at the upper edge of the band we've been stuck in for weeks. This is no one-direction accident, but a pincer move: The euro has gotten stronger, and the real has gotten weaker — both legs push EUR/BRL upward. For you as someone earning in EUR and living in BRL, this is a favorable exchange window; for the Brazilian saver, it's a warning light.

Leg 1 — the euro strengthens. The ECB raised rates on 11.06 for the first time since 2023, +25 basis points to 2.25 % deposit rate (effective 17.06).[1] The trigger is unusual: not overheating demand, but the Middle East war driving energy prices and supply-side inflation. The last confirmed reading — Eurozone inflation in the May Flash — stood at 3.2 %, above the 2 % target; energy alone +10.9 % YoY.[3] The June Flash is only due today around 11:00 CEST and not yet published at deadline. As long as inflation clings above the 2 % target, the ECB stays hawkish, and higher rates make the euro more attractive to hold → EUR strengthens.

Deposit rate: The interest rate the ECB pays commercial banks for parking money with it overnight. Like the rate on your savings account, just one level higher — for banks instead of you. When it rises, everyone wants to hold euros instead (because holding euros pays more), and the euro gets globally more expensive.

Leg 2 — the real erodes on fiscal grounds. Brazil's public coffers have flipped from black to red: In the first five months of 2026, a primary deficit of R$24.9 bn — exactly one year ago, that was still a surplus of R$69.1 bn.[5] May alone brought R$56.1 bn deficit (May 2025: R$33.7 bn). Gross debt reached 81.1 % of GDP — the highest level in five years, almost a full point higher than the prior month. And interest burden eats it: R$1.1 trillion in nominal interest costs over twelve months, roughly 8.5 % of GDP, at a policy rate of 14.25 %.[5] A country that spends an eighth of its economic output just on interest makes investors nervous — and nervous investors sell the real.

Primary balance: What the state collects minus what it spends — before adding in interest on old debt. Like your household budget without loan payments. A primary deficit means: the state already spends more than it takes in on day-to-day operations — debts grow even before you count interest.
ECB +25 bp → EUR stronger→ BR fiscal flips → BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL up, 5.90

The Counterweight: Brazil's Real Rate [6][7]

If fiscal and dollar both press down on the real — why isn't EUR/BRL already at 6.20? The anchor is called real rate. Selic sits at 14.25 %, current inflation (IPCA) around 4.7 % — that leaves a real rate of roughly 9.5 %, among the world's highest.[6] As long as Brazil pays so much real interest, it's worth global capital's while to hold reais, and that props up the currency from the other side.

Real rate: Interest after you subtract inflation — what your money truly gains in purchasing power. If the bank pays you 14 % but prices rise 5 %, your true gain is 9 %. Brazil's ~9.5 % real is almost unmatched — that's the magnet pulling capital into the country despite all the fiscal worries.

Copom (Brazil's rate committee) has already started cutting: third straight reduction on 17.06, from 14.50 % to 14.25 %.[7] The 2026 path: 15.00 % (hold Jan) → 14.75 % (Mar) → 14.50 % (Apr) → 14.25 % (Jun). The Focus survey (BR economist consensus, ~29.06) sees just one more cut to 14.00 % by year-end.[8] Key sideline: the 2026 IPCA forecast held at 5.33 % — the first time in 15 weeks with no upward revision. Inflation pessimism has stopped getting worse. That's quiet good news for the real: if Selic falls slower than inflation, the real-rate buffer stays intact.

My view: Focus consensus sees USD/BRL at 5.20 by year-end[8] — close to today's spot. I find that too gentle given the fiscal picture. The 9.5 % real rate is a strong anchor, but it shrinks with each cut, while the debt ratio heads the wrong way. Those who cheer the carry underestimate that exactly this high rate generates the R$1.1 trillion interest burden that tanks the fiscal picture in the first place. The carry is both drug and poison.

It's Wednesday, the first of July. The euro-real rate stands this morning at five point nine zero, at the upper end of the band we've been locked into for weeks. Two forces pull it there, and both work against you as someone earning euros and living in reais: The euro got stronger, and the real got weaker. For your exchange, it's still a good window.

The first force comes from Frankfurt. The European Central Bank raised rates for the first time in two years in mid-June, a quarter point to two point two five percent. The reason is unusual: not a booming economy, but war in the Middle East driving energy prices. The last confirmed reading — the May Flash — was at three point two percent, above target. The June number only comes this morning before deadline and isn't here yet. As long as inflation stays above target, the bank stays tough, and tough interest rates make the euro more expensive.

The second force comes from Brasília, and it's grimmer. Brazil's government budget has flipped. In the first five months of the year, there's a deficit of nearly twenty five billion reais — exactly one year ago that was still a surplus of sixty nine billion. Government debt is now at eighty one percent of economic output, the highest in five years. And the interest burden alone eats eight and a half percent of the whole economy. A country that spends that much just on interest makes investors nervous, and nervous investors sell the real.

Why isn't the rate already at six point two, then? Because Brazil has a powerful counterweight: a real rate of around nine point five percent, among the world's highest. That's the magnet pulling capital into the country despite all the worries. The central bank is cutting rates slowly — most recently to fourteen point two five percent — but the economists in the Focus survey see just one more cut before year-end. My view: the carry is both drug and poison. Exactly this high rate creates the interest burden that breaks the budget in the first place.

For today I expect the rate sideways-firm, between five point eighty seven and five point ninety two, most likely at five point ninety. The big news block is out, and the next real trigger is the American employment report Friday. These odds are my own judgment, not a market number.
