Forex in detail

Where we are & why — the causal path behind the 5.90 fix

Two drivers, one direction

The real is falling right now not because of a BR story, but because of two external forces that happen to pull in the same direction. First, the dollar is globally strong: The DXY is testing a 15-month high because the market is pricing in a "hawkish" Fed (a Fed that tends to keep rates high) and other G10 currencies are weakening.[1] Second, the ECB has raised rates instead of cutting — this makes the euro more attractive and pushes EUR/BRL higher on top of that.

DXY 15-month high→ USD globally strong→ BRL weaker→ EUR/BRL ↑ 5.90
DXY (Dollar Index): A basket that measures the US dollar against six major currencies — the euro makes up the bulk of it. Think of a seesaw: on one side the dollar, on the other a mix of euro, yen & co. When the seesaw tips toward the dollar (DXY rises), it pulls capital out of emerging markets like Brazil — the real gets weaker.

Cross-check hits exactly

The published ECB fix (5.9048) and the cross rate calculated from USD/BRL × EUR/USD agree today at 0.0% exactly — the anchors are consistent, no divergence to flag.[2] As a reminder: we check this because a single widget (e.g., Wise) can sometimes show stale rates for days. Two independent paths to the same result means: the 5.90 is real.

Cross-check: EUR/BRL should always ≈ USD/BRL × EUR/USD. It's like shopping: whether you trade euros to reals directly or first euros to dollars then dollars to reals, you should end up with the same amount. If it diverges >0.5%, one number was measured at a different time or is stale.
frankfurter.app · ecb.europa.eu

What supports the real (still)

Against the double pressure from outside stands a high Brazilian benchmark rate: Selic stands at 14.25% (Copom cut for the third time in a row on 06/17 by 25 bp).[3] As long as Brazil is paying so much more interest than the ECB (2.40% main rate) or the Fed (3.50–3.75%), it makes sense for global investors to hold reals — that's the floor that slows the freefall.

Carry (interest differential): Borrow where money is cheap and invest where interest rates are high — here Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the real doesn't collapse, the interest differential is the profit. This exact incentive keeps the real afloat despite a strong dollar; if it goes away, the real falls faster.

BR Selic cut, but inflation sticks

[BR] Copom cut Selic to 14.25% (06/17, third cut in a row), citing "higher uncertainty" and supporting growth without abandoning price stability.[3] The Focus report (as of 06/19) keeps 2026 Selic expectations at ~14%, but raised the IPCA forecast to 5.30% (from 5.11%) — pausing a 15-week streak of rising forecasts.[6]

IPCA: Brazil's official consumer price index — the basket used to measure how much more expensive life gets. If the forecast rises (5.11 → 5.30%), it means: experts expect more stubborn inflation, which gives the central bank less room for further cuts.

It's Thursday, July second. The Brazilian real is under pressure on two fronts today — and for you as a euro earner, that's the friendly side: Your euro is buying more reals than it was at the end of May.

The euro-to-real rate stands at five point nine zero four eight according to the European Central Bank's reference fix — just above five ninety. At the end of May it was around five eighty. The cross-check through the dollar route matches exactly today, so the number is reliable.

Two forces are pushing in the same direction. First, the dollar is globally strong: The dollar index is testing a fifteen-month high because the market expects a central bank that will hold rates high. Second, the European Central Bank surprised the market in mid-June by raising rates instead of cutting — the deposit rate now sits at two point two five percent. Both of these strengthen the euro against the real.

What brakes the real is the high Brazilian benchmark rate. The central bank cut the Selic for the third time in a row on June seventeenth; it now stands at fourteen point two five percent. As long as Brazil is paying so much more interest than Europe or the United States, it remains attractive to hold reals.

In the oil market, Brent fell by just over two percent to around seventy-one dollars. The reason is a supply glut: The Strait of Hormuz is open again, Iranian and Russian shipments are flowing in record volumes. Cheaper oil dampens inflation but hurts the revenues of Brazil's oil company.
