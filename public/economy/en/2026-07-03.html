Currencies in detail

Where we stand & why — plus the five forecast tiers

The move in the last 24–72 hours: US job market breaks down

Yesterday's (July 2) US jobs report for June triggered it: +57,000 new positions instead of the +113,000 economists expected, and April and May were revised sharply downward (May from +172K to +129K). The unemployment rate did fall to 4.2%, but for the wrong reason — fewer people actively seeking work. Bottom line: the US job market is cooling faster than anticipated.[1]

For the dollar, a weak jobs report means: investors expect less reason for the Fed to keep rates high — and the lower the expected US rates, the less attractive the dollar. The DXY (dollar index) fell to around 100.75. This is the causal path you need to understand:

US jobs +57K instead of +113K→ less Fed tightening expected→ DXY ▼ to 100.75→ BRL stronger vs. USD→ EUR/BRL more likely ▼
DXY (Dollar Index): a basket that measures how strong the US dollar is against six major currencies (the euro makes up the bulk). Imagine a seesaw: on one side the dollar, on the other a mix of euro, yen, and others. When the seesaw tips toward the dollar (DXY rises), the dollar is expensive globally — emerging currencies like the real typically suffer. When the index falls like now, the real gets breathing room.

Because the EUR/USD cross (see Dashboard) barely moved despite this, the dollar's loss translates mainly into BRL strength — which is exactly what keeps EUR/BRL stuck at 5.93 instead of rising. For you as a EUR earner: no buy signal, but also no "exchange now" alarm.

Central banks in lockstep

Copom: Selic at 14.25% — third cut in a row

The Copom (Brazil's rate-setting committee) cut the policy rate by 25 basis points to 14.25% on June 17 — unanimously, the third cut in a row (cycle start 15.00%, then 14.75% and 14.50% in April). Rationale: uncertainty around Middle East conflict and still-elevated inflation expectations warrant caution, but the disinflation path allows gradual easing.[2]

Basis point (bp): one hundredth of a percentage point. 25 bp = 0.25%. Central banks talk in basis points because small rate moves have big effects — like a pinch of salt in cooking: 0.25% sounds tiny, but it moves billions in capital flows.

Fed: holds 3.50–3.75% — now under pressure

The Fed left its target band at 3.50–3.75% on June 17 with a 12-0 vote. After the weak jobs report, debate is growing over whether it must cut soon. Both central banks meet next on the same day: July 28–29 — for this FOMC meeting no new economic projections (SEP) are planned, so the decision comes bare.[3]

Why this matters for EUR/BRL The rate gap between Brazil (14.25%) and the USA (3.50–3.75%) is the engine behind the carry trade that supports the real. If Brazil cuts faster than the US, the spread shrinks — bad for the real. If the Fed cuts under job-market pressure instead, that helps the real. The dual meeting in late July decides which way the scissors open.

Focus survey: Inflation ticks up

The Focus report (Brazil's weekly survey of ~130 market economists, last print June 22) upgrades expectations: 2026 IPCA inflation to 5.33% (from 5.11% three weeks earlier), Selic year-end 2026 to 14.00%. Year-end USD/BRL is held at R$5.20, GDP growth at ~1.98%.[4]

Trend The inflation expectation has climbed three weeks in a row — this is why Copom remains cautious despite cutting rates. Higher expected inflation constrains how fast Brazil can ease.

IPCA: Brazil's official consumer price index — the yardstick for inflation that Copom's target tracks. Like the receipt for the whole country's shopping cart: if it rises too fast, the central bank pulls the rate-hike trigger.

Macro / Economy

[BR] Fiscal warning: 2028–2030 goals "unachievable"

Brazil's Treasury warned (report June 30) that official primary surplus targets of 1.0–1.5% of GDP for 2028–2030 are unachievable without new measures — it projects only 0.2–0.3%. Financing gaps grow from R$10 billion (2028) to R$80.6 billion (2029) and R$136.4 billion (2030). Spending freezes alone won't cut it.[5]

Primary surplus: what the state collects minus what it spends — without interest on old debt. Like a household that checks whether salary covers daily life before debt payments kick in. A surplus shows: the state lives within its means. Without one persistently, debt grows — and investors demand higher yields to hold Brazilian bonds (and the real).

My take: This is the most important structural bear for the real — quieter than a jobs report, but more durable. As long as fiscal credibility wobbles, every BRL rally has an expiration date. The Focus consensus (R$5.20 year-end) doesn't fully price in this risk, in my view.

[BR] Fiscal targets closer: 2026/2027

Near-term looks less dire: the goal is a 0.25% GDP primary surplus (2026) and 0.5% (2027) — but the Treasury projects a small deficit of 0.4% (2026) and 0.1% (2027). So it's already falling short even now, widening sharply from 2028.[5]
