This week's move: an ECB hike on crumbling ground

The real story for the euro isn't a day-to-day tick, but a contradiction slowly building. The ECB raised all three policy rates by 25 basis points on 06-11 — the deposit rate rose to 2.25%, effective 06-17. The rationale was unusually direct: "The war in the Middle East creates inflation pressure." The ECB revised its 2026 inflation forecast upward to 3.0% — mainly from higher energy prices.[1]

Only: the energy price premise underpinning that is crumbling right now. Brent crude stands at $72.10/bbl on 07-03, the lowest level since late February. The reason is supply recovery in the Middle East: the UAE is shipping over 3.9 million barrels/day again, flows through the Strait of Hormuz run above 10 million barrels/day, and after the US naval blockade lifted, Iranian exports surged.[2] This is the causal chain that touches the euro:

Brent ▼ to ~$72→ ECB's inflation premise (energy) weakens→ less reason for high ECB rates→ euro loses an argument→ EUR/BRL tend ▼ medium-term
Basis point (bp): one hundredth of a percentage point. 25 bp = 0.25%. Central banks speak in bp because tiny rate moves have big effects — like a pinch of salt in cooking: 0.25% sounds tiny, but moves billions in capital flows. Higher rates make a currency more attractive to global capital — that's why the hike supported the euro.

My view: The euro is strong today because the ECB is the only major central bank currently raising rates instead of holding or cutting. But that strength rests on the oil argument. If Brent keeps falling and inflation cools, the ECB can flip its signal on 07-23 — and then the euro loses a leg. For you: the strong EUR/BRL rate is real, but not carved in stone.

Macro / Economy

[BR] Fiscal Pivot: From Target Surplus to Deficit

The most important structural bear for the Real comes from Brasília. The current bimonth fiscal report (RARDP 2nd Bimonth, published 05-25-2026) projects that Brazil will run a primary deficit of R$60.3 billion in 2026 before statutory deductions — worsened vs. the prior report's R$59.8 billion. Budget law (LDO) mandates a surplus at the target center of R$34.3 billion (0.25% of GDP). After deduction of statutory exceptions, a small surplus of R$4.1 billion remains — just above the lower target bound, sparing a contingenciamento. But to get there, the government sharply raised the spending freeze: from R$1.6 billion in the prior report to now R$23.7 billion.[7]

Primary surplus: what the state takes in minus what it spends — without interest on old debt. Like a household checking if salary covers living costs before credit payments kick in. If that chronically turns negative, debt balloons — and investors demand higher yields to hold Brazilian bonds (and the Real).

My view: That the government has to raise the pre-deduction primary deficit from R$59.8 to R$60.3 billion and multiply the spending freeze (R$1.6 → R$23.7 billion) just to barely hold the target is a quiet but serious confidence dent. The buffer to the lower target bound shrinks — as long as fiscal credibility is thread-bare, every Real rally has an expiration date. Primary source: RARDP 2nd Bimonth 2026, published 05-25-2026.

[BR] IPCA-15: Disinflation yes, but still above target

Brazil's inflation preview IPCA-15 rose 0.41% month-on-month in June — slower than May (0.62%), but the 12-month rate sits at 4.80%, still above Copom's target. Drivers were food (+0.74%) and housing (+0.72%), the latter mainly from electricity (+2.04%, "yellow bandeira" surcharge).[8]

IPCA-15: Brazil's early read on the official consumer price index — a mid-period snapshot of the nation's whole shopping basket, two weeks before the final figure. If it rises too fast, the central bank tightens the rate brakes.

[BR] Risk Premium Quiet: 5Y-CDS at 124.9 bp

Brazil's 5-year CDS closed on 07-03 at 124.90 bp, flat on the day. The signal: despite the fiscal pivot, the market hasn't yet priced in acute country risk escalation — CDS moves slowly and serves here as a calm risk anchor.[9]

CDS (Credit Default Swap): insurance against a state's default. The bp value is the annual premium: 124.9 bp means ~1.25% of the insured amount per year. Like fire insurance — if the price rises, the market thinks a fire is likelier.
