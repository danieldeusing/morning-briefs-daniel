FX in detail

Where we stand & why — plus the five forecast tiers

The move of the week: a 25% tariff threat with a hard deadline

The story that can move the real this week isn't a daily tick — it's a calendar entry with real force. The US Trade Representative (USTR), as part of a Section 301 investigation, has proposed a punitive tariff of 25% on nearly all imports from Brazil. The statutory deadline to determine a "responsive action" is July 15, 2026 — ten days from today. On Monday and Tuesday (07/06–07/07), the USTR holds a public hearing at the US International Trade Commission in Washington; the written comment period already closed on July 1.[1]

Section 301: a US trade law that lets the president impose punitive tariffs on countries whose trade practices Washington considers "unfair." Think of it like a citation with enforcement power: Washington alleges misconduct, sets a deadline, and if nothing changes, the surcharge lands on the bill — here, 25% on Brazilian goods at US customs.

Important for context: the draft is already heavily perforated. More than 1,600 tariff lines are exempted — coffee, beef, rare earths, other metals, energy, and around 430 aircraft-parts lines; metals already covered under Section 232 (steel, aluminum, copper) are excluded anyway.[2] That softens the real-economy blow — and is exactly why the real hasn't panicked so far. The causal path to EUR/BRL:

07/15 deadline approaches→ Tariff outcome uncertain→ Risk premium on BR exports/BRL→ BRL likely weaker on a hard outcome→ EUR/BRL likely ▲

My take: so far the market is treating this as "bark, not bite" — the many exemptions signal a political, negotiable move rather than a full strike. Lula said Brazil could "not accept the treatment" and would, if necessary, "seek other trade partners"; behind the scenes, the Bolsonaro camp is lobbying (Flávio Bolsonaro filed an 86-page USTR submission) against new tariffs ahead of the October election.[3] What that means for you: the calm EUR/BRL rate is real, but before 07/15 it's a discrete event risk. I'd set a soft outcome as the base case — but the spread of outcomes is wider than usual this week.

Central banks: the hawk from Washington

Fed: holds at 3.50–3.75% — but Warsh turns hawkish

The Fed left its target range at 3.50–3.75% on 06/17 — the fourth consecutive meeting with no change, and the first under new chair Kevin Warsh. But the real news isn't in the rate — it's in the "dot plot": 9 of 18 FOMC participants now project a rate hike in 2026 — a sharp turn from previous projections, which leaned more toward cuts or a long hold.[4]

Dot plot: an anonymous scatter chart in which each FOMC member marks where they see the policy rate at year-end. Think of it like a secret ballot with sticky dots on a wall — you see the spread of expectations without knowing who placed which dot. When the dots drift upward, the whole committee is turning more hawkish.

The market has absorbed the pivot: according to CME FedWatch, traders now see a over 50% chance of at least one hike by December (up from ~37% at the end of June). The next FOMC meeting is 07/28–29 — though without new economic projections at that meeting.[4]

Why this matters for EUR/BRL The carry trade lives off the rate gap: Brazil (~14%) pays far more than the US (~3.6%), which draws capital into the real. When the Fed starts credibly threatening hikes, that gap shrinks in expectation — the incentive to hold the real gets smaller, and the dollar becomes globally more attractive (DXY ▲). Both tend to push EUR/BRL higher. For now it's just a threat; if it turns into action, it's a genuine headwind for the real.

BR: Focus holds Selic expectations at 14.00%

The weekly Focus survey from Brazil's central bank (06/29) kept its key projections stable: year-end 2026 Selic at 14.00%, 2026 IPCA inflation at 5.33%, year-end USD/BRL at R$5.20. 2026 GDP growth was nudged up slightly to 1.99%.[5]

Focus report: a weekly survey by Brazil's central bank of roughly a hundred banks and funds on their expectations for rates, inflation, and the exchange rate. Like a weather forecast the central bank blends from many individual predictions — it doesn't show the future, but it shows the market's consensus, and the central bank reads it closely.

My take: the consensus holding the Selic at 14% and the real at R$5.20 says the market sees BR monetary policy as an anchor, not a risk. The risk sits elsewhere — in fiscal policy (next column) and now in the US tariff. Focus still barely prices the latter in.

Macro / Economy

[BR] Fiscal pivot: a target surplus turns into a deficit

The most important structural bear case for the real comes out of Brasília. The central government booked a primary deficit of R$53.3 billion in May 2026 — the worst May since 2024 (in real, inflation-adjusted terms); the accumulated deficit through May stands at R$44.4 billion. Worse still: the government's own updated year-end projection now shows a deficit of R$60.3 billion for 2026 — while the budget law (LDO) mandates a surplus of R$34.3 billion as the target (only the zero floor is legally binding).[6]

Primary surplus: what the government takes in minus what it spends — excluding interest on existing debt. Like a household budget checking whether the paycheck covers day-to-day living before loan payments are added on. If that flips into the negative for good, the debt load grows — and investors demand higher yields to hold Brazilian bonds (and the real).

My take: the fact that a legally mandated target surplus of R$34.3 billion turns into a projected R$60.3 billion deficit — a gap of nearly R$95 billion — is the real long-term pricing factor for the real. The government itself projects the debt ratio rising from 78.5% of GDP (2025) to 84.2% (2028). As long as fiscal credibility is stitched together this tightly, every BRL rally comes with an expiration date. Source: May 2026 central government data, reported late June/early July.

That explains why the real has stayed calm so far despite the fiscal pivot and tariff threat — the market is weighting the good news of the present more heavily than the risks on the horizon.

On Monday and Tuesday, the agency holds a public hearing in Washington. Here's the key context: the draft is already riddled with exemptions. More than sixteen hundred tariff categories are carved out, including coffee, beef, rare earths, and aircraft parts. That's why the real hasn't panicked so far.

My take is that the market is treating this as more bark than bite — the long list of exemptions points to a negotiable, political move rather than a full-blown strike. Lula said Brazil can't accept this treatment and would, if necessary, look for other trading partners. Still, July fifteenth is a discrete risk event: if the tariff comes in soft, the real gets some relief; if it comes in hard, the euro gets a lift against the real.

For you as a euro earner, EUR/BRL is holding steady at five point nine five, based on the central bank's reference rate from July third. The cross-check is clean — the published rate and the cross rate calculated from USD/BRL and EUR/USD match exactly. The dollar index sits just above one hundred.

This time, the headwind for the real is coming out of the US, not a tailwind. The Fed, under new chair Warsh, held its target range at three point five to three point seven five percent — but nine of eighteen members now see a rate hike still happening this year. That's a sharp hawkish turn. Traders now put the odds at around fifty-seven percent for at least one hike by December. If the Fed really does tighten, Brazil's rate advantage shrinks, and the real loses one of its pillars of support.

Inside Brazil, the picture is mixed. The weekly Focus survey keeps expectations steady: the Selic rate at fourteen percent by year-end, inflation at five point three three percent, the real at five point two to the dollar. Monetary policy is seen as an anchor. The risk lives in the budget: the central government posted a primary deficit of fifty-three point three billion reais in May, the worst May since twenty twenty-four. The government itself now projects a deficit of just over sixty billion reais for the year, even though the budget law calls for a surplus of thirty-four billion. That's the quiet, long-term bear case for the real.

On the brighter side: the Ibovespa closed Friday near a record above one hundred seventy-four thousand points, up eight percent since the start of the year, and oil held firm around seventy-two dollars. In the short run that's real-friendly — it explains the calm despite the tariff and fiscal worries.
