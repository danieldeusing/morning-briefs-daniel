FX in detail: where we stand & why

A quiet weekend. The last tradable anchor before the pause is a firmer real: EUR/BRL 5.9486, USD/BRL 5.1962 — both from Friday, July 3 (the ECB doesn't publish a fix on weekends). The real gained slightly against the euro and dollar over the week, carried by the high Selic rate advantage and a dollar that's only moderately firm globally.

Date honesty The three cards above do not come from the same moment: EUR/BRL and USD/BRL are Friday fixes (July 3), the DXY is a separate Sunday reading (July 6). Don't build a minute-precise snapshot out of this — the slightly firmer DXY today does not mean the real opens weaker on Monday. These are two different points in time.

The internal cross-rate check is clean: EUR/BRL (5.9486) against USD/BRL × EUR/USD (5.1962 × 1.1448 = 5.9486) — 0.0% deviation. Because EUR/USD was pulled independently (not back-derived from the two BRL legs), this is a genuine source cross-check, not circular reasoning. No caveat needed for this fix.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly match USD/BRL × EUR/USD — like two routes to the same destination that should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means they were measured at different times or a source is stale. Here they match exactly.

This week's driver wasn't a rate event, but a dollar-weakness pause plus BR carry. The causal path, in short:

High Selic rate advantage→ BRL carry attractive→ Dollar only moderately firm globally→ BRL firmer vs. USD→ EUR/BRL slightly down
Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap and invest it in Brazil at high interest rates. As long as the real doesn't collapse, the rate differential is your profit — if the real drops, the party's over. Brazil's high policy rate makes this trade lucrative right now and supports the real.

Macro: the AI capital stream becomes macro [1][2][3]

For Daniel as an analyst — not as an AI user — the relevant story this week is the size of the money stream, not a new product. Three data points, ordered by macro weight:

[Global] Record venture capital: $510B in the first half

Global venture funding hit $510B in H1 2026 — more than all of 2025 ($440B) and well above the old half-year record ($375B, H2 2021). Q1 was the largest quarter ever at $305B, Q2 followed with $205B. The key point: OpenAI and Anthropic alone pulled in $217B — 43% of the entire half-year total. Over 70% of global Q2 startup funding went to AI firms (versus just under 50% a year ago).[1]

Venture capital (VC): Money investors put into young, not-yet-public companies, hoping for big gains later. When this stream hits a record and almost half of it goes to two firms, that's a concentration usually only seen in bubbles — macroeconomically relevant because so much capital is riding on one bet.

[Global] OpenAI: IPO preparation — and a government stake

According to several reports, OpenAI has filed a confidential S-1 with the SEC, targeting a September 2026 listing. The valuation is the sore spot: sources scatter widely — from $730B to over $1T. The last private valuation was $852B (March round, $122B in fresh capital). Since the S-1 is confidential, there's no solid primary figure — I'm carrying the valuation as a range, not a number.[2]

S-1 / IPO: An S-1 is the form a company files with the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to announce an IPO and disclose its financials. "Confidential" means it's filed but not yet public — so no one outside can verify the real valuation.

Separately, and economically the more unusual bit: OpenAI has proposed giving the US government a 5% stake in the company — modeled on the Alaska Permanent Fund — in talks with Commerce Secretary Lutnick and Treasury Secretary Bessent. At $852B, that would be worth ~$42.6B. The talks are, per the FT, "conceptual" and would likely require Congress.[3]

Alaska Permanent Fund: A sovereign wealth fund Alaska built in 1976 from oil surpluses (~$91B), whose returns are paid out to residents. Using it as a model means: the government becomes a co-owner of a strategic asset and shares in the profit — a first for a US tech company.

[USA] Amazon's quiet $20B chip business

Amazon's in-house silicon (Trainium AI chips + Graviton CPUs + Nitro) crossed an annual run rate of over $20B, according to CEO Andy Jassy (Q1 2026 call) — about 40% quarter-over-quarter growth, triple digits year-over-year. As a standalone sales business, Jassy said, it would run at ~$50B/year — putting it among the top 3 data-center chip vendors worldwide.[4]

Run rate (annualized revenue): You take current revenue from a short period and project it out over a full year — like estimating annual mileage from today's pace. Shows the size right now, not revenue already booked for the year.

Context (not citable as a hard standalone figure): snippets also circulate >$225B in Trainium revenue commitments, plus Anthropic at up to 5 GW and OpenAI at ~2 GW of capacity — not primary-verified, so only included as an order of magnitude.

Why this matters for FX No direct EUR/BRL lever today. But if AI capex becomes a global financing question — record VC, a trillion-dollar IPO, chip billions — then risk sentiment in this one sector helps shape how strong the dollar runs and how risk-hungry emerging-market currencies like the real behave. That's the 12-month channel above.
