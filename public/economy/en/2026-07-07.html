The internal cross-rate check is clean: EUR/BRL (5.9130) against USD/BRL × EUR/USD (5.18 × 1.1415 = 5.9130) — 0.001% deviation. Because EUR/USD was pulled independently (not back-calculated from the two BRL legs), this is a genuine cross-source check, not circular reasoning.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — like two routes to the same destination that should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means they were measured at different times or a source is stale. Here they match exactly.

The driver remains the Selic carry — Brazil's rate advantage attracts capital and props up the real, even after Copom cut to 14.25% (still enormously high). The causal chain, briefly:

Selic 14.25% (still high)→ BRL carry attractive→ Dollar globally flat→ Real firm vs. USD→ EUR/BRL sideways-firm
Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap, invest in Brazil at high interest rates. As long as the real doesn't slide, the rate differential is your profit — if the real drops, the party's over. Brazil's high policy rate makes this trade lucrative right now and props up the real.

Macro: three central banks, three directions [1][2][3]

For Daniel as an analyst, the relevant story this week is the open divergence among the major central banks — and the reason they all cite in common. All three point to inflation driven by the Middle East war, yet draw opposite conclusions from it.

[DE/EU] ECB hikes — first increase in ~3 years

On 06/11 the ECB raised all three policy rates by 25 basis points: deposit rate 2.00 → 2.25%, main refinancing 2.40%, marginal lending 2.65% (effective 06/17). It's the first increase in nearly three years, explicitly justified by inflation pressure from the Middle East war. Staff projections see inflation at 3.0% (2026), 2.3% (2027), 2.0% (2028).[1]

Basis point (bp): One hundredth of a percentage point. 25 bp = 0.25%. Central banks move rates in such mini-steps to keep the effect dosable — like turning volume up in small clicks instead of one big jump. Higher rates tend to strengthen the currency (here, the euro).

[USA] Warsh's Fed turns hawkish — but holds still

At his first meeting as Fed chair (06/17), Kevin Warsh held the policy rate at 3.50–3.75% — but the rate outlook (the "dot plot") flipped: 9 of 18 members now see at least one hike in 2026, a reversal from an expected cut just three months earlier. Reason cited: US May inflation +4.2% year-on-year, the sharpest rise since April 2023.[2]

Hawkish / dot plot: "Hawkish" means leaning toward higher rates to fight inflation. The dot plot is a chart where each Fed member anonymously places a dot for their expected rate — like a sticker-dot survey. If the dots drift upward, the Fed itself expects higher rates.

[BR] Copom cuts against the trend — to 14.25%

While the ECB and Fed tighten, Brazil's Copom unanimously cut the Selic by 25 bp to 14.25% p.a. — while warning of "composure and caution" because Middle East conflicts make the inflation outlook uncertain. Next meeting: 07/28–29 (decision of 17 June 2026 — third straight cut).[3]

Why this matters for FX At 14.25%, despite the cut, Brazil still holds a huge rate advantage over the euro (2.25%) and the dollar (3.50–3.75%). That very gap props up the real via the carry trade — the core driver in the "FX in detail" thread on the left.

[USA] The jobs report that cooled the hike narrative

The US labor market report for June (released 07/02) came in at just +57,000 jobs versus the expected 115,000 — the weakest gain in four months. The unemployment rate did fall to 4.2%, but only because fewer people are looking for work at all: labor force participation dropped to 61.5%, the lowest since March 2021. April/May were revised down by a combined 74,000 jobs.[4]

Labor force participation: The share of adults who are working or actively looking. If it falls, the unemployment rate can drop even when nobody found a job — job-seekers simply vanish from the statistic. A falling rate for this reason is a bad sign, not a good one.

Context (commentary, not part of the BLS report): Separate 2026 analyses (including Goldman Sachs, PwC AI Jobs Barometer) attribute the decline in routine and entry-level jobs to AI adoption. Important: the official June report itself does not name AI as a cause — that's accompanying commentary, not a finding of the statistics. Don't conflate the two.

FX lever A weak labor market pushes down Fed hike probability — according to one (single, commentary-style) analysis, the July hike odds fell from around 29% to 22%. These exact percentages come from one secondary source (not a CME FedWatch feed), so read them only directionally: weak jobs report → less hike pressure → dollar tends soft → real-friendly.[5]

[Global] Brent −23% over the month undermines the inflation story

Brent crude trades around $72/barrel, roughly 23% lower than a month ago, after OPEC+ agreed an output increase of 188,000 barrels/day for August. This is the quiet counterforce to the "Middle East war → inflation" narrative that both the ECB and the Fed cite for their hawkish stance: if the oil price keeps falling, the foundation of the hike argument erodes.[6]

Now to the central banks. The European Central Bank raised rates in early June for the first time in about three years, lifting the deposit rate to two point two five percent. It explicitly blames inflation driven by the Middle East war.

The American central bank, under its new chair Kevin Warsh, held its policy rate steady, but its rate outlook turned hawkish. Nine of eighteen members now see at least one hike later this year. Three months ago, the average forecast was still a cut. That's a genuine reversal, justified by May inflation running at four point two percent.

Brazil is going the opposite way. The Copom committee unanimously cut the Selic rate to fourteen point two five percent. That sounds like a lot, but it's still a massive rate advantage over the euro and the dollar. That very gap is what props up the real through the so-called carry trade.

In the middle of all this, Wednesday brought the American jobs report, showing just fifty-seven thousand new jobs, versus an expected one hundred fifteen thousand. The unemployment rate fell to four point two percent, but only because fewer people are even looking for work. That's a bad sign, not a good one. Some analysts blame the decline on artificial intelligence, but be careful — the official report itself doesn't say that. That's commentary, not a finding.

Here's an interesting quiet counterforce. Brent crude oil has fallen about twenty-three percent over the month, to around seventy-two dollars. When oil gets cheaper, it undercuts the inflation argument that both the European and American central banks use to justify higher rates.

For the euro-real, the week ahead looks calm: slightly directional, roughly between five point eight five and five point nine eight, most likely around five point nine one.
