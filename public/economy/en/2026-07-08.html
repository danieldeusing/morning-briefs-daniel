FX in detail: where we stand & why

The anchor is a firmer real: EUR/BRL 5.8781 in Tuesday's ECB fix (07/07), versus 5.9130 on Monday — −0.59%. That's the same day the Strait of Hormuz escalated and Brent jumped. At first glance that looks backwards: war, an oil shock, and the emerging-market currency gets stronger?

No — exactly the right way round, once you know what Brazil sells. The causal chain:

Hormuz attacks 07/07→ Brent ~$76 (+6% over two sessions)→ BR terms of trade rise→ Real firm vs. USD→ EUR/BRL fix −0.59%
Terms of trade: The price ratio between what a country sells and what it buys. Picture two kids trading collectible cards: if your cards suddenly become more sought-after, you get more back for the same card. Brazil sells oil (it's a net exporter), so a high oil price improves its terms of trade — more dollars flow into the country, and the real gets support. For an oil importer like the eurozone, the exact opposite holds.

The cross-rate check is clean. The published ECB fix (5.8781) against the calculation USD/BRL × EUR/USD (5.1413 × 1.1433 = 5.878) gives a 0.001% deviation — both legs from 07/07. Because EUR/USD was pulled independently and not back-calculated from the two BRL legs, this is a genuine cross-source check.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two routes to the same destination have to be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means they were measured at different times, or a source is stale.
Date honesty The cards above do not come from the same moment: EUR/BRL is the ECB fix from 07/07 (measured at ~4:00 PM CET), while USD/BRL and the DXY are intraday readings from 07/08. The ECB-based USD/BRL for 07/07 was 5.1413 — the fact that today's intraday spot sits at 5.1729 is partly a genuine move and partly different sampling. Don't read this as "the real down 0.6% overnight."

What keeps propping up the real remains the Selic carry: Brazil's 14.25% policy rate against 3.50–3.75% in the US. That gap attracts capital, as long as nobody fears a real crash.

Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap, invest in Brazil at high interest rates. As long as the real doesn't collapse, the rate differential is your profit — if the real drops, the party's over immediately.

Positioning, sentiment & BR rates [5][7][8]

The most interesting number today is one that didn't move. Brazil's 5-year CDS sits at ~126 bp (as of 07/07) — right in the middle of the roughly 123–129 bp range seen in early June. A war shock in the world's most important oil chokepoint, plus a warning from Brazil's own Treasury that the fiscal framework breaks starting in 2028 — and the credit market doesn't flinch.

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against a borrower's default. 126 basis points means a premium of 1.26% of the insured amount per year. Like fire insurance — if the premium rises, the market considers a fire more likely. It's holding still: the market doesn't see a fire.

That fits a second signal from the US: the 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.51% (from 4.48%). In genuine panic, everyone buys US Treasuries, and their yields fall. They're rising — so the market is pricing inflation, not flight. My takeaway: so far, Hormuz is being traded as a terms-of-trade event, not an emerging-market credit crisis. That's why the real is firm.

The counterpoint, so you can argue with me: equity capital had already left Brazil before the shock. The EWZ ETF (iShares MSCI Brazil) shows −$742 million in net outflows over the last month, against +$637 million over three months — as of roughly 06/25. So the real's support is coming from the carry and from oil, not from confidence in Brazilian companies.

Flagging honestly BCB FX swap holdings, CFTC BRL futures (COT), and the B3 DI curve were not pulled today — not estimated, just not queried. The CDS source also delivered an internally inconsistent change figure; that's why only the level (125.74 bp) is used here, not a day-over-day change. The EWZ flows are two weeks old.

Macro: the oil shock that isn't a risk-off [1][2][3]

[Global] Hormuz: three ships hit, 80+ targets in the retaliation night

On July 7, Iran's Revolutionary Guard (IRGC) attacked three merchant ships: the Qatari LNG tanker Al-Rekayat, the Saudi crude supertanker Wedyan (missile hit), and a third, unidentified tanker (drone, light damage). All three were using a southern route near the Omani coast that Iran had previously warned against. That night, US Central Command (CENTCOM) struck back at "more than 80 Iranian targets" — air defenses, radar sites, anti-ship missiles — as well as "more than 60 fast boats" belonging to the Revolutionary Guard.[1]

Why Hormuz matters at all: The Strait of Hormuz is the only sea route out of the Persian Gulf. A large share of Gulf-state oil has to pass through it — it's the neck of a bottle that half the world economy drinks from. Hit ships there, and you make oil more expensive worldwide without touching a single drilling rig.

For this morning, additional missile and drone impacts were reported in Kuwait and Bahrain — source unclear, explicitly unconfirmed. I'm carrying this as a development, not a fact.[1]

[Global] Brent $76.35 — and the reversal from yesterday

Brent trades at $76.35/barrel, +2.95% versus the prior day.[2] On the attack day itself, per CBS, the September contract jumped roughly 5.5% to ~$76 — two slightly different percentage bases, the same level. Versus the ~$72 seen on 07/06, that's roughly 6% over two sessions.[1]

With this, I'm correcting yesterday's thesis. This space said on 07/07 that the falling oil price was undermining the ECB's and Fed's inflation argument. That undermining is gone for now. Anyone wanting to tighten has their argument back — and Brazil's Copom, which is currently easing, has one more problem on the table.

[BR] The Treasury itself says the math stops adding up from 2028

On June 30, Brazil's Tesouro Nacional published an unusually candid warning: from 2028, the fiscal targets (a primary surplus of 1.0–1.5% of GDP) will be unreachable without new measures — its own projection sees only 0.2–0.3%. The financing gaps: R$10 billion (2028), R$80.6 billion (2029), R$136.4 billion (2030). Gross debt rises from 83.5% of GDP (2026) to a peak of 87.9% (2029). For 2026/27 the agency still expects deficits of 0.4% and 0.1% respectively. Freezing spending alone won't be enough: mandatory spending is growing faster than the spending cap, which is tied to 2.5% real growth.[3]

Primary deficit: What the government spends minus what it takes in — before interest payments on old debt. Like a household ledger that doesn't yet include the loan interest. If this balance is already negative, debt grows even if you ignore interest entirely. That's exactly where Brazil stands.

The present confirms it: the central government's primary deficit adds up to R$142.3 billion over the twelve months through May — 1.06% of GDP. The official target was a surplus of 0.25% (with ±0.25 points of tolerance). May alone tore a R$53.3 billion hole.[4]

My position The CDS market isn't pricing this (~126 bp, unchanged). I think that's too relaxed: a Treasury that announces its own target miss three years in advance hands rating agencies a ready-made template in an election year. That's not a signal for tomorrow's rate — but it's why my 12-month point above is drifting higher.

[BR/USA] The central-bank divergence — unchanged, but now under oil pressure

A reminder, since it's the framework for everything above: Brazil's Copom cut the Selic by 25 bp to 14.25% on 06/17 — the third straight cut.[6] The Fed held unanimously (12:0) at 3.50–3.75% on the same day.[7] Both next decisions fall on 07/28–29. The fresh oil premium makes those meetings tougher: it strengthens every tightening argument and weakens every easing argument — which is exactly what the Copom is doing right now.

It's Wednesday, July eighth. Yesterday this space still said a falling oil price was taking away the central banks' inflation argument. That picture has looked different since July seventh. A war is escalating in the Strait of Hormuz, and Brent oil is back up to seventy-six dollars.

On July seventh, Iran's Revolutionary Guard attacked three merchant ships: a Qatari liquefied natural gas tanker, a Saudi crude oil supertanker, and a third, unidentified vessel. That same night, American Central Command struck back at more than eighty Iranian targets, plus more than sixty fast boats belonging to the Revolutionary Guard. This morning, impacts were also reported in Kuwait and Bahrain, but the source for that is unclear. I'm treating that as a development, not a fact.

The surprising part isn't the oil price — it's the real's reaction. It got firmer, not weaker. The European Central Bank's euro-real fix fell from five point nine one three zero to five point eight seven eight one. That's zero point five nine percent, and it happened on the very day of the attack.

The reason is simple once you know what Brazil sells. Brazil is a net exporter of oil. So a high oil price improves the country's terms of trade: the same goods bring in more dollars. For an oil importer like the eurozone, the opposite is true. An oil shock is, for now, good news for the real.

Two more numbers confirm that this isn't a global panic. First: the Dollar Index sits at one hundred one point zero nine and is practically flat. Despite the war headlines, nobody is fleeing into the dollar. Second, and this is the sharper signal: the yield on ten-year US Treasuries rose from four point four eight to four point five one percent. In genuine panic, everyone buys Treasuries and their yields fall. They're rising. So the market is pricing inflation, not flight.

The second big story comes from Brazil itself, and it's an uncomfortable one. Brazil's Treasury wrote in late June that its own fiscal targets will be unreachable from twenty twenty-eight onward without new measures. The financing gap grows to eighty point six billion reais in twenty twenty-nine, and to a hundred thirty-six point four billion the year after that. Gross debt rises to a peak of eighty-seven point nine percent of economic output.

The present confirms it. Over the twelve months through May, the central government fell short by a hundred forty-two point three billion reais — one point zero six percent of economic output. The official target had been a surplus of zero point two five percent. What's notable is that the credit market completely ignores this: Brazil's five-year default insurance sits unchanged at around a hundred twenty-six basis points. I think that's too relaxed. A Treasury that announces its own target miss three years ahead hands rating agencies a ready-made template in an election year.

For euro conversions, that means the real is about half a percent firmer than Monday, so today is a less favorable moment than it was forty-eight hours ago. Still, there's no rush. For today I expect sideways trading, roughly between five point eighty-eight and five point ninety-four, most likely around five point ninety. All probabilities here are my own assessment, not a market figure.
