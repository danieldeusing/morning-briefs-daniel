FX in detail: where we stand & why

The anchor is a only slightly weaker real: EUR/BRL 5.8802 in the ECB fix (Wed 07/08), essentially flat vs. Tuesday (5.8781) and still −0.55% below Monday's fix (5.9130). The sharp attack-day gain (07/07) is holding and unwinding only slowly; the derived spot today sits at ~5.898.

Two things are driving today's picture. First, the oil floor that refuses to move away:

Brent holds ~$79→ oil inflation risk in BR→ Copom cut fantasy shrinks→ Selic stays at 14.25%→ carry stays fat → real floor
Terms of Trade: The price relationship between what a country sells and what it buys. Picture two kids trading collector cards: if your cards become more sought-after, you get more back for the same card. Brazil is a net oil exporter — a high oil price improves its terms of trade, more dollars flow into the country, and the real is supported. For an oil importer like the eurozone, exactly the opposite holds.

Second, a correction to yesterday: this brief said on 07/08 that despite the war headlines, nobody was fleeing into the dollar. More precisely: there was a safe-haven jump — the DXY briefly climbed to ~101.18 on the US strikes — but it didn't hold for even a day and is back to ~100.97 this morning.[3] Net-net the thesis still holds (no lasting dollar flight), but "flat, nothing happened" was too crude — it was a round trip. The reflex was there; the real panic wasn't.

The cross-rate check is clean. The published ECB fix (5.8802) against the computed USD/BRL × EUR/USD (5.1563 × 1.1404 = 5.8802) yields a 0.001% gap — both legs from 07/08, EUR/USD pulled independently and not back-calculated from the BRL legs. A genuine cross-source check.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two paths to the same destination should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means the measurements were taken at different times or one source is stale.

What structurally supports the real remains the Selic carry: a 14.25% Brazilian policy rate against 3.50–3.75% in the US. That gap draws capital as long as nobody fears a real sell-off — and the longer oil keeps the Copom from its next cut, the longer that gap stays wide.

Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap and invest it in Brazil at high interest rates. As long as the real's exchange rate doesn't collapse, the interest-rate gap is your profit — if the real drops, the party ends instantly.

Positioning, sentiment & BR rates [6][7]

The most telling number today is the FX flow (fluxo cambial): in 2026 through July 3rd, a net +US$16.8bn flowed into the country. The story is in the breakdown — the trade channel brought in +$34.4bn, while the financial channel lost −$17.6bn.[6] That's exactly what explains the firm real: its support comes from goods sold (oil, agriculture, ore), not from portfolio investors' confidence, which is net outflowing.

Fluxo cambial (FX flow): The net sum of dollars that actually cross Brazil's border. Two buckets: the trade channel (dollars from exports minus imports) and the financial channel (portfolio, credit, and investment flows). Like two faucets running into the same tub — if the trade faucet runs strong and the financial faucet runs dry, the dollars are coming from goods sold, not from investor confidence.

Matching that, the credit market is standing still: Brazil's 5-year CDS is ~127bp (early July) — right in the middle of the 52-week range of 116–160bp and essentially unchanged from early June.[7] An oil war in the world's most important strait and a widening fiscal hole, and default insurance doesn't budge.

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against a borrower defaulting. 127 basis points means: 1.27% of the insured amount paid annually as a premium. Like fire insurance — if the premium rises, the market thinks a fire is more likely. It's holding steady: the market sees no fire.

One crack in the picture: the central bank lost R$9.3bn in June on its swap contracts (cash-settled, after +R$1.4bn in May) — the price of defending the real against weakness. Reserves: US$370.6bn (June).

Macro: why oil isn't fading this time [1][2][4]

[Global] Brent $78.90 — the premium sticks

Brent trades at $78.90/barrel, +1.12% vs. the prior day.[2] That's the notable part: after this week's strike-driven jump (around $77.90 on 07/08, +5%), the price isn't retreating — it's edging even higher. The backdrop is the escalated US-Iran confrontation — after the US strikes and an Iranian attack on three tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, the US Treasury revoked Iran's license to sell oil, pulling supply out of the market.[1]

Why Hormuz matters: The Strait of Hormuz is the only sea route out of the Persian Gulf. A large share of Gulf oil has to pass through it — the bottleneck of a bottle that half the world economy drinks from. Hit ships there or cut a producer off from selling, and oil gets more expensive worldwide.

For Brazil this cuts both ways. Good in the short term (net exporter, terms of trade), uncomfortable in the medium term: expensive oil fuels inflation — and that hits the Copom right in the middle of its easing cycle.

[BR] Oil turns the rate cut into an open question

As a reminder, since it's the frame for this whole brief: Brazil's Copom cut the Selic by 25bp to 14.25% on 06/17 — the third consecutive cut (after 14.50% on 04/29).[4] The next meeting isn't until 08/04–05[8] — and here I'm correcting yesterday's issue, which mistakenly placed the Copom meeting on 07/29.

My position: the sticky oil price makes a fourth cut in August less likely. Even the April cut came with an explicit warning about "deanchored inflation and Middle East risks" — exactly the risks that oil is now making real. A Copom that pauses instead of cutting keeps the carry at 14.25% — and that's the single strongest floor for the real. The market is also increasingly leaning toward caution/pause.

[BR] The fiscal hole — now in hard data, not just warnings

Yesterday it was a Tesouro warning about 2028; today the May print delivers the present. The consolidated public sector (central government + states + municipalities + state-owned companies) posted a May primary deficit of R$56.1bn — well above the R$33.7bn in May 2025. Over twelve months the deficit adds up to R$149.0bn = 1.14% of GDP.[5]

Primary deficit: What the government spends minus what it takes in — before interest on old debt. Like a household budget without the credit-card interest. If this balance alone is negative, debt keeps growing even ignoring interest.

The debt follows: gross debt reached 81.1% of GDP in May (R$10.6tn), +0.9pp vs. the prior month. And the interest bill keeps eating away: over twelve months, R$1,111bn in nominal interest accrued = 8.48% of GDP (7.74% a year earlier).[5]

Gross debt: Total outstanding government debt measured against annual economic output. 81.1% means the state owes a good four-fifths of what the country produces in a year. Manageable as long as interest stays affordable — dangerous if debt grows faster than income. That's exactly what's happening right now.
My position The CDS market isn't pricing this in (~127bp, unchanged). I think that's too relaxed: a gap widening month after month, an interest bill above 8% of GDP, and a treasury that's already flagging its own target miss starting 2028 — in an election year, that's a ready-made template for rating agencies. No trading signal for tomorrow, but the reason my 12-month point sits high.

[USA] The Fed holds — and the dollar round trip settles down

The Fed has held at 3.50–3.75% since 06/17; the next meeting is 07/28–29. For the real this week, the level matters less than the dollar's behavior: the round trip in the DXY described above (briefly ~101.18, back to ~100.97) is the best snapshot of sentiment. As long as no genuine dollar flight sets in, Hormuz gets traded as an oil story, not a global credit crisis — and oil-exporter Brazil benefits.
