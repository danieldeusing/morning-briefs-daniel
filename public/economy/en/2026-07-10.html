FX in Detail: Where We Stand & Why

The anchor is a still-firm real: EUR/BRL 5.8837 in the ECB fix (Thu Jul 9), −0.50% below Monday's fix (5.9130), Friday's intraday-derived rate at ~5.85. What's notable isn't the number but the reason: this week oil was carrying the real (higher oil price → more export dollars). Today oil falls — Brent −$2.45 to $76.80 —, and the real rises anyway. The support has rotated.

DXY 100.86 soft→ Dollar weaker globally→ Capital into EM assets→ Ibovespa +1.22%→ BRL firmer → EUR/BRL ~5.85

Two forces are replacing oil. First, the soft dollar: the Dollar Index stands at 100.86, near the year's low.[3] A globally weak dollar lifts emerging-market currencies regardless of their own situation. Second, risk-on: the Ibovespa jumped +1.22% to 172,742 points, ending a 3-day losing streak led by banks and retail.[9] Both pull capital into real assets.

What lies underneath structurally remains the Selic carry: Brazil's 14.25% policy rate against 3.50–3.75% in the US. That gap attracts capital as long as no one fears a real crash — and thanks to record reserves (US$370.6 billion) and a calm CDS market, that's currently far off.

Carry Trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap and invest it in Brazil at high interest rates. As long as the real doesn't crash, the rate differential is your profit — if the real falls, the party's over instantly.

The cross-rate check is clean — but with an important nuance. On the same day (Jul 9), the published fix and the calculation agree to within 0.001%. Friday's spot computes about 0.5% lower (~5.85). That's not a contradiction — and not a fresh 0.5% jump either: the investing.com spot already stood at 5.8514 on the morning of Jul 9, so the ~5.85 level existed in parallel with the 16:00 fix. The gap is the sampling delay of a single daily snapshot, not an overnight move. The ECB fix is the binding number; the spot keeps trading afterward.

Cross-Rate Check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two routes to the same destination must be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it's usually because they were measured at different times, or a source is stale. Here it's the timing, not the quality.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Bonds [7][8]

The credit market stays relaxed — notable, five days before the tariff deadline. Brazil's 5-year CDS trades at 125.23 bp (Jul 9), about 1.7 bp tighter on the day and slightly below the early-July level (~126.9 bp).[7] A looming 25% US trade penalty, a widening fiscal hole, an October election — and default insurance is actually getting cheaper. The market is betting on de-escalation.

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against a borrower's default. 125 basis points means: 1.25% of the insured amount per year as premium. Like fire insurance — if the premium rises, the market sees a fire as more likely. It's falling slightly: the market sees no fire.

Add the central bank's FX ammunition: the net FX position rose in June to US$262.3 billion (from US$256.3 billion at end-2025), international reserves to US$370.6 billion (from 358.2).[8] One crack in the picture is the cost of defense: on its swap contracts, the BCB lost about R$9.3 billion in June.

Macro: The Tariff Countdown Overshadows Oil [1][2][4]

[US/BR] July 15 — the Month's Biggest Single Risk

The US Trade Representative (USTR) made a Section 301 determination in June: a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods. The statutory deadline for the final US decision is July 15, 2026 — next Wednesday, in five days. The comment period closed on Jul 1, the public hearing was held on Jul 6, and negotiations are still ongoing.[1]

25% tariff takes effect→ BR goods more expensive in the US→ less BR export to the US→ fewer trade dollars→ Real-negative → EUR/BRL up

My read: the deadline is a date on the calendar, not an automatic trigger — Washington can extend it, soften it, or a deal could land. The market is pricing exactly that (CDS tighter, real firmer). If it's wrong, Jul 15 is the sharpest single-day trigger for EUR/BRL this month — asymmetrically to the upside.

Why Section 301 now? The background explains the new legal route: an earlier 40% tariff on Brazilian goods (2025, linked to the Bolsonaro proceedings) relied on the US emergency-powers law, IEEPA. On Feb 20, 2026, the US Supreme Court ruled 6-3 (Roberts) that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariffs; all IEEPA tariffs — including Brazil's 40% — ended on Feb 24, 2026.[12] That's exactly why Washington is now pursuing the tariff via Section 301, a standalone trade-law track — no longer the emergency-powers lever.

[BR] Fiscal Survey & Focus Anchor

The Fazenda's Prisma fiscal report (Jun 15) raised the median for the 2026 central government primary deficit to R$59.0 billion (from R$57.8 billion); the gross debt/GDP median stands at 83.0% for end-2026.[5] The BCB's Focus survey (Jul 6) sees for 2026: IPCA 5.30%, year-end Selic 14.00%, year-end USD/BRL R$5.20, GDP 1.99%.[6]

Primary Deficit: What the government spends minus what it takes in — before interest on old debt. Like a household budget without the loan interest. If this balance is already negative, debt grows even if you ignore interest entirely.

It's Friday, July tenth. Earlier this week, this brief argued that a stubborn oil premium was propping up the Brazilian real. Today that picture flips — and one date on the calendar now overshadows everything else.

Start with the exchange rate. The European Central Bank's euro-to-real fix sits at five point eight eight three seven, about half a percent below Monday's fix. By Friday, the rate works out to roughly five point eight five. So the real keeps firming for another day. What's striking is the reason: oil is falling hard today, with Brent shedding two dollars forty-five cents to land at seventy-six eighty. That's the exact support that had been carrying the real all week — and it's disappearing. Yet the real climbs anyway.

The support has rotated. In oil's place, two forces have taken over: a soft dollar and a hunger for risk. The Dollar Index sits at one hundred point eight six, near its yearly low. A globally weak dollar lifts emerging-market currencies regardless of their own fundamentals. On top of that, Brazil's Ibovespa stock index jumped one point two two percent, snapping a three-day losing streak. Both trends are pulling capital into Brazilian assets.

But the real story is on the calendar. In five days, on July fifteenth, the statutory deadline expires for Washington's final decision on a twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods — a so-called Section three-oh-one determination by the U.S. Trade Representative. The comment period has closed, the hearing took place on July sixth, and negotiations are still underway.

The mechanism is straightforward. If the tariff takes effect, Brazilian goods get pricier in the U.S., exports drop, fewer trade dollars flow into the country, and the real weakens — pushing the euro-real rate higher. But a deadline isn't an automatic trigger: Washington could extend it, soften it, or strike a deal. My read is that the market is betting on exactly that kind of de-escalation. If that bet is wrong, July fifteenth becomes the sharpest single-day trigger of the month for this exchange rate.

What's remarkable is how calmly credit markets are pricing all this. Brazil's five-year default insurance sits at one hundred twenty-five basis points and has actually gotten slightly cheaper. Five days before a possible trade penalty, with a widening fiscal hole and an October election ahead, insurance against default is getting cheaper, not more expensive. The market simply isn't seeing a fire.

The structural counterweight remains the fiscal hole. The finance ministry's Prisma survey raised its median estimate for this year's central government primary deficit to fifty-nine billion reais. Gross debt stands at eighty-three percent of economic output. And Brazil heads to the polls in October. Election year plus fiscal strain are exactly why my twelve-month estimate points higher.

One calendar clarification, since it's easy to mix up: Brazil's central bank doesn't meet again until August fourth and fifth — not July twenty-ninth. That date belongs to the U.S. Federal Reserve instead. Brazil's benchmark rate currently stands at fourteen point two five percent.

For anyone converting euros to reais: the real has firmed all week, so the exchange window is a touch less favorable than it was on Monday. But the tariff deadline is a genuine two-way risk five days out. If the tariff hits, the real could weaken back, meaning more reais per euro. There's no need to rush — the next hard anchor is today's Brazilian inflation data, followed by a stretch of central-bank decisions and then July fifteenth. Every probability in this brief reflects my own judgment, not a market-implied number.
