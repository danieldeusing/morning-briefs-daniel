FX in Detail: Where We Stand & Why

The anchor is a still-firm real: EUR/BRL 5.8505 in the ECB fix (Fri Jul 10), about 1% below Monday's fix (5.9130). Today's visible driver is a soft dollar — the Dollar Index sits at 100.85, near its yearly low.[5] A globally weak dollar lifts emerging-market currencies regardless of their own situation; the real benefits at the high end because the rate advantage is also luring capital in.

DXY 100.85 soft→ Dollar globally weaker→ Capital into EM + Selic carry 14.25%→ BRL firmer → EUR/BRL 5.85
Carry Trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap (US/Euro area) and invest it in Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the real doesn't crash, the rate gap is your profit — if the real drops, the party's over instantly.

The catch sits right in the driver itself. The soft-dollar prop is the shakiest leg under the real. That's because this week gave birth to a new factor: the market is once again pricing in a Fed rate hike (details in the macro column). If it comes, the dollar flips from soft to strong — and today's tailwind becomes a headwind.

Important for direction: a strong dollar hits EUR/BRL two ways. It pushes EUR/USD down (euro weaker → EUR/BRL lower), but pushes USD/BRL up (real weaker → EUR/BRL higher). Net, with a hawkish Fed, real weakness usually dominates — the risk-sensitive real gets hit harder than the euro, and the Selic-carry edge shrinks. My read: EUR/BRL higher if the Fed bet holds, but with a euro counterweight that dampens the move.

Cross-Rate Check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two routes to the same destination must be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means the readings were taken at different times or a source is stale. Today they match to within 0.001%.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Yields

The central bank is sending a quiet signal of comfort: per a February 2026 report, the BCB was running its FX-swap contract book down toward below US$100 billion — for the first time since May 2022 — by only partially rolling over maturing contracts.[13] This snapshot isn't re-confirmed for July, but it illustrates the stance: it doesn't need to defend the real right now; if anything, it's pulling ammunition back.

FX Swap (BCB): A forward contract through which the central bank sells the market dollar hedging without burning through actual reserves — like insurance against a falling real. When it winds the book down, it's saying: I don't see a need to prop up the rate right now.

At the long end, real yields are climbing in July — a reaction to Hormuz tensions and fiscal worries. The Tesouro IPCA+ 2035 was last quoted at around IPCA + 8.3%.[14] The figure is single-sourced and dated only to the month; I won't hang anything precise on it, but I take it as a directional signal.

NTN-B / IPCA+ Real Yield: A Brazilian bond that strips out inflation — you're guaranteed IPCA + X%. The X (real yield) shows how much genuine real return the market demands from the government. When it rises, Brazilian sovereign risk is being priced more expensively.

Macro: The Oil Shock and the Fed Pivot [1][2][3][4]

[Global] Hormuz Escalates Again — Oil Jumps

Iran's military fired rockets at at least two merchant ships in the Strait of Hormuz on Monday evening, and the Revolutionary Guard struck a third on Tuesday morning — including a Qatari LNG tanker and a Saudi crude tanker.[1] The US then revoked Iran's oil-export license; Brent jumped 5.6% to $76.04 and has held near that mark since (~$76 on Jul 10).[1][3] That breaks a memorandum Iran signed barely three weeks earlier — the pledge to halt attacks in the strait.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and the Arabian Peninsula — the only sea route out of the Persian Gulf. Roughly a fifth of world oil and gas trade squeezes through here. When it becomes unsafe, fears about supply spike immediately — and the oil price with them.
Hormuz unsafe→ Brent +5.6% to ~$76→ US energy prices up→ Inflation fears→ Fed hike bet

[USA] The Fed Turns Toward a Hike — Maybe

This is the real story. The odds of a Fed rate hike in September jumped, per CME FedWatch, from 27% to 49% in a single day (Jul 7)[4] and were quoted as high as 70% by Jul 10;[5] a month ago they stood at just ~33%. At the same time, the odds that the Fed simply holds this month fell to ~66%. I'm deliberately citing both ends with dates rather than a false precision — the range is wide and moving fast.

The trigger has two stages. First, the groundwork: May CPI came in at 4.2% year-over-year — the highest in three years — driven by energy (+23.5% YoY); core was at 2.9%.[6] Second, the spark: the fresh oil shock out of Hormuz threatens to fan exactly that energy inflation further.

Rate Hike Instead of a Cut: The Fed had been in cutting mode (lowering rates to support the economy). But if inflation jumps, it has to switch gears and raise rates again to rein in prices. Like a driver moving from the gas pedal to the brake because a curve suddenly appears. Currently 3.50–3.75%; a hike would take it to 3.75–4.00%.

My read: this hike bet is as oil-driven as it is fragile. If Hormuz calms down and Brent retreats, the bet could unwind as fast as it formed. For EUR/BRL, that means: I'd treat the Fed pivot as a real but reversible upside risk — not a new regime, at least until the September meeting draws closer.

[US/BR] Tariff Deadline Jul 15 — the Second Binary Trigger

In parallel, the trade countdown keeps running: the statutory deadline for the final US decision on a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods (USTR Section 301) is Jul 15, 2026 — next Wednesday, in four days.[12] The comment period and hearing are done, negotiations are ongoing. If the tariff hits, Brazil loses export dollars → negative for the real. It's a deadline, not an automatic trigger — Washington can extend it or soften it.

[BR] Copom: Brake Released, Pledge Scrapped

Copom cut the Selic by 25 bp to 14.25% on Jun 17 (unanimous, third straight cut) — but scrapped the pledge to keep cutting: future moves would depend on new data.[9][10] Reason: headline and core inflation have accelerated and broken through the upper target band. The BCB raised its own 2026 inflation forecast to 5.2% — well above the 4.5% tolerance ceiling.[10] The next meeting isn't until Aug 4–5, and it's a genuine pause-vs-cut toss-up.[11]

Target Ceiling Breached: The BCB targets 3% inflation, with tolerance up to 4.5%. If its own forecast says 5.2%, it's already above the permitted ceiling — like a 130 km/h speed limit with the speedometer reading 150. That's exactly why it no longer dares promise further cuts.

It's Saturday, July eleventh. The Brazilian real is stronger than it's been in a long while — but underneath that calm, a new fault line is forming, and it's coming from oil.

First, the rate. The European Central Bank's euro-real fix stands at five point eight five zero five, about one percent below Monday. The week's three daily fixes form a clean staircase downward: five point nine one on Monday, five point eight eight on Thursday, five point eight five on Friday. The real is firming up. The visible reason is a soft dollar — the Dollar Index sits at one hundred point eight five, near its yearly low.

The catch is baked right into that reason. The soft-dollar prop is the shakiest leg holding up the real. Because something happened in the Strait of Hormuz this week. Iran fired on three merchant ships, including a Qatari gas tanker and a Saudi oil tanker. The US responded by revoking Iran's license to sell oil, and Brent crude jumped five point six percent to seventy-six dollars. That breaks an agreement from just three weeks earlier, in which Iran had promised to halt the attacks.

Here's why that hits the real: roughly a fifth of the world's oil and gas trade passes through that strait. When it becomes unsafe, the price of oil rises — and with it, fears of more US inflation.

And that's the real story of the week. For the first time in a long time, the market is pricing in a rate hike from the Federal Reserve instead of the cut everyone expected. The odds of a September hike jumped from twenty-seven to forty-nine percent in a single day, and were quoted as high as seventy percent by Friday. A month ago, they stood at around thirty-three. The groundwork was already laid: US inflation came in at four point two percent in May, the highest reading in three years. The oil shock out of Hormuz is now the spark.

For the euro-real rate, that means: if the Fed really does turn, the dollar flips from weak to strong — and today's tailwind for the real becomes a headwind. My take, though, is that this hike bet is as oil-driven as it is fragile. If Hormuz calms down and oil falls back, it could unwind as fast as it appeared. I'm treating the Fed pivot as a real but reversible upside risk — not a new regime.

Meanwhile, the trade countdown keeps ticking. In four days, on July fifteenth, the US deadline expires for a twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods. If it hits, Brazil loses export dollars and the real weakens. A second two-way risk in the very same week.

In Brazil itself, the central bank eased off the brake but dropped the promise. Copom cut its policy rate to fourteen point two five percent in mid-June, but abandoned its pledge to keep cutting. Its own inflation forecast for this year was raised to five point two percent — above the allowed ceiling of four point five. The next meeting isn't until August fourth and fifth, and whether it cuts or pauses is an open question.

For your exchange plans, that means: the real has gotten firmer, so the window is a bit less favorable than on Monday. But the coming week brings two events that could weaken it back — the tariff deadline and the Fed bet. There's no need to rush. The next hard anchor is Monday's fix, then July fifteenth.
