Three days to the tariff deadline — the real holds firm

Sunday, July 12, 2026 · Ribeirão Claro/PR · Weekend diff ahead of Monday's rewrite

The real is as firm as it's been in a long time — and this time it's not just the soft dollar carrying it, but Brazilian inflation that came in softer than anyone expected. On Friday the statistics agency reported a June consumer-price rise of just 0.16% — the weakest monthly reading since October. The market instantly read that as a green light for a rate cut in August and flipped to risk-on: São Paulo's stock exchange jumped +2.97%, the dollar fell to R$ 5.1078, its lowest close since mid-June. The ECB euro-real fix stands at 5.8505. Against this strength runs a hard countdown: in three days, on 07/15, the legal deadline expires for the 25% US tariff on Brazilian goods — and around that very deadline a political fight is raging, with Flávio Bolsonaro pushing in Washington for a delay while Lula accuses the Bolsonaro family of "betraying the fatherland." A firm real, soft inflation — but a binary trigger on Wednesday.

FX in detail: where we stand & why

The anchor is a firm real — EUR/BRL at 5.8505 in the ECB fix (Fri 07/10), the dollar at R$ 5.1078, its lowest close in nearly a month. What's new is the second driver: until now the real was mostly carried by the soft dollar (DXY near the year's low). On Friday a homegrown one joined it — Brazilian inflation.

IPCA: Brazil's official consumer-price index — the basket used to measure how fast life is getting more expensive (like Germany's CPI). The June monthly reading: just 0.16% price growth — nearly a standstill. That's the number that set everything off.

Such soft inflationary pressure means, for the market, that the central bank once again has room to cut rates without letting prices spiral. That's exactly what the market priced in immediately — a Selic cut in August — and it flipped to risk-on. Friday's chain of events:

June IPCA 0.16% (soft)→ rate-cut bet for the August Copom→ risk-on: Ibovespa +2.97%→ BRL firmer, USD/BRL 5.1078 → EUR/BRL 5.8505
"Good" vs. "bad" rate cut: Normally a rate cut weakens a currency — less interest means less incentive to keep money there. But when the cut becomes possible because inflation is falling (rather than through political pressure), the market reads it as a sign of strength: the central bank has things under control. That's why the real got firmer on Friday even as the market simultaneously priced in a cut.
Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap (US/euro area) and invest it in Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the real doesn't crash, the interest-rate gap is your profit — if the real drops, the party's over. A cut to 14.00% takes away a bit of carry but leaves the gap huge.

The catch sits in the calendar, not the rate. The firmness is real, but the week brings a binary trigger: the US tariff deadline on 07/15 (details in the macro column). If the 25% tariff kicks in, Brazil loses export dollars → real-negative, EUR/BRL up. If it's postponed or defused via a deal, that very worry disappears. My read: the real is standing on two solid legs (soft inflation, soft dollar), but Wednesday is a genuine coin-flip event that can move the rate in a single jump.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two paths to the same destination have to be the same length. If they deviate by more than ~0.5%, it's usually because they were measured at different times, or one source is stale. Today the deviation is 0.32% — clean, and the small remainder is just the time lag between the ECB fix and the daily close.

Focus survey: A weekly survey run by Brazil's central bank among roughly a hundred banks and funds — the market's consensus view on rates, inflation, and the exchange rate. Like a weather app that shows the average of many forecasts.

Macro: tariff countdown, soft inflation & the politics [1][4][5][10]

[US/BR] Tariff deadline 07/15 — the week's binary trigger

The legal deadline for the final US decision on a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods is 07/15/2026 — this coming Wednesday, in three days.[1] It's grounded in a USTR determination under Section 301 from 06/01 (allegations covering digital trade, tariff preferences, anti-corruption enforcement, intellectual property, and ethanol market access, among others). The comment period (07/01) and hearing (07/06) have both concluded.[2] Importantly: over 1,600 product categories are exempt — including coffee, beef, orange juice, iron ore, pharmaceuticals, and aircraft parts.[3] It's a deadline, not an automatic trigger — Washington can extend or soften it.

Section 301 / tariff: A US statute that lets Washington impose punitive tariffs against a country whose trade practices it deems unfair. A tariff is a surcharge on imports — it makes Brazilian goods more expensive in the US, squeezing Brazil's exports and thus its dollar earnings. Fewer export dollars tend to mean a weaker real.

[BR] The tariff is also a campaign issue

An intense domestic political fight is raging over the deadline. Flávio Bolsonaro — senator and son of the former president — asked the Trump administration (including Secretary of State Rubio) to postpone the tariff decision until after Brazil's October election.[4] Lula calls that "yet another act of betrayal of the fatherland" and accuses the Bolsonaro family of having triggered the tariffs themselves. A Quaest poll shows the mood: 47% believe Lula's accusation, 35% back Flávio's denial. For the exchange rate, this reads as a signal that a quick, mutually agreed solution becomes less likely the closer the election gets.

[BR] Soft inflation ignites the rally

The trigger for Friday's move: the June IPCA came in at 0.16% monthly (after 0.58% in May) — the weakest monthly reading since October (0.09%).[5] The 12-month rate fell to 4.64% (from 4.72%), still above the 4.5% upper target band.[6] The market reacted immediately: the Ibovespa jumped 2.97% to 177,866 points, and analysts once again priced in a 25-bp cut at the next Copom meeting (08/04–05) — a reversal from the previously expected pause.[5][8]

Selic / Copom: Selic is Brazil's benchmark rate, and Copom is the central bank's rate-setting committee (like the ECB Governing Council). When the Selic is high (currently 14.25%), it pays to hold money in reais — that props up the currency. When Copom cuts, it removes some of that incentive.

[US] Fed holds — and September is an open question

For context on the dollar: the Fed left its benchmark rate at 3.50–3.75% on 06/16–17 (Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair).[10] The next meeting is 07/28–29; consensus expects another hold.[11] As long as the Dollar Index stays near its year's low, that's the second pillar under the real. The wildcard remains an oil shock: Brent closed on 07/10 at roughly $76 (~+5% on the week), driven by fresh US–Iran clashes in the Strait of Hormuz.[14] If the oil premium holds, it could tighten US inflation and, with it, the Fed's tone — turning the dollar tailwind into a headwind.
