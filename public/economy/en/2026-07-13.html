FX in Detail: Where We Stand & Why

The anchor is a surprisingly firm Real — EUR/BRL 5.8505 in Friday's ECB fix, with a thin Monday Asia tick even slightly below that at 5.8241. That's notable, because a real oil shock hit over the weekend, and the dollar index ticked up. Both sound like Real weakness — the opposite happened. Why is the real story of the day.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait in the Persian Gulf through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil is shipped — the only way out for half of Arabia's oil exports. If it's blocked — or even just declared blocked — oil gets tight overnight and the price jumps worldwide. Like the only road out of a city: block it, and everything backs up.

That's exactly what drove the price: after a new wave of US strikes and an attack on a container ship, Iran's Revolutionary Guard formally declared the strait closed on Jul 12 — US Central Command disputes the closure, but real traffic fell to around 21 transits (from ~140/day). On top of that, Washington is letting a waiver that had shielded Iranian oil from sanctions pressure lapse effective Jul 17.[4] Brent jumped today by +4.32% to $79.29.[5] The chain over the weekend:

US-Iran escalation + Hormuz "closed"→ Brent +4.3% ($79)→ global risk-off, DXY +0.12%→ Euro softens against a firmer dollar → EUR/BRL slightly down
Why the oil shock doesn't crash the Real: Brazil is itself a major oil exporter (Petrobras, the pre-salt fields). When the oil price rises, the country earns more export dollars — that supports the Real. Working against that is the risk-off effect: in wartime, capital flows out of emerging markets into the "safe" dollar, and expensive oil fuels global inflation, which keeps the Fed hawkish and strengthens the dollar. Both forces pull against each other — which is why the Real held up instead of dropping. For Daniel's EUR/BRL there's an added wrinkle: the slightly lower tick is mostly about the euro, which softened against a firmer dollar, not about the Real.

The catch sits in the calendar, not in the rate. The firmness is real, but Wednesday brings the binary trigger — the US tariff deadline on Jul 15 (details in the macro column). My read: the Real stands on solid ground (oil terms of trade, high carry), but two two-sided risks hang over it — the live oil shock and Wednesday.

Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two routes to the same destination should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, it usually means the measurements were taken at different times or a source is stale. Today the deviation is 0.001% (calculated) or 0.32% against an independent close — clean; the small remainder is just the time lag.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Rates

Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap (US/euro area) and invest it in Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the Real doesn't crash, the interest-rate gap is your profit — if the Real falls, the party's over. A cut to 14.00% trims some carry but leaves the gap enormous.

One quiet bright spot against the tariff/oil noise: the 5-year CDS fell on Jul 10 to 123.90 bp (−1.33 bp / −1.06%).[9] So the market is demanding less premium against a Brazilian default — quiet confidence, despite the headlines.

5-year CDS: A kind of insurance premium against the chance that Brazil fails to service its debt over five years — quoted in basis points. If it falls, the market considers the country safer; if it rises, nervousness grows. Like car insurance getting cheaper because you've gone longer without an accident.

Macro: Oil Shock, Tariff Countdown & the Central Banks

[Global] Hormuz Declared "Closed" — the Live Oil Driver

Over the weekend the US-Iran conflict escalated further: after a new wave of US strikes (the "third strike," ~140 targets on Jul 11) and an attack on a Cyprus-flagged container ship in the Strait of Hormuz (one crew member missing), Iran's Revolutionary Guard declared the strait "closed until further notice — until the American interventions end" on Jul 12.[4] US Central Command explicitly disputes this: "The Strait of Hormuz is open to all vessels … Iran does not control the strait. Traffic is flowing."[10] In substance, both are true at once — legally and militarily the strait isn't sealed, but real traffic collapsed: Windward Maritime Intelligence counted only around 21 commercial transits on Jul 11, against ~140/day before the war.[11] On top of that, Washington is letting a 60-day waiver for Iranian oil lapse effective Jul 17. The result today: Brent at $79.29, +4.32% on the day — still −4.66% for the month, but +14.57% year over year.[5] For the Real this cuts both ways (see the FX column): the oil-export upside against a global risk-off — and a declared closure lifts the supply risk materially above a mere threat.

[USA/BR] Tariff Deadline Jul 15 — The Week's Binary Trigger

The statutory deadline for the final US decision on an additional 25% tariff on Brazilian goods is 2026-07-15 — Wednesday, two days from now.[1] It's based on a USTR finding under Section 301 from Jun 1 (allegations including digital trade, tariff preferences, corruption enforcement, intellectual property, and ethanol market access); the comment period (Jul 1) and hearing (Jul 6) have already taken place.[1] A deadline, not an automatic trigger — Washington can extend it, soften it, or confirm a long exclusion list (including aircraft parts).

Section 301 / tariffs: A US statute that lets Washington impose punitive tariffs on a country whose trade practices it deems unfair. A tariff is a surcharge on imports — it makes Brazilian goods more expensive in the US, squeezing Brazil's exports and thus its dollar earnings. Fewer export dollars tend to mean a weaker Real.

[USA/BR] The Tariff Nuance: What's Actually Already Stacked On?

Important, against a common mix-up: the earlier 50% tariff from the IEEPA track (imposed in 2025, partly as retaliation over the Bolsonaro trial) was struck down by the US Supreme Court in February 2026 — the current base is now only a ~10% tariff under Section 122.[2] Whether the new 25% Section 301 tariff stacks on top of that or is mutually exclusive with the Section 122 levy is itself unresolved: between Jul 15 and Jul 24 a window of regulatory uncertainty applies.[2] In parallel, a forced-labor investigation is underway with a potential additional 12.5%, due Jul 24; industry group CNI puts the exposure at ~4,200 products / ~$15B.[3]

[BR/USA] Central Banks: Selic Falls, Fed Holds

On the rate framework: Copom cut the Selic on Jun 17 to 14.25% — the third cut in a row; the next meeting is Aug 4–5, where the market is pricing in another cut after soft June inflation.[6] The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% (Jun 17, Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair); next FOMC Jul 28–29, consensus hold.[7] As long as the dollar doesn't become durably firmer from the oil shock, Brazil's rate advantage remains the second pillar under the Real — alongside oil export revenue.

It's Monday, July thirteenth. The week opens with two risks stacked on the same exchange rate: a live oil shock out of the Middle East, and an American tariff deadline on Wednesday. And yet the Real is holding remarkably firm.

Start with oil. Over the weekend, the conflict between the United States and Iran escalated further. There was a new wave of American strikes, and on July twelfth, Iran's Revolutionary Guard formally declared the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway that carries about a fifth of the world's oil — closed. US Central Command disputes that, saying the strait is open to all vessels. But actual ship traffic collapsed, down to around twenty-one transits in a single day, versus roughly one hundred forty on a normal day. On top of that, Washington is letting a waiver for Iranian oil lapse on July seventeenth. Brent crude jumped more than four percent today, to around seventy-nine dollars.

You'd expect an oil shock and a firmer dollar to weaken the Real. The opposite happened, and the reason matters: Brazil is itself an oil exporter. When the price of oil rises, the country earns more dollars — and that supports the Real. Working against that is a broader risk-off mood, with capital fleeing into the safe-haven dollar. But those two forces are pulling against each other, which is why the Real didn't buckle.

On the rate itself: the last hard anchor is the European Central Bank's euro-Real fix from Friday, at five point eight five zero five. A thin Monday tick out of the Asian session sat even a touch lower, around five point eight two. A live dollar-Real rate for today wasn't available — the source was blocked — so the Friday close of five point one one carries over here, flagged honestly rather than guessed at.

The second big story is the tariff. On Wednesday, July fifteenth, the statutory deadline hits for the American decision on an additional twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods. Here's a nuance that often gets confused: the earlier fifty percent tariff was struck down by the US Supreme Court back in February. The current baseline is only about ten percent. Whether the new twenty-five percent stacks on top of that or replaces it entirely is still unresolved, with a window of uncertainty running through July twenty-fourth.

For Daniel's currency exchange, that means the Real is firm and the window is a bit unfavorable right now — you get somewhat fewer Reais for your euro. But Wednesday is a genuine two-way risk. If the tariff goes through, the Real would likely weaken back, pushing the euro-Real rate back up — better for Daniel. If there's a delay or a deal, that worry disappears. No need to rush.

In the background, interest-rate policy still favors the Real. Brazil's benchmark Selic rate sits at fourteen point two five percent, and the market expects another small cut in August. The US Federal Reserve is holding at three point five to three point seven five percent. And Brazil's default-risk premium eased slightly on Friday — a quiet bright spot against all the noise.

The next hard anchor is this afternoon's fix, followed by Wednesday's tariff deadline. Tomorrow we'll see how the market digests the oil shock overnight. Every probability in this briefing is my own judgment call, not a market-derived number.
