The oil mechanism remains the one from Monday — just one notch sharper. On July 13, Iran struck two tankers (the Mombasa and the Al Bahiyah) with cruise missiles in the Strait of Hormuz; an Indian sailor died, eight were injured, and both ships caught fire.[3] Trump then announced he would blockade the strait and demand a "toll" for safe passage; early Tuesday the US struck back again.[3] Brent climbed to $84.78 (+1.77% on the day).[4] Why this doesn't tank the real was Monday's big explanation, and it still holds:

Why expensive oil props up the real instead of pressuring it: Brazil is itself a major oil exporter (Petrobras, pre-salt fields). When the oil price rises, the country earns more export dollars — which supports the real. Pulling against that is global risk-off, where capital flees to the "safe" dollar during conflict. The two forces pull against each other — which is why the real holds instead of tipping over. You can see it today in Petrobras: PETR4 gained +2.55% yesterday while the overall index slipped.

New on the dollar side: Today it's not Brazil deciding EUR/BRL, but Washington. June CPI (soft consensus) and Warsh's first congressional testimony collide 90 minutes apart — both move the dollar and, through it, the euro leg of Daniel's rate (details in the macro column). My read stands: firm footing under the real (oil terms-of-trade, soft BR inflation, high carry), but a data-heavy day today and a binary Wednesday looming.

Macro: US Data Day, Oil Escalation & the Tariff Countdown

[USA] CPI + Warsh — the Day's Dollar Double-Header

At 8:30 ET (9:30 BRT), the US Consumer Price Index for June comes out, just 90 minutes before Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first semiannual testimony before the House Financial Services Committee (10:00 ET / 11:00 BRT; the Senate follows Jul 15).[1][2] Market consensus is soft: headline −0.1% vs. the prior month (after +0.5%), core +0.2%.

Not out yet At press time (early morning BRT), the CPI had not been published. The −0.1% / +0.2% figures above are the consensus, not the result. Only the actual print at 9:30 BRT sets the dollar's direction.
CPI (Consumer Price Index): Measures how much the prices of a typical basket of goods rise — the most closely watched inflation figure; a negative monthly reading would mean prices fell slightly in June. For the rate, what matters: soft inflation → the Fed has more room to cut → weaker dollar → stronger real → EUR/BRL tends down. Warsh is the wildcard: seen as relatively hawkish — if he keeps the door closed on cuts, he could support the dollar even with a soft CPI.

[Global] Hormuz Escalates Further — Tankers Under Fire

The oil driver got sharper overnight. On July 13, Iran struck two tankers — the Mombasa and the Al Bahiyah — with cruise missiles in the Strait of Hormuz; an Indian sailor was killed, eight others were injured, and both ships caught fire (fires extinguished).[3] Iran's Revolutionary Guard claimed the attack, saying the ships had "ignored repeated warnings." President Trump announced he would blockade Iran in the strait and demand a "toll" for safe passage; early Tuesday the US struck back against Iran again.[3] Result: Brent $84.78, +1.77% on the day (from ~$79 on Monday).[4] Double-edged for the real (see FX column): export upside against risk-off.

[USA/BR] Tariff Deadline Jul 15 — the Binary Trigger Remains

Still sharp on the calendar: the statutory deadline for the final US decision on an additional 25% tariff on Brazilian goods is tomorrow, Wed Jul 15 (USTR Section 301) — a deadline, not an automatic outcome.[8] On the nuance from Monday: the earlier 50% tariff was struck down by the US Supreme Court in February (base now ~10%, Section 122); whether the 25% stacks on top or excludes it remains open until Jul 24. In parallel, the forced-labor investigation is running (+12.5% possible, due Jul 24).

Tariff (Section 301): A surcharge on imports. It makes Brazilian goods more expensive in the US, squeezing Brazil's exports and, with them, its dollar earnings. Fewer export dollars tend to mean a weaker real — which is why Wednesday is binary for EUR/BRL.

[BR/ECB] Central Banks: Soft BR Inflation, ECB Next Week

The Focus survey (Jul 13) lowered the 2026 IPCA expectation to 5.16%, with the Selic year-end expectation holding at 14% for the third week.[6] The Selic has stood at 14.25% since Jun 17; next Copom Aug 4–5, where the market is pricing another cut.[7] On the euro side, the ECB Governing Council meets Jul 22–23 (rate decision + Lagarde press conference on the 23rd).[10] As long as the dollar doesn't become durably firmer from the oil shock, Brazil's rate advantage — now underpinned by soft inflation — remains the second pillar under the real.

Overnight, the oil shock out of the Middle East also got hotter. And yet, the Brazilian real is holding.

Let's take it in order. The key anchor for Daniel is the euro-real rate, and Monday's fix from the European Central Bank is in: five point eight four three nine. That's actually a touch firmer than Friday's reading of five point eight five zero five. So the real shrugged off the oil shock, exactly as expected on Monday.

On oil: on July thirteenth, Iran struck two tankers in the Strait of Hormuz with cruise missiles. An Indian sailor was killed, eight were injured, and both ships caught fire. President Trump then announced he'd blockade the strait and charge a toll for safe passage, and early Tuesday the US struck back against Iran again. Brent crude climbed to around eighty-four dollars. The reason the real can absorb that is the same as Monday: Brazil is itself an oil exporter. When the oil price rises, the country earns more dollars. You saw it yesterday in Petrobras, which gained ground even as the broader market fell.

But the real news today is in Washington, not Brasília. At eight-thirty New York time, the Consumer Price Index for June comes out. The market expects a soft number, with the headline figure even slightly negative. Important: that number hadn't come out yet as of this writing. It's an expectation, not a result. Only the actual print sets the dollar's direction. Ninety minutes later, Fed Chair Kevin Warsh speaks before Congress. Warsh is seen as relatively hawkish. Even with soft inflation, he could support the dollar if he keeps the door closed on rate cuts.

For the exchange rate, that means: a soft inflation number and a cautious Warsh would weaken the dollar and strengthen the real, pushing the euro-real rate down. A hawkish Warsh or an inflation surprise would flip that. That's exactly why today's range is wider than usual.

Meanwhile, Brazilian rates are providing a tailwind. The Focus survey lowered this year's inflation forecast to five point one six percent, following the lowest monthly reading since October. The Selic stands at fourteen point two five percent, and the market expects another small cut in August. Soft inflation plus high interest rates form the quiet floor under the real.

Over everything still hangs Wednesday: tomorrow the American deadline runs out for an additional twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods. That remains the binary trigger of the week. For Daniel's currency exchange, that means: the window is slightly unfavorable at five point eight four, but there's no need to rush.
