FX in detail: the oil shock hits both legs

Update since Mon The basic picture holds, but the explanation behind it is sharper today. First: the Real stands firm — the last ECB fix (5.8431, Tue July 14) sits practically on Monday's anchor (5.8439). Second: the oil driver is red-hot for a third round. Third — and this is today's real insight — the same oil shock moves both legs of Daniel's exchange rate, not just the Real.

The two legs of EUR/BRL: Mathematically, EUR/BRL is USD/BRL × EUR/USD — built from two parts: the Real leg (how strong is the Real against the dollar) and the euro leg (how strong is the euro against the dollar). For Daniel — he earns in euros, spends in reais — a higher EUR/BRL is good (more reais per euro). Both legs can swing independently; today's oil shock pushes on both, but in opposite directions.
Oil shock (Hormuz, Brent $85)→ Real leg: oil exports prop up the Real → EUR/BRL down
Oil shock (energy inflation)→ Euro leg: ECB stays hawkish → euro firm → EUR/BRL up

The Real leg is the one from Monday: Brazil itself exports oil (Petrobras, Pré-Sal), rising oil prices bring in more export dollars, which supports the Real — against the global risk-off dynamic where capital flees to the dollar during wars. The two forces pull against each other, which is why the Real holds instead of tipping over.

The euro leg is what's new today. The ECB raised all three key rates by 25 bp on June 17 (deposit rate now 2.25%) — the first hike since 2023 — and explicitly justified it with the Middle East war: "The war in the Middle East is generating inflation pressures." The central bank's economists raised their 2026 inflation forecast to 3.0%, "owing to a higher path for energy prices."[6] In other words: the exact oil shock now escalating is the reason the ECB is keeping rates elevated — and a firm rate keeps the euro firm.

Why this matters for Daniel As long as the Real cushions the oil shock via the export side, EUR/BRL stays low (unfavorable for his exchange). But if the ECB responds to persistent energy inflation on July 22–23 with another hike or a clearly hawkish tone, the euro leg could pull EUR/BRL upward — even if the Real holds. That would be favorable for Daniel's exchange. The oil shock is thus double-edged — and the euro leg is the side almost everyone has recently overlooked.

[Global] Hormuz enters round three — Brent above $85

Why expensive oil props up the Real: Brazil is itself a major oil exporter. When the oil price rises, the country earns more export dollars — that supports the Real, even though capital would otherwise flee to the dollar during a war. The two forces pull against each other; so far the export effect narrowly wins.

[ECB] The reason the euro stays firm

The ECB raised all three key rates by 25 bp on June 17 — deposit rate 2.25%, main refinancing 2.40%, marginal lending 2.65% — the first hike since 2023.[6] The justification was explicitly the Middle East war as an inflation driver; the central bank's economists raised the 2026 inflation forecast to 3.0%, "owing to a higher path for energy prices." For Daniel's euro leg, that means: as long as oil stays expensive, the ECB has a reason to stay hawkish — and a hawkish central-bank signal keeps the euro firm. The next ECB Governing Council meeting on July 22–23 (Lagarde press conference July 23) is therefore just as important for EUR/BRL as the Real is.

[US/BR] Tariff deadline today — the binary trigger

Sharp on the calendar: the statutory deadline for the final US decision on an additional 25% tariff on Brazilian goods is today, Wed July 15 (USTR Section 301) — a deadline, not an automatic trigger.[10] At press time, no confirmed outcome was available; the deadline is carried over from yesterday's status, and the result remains open. For nuance: the earlier 50% tariff was struck down by the US Supreme Court in February (base now ~10%, Section 122); in parallel, a forced-labor investigation is running (+12.5% possible, due July 24).

Tariff (Section 301): A surcharge on imports. It makes Brazilian goods more expensive in the US, dampening Brazil's exports and thus its dollar earnings. Fewer export dollars tends to mean a weaker Real — which is why today's deadline is binary for EUR/BRL.

[BR/USA] Rates: Selic 14.25%, Fed holds

The Selic has stood at 14.25% since June 17 (third straight cut, 25 bp each); the next Copom meets Aug 4–5, where the market is pricing in another cut.[11] The US Federal Reserve held its target range at 3.50–3.75% on June 17; the next FOMC meeting is July 28–29 (hold expected).[12] Brazil's high interest rate remains the Real's second pillar: as long as the Selic declines only slowly, carry stays high.

Carry Trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap (US/euro area) and invest it in Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the Real doesn't crash, the interest-rate gap is your profit. A high rate that falls only slowly makes this edge reliable — and the Real attractive.
