FX in detail: why the Real gains on Iran escalation

Update since Mon Today's real story is an apparent contradiction: the US flew a second wave of strikes on Iran, the blockade of Iranian ports is back, oil jumped double digits — and the Real stands firmer, not weaker. The ECB fix fell from 5.8431 (Tue 07/14) to 5.7954 (Wed 07/15), roughly a 0.8% gain for the Real. Anyone watching only the Gulf won't understand this. The driver sits in Washington.

US June CPI 3.5% (well below expectations)→ Dollar slides below 101 (DXY)→ Real leg firmer → EUR/BRL down

The mechanism: a soft inflation print reduces pressure on the Fed to prop up the dollar with high rates — the dollar retreats (DXY below 101, second losing session). A weak dollar lifts nearly every emerging-market currency, the Real first among them. On top of that, two Brazilian buffers are pushing back against the war-driven risk-off: Brazil exports oil itself (higher oil prices = more export dollars), and the rate carry is enormous at ~10.5 pp over the Fed. Three supportive forces against one negative one (risk-off) — the Real edges out the win.

Why expensive oil supports the Real: Brazil produces and sells a lot of its own oil (Petrobras, the pre-salt fields). When oil prices rise, the country earns more dollars from exports — foreign currency flows in, and the Real holds up. That runs counter to the usual rule that capital flees to the "safe" dollar during a war. So far, the export effect is winning.
The two legs of EUR/BRL: EUR/BRL is arithmetically USD/BRL × EUR/USD — built from two parts: the Real leg (Real against the dollar) and the euro leg (euro against the dollar). For Daniel — earning in euros, spending in reais — a higher EUR/BRL is good (more reais per euro). Today the Real leg (firmer Real) is pulling EUR/BRL down — so for exchanging money, that's on the less favorable side.
Why this matters for Daniel As long as the soft dollar and firm Real persist, EUR/BRL stays down — when you exchange, you'll get fewer reais for your euro. The window is slightly unfavorable, but it could flip: if the escalating oil shock eats the US inflation relief back up (see the Fed story below), the dollar could turn and pull EUR/BRL back up — good for you. No need to rush.
Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should roughly equal USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two roads to the same destination should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, usually one source is stale; today it's exactly 0.0%.

Macro: Iran escalation, soft US inflation & the fiscal hole

[Global] Second US strike wave — and the toll is off again

On 07/15 the US reported a second wave of strikes on Iran, targeting capacity that could threaten shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. The naval blockade of Iranian ports is back in force (reactivated a day earlier); the US military disabled an empty tanker headed for Kharg Island — the first vessel hit since the blockade returned.[2] Oil reacted sharply: Brent jumped more than 9%, trading between $83.35 and $86.55 on 07/15 (previous close $84.73), and now sits at $84.51 (07/16, −0.52%).[1][3]

Don't mix these up Two different things that got tangled together yesterday: (a) the blockade of Iranian ports has been reactivated and stays. (b) The initially announced 20% "reimbursement fee" on all Hormuz cargo was dropped by Trump on 07/14 — a day after the announcement, under pressure from the Gulf states — and replaced with "trade and investment deals" with the Gulf states.[4] So the toll is off the table; the blockade is not.
Why Hormuz matters: Roughly 20% of the world's oil and gas supply normally passes through the Strait of Hormuz. When it narrows, fear of disruption grows — and oil prices jump before a single barrel actually goes missing. Traffic there has already been disrupted since the US/Israeli air war against Iran began (02/28).

[USA] Soft inflation today — hike risk tomorrow

US June inflation cooled more than expected: the annual rate fell to 3.5% (from 4.2% in May, below the 3.8% forecast) — the first decline in five months; the core rate fell to 2.6% (from 2.9%).[10] That pushed the dollar below 101 and, in turn, supported the Real (mechanism, left). The catch: May's outlier was energy-driven; June's relief came via the June ceasefire and a briefly reopened Hormuz (oil fell ~21%) — and that very ceasefire has now collapsed.

A September turn? That's why futures markets are pricing something notable: for the FOMC on 07/28–29, a hold is seen as nearly certain (~89%). But for September, the probability of a Fed hike rose to 63% (from over 75% the day before) — the oil shock could bring inflation back before the Fed gets a chance to cut.[11][12]
Why a hike, not a cut: Normally a central bank cuts rates when the economy weakens. But if inflation rises again because of an oil shock, it has to do the opposite — hike — to cool prices. For the dollar, "hike expected" means: a stronger dollar — and that would reverse today's tailwind for the Real.

[BR] Interest rates & the fiscal hole

The Selic has stood at 14.25% since 06/17 (third consecutive cut, 25 bp each); there's no Copom meeting in July, the next one is 08/04–05, where the market is pricing another cut.[13] The gap over the Fed (3.50–3.75%) remains huge at ~10.5–10.75 pp — the second pillar under the Real alongside the soft dollar.

Less good is the fiscal side: the public sector ran a primary deficit of R$56.1bn in May (May 2025: R$33.7bn); over 12 months, the primary deficit totals R$149bn = 1.14% of GDP. Gross debt climbed to 81.1% of GDP — above market expectations.[14]

Primary deficit: What the government is short before counting interest on existing debt — the hole from day-to-day operations alone. A growing primary deficit means the government is going structurally deeper into debt, not just because of old interest costs. With gross debt at 81% of GDP, this is the slow bear under the Real — it doesn't bite today, but it's building.
Carry trade (BRL): Borrow money where it's cheap (US/euro area) and invest it in Brazil at 14.25%. As long as the Real's exchange rate doesn't collapse, the interest-rate gap is your profit. The ~10.5-pp edge makes the Real attractive — even as the Selic slowly comes down.

It's Thursday, July sixteenth. The US flew a second wave of strikes against Iran, the blockade of Iranian ports is back in force, and oil prices have jumped more than nine percent. And yet — and this is the real story today — the Brazilian real is gaining ground.

The most important anchor for Daniel is the euro-real exchange rate. The latest published fix from the European Central Bank sits at five point seven nine five four, from Wednesday. That's roughly eight tenths of a percent below Tuesday's level. So the real actually strengthened, not weakened — even with an oil war raging right next door. Today's afternoon fix is still pending.

Why would the real gain ground during an escalation? The key sits in Washington, not the Persian Gulf. US inflation for June cooled a lot more than expected, dropping to three point five percent. That takes pressure off the Federal Reserve, and the dollar has slipped below one hundred and one, its second straight losing session. A weak dollar lifts nearly every emerging-market currency, the real first among them. On top of that, there are two Brazilian buffers at play: the country exports oil itself, so expensive oil actually brings in more dollars, and its interest-rate advantage over the US is enormous, at more than ten percentage points.

Now, on oil and an important clarification. There are two separate things that got tangled together yesterday. First: the naval blockade of Iranian ports is back in force and staying that way. The US military disabled an empty tanker. But second: the twenty-percent toll that was initially announced on all cargo passing through the Strait of Hormuz was dropped by President Trump on July fourteenth, just a day after it was announced, under pressure from the Gulf states. So the toll is off the table, but the blockade isn't. Brent crude is trading around eighty-four dollars and fifty cents.

The catch behind this good news for the real is the risk lurking underneath it. That very oil shock now escalating threatens to eat back up the relief in US inflation. Futures markets are now even pricing in a possible Fed rate hike for September, putting the odds at sixty-three percent, sharply higher than just a short time ago. If that happens, the dollar could turn around, and today's tailwind for the real would vanish.

Out of Brazil itself, the signals are calm. The stock market closed only slightly lower on Wednesday, and default insurance on government bonds remains low. The benchmark interest rate stands at fourteen point two five percent, and the next rate decision isn't until early August. The budget picture is less pretty: the twelve-month primary deficit stands at one point one four percent of economic output, and gross debt has climbed above eighty-one percent. That's the slow-moving bear underneath the real — it's not biting today, but it's building.

For anyone exchanging money, the picture is this: at around five point seven nine, it's a slightly unfavorable window, because a strong real means fewer reais for your euro. But there's no need to rush — if the oil shock eats away at the inflation relief, the rate could turn back in Daniel's favor. Tomorrow we'll be watching the next fix and the oil situation. All probabilities in this briefing are my own assessment, not a market figure.
