FX in Detail: The Tariff Hit the Stock Market, Not the Currency

Update since Mon When a superpower slaps a 25% tariff on your exports, you'd expect a currency crash. On Thursday, something else happened: the Ibovespa fell 1.24% to 173,825 points[2][3] — the Real gave up only 0.40% over the session (spot close R$5.098).[3] At the ECB fix, it was even milder: USD/BRL +0.09%. And yet EUR/BRL rose 0.63% to 5.8318. Anyone attributing that to the tariff is misreading the rate:

EUR/BRL +0.63%= Euro leg +0.53 pp+ Real leg +0.09 pp

85% of the move came from Europe, not Brazil. EUR/USD jumped from 1.1406 to 1.1467 — the euro firmed against the dollar, and that mechanically lifts EUR/BRL with it, without the Real lifting a finger.

The two legs of EUR/BRL: EUR/BRL is arithmetically USD/BRL × EUR/USD — built from the Real leg (Real against the dollar) and the euro leg (euro against the dollar). Like a scale with two weights: you can see it tip, but not which weight moved. That's why it's worth doing the math — today it was the euro.

Why did the market let the Real off the hook? Because the tariff hits the wrong goods: beef, coffee, and other items are exempt[4] — precisely the agricultural exports that bring foreign currency into the country. A tariff on industrial goods hurts the stocks of manufacturers (hence the Ibovespa) and leaves the currency inflow intact (hence the calm Real).

Zoom out and it becomes clearer: EUR/BRL fell from the month's high of 5.9486 (07/03) to 5.8318 — −2.0%; USD/BRL from 5.2019 (06/25) to 5.0857 — −2.2% (ECB fixes, chart above).[1] A 25% tariff delayed the trend by half a session, not broken it. Behind it, two forces: a carry of ~10.5 pp over the Fed and an oil price around $84.7 that's making an oil exporter richer.[5]

Why this matters for Daniel For two weeks you've been getting fewer reais per euro — not because anything is breaking in Brazil, but because the Real is structurally strong (carry + oil). The tariff barely changes that. What can actually move your window sits in Frankfurt: if the ECB sounds hawkish on 07/23, the euro leg rises — good for you. No need to rush.
Cross-rate check: A published EUR/BRL rate should match USD/BRL × EUR/USD — two routes to the same destination should be the same length. If they diverge by more than ~0.5%, one source is usually stale. Today: 5.0857 × 1.1467 = 5.8318, an exact match to the fix, 0.0%. The EUR/USD leg is sourced independently from the ECB reference — only that way does the check carry any weight.

Positioning, Sentiment & BR Yields

Sentiment confirms the split from above: stocks wobble, credit risk doesn't. Brazil's 5-year CDS closed 07/16 at 122.92 bp — calm despite the tariff day.[6] The Brazil ETF EWZ, by contrast, fell to $35.33 (−1.53%, 07/16) and moved in lockstep with the Ibovespa, not the Real.[7]

CDS (Credit Default Swap): Insurance against a country defaulting on its debt. 122.92 basis points means: the coverage costs 1.23% of the notional per year. That this price didn't rise on tariff day is the real takeaway: the market sees a sector problem, not a country problem.

There is a counter-signal, though: the speculative net-long position in the Real fell to 30.8K contracts (07/07, published 07/10) from 44.7K — a third of the bets on a stronger Real have been unwound, even though the rate held firm.[8] Attribution: from the secondary aggregator FX.co, not cftc.gov — one trust tier below the FX spot numbers. Today's COT report (data as of 07/14) shows positioning after the tariff for the first time — the data point of the week.

Net-long positioning: How many net "Real rises" bets speculators have open. What matters is the direction, not the level: unwinding at a firm rate means buyers are running out — like a party where everyone's still standing, but the first guests are already reaching for their jackets. That makes the Real more vulnerable to a shock, without moving it today.

Section 301: A US statute that lets Washington unilaterally impose tariffs on a country whose trade practices it deems unfair — without asking the WTO. In practice: the tariff arrives fast and can be negotiated away just as fast — a pressure tool, not a law of nature. That's why it would be wrong to price it in as a permanent state.
Where the risk really lies Not in the tariff itself — thanks to the exemptions, it's a sector story (see left). But in the response: the Lula administration is "reviewing reciprocal measures."[4] If Brasília strikes back, this turns into a trade war — and that would hit the agricultural exports that are propping up the Real today.
Oil, the double-edged sword: Brazil produces a lot of its own oil — when the price rises, the country earns more export dollars, and the Real holds up. That runs counter to the usual rule that capital flees to the "safe" dollar during a war. The catch: that same expensive oil drives inflation — and is the reason the ECB is hiking again. So a barrel of oil moves both legs of Daniel's rate, in opposite directions.

It's Friday, July seventeenth. Starting next Wednesday, the United States is putting a twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods. And the market's reaction is the real story today — because it was strikingly selective.

Brazil's stock market took the hit: the Ibovespa fell one point two four percent on Thursday, closing around one hundred seventy-three thousand eight hundred points. The real, though, gave up just four tenths of a percent over the whole trading day. The dollar closed at five point zero nine eight reais. For a tariff of this size, that's almost nothing.

And Daniel's key number, the euro-real rate? The European Central Bank's latest fix rose to five point eight three one eight, a bit over six tenths of a percent above Wednesday. But blaming that on the tariff misreads the number. Break the move apart, and five tenths of a percentage point came from the euro, only nine hundredths from the real. Eighty-five percent of the move came out of Europe, not Brazil. The euro firmed against the dollar, and that lifts the rate mechanically — the real barely moved at all.

So why did the market let the real off the hook? The answer is in the tariff's fine print. Beef, coffee, and several other goods are explicitly exempt — precisely the farm exports that bring foreign currency into the country. A tariff on manufactured goods hurts the stocks of the companies that make them, but it doesn't choke off the flow of dollars. That's why stocks fell and the currency stayed calm. The real risk sits elsewhere: the Lula government says it's reviewing reciprocal measures. If Brasília actually strikes back, a sector-specific tariff turns into a trade war.

Zoom out and the picture gets even clearer. The real has been on an uptrend for weeks. The euro-real rate has fallen about two percent since July third, and the dollar-real rate is down more than two percent since late June. A twenty-five percent tariff delayed that trend by about half a trading session — it didn't break it. Behind it are two strong forces: an interest-rate advantage of roughly ten and a half percentage points over the US Federal Reserve, and an oil price near eighty-five dollars that's making an oil-exporting country like Brazil richer.

The real driver of the next few weeks, then, sits not in Washington and not in Brasília, but in Frankfurt. The world's big central banks are pulling in three different directions right now. Brazil is cutting rates — the benchmark stands at fourteen point two five percent after a third straight cut. The US Federal Reserve is standing still, between three point five and three point seven five percent. And the European Central Bank is hiking, having raised rates in June for the first time in almost three years, to two point two five percent, driven by inflation from the Iran war. It meets again this coming Thursday. The market is pricing a ninety-three percent chance of no change — so it's not the decision that matters, it's the tone.

One warning sign is worth flagging. Speculative bets on a stronger real have been unwound sharply at the futures exchange, dropping from about forty-five thousand contracts to under thirty-one thousand — even though the exchange rate itself held firm. That figure comes from a secondary data provider rather than the regulator itself, so I hold it a bit more loosely. Today's weekly report brings the first real look at positioning after the tariff announcement. For what it's worth, insurance against a Brazilian government default stayed calm, at around one hundred twenty-three basis points — the market sees a sector problem here, not a country problem.

For exchanging money: at five point eight three, you're getting roughly two percent fewer reais per euro than you were in early July. The tariff is unlikely to change that. Your best realistic shot at a better rate is the European Central Bank's tone next Thursday — and, less likely but more powerful, a genuine Brazilian countermeasure. Both are dated events worth watching for. No need to rush.

Next week is worth watching closely: the tariff takes effect Wednesday, the European Central Bank decides Thursday, and the following week brings both the Federal Reserve and Brazil's own rate decision. All the probabilities in this briefing are my own assessment, not a market figure.
