The reason isn't calm — it's a standoff: Brazil's central bank is cutting, the ECB is hiking — and an oil shock out of the Middle East is pushing both currencies up at the same time. Weekend, no fix, no trading: don't exchange today.

FX in Detail — Where We Stand

24–72 hour recap, with the transmission mechanism spelled out

The week ended almost exactly where it began: 5.8505 on the previous Friday, 5.8499 on the Friday after. In between, though, there was real movement — on Wednesday the rate fell to 5.7954, the lowest ECB fix since mid-May, and worked its way back up to 5.85 within two days. Direction matters for you here: a falling EUR/BRL is the worse rate, because your euro then buys fewer reais. Anyone who exchanged on Wednesday would have gotten roughly 545 fewer reais per €10,000 than on Friday. The practical lesson: the range within a flat week is bigger than the week's net change.[1][2]

The trigger for Friday's countermove is easy to pin down. Brent rose 4.58% to $88.09/barrel on Jul 17, driven by the escalation in the Middle East.[4] An oil shock has offsetting effects on EUR/BRL — which is exactly why so little net movement resulted:

Net oil exporter: Brazil produces more oil than it consumes and sells the rest abroad. Picture a family that sells eggs from its own chickens — when egg prices rise, they're thrilled, while the neighbor who has to buy eggs gets annoyed. The eurozone is the neighbor here. That's why expensive oil helps the real and hurts the euro area.

The second, slower path matters more — and it explains why the yearlong trend is flattening out right now. For the first time in this cycle, the two central banks are converging instead of diverging:

Selic 14.25% (falling)↓ ECB 2.25% (rising)→ Rate gap ~12.0 pp shrinking from both sides→ Carry incentive declining→ EUR/BRL finds a floor
Carry Trade: Borrow money where rates are low (eurozone: 2.25%), and invest it where they're high (Brazil: 14.25%). Like an allowance deal: you borrow from Mom at 2% and invest with Dad at 14% — as long as neither of them changes the rules, the 12 percentage points of difference is your profit. This exact incentive has carried the real for a year. Every Selic cut and every ECB hike chips away a piece of it.

My view, against the consensus: The market is reading June's Copom statement as largely hawkish — guidance dropped, its own inflation forecast raised to 5.2%.[7] The actual data say something else: June IPCA came in at +0.16% m/m and 4.64% over twelve months, and the IPCA-15 preview came in below expectations at +0.41%.[8] So Copom is forecasting inflation that its own dataset is currently undershooting. I therefore consider a cut on Aug 5 more likely than the statement suggests — and the BRL carry somewhat more vulnerable than the consensus assumes.

A single event with real leverage: the ECB decision on Thursday, Jul 23. "No change" is already ~93% priced in[10] — meaning a hold will barely move the euro, because the market has already paid for it.

"Priced in": The market bets on every decision in advance and adjusts prices immediately. Like a soccer match where the favorite is at 9-to-1 odds — if they win, nobody cheers and the payout is almost nothing. Only a surprise moves the price.

So any movement will come from the fine print: if Lagarde confirms the September hike to 2.50%, the euro edges up (toward 5.89); if she hedges it because of oil-price uncertainty, EUR/BRL falls toward 5.81. Brent also runs as a two-way daily risk alongside it.

Most likely 5.86 (~35%) — a slight upward tilt, since a hawkish hold is the somewhat more likely outcome.

60% 3–6 Months (through ~Jan 2027) — higher with swings, 5.70–6.25

A single event dominates here: Brazil's presidential election in October 2026. Ahead of an election, investors demand a premium for not knowing future fiscal policy — they sell first and ask questions later. That collides with a fiscal position that's genuinely deteriorating: a primary deficit of R$ 56.1 billion in May versus R$ 33.7 billion in the same month a year earlier, a 12-month cumulative deficit of 1.14% of GDP, and a monthly interest burden of R$ 107.5 billion.[9]

Primary deficit: What the government spends minus what it collects — before counting interest on old debt. Like a household budget where you first tally only groceries and rent and note loan payments separately. If that part alone is negative, the debt pile grows even if you owed zero interest.

Counterweight: a rate gap of roughly 12 points doesn't vanish overnight, and Brazil's own central bank raised its 2026 BR growth forecast from 1.6% to 2.0% in its June Monetary Policy Report.[12] Hence a wide range instead of a clear trend.

Most likely 5.95 (~25%) — with a sharp downside reversal if the election outcome is read as market-friendly.

55% Next 12 Months (through July 2027) — wide and directionless, 5.60–6.40

Twelve months out, it's not the central bank that decides — it's the new government's fiscal regime. The question is whether gross debt (recently near 81% of GDP[15]) credibly comes down, or just keeps running. If fiscal discipline arrives, the real can convert its rate gap into a genuine appreciation and test 5.60. If it doesn't, the risk premium eats up the carry, and 6.40 is within reach — without anything "dramatic" needing to happen.

The range is deliberately wide: after twelve months of −9.5%, a narrow forecast would be false precision.

Most likely 5.95 (~20%) — slightly above today, because one year of real strength rarely turns into two.

It's Saturday, July eighteenth. The currency markets are closed, so there's no new rate today — which makes this a good moment to look back a year instead of a day.

Exactly one year ago, on July seventeenth, twenty twenty-five, the euro stood at six point four six two four reais. Yesterday it closed at five point eight four nine nine. That's down about nine and a half percent. In practical terms: the same euro amount buys roughly six hundred fewer reais per thousand euros than it did twelve months ago. The real has had a very strong year.

This week, though, the trend stalled. Monday through Friday, almost nothing moved — from five point eight five to five point eight five. But there was real movement in between: on Wednesday the rate dipped to five point eight oh, the lowest level since mid-May, and then climbed back over two days. Direction matters here: a falling rate is the worse one for you, because your euro then buys fewer reais. Anyone who exchanged on Wednesday would have gotten about five hundred forty-five fewer reais per ten thousand euros than on Friday. The practical takeaway: the swing inside a quiet week can be bigger than the week's net change.

The reason for Friday's rebound is oil. Brent jumped four and a half percent in a single day to eighty-eight dollars, on escalating tensions in the Middle East. Expensive oil pulls this exchange rate in two directions at once. Brazil produces more oil than it uses and benefits, while Europe has to import energy and feels more inflation pressure. One effect strengthens the real, the other keeps the European Central Bank hawkish — and together they mostly cancel out.

The slower, more important story is interest-rate policy. For the first time in this cycle, the two central banks are moving toward each other instead of apart. Brazil cut its benchmark rate to fourteen and a quarter percent at its June meeting, the third straight cut. The European Central Bank, meanwhile, raised its rate in June to two and a quarter percent. The rate gap that carried the real for a year is now shrinking from both sides.

Here's where I break from the consensus. The market is reading Brazil's June statement as hawkish — the central bank dropped its pledge to keep cutting and raised its own inflation forecast to five point two percent. But the data tell a different story. June inflation, according to Brazil's statistics agency IBGE, came in at zero point one six percent for the month and four point six four percent over twelve months. So the central bank is forecasting a path its own numbers are currently undershooting. I think another cut in early August is more likely than the tone suggests.

The counterweight is the budget. Brazil's primary deficit hit fifty-six billion reais in May, up from thirty-four billion a year earlier. Interest payments alone are costing the government over one hundred billion reais a month. As the October election gets closer, that number is exactly what will drive the risk premium on the real.

For the coming week, there's only one event with real weight: the European Central Bank's rate decision on Thursday, July twenty-third. A hold is already about ninety-three percent priced in — any movement will come from the fine print, namely whether the September hike gets confirmed. I expect the week to stay steady, between five point eight oh and five point nine oh, most likely around five point eight six.

One more honest note: positioning data is missing today. Swap positions, futures data, the yield curve — none of it has a fresh reading on a Saturday, and nothing here is estimated to fill the gap.

If you need to exchange money in the coming weeks: next week looks like the calmer window. The real upside risk sits on Brazil's rate decision on August fourth and fifth. We'll cover that in detail in the next brief.
