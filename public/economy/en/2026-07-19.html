FX in Detail — Where We Stand

The two moves of the last 72 hours, spelled out causally

Friday's fix of 5.8499 looks like a quiet week. It isn't — it's the result of two forces that nearly cancel each other out.

1 · The Tariff That Hits the Wrong Goods

The US Trade Representative issued a Section 301 order on Jul 15: a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods, effective Wednesday, Jul 22. It's justified by digital trade and electronic payments, preferential tariffs, obstruction of corruption investigations, intellectual property, market access for ethanol, and deforestation.[8]

Section 301: a US legal statute that lets Washington unilaterally impose tariffs when it accuses a trade partner of "unfair practices" — without waiting for a WTO ruling. Like a landlord who changes the locks without a court order and says: go ahead and sue.

What matters is the exemption list. Exempted are, among others, beef, orange juice, aircraft and aircraft parts, and energy products.[8] Those are exactly the lines with the greatest FX weight. A tariff that largely spares dollar revenue is mechanically a weak event for the exchange rate — and that's exactly how the market reacted:

USTR: 25% as of Jul 22→ FX-heavy exports exempted→ Trade-balance channel weak→ USD/BRL only +0.6% in two days

Equities read it differently: the Ibovespa lost 1.24% to 173,825 points, Vale −2.1%, Petrobras −1.7%.[5] That's not a contradiction, just two different questions. FX asks "is less dollar money flowing in?", equities ask "is the business climate getting worse?" Today's answer to the first is no, to the second, yes.

My take: the real FX risk isn't the tariff, it's Brazil's response. Brasília is activating the reciprocity law and heading to the WTO; on the table are moves against patents (pharma, agriculture) and US audiovisual providers instead of mirror-image tariffs.[9] Counter-tariffs would be FX-neutral. An attack on patent rights would not be: it hits legal certainty, and legal certainty is the price foreign direct investors factor in. The finance minister has been holding back so far — as long as that holds, the effect stays small.

2 · Oil as a Counterweight

Brent closed Friday at $88.10, +4.59% in a day and over 14% for the week.[6][7] Drivers: Kuwait reported an Iranian attack on a power plant with seawater desalination, CENTCOM carried out its sixth consecutive night of strikes on Iran, and the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil transport flows — has been disrupted since February.[16]

For Brazil, that's not purely a burden. The country is a net oil exporter, and crude oil happens to be exempt from the new US tariffs. A higher Brent price means more export revenue in dollars:

Iran escalation→ Brent +14% in a week→ BR export revenue rises (oil tariff-free)→ supports BRL, cushions the tariff shock

The flip side comes later and hits inflation: expensive oil means expensive diesel, expensive diesel means expensive freight, and in Brazil almost everything moves by truck. That's a story for the August IPCA, not this week.

It's Sunday, July nineteenth. The foreign-exchange markets are closed, so Friday's European Central Bank fix carries through until Monday morning: five point eight four nine nine reais to the euro. That looks like a quiet week. It wasn't.

The big story comes out of Washington. The U.S. Trade Representative issued a twenty-five percent tariff on Brazilian goods on July fifteenth. It takes effect this coming Wednesday, July twenty-second. The stated reasons: digital trade and electronic payments, preferential tariffs, obstruction of corruption investigations, intellectual property, ethanol market access, and deforestation.

But the interesting part is the exemption list. Beef, orange juice, aircraft and aircraft parts, and energy products are all excluded. Those are exactly the export lines that bring Brazil the most dollars. A tariff that largely spares foreign-currency earnings is mechanically a weak event for the exchange rate — and that's how the market read it. The real gave up only about half a percent over two days.

Equities were less relaxed. The Ibovespa dropped one point two four percent to one hundred seventy-three thousand eight hundred twenty-five points, with Vale and Petrobras each down around two percent. That's not a contradiction. The currency market asks whether fewer dollars are coming in. The stock market asks whether the business climate is worsening. Today's answer to the first is no; to the second, yes.

The second big theme is oil. Brent closed Friday at eighty-eight point one zero dollars, up four point six percent on the day and more than fourteen percent for the week. The trigger was the escalation between the U.S. and Iran: Kuwait reported a strike on a power plant with seawater desalination, and U.S. Central Command flew its sixth straight night of strikes on Iran. For Brazil, expensive oil isn't purely bad news — the country is a net oil exporter, and crude is exempt from the new tariffs. That supports the real and cushions the tariff shock. The bill comes due later, through pricier diesel and freight, and from there into August inflation.

Two dates will shape the coming week. On Wednesday the tariff takes effect — what matters isn't the tariff itself, but Brazil's response. Brasília is preparing its reciprocity law and a case at the World Trade Organization. Under discussion: moves against patents and against American streaming providers, rather than mirror-image tariffs. Counter-tariffs would be harmless for the exchange rate. An attack on patent rights would not be, because it hits the legal certainty that foreign investors price in. Then on Thursday, the European Central Bank meets in Frankfurt.

My call for this week: sideways, between five point eight zero and five point nine two, with five point eight six as the most likely level. For the month, slightly higher, between five point seven eight and five point nine eight. Those percentages are my own confidence, not what the market is pricing in. The rates themselves are fetched live and dated; the read on them is judgment.

What keeps the real propped up is still the interest-rate gap. Brazil's Selic rate sits at fourteen point two five percent, against the American Fed's three point five to three point seven five. That's a good ten and a half percentage points a year. As long as the real doesn't fall faster than that spread, holding reais pays — which is why it shrugs off bad news. The central bank's Focus survey sees the dollar at around five point two zero reais by year-end, which works out to roughly five point nine five for the euro.

Two gaps I want to be upfront about. The dollar index was reachable through only a single source today, sitting around one hundred point seven on Friday. And the entire positioning section — the central bank's swap book, futures-market positioning, the B3 yield curve — wasn't available. I'd rather leave it blank than make up a number.

In the coming days, Wednesday is what matters most: whether Brazil responds with restraint or with patents. Tomorrow's brief looks at Monday's open and previews the ECB.
