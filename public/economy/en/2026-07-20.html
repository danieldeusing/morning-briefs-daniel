The Tariff Hits Wednesday — Why the Real Is Holding Anyway

The week's top story, and the one with the most direct math for Daniel

Section 301 tariff: A US statute that lets Washington unilaterally impose punitive tariffs when it accuses a partner of "unfair practices" — no WTO proceeding required. Like a neighbor who charges a toll on everything you carry across his yard because he doesn't like your fence. It's paid by US importers, but it dampens demand for Brazilian goods — and with it, the dollar inflow into Brazil.

From Wednesday, July 22, 25% applies to most Brazilian goods; announced July 16, justified by "unfair trade practices."[1] The exemption list is what matters: coffee and beef are exempted — two of Brazil's most reliable sources of foreign exchange. A tariff that spares the biggest dollar inflows is an equities event, and only a limited currency event.

Brasília's tone is tough, its tactics cautious. Finance Executive Secretary Dario Durigan: "We will not bow our heads or bend to any foreign interests." An unnamed government source in the same report said it was "highly unlikely there won't be a tough response."[1] Vice President Geraldo Alckmin explicitly pushed back on the record: "The law is not retaliatory. There is no retaliation" — it is "a legitimate legal instrument to defend the national interest."[1] That gap between anonymous toughness and official restraint is itself the signal. Either way, the tools are not retaliatory tariffs: restrictions on dividend and licensing repatriation for US media conglomerates, suspension of pharma and agricultural patents, reviving the WTO dispute — all under the reciprocity law.[2]

The Catch Skipping retaliatory tariffs is self-interest: tariffs on US goods would push up Brazilian consumer prices, and the government is already fighting a 5.16% IPCA expectation in an election year. From an FX standpoint that's real-friendly — no imported inflation, no extra rate pressure.

My take: The market is pricing the tariff as a negotiating position, not a permanent state — Lula's government is circulating the cumulative US surplus of $424.5 billion over 15 years, an argument aimed straight at the US justification logic.[3] As long as coffee and beef stay out and no retaliatory tariffs materialize, the FX channel is narrow. This week's event risk sits in implementation details that someone will read for the first time on Wednesday.

Oil at $90 — and Why That Hits Brazil Differently Than Germany

Brent is trading at $90.29 per barrel, +2.48% versus the prior day (pulled Jul 20); as recently as Jul 14 it was still around $83.[4][5] The trigger is an escalation that has kept intensifying since mid-July — the figures age fast, so each one here carries its as-of date: 57 transits from Friday to Sunday and more than 50% below the prior week was the reading as of Jul 14;[6] on Thursday, Jul 16, Kpler counted only eight transits (prior day: 15) — against roughly 88 per day before the crisis. US strikes on Iranian targets ran, per CENTCOM, through Friday morning, Jul 17, for a sixth consecutive night; add to that the reinstated blockade of Iranian ports. A clean figure for Jul 19/20 wasn't obtainable by press time — the most recent solid numbers are those from Jul 16/17. cnbc.com — Hormuz traffic (Jul 17) · cbsnews.com — CENTCOM, sixth night (Jul 17) · straits.live — IMF PortWatch baseline of 88/day

What no longer applies The 20% toll on cargo passing through the Strait of Hormuz still haunts plenty of market commentary — it is no longer a live threat. Washington announced it around Jul 13 and reversed it a day later, on Jul 14; in its place came pledged investment and trade deals from the Gulf states. Anyone explaining today's oil price with the toll is citing a policy that has been gone for nearly a week. What remains — strikes, the blockade, and the collapsed tanker traffic — is fully sufficient as an explanation on its own. pbs.org (Jul 14) · foreignpolicy.com (Jul 14) · thehill.com (Jul 14)
Terms of trade: The ratio of what a country gets for its exports to what it pays for imports. Like trading collectible cards: if the value of your cards rises while everyone else's stays flat, you get more for the same card. For Brazil as a net oil exporter, pricier oil means more dollars per barrel — a tailwind for the real. For Germany as an importer, the same price is a headwind.

That's the second half of the explanation. The two forces run against each other:

Hormuz escalation→ Brent +2.48% to $90.29→ BR terms of trade ↑→ BRL supported→ EUR/BRL capped
25% tariff from Jul 22→ Export revenue ↓→ but coffee/beef exempted→ BRL pressure dampened

Add the third and currently strongest factor: a soft dollar (DXY 100.70) alongside a 10.5 percentage-point rate advantage for the Selic (14.25%) over the Fed (3.50–3.75%).

Carry trade: Borrow money where it's cheap and invest it where rates are high — here, borrow dollars at ~3.6% and invest in Brazil at ~14.25%. As long as the real doesn't crash, the rate gap is the profit. If the rate turns, the party's over — and everyone rushes for the door at once.

Starting point: EUR/BRL 5.8499, the ECB reference fix from Friday, Jul 17; Monday's fix (~16:00 CET / 11:00 BRT) wasn't available at press time.[7]

Cross-rate control — and why the usual math proves nothing The obvious check is USD/BRL × EUR/USD = EUR/BRL. That's exactly the one that doesn't work here: this brief's USD/BRL figure is calculated from the ECB fixings (BRL fix ÷ USD fix), and EUR/USD is an ECB fix. Both legs come from the same dataset — so the equation resolves purely algebraically and inevitably produces 0.0% deviation, regardless of where the real market rate stands. A zero that validates nothing.
An honest check needs a second, independently collected source. One exists: the PTAX rate from Brazil's central bank — its own survey of currency dealers in Brazil that doesn't route through the ECB's euro fix. PTAX close for Jul 17 (sampled ~13:10 BRT): EUR/BRL 5.8542 (bid 5.8533 / ask 5.8550) versus the ECB fix of 5.8499.
Real divergence: +0.07% — clearly below the 0.5% warning threshold, with the small remainder explained by the different sampling times (the ECB fixes around 14:15 CET, PTAX closes around 13:10 BRT). Same picture on the USD leg: PTAX 5.1173 versus 5.1158 from the ECB fixings, +0.03%. The anchors are holding today — provably this time. bcb.gov.br — PTAX rate history (Jul 17, 2026) · ecb.europa.eu — reference-fix XML

The move that matters: Over 30 days, EUR/BRL fell from 5.8989 (Jun 18) to 5.8499 — −0.83%; from the monthly high on Jul 3 (5.9486) that's −1.66%. The real has firmed into the tariff announcement, not against it. Within the 90-day band (5.7449–5.9896), the rate sits at 43%, just below the mean of 5.8601.

What this means for converting money The rate currently isn't on Daniel's side: at 5.8499, €5,000 gets exactly R$29,250; at the Jul 3 level it would have been R$29,743. Anyone with flexibility on timing has no reason to rush — this week's event density (tariff Wed, ECB Thu) points more upward, and converting at the bottom of the monthly range gives away that optionality. This is a description of the situation, not a recommendation: the decision hinges on liquidity needs, not the chart.

Where I diverge from consensus: Itaú BBA raised its year-end USD/BRL forecast from R$5.15 to R$5.30 on Jun 26 — citing, among other things, worsening terms of trade from falling oil.[8] That driver has since reversed: Brent is at $90.29. The forecast can still come true via the fiscal or tariff channel — but one load-bearing pillar has collapsed, and anyone citing the number should cite the caveat with it.

It's Monday, July twentieth. In two days, on Wednesday, the American tariff of twenty-five percent on most Brazilian goods takes effect. That's the story of the week — and what's remarkable is how calmly the Brazilian real is taking it.

Friday's European Central Bank reference rate puts the euro at five point eight four nine nine reais. That's just under a third of a percent firmer than Thursday, but well below the monthly high from July third. Over thirty days, the euro has lost about eight tenths of a percent against the real. For someone billing in euros and living in reais, that's the wrong direction. In concrete terms: five thousand euros gets you twenty-nine thousand two hundred fifty reais today. Earlier this month, it would have been about four hundred ninety reais more.

On to the cross-rate test — and a correction on my part. The usual math, dollar-real times euro-dollar equals euro-real, is worthless here, because both inputs come from the same European Central Bank dataset. That equation always checks out and proves nothing. An honest check needs a second, independently collected source. Brazil's central bank provides one with its PTAX rate, its own survey of currency dealers. Friday's PTAX close puts the euro at five point eight five four two reais, against the European Central Bank's fix of five point eight four nine nine. The real gap is seven hundredths of a percent — well under the half-percent warning threshold, with the rest explained by the different sampling times. The anchors are holding today for real, not just on paper.

So why isn't the real buckling ahead of the tariff? Three reasons. First, coffee and beef are exempt from the tariff — two of the country's most reliable sources of hard currency, of all things. Second, Brasília is deliberately not answering with retaliatory tariffs but through its reciprocity law instead, meaning restrictions on American media companies and patent suspensions. That keeps the government from importing inflation onto itself. And third, the dollar is soft worldwide — the dollar index sits at one hundred point seven.

Then there's oil. Brent crude is trading at ninety point two nine dollars a barrel, up two and a half percent in a single day. Behind that is an escalation that has kept getting worse since mid-July: American strikes ran through Friday morning for a sixth straight night, and tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz was down to just eight transits on Thursday — before the crisis it ran around eighty-eight a day. One number still floating around doesn't hold anymore, though: the announced twenty percent toll on cargo through the strait was reversed on July fourteenth, a day after it was announced, and replaced with investment pledges from the Gulf states. Explaining today's oil price with that toll means citing a policy that no longer exists. For Brazil, a net oil exporter, expensive oil is still a tailwind — more dollars per barrel exported. What's a headwind for Germany is propping up the currency here.

Thursday, the European Central Bank makes its call. That nothing changes is the consensus — though sources scatter between roughly eighty-eight and ninety percent market-implied and nearly ninety-nine percent on prediction markets; of seventy-four economists surveyed, not one expects a change. That's why I'm giving a range here rather than a single decimal. So the decision itself isn't news. Only the tone of the press conference afterward can move things. One thing worth remembering: if the euro rises, that looks exactly like a weak real in the euro-real number — but it's the opposite.

A word on the data, because honesty matters more here than completeness. There's only a single source today for the Brazil fund's money flows — a direct pull failed. For futures-market positioning, no clean net figure was available, so I'm not giving one. And the Brazilian central bank's swap book is still showing its late-February level — nearly five months old.

As for the forecast: today, I expect sideways trading between five point eight three and five point eight nine. For the week, a slight upward tilt toward five point nine five, driven by Wednesday's tariff deadline. Over twelve months, I'm spreading the range wide, from five point seven to six point three — because the sell side itself is split. Itaú sees the dollar ending the year at five point three reais; BTG Pactual sees four point nine. But BTG's number dates back to May tenth, before the tariff and before the oil spike — it's the oldest of the three figures. And Itaú's reasoning leans partly on falling oil prices, which has since reversed. All the probabilities here are my own assessment, not a market figure.

Tomorrow is the last quiet day before the tariff. The brief will mainly be watching how Monday's and Tuesday's central-bank fixes come in and whether Brasília produces any concrete countermeasures — and starting Wednesday, whether the real keeps its composure.
