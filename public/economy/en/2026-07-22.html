Oil Jumps — the Iran Shock Is the New Force

Overnight's fresh move: why pricier oil is carrying the real through the tariff

Brent is trading at $92.23 a barrel, +1.35% versus the prior day (as of Jul 22) — the highest level since mid-June.[1] The trigger is geopolitical and squarely current: overnight, the US struck targets in Iran for an eleventh consecutive night (military command centers, drone depots, logistics); the Houthis are threatening a naval blockade of Saudi Arabia, and Defense Secretary Hegseth put the war's cost so far at $37.5 billion on Jul 21.[2] The oil market is pricing in sustained supply uncertainty around the Strait of Hormuz.

Terms of trade: The ratio of what a country earns for its exports to what it pays for imports. For Brazil as a net oil exporter, pricier oil means more dollars per barrel — a tailwind for the real. Like a farmer whose harvest is suddenly worth more: he can afford more without working any harder.

That's exactly why today's oil jump is real-friendly — it works against the tariff. On Tuesday it was still the weak dollar alone carrying the real; now the oil tailwind joins in, even though the dollar actually firmed slightly overnight. That's the real story: the real is getting a second engine while the tariff risk is already priced in.

Iran escalation (11th night)→ Brent +1.35% to $92.23→ BR terms of trade ↑→ Real tailwind→ EUR/BRL holds near 5.80 despite the tariff
The flip side An oil shock isn't purely a blessing. Globally, pricier oil is inflationary and risk-off — if Brent climbs toward $100 and stays there, Brazil re-imports price pressure too (fuel, freight), which could slow the disinflation path and, with it, Selic cuts. Short term, the currency effect dominates (more dollar inflows); medium term, the oil price is a double-edged sword.

The Tariff Is Here — the First (Quiet) Look

Monday/Tuesday's story, now reality. What the start triggered — so far, little.

As scheduled: as of 0:01 US Eastern Time last night — 01:01 in Ribeirão Claro — the 25% tariff applies to "essentially all" Brazilian goods. The narrow transition for goods already in transit runs through Jul 29; the broad exemption list (coffee, beef, orange juice, pharmaceutical inputs, civil aviation, energy) remains the reason this is primarily a stock and sector event, not a currency event.[3]

The best evidence for that came from the market itself: the night before (Jul 21), the Ibovespa closed at 173,325.65 points, −0.03% — essentially unchanged, despite the tariff countdown and Middle East tensions.[4] The stock market had already digested the tariff; the currency, even more so. As long as no new escalation arrives (the additional investigation still expected this week, which could tack on another 12.5%, remains supported only by news summaries as of today, not primarily confirmed), the tariff stays a known, priced-in event.

It's Wednesday, July twenty-second. The twenty-five percent American tariff on nearly all Brazilian goods took effect overnight, at one-oh-one a.m. here in Ribeirão Claro. And here's the notable part: the real just keeps firming up, as if nothing happened.

The latest reference rate from the European Central Bank is Tuesday's: five point seven nine nine seven reais per euro. That's another half a percent or so below Monday — and, for the first time in this series, below five point eight zero. For someone billing in euros and living in reais, that's the uncomfortable direction. In concrete terms: five thousand euros buy just under twenty-nine thousand reais today. That's about one hundred sixty-two reais less than Monday and nearly seven hundred forty-five reais less than on July third.

The new piece is the second engine: oil. Brent has jumped to ninety-two point two three dollars, up a good one percent, and the highest level since mid-June. The reason is geopolitical and very current: the US struck targets in Iran overnight for an eleventh straight night, and the Houthis are threatening a naval blockade. For Brazil as a net oil exporter, pricier oil is a tailwind — more dollars per barrel. That's exactly what's carrying the real through the start of the tariff, even though the dollar actually firmed a bit overnight.

But oil cuts both ways. Globally, pricier oil also pushes prices higher. If Brent climbs toward one hundred dollars and stays there, Brazil re-imports price pressure too — and that could slow interest-rate cuts. Short term, the currency effect wins out; medium term, the oil price isn't just a blessing.

On the cross-rate check: the usual math of dollar-real times euro-dollar proves nothing here, because both pieces come from the same European Central Bank data set. As an independent second source today, I'm using the Brazilian stock exchange's dollar close from Tuesday: five point zero seven three, against the central bank's five point zero seven nine. The gap is a bit over a tenth of a percent — well under the warning threshold. The anchors line up.

The tariff itself stayed remarkably quiet. The night before, Brazil's stock index closed essentially unchanged — the market had already digested it. The broad exemption list remains the reason: coffee, beef, orange juice, civil aviation, and energy are spared — precisely the most reliable dollar earners. A narrow transition window for goods already in transit runs through July twenty-ninth.

The sore spot remains fiscal, not the tariff. The central government posted a primary deficit of roughly fifty-three billion reais in May; over twelve months, that adds up to one point zero six percent of economic output — even though the government's target was a surplus of a quarter percent. That gap is what's shaping the outlook for the next three to six months. The risk barometer, though, stays calm: default insurance on Brazilian government bonds sits at one hundred twenty-three basis points, even a touch lower than last week.

On the forecast: for today, I expect sideways to firm, between five point seven eight and five point eight five. For the week, sideways up to five point nine, with the European Central Bank deciding tomorrow. That nothing changes there is consensus — only the tone of the press conference could move things, and that works through the euro, not the real. Over twelve months, I'm spreading the range wide, from five point seven to six point three. Every probability here is my own judgment, not a market number.
