The Oil Shock — and Everything Hanging Off It

One attack on two tankers, and suddenly the Fed is caught up in it

The Houthis say they attacked the Saudi oil tankers Encelia and Layla with drones and missiles — the first breach of the blockade on Saudi shipping they themselves had declared.[2] Trump announced he would hold Iran responsible; CENTCOM reported overnight the twelfth round of US strikes against Iran, and at least seven ships turned away from the Bab el-Mandeb strait.[3] The price reacted brutally: Brent +6.46% to $100.15 — the triple-digit mark for the first time in this series.[1]

What's interesting isn't the oil price itself, but what it triggers. The US bond market immediately read the jump as an inflation event, not a growth brake: the 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.70%, the highest level since January 2025.[4] That puts next week's Fed meeting under an entirely different light.

Terms of trade: The price ratio between what a country sells and what it buys. Like a kid whose trading cards suddenly become worth more while candy stays the same price — they can afford more without working any harder. Brazil is a net crude oil exporter; when the oil price rises, more foreign currency flows into the country and the real gets a tailwind.
My counter-position Today the market is trading the friendly half of the story: oil up, Brazil an exporter, real firm. I think that's a fair-weather trade. The same barrel that flatters Brazil's trade balance is also driving US inflation and putting a Fed rate hike on the table — and a Fed that hikes drains capital out of emerging markets faster than oil revenue can refill it. Today, the real wins. Over three months, that's exactly the more fragile side.

Cross-rate check — clean today The check only means something if the EUR/USD leg is pulled independently — calculated from the same fixings, the equation checks out purely algebraically and proves nothing. The move that matters. From the monthly high on Jul 3 (5.9486), that's −2.88%; over twelve months, −11.5% (Jul 23, 2025: 6.5298). The trend is a year old and gained a third pillar today: alongside the soft dollar environment and the fat carry, oil now joins in. Notable: the DXY rose 0.31%, yet USD/BRL still fell 0.16%. The real is gaining against a globally stronger dollar — it can only pull that off with a driver of its own making.

What this means for exchanging money Still not in Daniel's favor. At 5.7775, €5,000 gets exactly R$28,887.50 — about R$87 less than yesterday and R$855 below the Jul 3 level. Anyone exchanging today locks in the worst value since mid-May. My base case sees EUR/BRL higher over 30 days — exchanging at the bottom gives away optionality. This describes the situation, not a recommendation.

It's Thursday evening, July twenty-third. The trading day in Europe and the US is done, and it was all about oil. The Houthis say they attacked two Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea with drones and missiles. Brent crude jumped in response, up six point five percent to one hundred point one five dollars a barrel.

What's interesting isn't the oil price itself, but what it sets off. In the US, the bond market immediately read the jump as an inflation threat. The yield on the ten-year Treasury rose to four point seven percent, the highest level since January twenty twenty-five. That puts next week's Federal Reserve meeting under a whole new light.

Here's why: the Fed has been led by Kevin Warsh since May. According to a Forbes analysis of market pricing, investors see roughly a one-in-three chance of a rate hike at the meeting on July twenty-eighth and twenty-ninth. Not a cut — a hike. That was triggered by the June price index, which, according to Fed Governor Lisa Cook, rose three point seven percent from a year earlier — one point seven percentage points above the Fed's target.

In Europe, the European Central Bank held all three key rates steady at midday today. The deposit rate stays at two point two five percent. For the euro, that means no tailwind.

Now to the rate that matters for Daniel. The European Central Bank's reference rate today stands at five point seven seven seven five reais per euro. That's zero point three percent less than yesterday, and the lowest fix since May thirteenth. The cross-rate check came through completely clean: zero point zero zero one percent divergence, against a warning threshold of half a percent. There's nothing to doubt in today's numbers.

In concrete terms: five thousand euros buy about twenty-eight thousand eight hundred eighty-seven reais today. That's roughly eighty-seven reais less than yesterday, and about eight hundred fifty-five reais less than the monthly high on July third. Anyone exchanging today locks in the worst value since mid-May.

The reason behind the real's strength is that same oil price. Brazil is a net crude exporter, so pricier oil boosts the value of the country's export basket. What's notable: the dollar was firmer globally today, and the real gained anyway. My counter-argument: the market is only trading the friendly half of the story right now. The same barrel of oil that flatters Brazil's trade balance is putting a rate hike on the Fed's table — and a tighter Fed drains capital out of emerging markets faster than oil revenue can refill it. Today, the real wins; over three months, that's the more fragile side.

Out of Brazil itself, little was exciting. The Selic rate sits at fourteen point two five percent, with the next rate decision due on August fourth and fifth. The risk premium on Brazilian government bonds is calm, at around one hundred twenty-two basis points, and the Finance Ministry's deficit estimate for this year was trimmed slightly to fifty-eight point zero eight billion reais. The stock market, though, gave up ground: the Ibovespa fell zero point six percent.

Tomorrow, Friday's fix is the next fresh anchor, and what matters most is whether Brent holds above one hundred dollars. But the real event is next Tuesday and Wednesday: the Fed meeting, where for the first time in years, a rate hike is seriously on the table.
