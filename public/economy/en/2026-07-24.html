The $750 Billion AI Bet — Why the Dollar Is Listening

A data-center wave in Silicon Valley reaches all the way to Daniel's exchange rate

This week, the American AI camp put numbers on the table that are new even for this sector. OpenAI has raised its planned budget for compute infrastructure through 2030 to $750 billion, according to the Wall Street Journal — roughly 25% more than its own estimate from earlier this year.[2] Concretely, that shows up in "Project Camellia": a $30 billion data center in Effingham County, Georgia, on 1,400 acres of land, designed to deliver 3.2 gigawatts of compute capacity once fully built out; construction phases run from 2028 to 2032.[1]

In parallel, AMD held its "Advancing AI 2026" event in San Francisco, where the chipmaker unveiled its rack system Helios (MI455X accelerators, "up to 30% more tokens per dollar") — and, more important at the macro level, the purchase commitments behind it. Anthropic is taking up to 2 gigawatts.[3] Meta and OpenAI reportedly come in at around 6 GW each, roughly 12 GW combined — figures I'm deliberately citing loosely, since they come from market reports rather than AMD's own statement.[4]

Capex (capital expenditure): money a company spends on long-lived assets — here, data centers and chips — as opposed to running costs like salaries. Like the difference between buying a house and the weekly grocery run. $750 billion in capex means OpenAI is pouring, over several years, a sum into concrete, power, and silicon that matches the GDP of a mid-sized country. (AI capacity is measured in gigawatts of power demand — one GW ≈ the consumption of a major city — because electricity shows how much physical world sits behind the "cloud.")

And what does this have to do with EUR/BRL? On second look, quite a lot. Hundreds of billions of dollars being built into the ground in the US are the core of what the market calls "US exceptionalism": the narrative that American growth is outpacing the rest of the world. That narrative pulls capital into dollar assets — and capital that buys dollars supports the dollar.

My take — and the counter-bet This is a slow driver, not a same-day catalyst: it moves EUR/BRL over quarters, not this week. And it has an open flank — a capex boom of this size is partly debt-financed. If the narrative flips from "boom" to "bubble," the dollar tailwind turns into a headwind within seconds. Soberly for Daniel: the structural bias leans higher for EUR/BRL — but it's a bias, not a trading signal. Brazil's Fiscal Credibility Gap

When the government misses its own target by more than 50 billion

The real Brazilian thread this week isn't the exchange rate, but trust in public finances. The government now officially expects a primary deficit of at least R$22.6 billion (~$4.4 billion) for 2026 — the budget had planned a surplus of R$34.3 billion.[10] Between target and reality, there's a gap of nearly R$57 billion, and it would be the fourth deficit in a row under this government.

Primary deficit: the government's balance before interest payments — does the state take in more from taxes than it spends (excluding debt interest), or less? Like a household budget where the loan interest hasn't been entered yet: if there's already a minus there, you have to borrow even more just to cover the interest. A primary deficit means debt grows even when you strip out the interest burden.

Notable: the market trusts the government even less than it trusts itself — the median of the Finance Ministry's Prisma Fiscal survey puts the deficit at R$58.08 billion (slightly better than R$59.02 billion in June).[11] For context: strip out certain line items and the tolerance band from the official R$22.6 billion target, and the government itself cites a broader projection of around R$60.3 billion — already above the market median.[10] This gap between the official forecast and the market estimate is the credibility gap — and explains why the risk premium ticked up slightly this week.

Risk premium slightly higher. Brazil's 5-year CDS stands at 124.25 bp (close Jul 23, +2.20% on the day) — higher than the 121.6 bp seen midweek, but still calm by historical standards.[12]

CDS (Credit Default Swap): insurance against a borrower defaulting — here, the Brazilian state. The price in basis points is the annual premium: 124 bp ≈ 1.24% of the insured amount. Like theft insurance: if the premium rises, the market thinks a claim is somewhat more likely. Going from 121 to 124 bp is a twitch, not an alarm.
