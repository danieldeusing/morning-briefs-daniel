Health & Safety

Heads up — RSV season Fiocruz (InfoGripe, week 21/2026) reports rising severe respiratory cases (SRAG) for six weeks across 18 states — Paraná included. The driver is RSV, accounting for 48.5% of all positive tests; the highest disease rate is in young children. Nationwide already 77,153 SRAG reports in 2026.[4]
RSV: a cold virus that just gives adults a runny nose, but in babies it can clog the tiny airways — like trying to breathe through a straw. That's why so many Brazilian infants end up hospitalized with RSV bronchiolitis during the winter (May–August).

What it means for a 1-month-old girl

The season is ramping up, and newborns are the highest-risk group. Practical steps: avoid crowds with the baby or keep visits short (stay at the edge of Arraiás, not in the crush), wash hands before touching, keep sick visitors away, keep siblings with colds at a distance from the baby. Fiocruz emphasizes RSV vaccination during pregnancy as the most important protection for the first six months of life — if not yet done, ask your pediatrician or local health clinic about current options for infant protection (maternal vaccination or Nirsevimab antibodies).[4]

Explaining Festa Junina to the kids

If you want to explain the June hustle and bustle to the older ones in English: Festa Junina celebrates the Santos do Junho (Anthony, John, Peter) and the harvest season — a blend of harvest festival and street fair. The dance is called quadrilha (a kind of square dance), the clothing is "caipira" (country wear), and the food is all corn-based: pamonha, canjica, pé de moleque, bolo de fubá. A nice weekend bilingual project: bake cornbread together and learn the Portuguese words.
