Health & Safety

Heads up — respiratory season The latest Fiocruz bulletin (InfoGripe, SE 22/2026, reference week 31.05–06.06, published 11.06) reports continued rising hospital admissions from RSV and flu. Children under 4 are hit hardest, and for them RSV is the main driver. Paraná ranks among eleven states with SRAG readings at alert/risk level and trending upward.
RSV: a cold virus that in adults usually runs harmless — like a normal runny nose. But in babies it can clog their tiny airways and lead to severe breathing trouble (bronchiolitis). Picture a straw that clogs up with just a little mucus: that's what happens in a baby's lungs. That's why it matters with your 1-month-old and your 2-year-old.

In the bulletin, SRAG (severe acute respiratory infections) is rising nationally, driven in the Northeast, Southeast and South — including Paraná — mainly by RSV, which accounts for 49.6 % of lab-confirmed cases in hospitalized patients. Across the state, 2026 has seen 82,544 SRAG cases and 3,591 deaths so far.[3] For your family the message is simple: be extra careful with the baby and toddler, especially in crowds.

Practical for baby at the Arraiá: Avoid crowds, or take the newborn for just a short time in a carrier close to your body. Wash hands before handling, keep sick visitors away. When in doubt, let the older kids have the party and stay home with the baby.
Warning signs: fast/labored breathing, rib retractions, poor feeding, bluish lips → get medical advice, don't wait it out.

Dengue: all clear

Unlike 2025, dengue in Paraná is calm right now: cases and deaths fell over 96 % in the first months of 2026.[4] No acute Aedes alert for Norte Pioneiro — but standard precautions with standing water in the yard still make sense, especially after yesterday's rain.

Worth it if …you go with an overnight stay. 4–6 hours each way is too much for a day trip with four kids; as a planned São João weekend anchored by a city park, very doable — and entry is free. For today, though, Londrina is the realistic choice: close, dry, three fests at once.

German-Brazilian Angles

Festa Junina explained — for the German ear

If Arraiás are new: Festa Junina is Brazil's spin on harvest festival meets village fair, in June around Saints Antônio, João and Pedro. Checked shirts, straw hats, Quadrilha dance and a bonfire — for the kids mostly games and sweet corn cake. A low-barrier way into local culture, almost always free — and exactly right for your two school kids (8 and 7) to pick up words like "quadrilha" and "pescaria" in the original. Practical tip for today: wear a checked shirt, pocket some spare real — the rest sorts itself.

Quentão, Pinhão & Co. — what's on the table

First time at an Arraiá? Quentão is hot spiced wine (with ginger, for the grown-ups); Pinhão is boiled pine nuts, a Paraná winter specialty; add Canjica (sweet corn porridge), Pamonha, Bolo de fubá (cornmeal cake) and non-Quentão versions for the kids. For the German palates at your table: nothing is spicy, nearly everything is sweet — the kids will find something here.
