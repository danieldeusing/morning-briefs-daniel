Health: RSV wave in winter — and why it matters with a baby

The most important topic of the week, and it hits our household directly: Brazil is in the middle of the winter wave of severe respiratory infections, and the RSV virus (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) is the main driver. The Fiocruz InfoGripe network is sounding the alarm for epidemiological week 19/2026 (May 10–16): across Brazil 63,634 reported SRAG cases in 2026, and over the last four weeks 44.5% of positive tests came back RSV.[1] The incidence is highest in the youngest.

RSV: a cold virus that in adults usually just causes a runny nose — but in a baby it can block the tiny airway. Think of it like a straw: in you it stays wide, in an infant it's so thin that a bit of mucus almost closes it. That's why the exact same cold is dangerous for a four-week-old child.

SRAG stands for "Síndrome Respiratória Aguda Grave" — severe acute respiratory syndrome, meaning cases that land in the hospital. Brasil Agency reported on May 14, 2026 roughly 57,585 SRAG cases nationwide and 2,660 deaths in the year; RSV was responsible for 41.5% of virus-confirmed cases, and children under two years are the group with the highest risk.[2]

For Paraná, InfoGripe lists the state among the 18 showing "signs of an increase in the long-term trend" for flu hospital admissions — clearly on an upward trajectory, not declining.[1]

Update (as of June 11, 2026): The more recent InfoGripe bulletin SE 22/2026 shows the wave has accelerated — nationwide around 82,544 SRAG cases in 2026 (compared to 63,634 from SE 19) and an RSV share of ~49.6% among positive tests. Paraná remains on an upward trend. The SE 19 figures above are the baseline; this is the latest status.[7]
Heads up With a four-week-old baby, this week calls for: avoid crowds (full festivals, shopping malls at peak times), wash hands before touching the baby, keep sick visitors away, don't let anyone with a cold hold the baby. Breathing warning signs in an infant: rapid breathing, retracted ribs, bluish lips, refusing to feed — see a doctor immediately.

Concrete protection: The RSV vaccine is available in Brazil for pregnant women from week 28 of pregnancy onward; the SUS offers a monoclonal antibody for premature babies and high-risk infants.[2] Whether the baby (or the mother retroactively) received one of these options is worth a quick conversation at the next pediatrician visit.

Good news: dengue nearly gone

A bright spot alongside the RSV wave: the dengue situation in Paraná has eased dramatically in 2026. In the first 14 epidemiological weeks, the state recorded only 2,028 confirmed cases and one death — against 52,193 cases and 90 deaths in the same period in 2025. That's a decline of 96.1% in cases and 98.8% in deaths.[3]

The regional hotspot remains around Londrina (17th Health District, 534 confirmed cases in 2026) — but at overall low levels. The latest state bulletin (Epidemiological Report No. 19) was published June 9, 2026.[4]

Still: the Aedes mosquito doesn't care about a cool, rainy week — keep emptying standing water after the rain (saucers, buckets, clogged gutters). Low numbers are the result of exactly this routine, not a reason to stop.

Festa Junina season: what's on these and next weeks

Brazil's loveliest family season is starting. From June 19, several parish festivals open in the Londrina area (~110 km) — Quentão, Quadrilha dancing, Pão de Queijo, corn dishes, games for the kids. Today is also the last day of Festa Junina in Ibiporã (Praça Pio XII, June 6–14) — though with 95% chance of rain, it's a crapshoot; check with the organizers before heading out.

Festa Junina: Brazil's harvest/St. John's festival in June. Think of it like a village fair — campfire atmosphere, plaid shirts, row dancing (Quadrilha), sweets made of corn and peanut. Family-friendly, mostly free or small donation, evenings outdoors.

The event radar below lists the confirmed dates. Important this week: RSV wave plus baby — big, crowded festivals are exactly the crowds you want to avoid with a four-week-old infant right now. The older kids can go; the baby stays home the smarter choice. Come early July, with some distance from the wave peak, it's a different story.
