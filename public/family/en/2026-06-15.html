Health — RSV surge in infants [1][2][3]

Heads up — baby context Brazil-wide SRAG wave climbing; all 27 states in alert, warning, or high-alert status; Paraná among states with rising trend, Curitiba among the 15 capitals with concerning activity. Hardest hit: babies under 2 — exactly like the one-month-old girl in the house.

The Fiocruz InfoGripe analysis for epidemiological week 21/2026 (published June 3, 2026) shows clear momentum: SRAG (Síndrome Respiratória Aguda Grave, severe acute respiratory illness) is climbing nationwide, and the driver is RSV — Respiratory Syncytial Virus. [1] In the previous analysis (SE 18/2026, May 14) RSV made up 41.5% of all positive SRAG tests; the latest wave pushed that to roughly 48.5% in recent samples. [2] In Paraná through SE 13/2026 (April 8) there were already 2,299 SRAG cases with 92 deaths — roughly one case per hour. [3]

RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus): a very common cold virus that feels like a bad cold in older kids and adults — but in infants and preemies it can reach the bronchioles and cause severe breathing trouble. Like a harmless puddle that becomes a real obstacle for a toddler just learning to walk.
SRAG (Síndrome Respiratória Aguda Grave): Brazil's umbrella term for "severe acute respiratory infection" — anything that needs hospital care, not outpatient treatment. When this number climbs, the ERs fill up, not the doctor's office.

What this means in practice, today:

  • Older siblings keep going to daycare/school — they're the likeliest route for bringing it home to the baby. Wash hands rigorously before they touch the baby; reduce time spent near the infant if someone is coughing or sniffling.
  • Lower the bar for calling an ambulance or going to the ER, don't raise it: fast breathing ("peito chiando"), flaring nostrils, rib retractions, blue lips, or poor feeding in such a tiny baby — go to Pronto-Atendimento Ribeirão Claro or straight to UPA Jacarezinho.
  • De-prioritize crowded indoors with small kids (birthday parties, enclosed playgrounds) these weeks — and if you do go, keep the baby out of the chaos as much as you can.

Brazil has approved an RSV antibody treatment (Nirsevimab) for infants in the private sector since 2024; availability varies by region in the public system. If your infant hasn't gotten it yet, ask the pediatrician — peak season is right now.

Dengue — the rare good news [4]

The 2026 season is dramatically milder in Paraná compared to 2025. As of SE 1–14/2026 the state reports 2,028 confirmed cases and 1 death — against 52,193 cases and 90 deaths in the same window last year. [4] That's a drop of 96.1% in cases, 98.8% in deaths. In 2025 the Norte Pioneiro (19th district, anchored on Jacarezinho) posted 6,705 cases, fourth in the state — don't project that effect into 2026; that year was an outlier.

Data gap The weekly "Informe Epidemiológico" from SESA Paraná is at report No. 19 (06-09-2026) — but the PDF is image-based, text not machine-readable. So the 2,028 cases above stay our last verified count until a newer, machine-readable bulletin appears. Keep up the mosquito routine (drain checks, no standing water, repellent for the older kids) anyway — the season still has time to run.
