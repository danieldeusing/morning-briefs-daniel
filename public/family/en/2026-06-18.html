Health first: the RSV winter and your baby

Heads up Brazil is in the middle of respiratory winter. According to InfoGripe (Fiocruz, week 21/2026, as of 03.06.2026), all states are at warning, risk, or high-risk level for serious respiratory illness—and RSV accounts for 48.5% of positive cases over the past four weeks. Hit hardest: children under 2 years old. Paraná is among 18 states with sustained uptrend over six weeks, Curitiba under alert status.[1]
RSV: "Respiratory syncytial virus" is a cold virus that usually just causes a runny nose in older kids and adults—but in tiny babies, it can clog their small airways (bronchiolitis). Think of a straw an adult can drink from easily: for a baby, the same straw is so thin a little mucus can almost block it. That's why infants especially end up in the hospital with it in winter.

This is not a reason to panic, but reason for a few simple reflexes while your little daughter is just four weeks old. Transmission happens via droplets and hands—exactly the paths you can interrupt:

Protects the baby
  • Wash hands before anyone touches the baby—visitors included.
  • Keep people with colds at a distance; siblings with runny noses away from baby's face.
  • Skip crowded indoor spaces with the infant these weeks—open air is clearly better.
  • Breastfeed if you can—passes on antibodies.
Doctor/emergency if baby has
  • fast, labored breathing or rib retractions,
  • blue lips, refusing to eat, noticeably fewer wet diapers,
  • fever under 3 months old—always get it checked out.

Two protection options for the tiniest exist through the public system: maternal vaccination during pregnancy (from week 28 on, gives newborn passive immunity) and a monoclonal antibody against RSV, available through the SUS for especially at-risk infants (e.g., premature babies) according to Agência Brasil. Both are questions for the pediatrician at the next visit—we mention it here so you can ask about it specifically.[1][2]

Dengue: the month's good news

While respiratory illness is climbing, the mosquito front is refreshingly quiet this winter. Paraná counted only 2,028 confirmed dengue cases through calendar week 14 (through 14.03) and one death—a drop of 96.1% from last year (2025: 52,193 cases, 90 deaths). The latest state bulletin is Epidemiologic Report No. 20 from 16.06.2026; we don't have the exact day-by-day figures from it, so we're citing the last firmly confirmed tally.[3]

Doesn't mean "all-clear forever": the region around Londrina (17th health region) had 534 cases through week 14, the highest count in the state. The simple reflex stays—after rainy days like this Saturday, empty standing water in pots, buckets, saucers, and gutters. A task a 7- and 8-year-old can take on nicely as "mosquito patrol."

German-Brazilian notes

June 24 is São João—the peak of Festa Junina season. A nice chance to show the kids the difference: in Germany around this time people mostly celebrate summer solstice and midsummer bonfires, but here in Brazil it's quadrilha dances, quentão, corn dishes, and paper lanterns—in the middle of winter. If a local festa pops up in Ribeirão Claro or a neighboring town in the next few days, it's a perfect, nearby family evening; keep an eye on the prefeitura channels.
