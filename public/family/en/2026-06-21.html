Health & Safety

Respiratory alert According to InfoGripe (Fiocruz), Paraná ranks among 11 Brazilian states with respiratory hospitalizations at alert/risk level and rising trend over the past two weeks — epidemiological week 22 (May 31–June 6, 2026). With a one-month-old baby in the house, this is the week's most important story.
SRAG & RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus): "SRAG" is Portuguese for "severe acute respiratory illness" — respiratory infections so serious they land someone in the hospital. RSV is one of the main culprits: think of it like an especially persistent cold that's usually harmless in adults but can clog the tiny airways in newborns (bronchiolitis). In infants, that's why we're extra watchful here.

In concrete terms: of confirmed SRAG cases tested positive in the past four weeks, 48.5% were RSV — by far the largest share (InfoGripe, week 21/2026, Fiocruz).[1] The hardest-hit group is children under 4, and the rise in this age group is "primarily driven by RSV."[2] At the same time, influenza-A hospitalizations are climbing across the broader South region (Sul) — including here in Paraná.[1] Brazil-wide through week 22 of 2026, there have already been 3,591 SRAG deaths recorded.[2]

What that means in practice No need to panic, but a good reason to stick to baby-safety routines: wash hands before handling the baby; keep anyone with a cold at a distance; avoid crowded indoor spaces with the infant (so Arraiás should be quick, early evening, and outdoors rather than packed inside a tent); watch for fast breathing, trouble feeding, or rib retractions, and call your pediatrician if you're unsure. Parents of preemies or at-risk babies: the SUS has offered RSV protection via Nirsevimab for premature and at-risk infants since February 2026 — ask your local health post.[3]

Dengue: locally relaxed

Good news from the dry season: in health region 19 (Jacarezinho) — which includes Ribeirão Claro — there have been 67 confirmed dengue cases so far in 2026 (weeks 1–23, report no. 20 from June 16, 2026), with 55 acquired locally, at an incidence of 18.31/100k.[4] That's well below neighboring regions (Londrina 122.02/100k; Cornélio Procópio 161.51/100k).[4] Across Paraná, it's 5,129 confirmed cases and 2 deaths.[4] The dry winter keeps mosquito breeding down — but standing-water checks in your yard remain worthwhile.

Weather: wet first, then clear skies

Today (Sun, June 21) will stay dry in Ribeirão Claro with a low of 11°/high of 18°, fog forming in the evening; humidity at 96%.[5] Then a wet front moves in: Monday through Wednesday (June 22–24) showers and thunderstorms with 90 / 80 / 90% chance of rain, cool around 16–18°.[6] São João falls on Wednesday, June 24 — the wettest and coldest day — so plan indoor celebrations for that date.

From Thursday (June 25) onward, the weather shifts: dry, starting with fog then a stable sunny stretch with 0% rain extending well into the week and the July weekend (Climatempo's 15-day trend shows sunny conditions June 27–July 5, with highs to 25°).[7] Bottom line for outdoor plans: not early in the week, but from Sat, June 27. — that's when Festa and weather align.

Weekend Plans & Festa Junina

It's high season for Festas Juninas and Julinas — Brazil's winter festivals with quadrilha dancing, bonfires, corn, quentão, and game booths, traditionally centered around São João (June 24). Since the 24th is rainy this year, outdoor celebration shifts to next weekend and into July.

This weekend (June 27/28)

In the Londrina radius (~110 km, just over 1 hr 40 min drive), two parish Arraiás kick off: the Santuário N.ª S.ª Aparecida celebrates Sat & Sun, June 27/28, and Recanto of the Irmãs da Toca de Assis in Sertanópolis (Rod. Celso Garcia Cid, km 52.5, ~95 km) on Sat, June 27.[8] Both are free; you pay only for food and games. With dry, sunny weather (15°/24°), it's an ideal family afternoon.
