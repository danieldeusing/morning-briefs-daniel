Health & Safety — RSV winter is here

Heads up — respiratory season Paraná is at the highest SRAG alert level with a long-term growth trend in epidemiological week 23 (June 7–13, 2026) — together with Curitiba and 13 other states. With a one-month-old baby in the household, this is the most important point of the week.[1]
SRAG / RSV: SRAG is Portuguese for "severe acute respiratory syndrome" — the category Brazil uses to track severe respiratory hospitalizations. RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) is currently the main driver. Think of it as a particularly stubborn cold: usually harmless in older kids and adults, but in the very young, the tiny airways can become inflamed and obstructed — that's why babies under six months especially end up in the hospital.

The virus mix from the last four weeks (national, Week 23) shows why the youngest are at risk: RSV 51.4% of all positive samples, plus Rhinovirus 23.9%, Influenza A 19.1%, Influenza B 7.1%, and SARS-CoV-2 just 2.2%. Nationwide, Brazil has reported 89,725 SRAG cases so far in 2026, with just under half (44,485) virologically confirmed.[1]

The good news The trend is in the right direction. In Paraná, SRAG hospitalizations in 2026 dropped 83.5% compared to last year (as of Week 19). In newborns up to six months — exactly Daniel's age group — cases fell from 515 to 60 (−88.3%); under-twos overall dropped from 903 to 149. Why: Paraná has been vaccinating pregnant women against RSV since December 2025 (by May 2026, already 47,213 doses, 87.12% coverage), and the baby gets maternal protection through the placenta.[2]

What this means in practice — three simple habits

Protects the baby
  • Wash hands before anyone touches the baby — including visitors.
  • Anyone coughing or sniffling keeps distance or wears a mask near baby.
  • Older siblings (8/7/2 yrs.) bring school and daycare germs home: wash hands after coming in, don't sneeze or kiss near the baby's face.
  • Keep breastfeeding — it passes antibodies.
Doctor signals (don't wait)
  • Fast, labored breathing; flaring nostrils; rib cage retractions.
  • Poor feeding, noticeably fewer wet diapers.
  • Bluish lips or skin; fever in such a young infant.
  • When in doubt, go to urgent care — better to go too early with a newborn.

Not fearmongering — just the seasonal basics that make the difference for an infant in RSV winter.

Dengue — All clear, with a caveat

Unlike respiratory illness, the Aedes situation in 2026 is pleasantly calm. The latest PR bulletin (Report No. 20, June 16, 2026) reports a 96.1% drop compared to 2025: 2,028 confirmed cases and 1 death, versus 52,193 cases in the same period last year. Regionally, the 17th health region based in Londrina leads with 534 confirmed cases.[3]

Reading the number Important context: these figures cover epidemiological weeks 1–14 (through mid-March 2026) — the tail end of the hot, rainy dengue season. They're not current-week numbers. In dry winter, mosquito risk is low anyway; the next active season arrives with summer rains in late 2026. Until then, the routine stands: avoid standing water — the immediate pressure is low.

It's Monday, the twenty-second of June, and Brazilian winter shows two faces this week — a wet, cold front at the start and respiratory season, which really matters for a one-month-old baby in the house.

Let's start with health, because that's the most important line today. Paraná is at the highest alert level for severe respiratory illness in epidemiological week twenty-three. The main driver is RSV, which now accounts for roughly fifty-one percent of all confirmed samples. For older kids and adults it's usually just a stubborn cold, but in the very young, the tiny airways can become inflamed, and that's exactly why babies under six months especially end up in the hospital.

The good news stands right next to it. In Paraná, hospitalizations for severe respiratory infections this year have dropped by more than eighty-three percent — in newborns up to six months, from five hundred fifteen down to just sixty cases. Why: The state has been vaccinating pregnant women against RSV since December, and the baby gets protection through the mother. So things are clearly moving in the right direction.

Still, two or three simple habits are worth it now. Wash hands before anyone touches the baby, including visitors. Anyone coughing or sniffling keeps their distance. And older siblings, who bring school and daycare germs home, wash their hands right after coming in before they go to the baby. No reason to panic — just the basics that make a difference this season.

On the weather front, the week starts uncomfortably. Today ninety-four percent rain chance, tomorrow even ninety-five, and Tuesday stays the coldest and wettest day at only nineteen degrees. A day indoors with the kids. From Wednesday, for São João, it clears up, but mornings get crisp — Thursday dawn only ten degrees with fog. The weekend itself is then dry and pleasant, up to twenty-four degrees in the afternoon.

Dengue is all clear, with a small note. Cases in Paraná have crashed by over ninety-six percent this year. These numbers, though, only cover the end of the hot season through mid-March, not this week — in dry winter, mosquito risk is low anyway.

And São João is coming, on Wednesday. No local festival is confirmed in the immediate area, but in Londrina starting early July there's a whole series of Festas Juninas with quadrilhas, country music, and typical food. For school break, Família no Parque stands out in the greater São Paulo area — free entry (rides cost extra), from the twenty-ninth of June through early August, with a giant slide, bounce houses, and a train ride.
