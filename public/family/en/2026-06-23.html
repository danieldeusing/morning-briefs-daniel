Health & Safety—Airways in focus

RSV and flu alert: Paraná on "high risk"

Paraná is listed by Fiocruz InfoGripe (week 23, Jun 7–13, 2026) as "high risk with long-term growth trend" for severe acute respiratory syndrome (SRAG)—Curitiba and the entire state are explicitly named[3]. Driving admissions among the youngest is RSV/respiratory syncytial virus, which dominates hospital stays for children under 4; flu hits adults and seniors harder[4]. Across the country, 14 states show an upward trend in severe respiratory syndromes in the current bulletin (week 23), Paraná among them[5].

RSV (respiratory syncytial virus): A common cold-causing virus. For most it ends as a runny nose—but in infants in their first months, it can narrow the tiny airways (bronchiolitis) and trigger bad coughs and trouble breathing. That's exactly why it's risky to have a few-weeks-old baby in the house during Brazilian winter (May–August).

Practically, that means right now: wash hands before anyone touches the baby, avoid large crowds with the infant, reduce contact with people who are sick, and at the first sign of rapid breathing, chest retractions, or poor feeding, get to the pediatrician quickly. The PR health authority also recommends that pregnant women get vaccinated against RSV starting at week 28 to protect newborns via maternal antibodies[3].

Sources: bemparana.com.br · agenciabrasil.ebc.com.br
Book Vaccination Appointment Starting Monday, Jun 29, Paraná opens flu vaccination to the entire population 6+ months old (previously just risk groups)—free at over 1,800 vaccination sites[6]. Easy to plan for the bigger kids; the one-month-old is still too young for the flu shot, but benefits when the whole household is vaccinated (cocooning). arede.info

Good news on dengue

A bright spot: dengue runs much milder in Paraná in 2026. Through weeks 1–14 (Jan–Mar 14) the state counted just 2,028 cases vs 52,193 the year before—a drop of −96.1%; one death instead of 90 (−98.8%)[7]. Recent bulletins (through week 20, Jun 16) show no outbreak signal for the Norte Pioneiro. Mosquito prevention still makes sense, but this season isn't an alarm.

Festa junina season winding down—winter vocab for Portuguese

The festas juninas wrap up by end-June, and real Brazilian winter is showing its cold side. Three handy words for the moment: frio (cold), geada (ground frost), and friagem—the typical cold snap pushing up from the south that the news is describing right now. A good chance to read weather reports with the school kids and pick up a few words.

It's Tuesday, June 23rd, and the day opens icy and wet in the Norte Pioneiro. The winter's strongest cold snap is moving in—and it's the top priority for the family right now.

Today in Ribeirão Claro we'll see only fourteen to nineteen degrees with ninety percent rain likelihood. According to Climatempo, Wednesday will be the coldest day at eleven to sixteen degrees, then it dries out but stays frosty. Paraná's weather service has put one hundred thirty-five municipalities under cold and frost warnings; Santo Antônio da Platina, just nearby, already reported nine point five degrees at sunrise. With a one-month-old in the house, that means: keep the home warm, dress the baby in layers without overheating.

Linked to that is the second big issue. Paraná is listed by Fiocruz as high risk for severe respiratory infections. The respiratory syncytial virus, or RSV, is what's driving hospital admissions among the youngest—kids under four. For an infant just a few weeks old, that's exactly the risky window. Practically, that means: wash hands before anyone touches the baby, avoid crowds, and at the first sign of fast breathing or poor feeding, see the pediatrician immediately. Health authorities also recommend that pregnant women get vaccinated against RSV starting in the third trimester.

There is good news too. Dengue is running far milder this year in Paraná—just over two thousand cases through mid-March, a ninety-six percent drop from last year. And as of Monday, June 29th, the state opens flu vaccination for everyone from six months up. The baby is too young for the flu shot, but the whole household vaccinated protects even the tiniest.

For the weekend, Londrina is worth a look. The big theater festival FILO runs through Sunday with several free kids' shows. The standout is Mexican clown Aziz Gual, performing free on Saturday and Sunday at Lago Igapó—outdoors, visual, and fun even without Portuguese. Dress warm in the cold snap. Santo Antônio da Platina has a motorcycle festival the same weekend, but that's more for adults.
