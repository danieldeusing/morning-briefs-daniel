Health & Safety

Heads up — newborn in the house The respiratory virus season (Brazilian winter) is ramping up. In the latest InfoGripe bulletin (SE 22, 05-31 through 06-06-2026, published 06-11-2026), Paraná ranks among 11 states with SRAG (severe acute respiratory illness) incidence at alert, risk, or high-risk levels. In children under 4, the spike is "driven mainly by RSV".[1]
SRAG / RSV: SRAG is Portuguese for "severe acute respiratory illness" — the cases that land in the hospital. RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) is like a stubborn cold: mostly harmless for older kids and adults, but for a newborn it can clog the airway and turn dangerous — babies' windpipes are just too narrow.

Why it matters for a 1-month-old baby

In the 2026 season's current four-week window, 48.5% of positive samples are RSV (InfoGripe SE 21, as of 06-03-2026) — the most common detected pathogen, ahead of rhinovirus (24.3%) and influenza A (21.9%).[2] Exactly this mix hits the youngest hardest. Practically, over the next weeks: wash hands before touching the baby, keep sick people at distance, skip crowds with the newborn (shows, packed venues). If the baby shows trouble breathing, rapid breathing, poor feeding, or fever, get medical care right away — don't wait.

Fiocruz names RSV vaccination in pregnant women as protection for newborns: "RSV vaccination is given to pregnant women so they build antibodies and pass them to the baby — protection in the first six months of life".[1] For a baby already born, that path doesn't work; exposure avoidance is what counts most. Talk to your pediatrician or health center about your baby's current protection status.
