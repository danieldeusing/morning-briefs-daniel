Health & Safety

Heads up — Respiratory Paraná is under InfoGripe (Fiocruz) alert at "high risk with upward trend" — for severe acute respiratory infections (SRAG). This applies to the entire Southern region. For a four-week-old baby, this is the week's most urgent line.

Why now — and why especially for the baby

SRAG & RSV: SRAG is the catch-all term for a respiratory infection severe enough to require hospitalization. The most common culprit in the very young is RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus) — think of it as a particularly stubborn cold that's harmless in adults but can clog a newborn's tiny airways.

Brazil's Fiocruz monitoring system InfoGripe is clear in the latest assessment: "A incidência de SRAG é mais elevada em pequenas crianças e está associada principalmente ao vírus sincicial respiratório." — Cases hit small children hardest and are driven mainly by RSV. In the assessment, RSV accounted for roughly 48.5% of detected respiratory viruses.[2] Nationally, over 89,000 SRAG cases have been reported so far in 2026, nearly half laboratory-confirmed.[1]

Status: InfoGripe weeks 21–23/2026 (reporting weeks May 24–June 13, 2026).

What actually protects

Do
  • Baby's first weeks avoid crowds — stay away from enclosed, packed spaces (that's exactly where festivals and theaters pose risk).
  • Handwashing before anyone touches the baby — siblings often bring viruses home from school or daycare.
  • Anyone coughing or sniffling keeps distance or wears a mask near the baby.
  • Exclusive breastfeeding where possible — passes antibodies along.
Warning signs → doctor
  • Fast, labored breathing; retractions between the ribs.
  • Feeds significantly worse, fewer wet diapers.
  • Bluish lips, fever in an infant under 3 months — go immediately.

Practical for the weekend: Outdoor festivals and theater are great for the older kids, but the baby doesn't belong in a packed hall or crowds. One adult stays outdoors or at home with her — this season that's not overcautious; it's standard guidance from the monitoring system.

Good news: Dengue this summer relaxed

While respiratory illness worries through winter, dengue in Paraná this summer has been notably mild. SESA reports for early 2026 (weeks 1–14, through mid-March) 2,028 confirmed cases and 1 death — a drop of −96.1% from the same weeks in 2025 (52,193 cases, 90 deaths).[3] The Aedes mosquito cycle runs slower in dry winter anyway; even so, keep draining standing water in the yard so it stays calm next summer.

Status: SESA bulletin, weeks 1–14/2026.
