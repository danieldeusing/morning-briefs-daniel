Health & Safety

RSV: "Respiratory Syncytial Virus" is a cold virus that in most adults feels like a runny nose. Think of it as a normal cold that in very young children goes deeper into the airways: in infants, it can narrow the fine bronchioles (bronchiolitis) and make breathing difficult. This is precisely why, in a household with a one-month-old baby, it's the virus to watch right now.

RSV wave: dominant pathogen in Brazilian winter

The InfoGripe bulletin from Fiocruz (reporting week 24, June 14–20, 2026, published June 25, 2026) counts 97,813 reported SRAG cases nationwide in 2026, of which 50.6% are lab-confirmed. Among positive samples, RSV at 53.1% is by far the most common pathogen (Influenza A 16.4%, Rhinovirus 23.9%). SRAG stands for "severe acute respiratory syndrome" — the serious respiratory cases that land in hospitals. Paraná ranks among the southern states with ongoing RSV growth; Curitiba is at alert/risk level among state capitals.[2]

In young children the curve flattens somewhat, with growth shifting to adults and elderly — but overall burden stays high and RSV dominates. For a household with an infant, it simply means: wash hands, keep sick people away from the baby, avoid crowds indoors, and go to your pediatrician early at any sign of breathing trouble (rapid breathing, chest retractions, difficulty nursing).
