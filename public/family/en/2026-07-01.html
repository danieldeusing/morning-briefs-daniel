Today & tomorrow: FESCAFÉ is on your doorstep

The festival of the year — right in your backyard

Starting tomorrow, the 28th FESCAFÉ (Festa do Café, Coffee Festival) runs for four days at Centro de Eventos on Av. Joaquim Néia de Oliveira, km 3 — just minutes from home. This is the biggest event of the year in Ribeirão Claro, and organizers expect around 50,000 visitors. For families there's an amusement park, indoor rodeo, exhibitions, a food court (praça de alimentação), and evening shows.[1]

The highlight for the kids is Sunday, 07/05: the afternoon cavalcade (a rider's parade) with regional music and the kids' show "Sonhos e Magia" ("Dreams and Magic").[1] That's the day that makes the trip most worth it with your 8-year-old and 7-year-old.

As of 07/01/2026 · npdiario.com.br
Check the price FESCAFÉ has historically been "entrada franca" (free admission on all days) — confirmed for 2024. For 2026, no official source confirms the ticket price. So don't assume it's free; check it before you go or look at the city's social channels. Rides in the amusement park are separate anyway (pay-per-ride with tickets).[2]

Weather looks good — with a cool weekend

Today and tomorrow will be sunny with highs of 27 °C and lows of 15 °C, no rain — perfect for a first evening at FESCAFÉ. Friday afternoon might see scattered showers (40%, but only ~0.8 mm), clearing by evening. The weekend gets noticeably cooler: Saturday only 12–20 °C, dry. For open-air evenings, that means bring a jacket and long pants, especially for the little ones.[3]

The national weather service INMET reports general instability for Nord-Paraná only through Thursday (07/02) — no orange or red severe-weather alerts for the region.[4]

Health & safety

RSV & flu season We're in the Brazilian winter, and respiratory season is in full swing. According to InfoGripe, Paraná was one of 11 states with SRAG (severe acute respiratory syndrome) readings at alert/risk level with rising trend in week 22/2026 (05/31–06/06).[6] The rise hits children up to 4 years old hardest — the group most affected by RSV. With a one-month-old baby in the house, it's relevant but no reason to panic: standard precautions are enough.
SRAG & RSV: SRAG (Síndrome Respiratória Aguda Grave) is Brazil's umbrella term for severe respiratory infections that land kids in the hospital. RSV (VSR in Portuguese) is a cold virus — a harmless sniffle for big kids and adults, but in tiny babies it's the #1 reason for a clinic stay because their airways are still tiny. Think of it like a straw: a thick hose, a little mucus is nothing; a thin straw, it gets clogged.

What this means in practice

No alarm, just common sense for the baby: wash hands before anyone touches the little one; ask visitors with colds to keep their distance or wear a mask; avoid large crowds with the baby — so skip FESCAFÉ and Disney On Ice with the infant. Your two school kids and 2-year-old pick up viruses at school/daycare, so remind them to wash hands before they go near the baby.
