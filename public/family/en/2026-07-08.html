Fetexas — Four Days of Festa, 20 Minutes Away

The 31st edition: rodeo, a fairground, and two big names

From Thursday, the 9th, to Sunday, July 12, the Centro de Eventos on PR-431 in Jacarezinho turns into the biggest folk festival the Norte Pioneiro has to offer this year. Admission is free ("entrada franca") — you only pay for what you eat and ride. There's a rodeo, a funfair, a food court, and exhibits.

The stage lineup builds toward the weekend: Thursday it's Ícaro & Gilmar and Panda, Friday Coleto & Gabriel and Jiraya Uai, Saturday brings Leonardo — one of Brazil's biggest sertanejo names — and Sunday closes with Sorriso Maroto. More interesting for the kids: motocross races run on the grounds on both weekend days.

Honest caveat: the dates are confirmed via the organizers' official announcement and the local press report. A state tourism page does also list a "Fetexas" — but with the dates of the 28th edition, from 2023. We deliberately did not use that page; anyone looking it up themselves should watch for "31ª."

Health & Safety

RSV wave is growing — baby context Fiocruz reports in the InfoGripe bulletin for Semana Epidemiológica 25/2026 (observation period June 6–27, 2026, published July 2, 2026): in Paraná, severe respiratory cases from RSV keep growing. The bulletin explicitly notes that SRAG incidence is highest among young children and is mainly driven by RSV. For a household with a one-month-old infant, that's the most relevant number this week: no crowds, no fairground, wash hands before anyone touches the baby — and anyone with a cold keeps their distance.
RSV: A cold virus that catches almost every child at least once before their second birthday. For adults it's just a runny nose; for an infant it can turn into bronchiolitis — an inflammation of the very smallest airways. Picture it this way: an adult breathes through a drainpipe, a newborn through a drinking straw. A little mucus barely bothers the pipe, but the straw clogs right up. That's exactly why babies with RSV end up in the hospital while their older siblings just cough a little. In Portuguese the virus is called VSR; in German and English it's RSV — same thing.

National context (not verified in depth)

Coverage of this same InfoGripe update also circulates two national figures: 41.5% of virologically confirmed SRAG cases in the respective last four weeks were reportedly attributable to RSV, ahead of influenza A (27.2%) and rhinovirus (25.5%); and Brazil is said to have crossed 82,000 SRAG cases this winter. Both figures come from media summaries and were not checked by us against the primary bulletin — they're presented here as a rough order of magnitude, not as a solid number. The Paraná statement in the paragraph above is the solid one.

Dengue: no current status — and why we're not making anything up

The state of Paraná's dengue portal (dengue.pr.gov.br) responded with HTTP 503 across three retrieval attempts today, and its mirror on saude.pr.gov.br with 403. We therefore could not verify a July bulletin or an epidemiological week. The last status we can confirm — by number and calendar date only, with no SE figure — is Informe Epidemiológico nº 20 from SESA-PR, published on June 16, 2026. That's more than three weeks old.

Be careful with the number circulating everywhere: the drop from 52,193 to 2,028 confirmed cases (−96.1%) and from 90 to one death refers to epidemiological weeks 1–14 of 2026, i.e., January through mid-March. That's a season-to-date tally from the start of the year, not a statement about the dengue situation in July. Anyone reading it as an all-clear for right now is reading it wrong.
