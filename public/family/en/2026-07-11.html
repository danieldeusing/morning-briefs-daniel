RSV: A cold virus that catches almost every child before their second birthday. For adults it's a sniffle; in an infant it can turn into bronchiolitis — an inflammation of the very smallest airways. Picture an adult breathing through a drainpipe, a newborn through a drinking straw: a little mucus barely bothers the pipe but clogs the straw. Portuguese: VSR; German/English: RSV — same thing.

German-Brazilian Corner

Festa Julina: The Festa Junina — Brazil's big folk festival with bonfires, quadrilha dancing, flags, and hot quentão — has a little sibling in July: the Festa Julina. Because June is often still busy with exams and work, but many people have more time during the July school break, schools and communities simply push the festival a month later. For a German parent, that's doubly strange: first the calendar flips (July is deep winter here, not high summer), and then people celebrate a "June festival" in July. Fetexas is the cowboy/rodeo take on that same winter festival season — instead of the pool and ice cream, there's a winter jacket, bonfire mood, and pancake stalls.

It's Saturday, July eleventh. The big local fair right on our doorstep, the thirty-first Fetexas in Jacarezinho, hits its second-to-last day today and wraps up tomorrow — free admission, rodeo, motocross, and a live stage, less than twenty minutes down the road. Tonight it's Leonardo; tomorrow, Sorriso Maroto. But the real question this weekend is the weather.

Three sources are sitting side by side this week. For today, at least two portals agree: Climatempo and theweather dot com both call for zero percent rain, with a warm twenty-eight degrees. That satisfies the two-source rule for today. Against that, though, stands the national weather service INMET: its bulletin from the ninth of July flags a thunderstorm risk for Friday and Saturday, stretching from Rio Grande do Sul up to the central Paraná axis — and today falls right inside that window.

Worth keeping in perspective: this is a broad regional bulletin with no advisory number of its own and no concrete millimeter figures, and our area isn't named specifically. For tomorrow, Sunday, the two portals openly disagree: Climatempo says zero percent, the other portal says fifty percent light rain. INMET's window only runs through Saturday, so it doesn't cover Sunday at all. We're deliberately not picking one of these numbers over the other. For planning with four kids, that means packing a rain jacket, keeping an eye on the sky, and enjoying Fetexas on one of the two days — but with a backup plan in your pocket.

And that backup plan is a genuinely nice one this time. In Londrina, a good hundred ten kilometers away, Sunday afternoons in July bring a real children's play: Hansel and Gretel, João e Maria in Portuguese. Twenty reais full price, ten discounted, open to every age group — a rain-proof indoor option if Sunday turns wet. Anyone headed to Londrina anyway will find free craft and storytelling workshops for kids at SESC Cadeião in the afternoons.

Starting Monday, the state schools' winter break begins, running two weeks through the twenty-fourth. That opens up a whole window of kids' programming in greater São Paulo: a bounce park with Turma da Mônica, the forty-second strawberry festival in Atibaia, and a free break workshop at the Museum of the Portuguese Language. One honest caveat: all these São Paulo picks come from a single kids' guide, not independently confirmed — and the drive runs four to six hours. So think of it more as a destination for break week than a quick weekend trip, and check with the organizer beforehand.

On health, it's two calm items. The respiratory wave keeps easing: the latest bulletin, with data through calendar week twenty-six, counts Paraná among the states showing early signs of a decline. There's nothing fresher today. But the RSV virus still accounts for nearly fifty-six percent of cases and hits the youngest the hardest — for the one-month-old baby in the house, that means breathing easier, but no fairground with the baby.

And on dengue, it's worth checking the date again. The number going around — ninety-six percent fewer cases — covers weeks one through fourteen, meaning January through mid-March. That's a tally from the start of the year, not a statement about July, and there's no active alert.
