Health & Safety

Heads up · Baby & Respiratory The new InfoGripe bulletin (SE 26/2026, published Jul 9, 2026) reports a nationwide decline in SRAG cases — severe acute respiratory illness — for the first time after roughly five months of increases. That's not the all-clear for us, though: over the past four weeks, more than half of all positive lab tests (55.9%) came back for RS virus, and the South — including Paraná — still shows a rising RSV trend against the national trend. With a not-quite-four-week-old infant at home, that means: avoid close contact with anyone who has a cold, wash hands before holding the baby, and skip crowded indoor spaces with the baby (concert hall, play park, mall included) when possible.
RSV: a very common cold virus. In older kids it's usually just a sniffle — in infants it can swell the tiny airways shut, like the breathing straw getting too narrow. That's why RSV is the most common reason babies under six months end up in the hospital during winter. That's exactly why it leads today.

Why the national trend isn't the whole story: nationwide, about 109,000 SRAG cases have been reported so far in 2026, and the curve bends downward for the first time in months — that's good. But flu viruses are driving the decline, while RSV keeps dominating (influenza A 12.7%, influenza B 8.4% of positive tests versus 55.9% RSV). And in the three southern states — Paraná, Santa Catarina, and Rio Grande do Sul — RSV is still climbing, which is why we're staying more cautious locally than the national headline suggests.

Dengue · freshly documented & calm Good news, and finally with a current number: the new bulletin is out (Informe Epidemiológico nº 22, published Jul 7, 2026, SE 1–26/2026). In our health region, 19ª RS Jacarezinho, it counts 73 confirmed dengue cases and 0 deaths for the year so far; nationwide, 5,554 cases and 2 deaths — still far below last year. The Aedes routine (emptying standing water where the mosquito breeds) stays, but the situation isn't alarming. This figure replaces the stale March number from earlier briefs.
