Health & Safety

Heads up · Baby & Respiratory The current InfoGripe bulletin (SE 26/2026, Jun 28–Jul 4, published Jul 9, 2026) reports a nationwide decline in SRAG cases — severe acute respiratory illness — for the first time after about five months of increases. That's not the all-clear for us, though: RSV-driven SRAG cases keep rising across the entire South region — Paraná, Santa Catarina, Rio Grande do Sul — against the national trend, and Curitiba is among the state capitals with respiratory activity at alert to high-risk levels, with growth especially among children under two to four years old. With a not-quite-five-week-old infant at home, that means: avoid close contact with anyone who has a cold, wash hands before holding the baby, and skip crowded indoor spaces with the baby (concert hall, play park, mall included) when possible — with this nice weather, fresh air is the better place anyway.
RSV: a very common cold virus. In older kids it's usually just a sniffle — in infants it can swell the tiny airways shut, like the breathing straw getting too narrow. That's why RSV is the most common reason babies under six months end up in the hospital during winter. That's exactly why it leads today.

Why the national headline isn't the whole story: nationwide, the SRAG curve bends downward for the first time in months — that's good, and it's being driven by declining flu viruses. RSV, on the other hand, keeps dominating in the South and is even still climbing here. That's exactly why we stay more cautious locally than the nationwide "it's going down" message suggests — winter caution still applies for Curitiba and Paraná.
