Health — good news twice over

The Informe Epidemiológico nº 22 from SESA-PR (published 07/07/2026, data through weeks 01–26/2026) shows just how unusual this dengue year is. Paraná counts 5,554 confirmed cases and 2 deaths. In the same period in 2025, there were 88,687 cases and 140 deaths — a drop of 93.7% in cases and 98.6% in deaths. The incidence of locally-acquired cases fell from 648.41 to 39.00 per 100,000 residents.

Confirmed, reported, locally-acquired: A reported case is any suspicion a doctor notes — 56,722 statewide. A confirmed case is what the lab test holds up — 5,554. Locally-acquired means infected here, not brought back from travel. It's like a smoke alarm: many triggers, few real fires, and only some of those in your own house. Incidence per 100,000 then makes regions of different sizes comparable.

Locally, it's even more striking. Our region, the 19ª RS Jacarezinho (300,313 residents, includes Ribeirão Claro), reports 73 confirmed cases — 59 of them locally acquired — and zero deaths, an incidence of 19.65 per 100,000. Neighboring Londrina (17ª RS) sits at 1,574 cases and an incidence of 124.37, a good six times higher. Standing water in pots and tires still isn't harmless — but the 2026 season is, measured against 2025, practically a no-show.

Status Nº 22, dated 07/07/2026 (week 26), is still the latest bulletin on the SESA bulletin page as of this morning — verified, not assumed. The series has previously run weekly to biweekly, so a nº 23 is overdue. We'll follow up once it's posted; nothing here is being extrapolated.

RSV in babies: −81.1% in Paraná

The second story is the more important one for this household. Severe RSV cases in infants under six months have fallen in Paraná over the first 26 weeks of 2026 to 269 cases — down from 1,421 in the same period in 2025. That's 81.1% fewer. The reason isn't a fluke of the season, but a vaccine: since December 2025, the RSV vaccine for pregnant women from the 28th week of pregnancy has been part of the Health Ministry's vaccination schedule.

Nest protection: A baby can barely produce its own antibodies in the first few months. If the mother is vaccinated in the last trimester, her ready-made antibodies cross the placenta to the baby — it's born with borrowed defenses. Like a fully-charged power bank handed over until your own battery is up and running. It lasts around six months — exactly the window when RSV is most dangerous for babies.

Paraná has the highest vaccination rate in the South: 89.84% of eligible pregnant women, ahead of Rio Grande do Sul (87.58%) and Santa Catarina (86.32%). For premature babies and at-risk children up to 23 months, there's also nirsevimab (an antibody preparation) available through SUS. If you were asked about the vaccine in the spring and said yes: this is what it bought you.

Context Fewer severe cases doesn't mean less virus circulating. In Fiocruz's InfoGripe bulletin (week 26/2026, published 07/09), RSV still accounts for 55.9% of positive samples over the last four weeks and dominates among children up to age four. Nationally, the SRAG curve is bending down for the first time after five months of increases; Fiocruz does not list Paraná among the six states showing a growth signal, though it remains elevated. On the source conflict: a secondary summary counted PR among the growth-signal states, the primary source does not — we follow the primary source. In practice: avoid crowded indoor spaces with the infant.

The second story matters even more for a house with a one-month-old baby. Severe RSV cases in infants under six months have fallen in Paraná this year to two hundred sixty-nine cases, down from one thousand four hundred twenty-one last year. That's a drop of eighty-one percent. The reason is the vaccine given to pregnant women from the twenty-eighth week onward, part of the national schedule since December.

The principle behind it is called nest protection. A newborn can barely produce its own antibodies. If the mother is vaccinated in the last trimester, her ready-made antibodies cross the placenta to the baby — like handing over a fully-charged power bank until the baby's own battery gets going. It lasts about six months, exactly the window when the virus is most dangerous for infants. Paraná has the highest vaccination rate in the South, at just under ninety percent. For premature babies and at-risk infants, there's also nirsevimab available through the public health system.

One caveat: fewer severe cases doesn't mean less virus circulating.
