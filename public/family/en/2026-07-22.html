Today: storms this evening — a day for indoors

Brazil's national weather service INMET has issued a yellow thunderstorm advisory ("perigo potencial de tempestades") for large parts of Paraná today, Wednesday, with possible hail. For the Londrina region, o tempo cites up to ~50 mm per day and gusts up to ~60 km/h. Two local sources confirm the same direction — Climatempo (Jacarezinho: 26°/17°, sunny with clouds by day, "à noite chove forte") and the state weather service SIMEPAR (high of 26 °C, rain almost entirely at night). So the day stays largely dry; the heavy rain arrives in the evening and overnight.[2][3][4]

What this means in practice Morning and early afternoon are still usable — the children's library at 10 am fits right into the dry window. For the evening: get home in time, secure loose items in the yard, and if you're making the long drive to Londrina, plan the return trip before dark. The portals differ on the exact rain probability (Climatempo cites 69% as a daily figure, SIMEPAR splits it into nearly 0% by day and ~98% at night) — but they agree on when it arrives: tonight.
INMET warning levels: The weather service works like a traffic light — yellow "Perigo Potencial" (stay alert), orange "Perigo" (be prepared), red "Grande Perigo" (act now). Today's advisory is the yellow, lowest tier: something to keep in mind, not a severe-weather disaster. Like a yellow DWD early advisory in Germany.

After the heavy rain: empty standing water Today's thunderstorm fills buckets, plant-pot saucers, clogged gutters, and old tires in the yard — and that's exactly where the Aedes mosquito lays its eggs. Walking through the yard after the rain and tipping out every pool of standing water is the most effective dengue prevention there is, and it costs nothing.
Aedes cycle: The dengue mosquito needs clean standing water to breed — even a bottle cap's worth is enough. From egg to fully grown mosquito takes about a week. A heavy rain like today's leaves small new puddles everywhere; emptying them the next day breaks the cycle before it starts.
Relevant for the baby Brazilian winter (May–August) is RSV and flu season. The last status verified by our team — InfoGripe epi week 26/2026 (published 07/09/2026) — listed Paraná at an elevated RSV level (55.9% of positive respiratory samples). A newer bulletin wasn't reachable today; we're carrying forward the last status and not extrapolating. For a one-month-old, that means, unchanged: postpone visits from anyone with a cold, wash hands before handling — and once school starts back up 07/27, keep the two school-age kids in mind as the most likely route in.[9]
RSV (Portuguese VSR): a cold virus that usually just causes a runny nose in adults and school-age kids, but in infants can swell the fine bronchial tubes and lead to breathing trouble. Like a straw you normally drink through — until someone pinches part of it shut. The same infection is harmless for the eight-year-old and a hospital risk for the youngest.

It's Wednesday, July twenty-second, the last week of break — and the most important line of the day is written in the sky: there's a thunderstorm coming tonight.

The national weather service INMET has issued a yellow thunderstorm advisory for large parts of Paraná — possible hail, and for the Londrina region, up to around fifty millimeters of rain and gusts up to around sixty kilometers per hour. Two local sources back that up: Climatempo and the state weather service SIMEPAR both call for a high near twenty-six degrees and rain concentrated overnight. So the day stays largely dry, with the heavy rain arriving this evening. Anyone driving to Londrina should plan the return trip before dark.

And driving to Londrina is worth it: the children's library is running its vacation program through Friday, every morning at ten, free of charge. Today it's dance and movement, tomorrow letter writing, Friday closes out with paper folding. It's all indoors — the ideal plan for a stormy day. Registration is by phone, there are fifteen spots per workshop, and kids under twelve must come with an adult.

For the weekend, there's hardly any way around an indoor plan, since the forecast stays wet through Saturday. That's why the weekend pick is Parque Fundo do Mar at Catuaí Londrina — a covered play park with an elastic tower, slides, and a ball pit, open Saturday and Sunday, just under fifty reais for forty minutes. It's the highlight for the two older kids; the baby just watches.

Break ends Friday, and on Monday, July twenty-seventh, classes resume across Paraná. Friday is the last day off, the weekend the buffer. School supplies and uniforms are best handled during the dry morning hours.

Two health items.
