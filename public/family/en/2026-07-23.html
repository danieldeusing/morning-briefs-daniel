Health first: RSV at 58.5%, Paraná still in the risk zone

Fiocruz released its new InfoGripe bulletin today, 07/23/2026. The key point for us: Paraná remains one of the five Brazilian states where the incidence of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SRAG) sits at risk or high-risk level — together with Acre, Maranhão, Rio Grande do Sul, and Santa Catarina. Nationwide, 2026 stands at 120,920 SRAG cases, of which 63,698 are laboratory-confirmed. Among the positive samples from the last four epidemiological weeks, 58.5% trace back to RSV (Portuguese: VSR), followed by rhinovirus at 24.6%, influenza A at 9.1%, influenza B at 8.8%, and COVID-19 at 1.5%.[2]

The more important observation is in the trend. Absolute hospitalization numbers have been falling nationwide for the past few weeks — but the RSV share of positive samples keeps rising all the same: 55.2% in the bulletin from 07/06 (SE 25/2026), 55.9% on 07/09 (as of SE 26/2026), now 58.5%. And for the South, the bulletin from 07/16 (SE 27/2026) explicitly noted that SRAG cases from RSV "keep increasing across the entire South region (Paraná, Rio Grande do Sul, and Santa Catarina)." In other words: the wave overall is receding, but the very virus most relevant to infants is holding its share and still growing here in the South.[1][3][4]

What this means in a house with a one-month-old baby The bulletin says only two things: the curve in Paraná keeps pointing upward, and the dominant virus is RSV. In practice, that leads to little that's dramatic, but plenty that's effective. Ration contact: postpone a visit from anyone with a cold, wash hands before every touch, don't let anyone kiss the baby's face. Avoid crowded indoor spaces: a shopping mall on a rainy Saturday, a packed church service, a long line — outdoors is clearly less risky than indoors. And starting Monday, 07/27, the two school-age kids will again come home daily from a classroom; that's the most likely route by which a virus like this enters the house. When not to wait: fast or visibly labored breathing, rib retractions, noticeably poor feeding, or unusual listlessness in an infant call for a medical check. This last sentence is general parental knowledge and is not stated in the bulletin itself — the bulletin only supplies the reason to look more closely right now.
SRAG: not a disease in its own right, but a reporting category — any respiratory infection severe enough that someone has to go to the hospital for it. Think of it like a fire department's statistics: not how often something smokes somewhere, but how often the engine actually had to roll out. So the number says something about severity, not about how many colds are going around.
RSV (Portuguese VSR): a cold virus. In adults and school-age kids it usually just causes a runny nose and cough. In an infant, the fine bronchial tubes swell shut — as if someone were pinching the straw the child breathes through. The same infection that bothers the eight-year-old for two days can be a hospital case for a one-month-old.
Honest about the data No reachable source gives an epi-week number for today's bulletin — that's why the publication date (07/23/2026) stands next to the 58.5% and the 120,920 cases instead of an SE number. The last SE-dated status for Paraná is SE 27/2026 (07/05–11, published 07/16), and the last SE-dated virus share is SE 26/2026 at 55.9%. Nothing here is extrapolated.

After the rain: walk the yard Rain falls tonight and on Saturday — and afterward, buckets, plant-saucer trays, gutters, and old tires fill up again. That's exactly where the Aedes mosquito lays its eggs. Walking through the yard once and emptying out everything standing is the most effective dengue prevention there is, and it costs nothing — and it works regardless of how quiet the statistics look right now.
Aedes cycle: The dengue mosquito needs clean standing water to breed — a bottle cap's worth is enough. From egg to full-grown mosquito takes about a week. A rainy day creates small new puddles everywhere; tipping them out the next day breaks the cycle before it even starts.

It's Thursday, July twenty-third. Tomorrow is the last day of break, and the weekend after that is the final one before school starts back up. Even so, this brief doesn't open with a day trip — it opens with a health number.

Paraná remains one of five Brazilian states where severe respiratory illness is running at risk or high-risk levels, alongside Acre, Maranhão, Rio Grande do Sul, and Santa Catarina. Nationwide, just over one hundred twenty thousand such cases have been logged so far this year. And among positive samples from the last four weeks, fifty-eight point five percent trace back to RSV.

What's notable is the trend line. Hospital numbers are falling nationwide, but the RSV share keeps climbing: fifty-five point two percent in early July, fifty-five point nine percent a week later, now fifty-eight point five. For the South, the bulletin from July sixteenth specifically noted that RSV cases keep rising in Paraná, Rio Grande do Sul, and Santa Catarina. With a one-month-old baby in the house, that's the news that actually matters.

In practice, that calls for nothing dramatic, just something concrete. Postpone visits from anyone with a cold. Wash hands before picking up the baby. Avoid crowded indoor spaces — a mall on a rainy Saturday is the worst spot of the week. And starting Monday, the two school-age kids will again come home daily from a classroom — the most likely way a virus like this gets into the house. If an infant is breathing fast or with visible effort, feeding poorly, or seeming unusually limp, that's not something to wait out — get it checked.
