The warning: INMET Aviso 27882, valid through today at 23:59

Brazil's national weather service INMET has had a storm-type Aviso running since yesterday at 18:00, and it expires today at 23:59 BRT. It covers roughly 350 municipalities in Paraná, and Ribeirão Claro (IBGE 4121802) and Jacarezinho (4111803) are both on the list. The severity level is Perigo Potencial, color-coded yellow. The Aviso literally names as risks: "Chuva entre 20 e 30 mm/h ou até 50 mm/dia, ventos intensos (40-60 km/h), e queda de granizo" — that is, 20 to 30 millimeters of rain per hour or up to 50 per day, gusts between 40 and 60 km/h, and hail. Plus a low risk for power outages, crop damage, fallen branches, and localized flooding.[1]

Perigo Potencial (yellow level): INMET works with three colors — yellow, orange, red. Yellow is the lowest and roughly means "the weather has what it takes today," not "it will definitely hit you." Like a wildfire-danger sign at the edge of a forest: it says conditions are right for a fire, not that one is burning. In practice, yellow means: have a plan B, but don't cancel the day.

And that exact distinction is visible today. The warning names 50 mm in a day as its ceiling; the point forecast for Ribeirão Claro sits at 12.4 mm with a 93% chance of rain and 18°/15°. An Aviso spans a band across roughly 350 municipalities — only a small part of them hits the extreme value. For us that means: definitely wet, very likely harmless, with a residual risk of a strong cell bringing gusts and hail.[2][4]

What INMET specifically recommends From the warning text itself: during gusts, don't take shelter under trees, don't park vehicles near power poles, and don't use appliances plugged into the grid while the storm is active. For information, the Aviso names two numbers: Defesa Civil 199 and Fire Department 193. The practical core with four kids in the house: bring bikes, the trampoline, and anything light in the yard indoors before dark.
Honest about the sourcing This warning rests today on one source — INMET's own public Aviso API. That's the primary source, so the best available one; a second independent channel still wasn't obtainable: INMET's website unresponsive, SIMEPAR HTTP 403, the @DefesaCivilPR and @inmet_ accounts unreachable through the usual workaround. After the rain: the five-minute walk through the yard

Twelve millimeters today, more on Saturday — and then 26 degrees of sun on Sunday. That's exactly the weather sequence the Aedes mosquito uses to breed its next generation. This week's most effective dengue protection costs nothing and takes five minutes: on Sunday morning, walk once through the yard and empty out everything holding standing water — saucers under flower pots, buckets, tarps with dips, clogged gutters, old tires, toys left outside.

Why emptying specifically: The dengue mosquito doesn't lay its eggs in streams or ponds, but in small amounts of clean, standing water — a bottle cap's worth is enough. From egg to flying mosquito takes barely a week. Tipping out the puddles the day after the rain interrupts that week before it starts. Insecticide and spray only come after that — and work less well.

The word of the day sits right in the warning text: granizo — hail. Then rajada de vento, the gust of wind, and aviso amarelo, the yellow warning. What's interesting is the system difference: Germany's DWD numbers its warning levels from 1 to 4, while INMET works with three colors and a term that's more of a stance than a number — perigo potencial, "possible danger." It can happen, but it doesn't have to. It's Friday, July twenty-fourth, and Ribeirão Claro is under an official storm warning. Brazil's weather service, INMET, has had a storm advisory in effect since six p.m. yesterday, and it runs through eleven fifty-nine tonight.

The advisory is numbered twenty-seven thousand eight hundred eighty-two and covers three hundred fifty-three municipalities across Paraná state. Both Ribeirão Claro and Jacarezinho made the list. INMET is warning of twenty to thirty millimeters of rain per hour, or up to fifty over the course of the day, plus wind gusts of forty to sixty kilometers an hour and the chance of hail. The risk of power outages, downed branches, and localized flooding is rated low.

The key word is the color: yellow, or perigo potencial in Portuguese — potential danger. That's the lowest of three levels, meaning conditions are right, not that anything is guaranteed to happen. You can see that gap in the numbers: the warning's ceiling is fifty millimeters, while the forecast for our town sits at twelve point four. So: definitely wet, very likely harmless, with some chance of a stronger storm cell. INMET's own advice: stay out from under trees during gusts, don't park near power poles, and unplug anything connected to the grid while the storm passes through. Civil defense can be reached at one-nine-nine, the fire department at one-nine-three.

Today turns cold and wet: a high of eighteen degrees, fifteen overnight, and a ninety-three percent chance of rain.
