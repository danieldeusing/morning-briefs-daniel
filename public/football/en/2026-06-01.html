SC Freiburg — the greatest season, the bitterest end

Let's start with what weighs on Daniel most. SC Freiburg closed out the 2025/26 season as the most successful in club history — and yet it ended with tears. On May 20, Freiburg played in a European final for the first time ever: the Europa League final in Istanbul (Beşiktaş Stadium, "Tüpraş Stadyumu"). Opponent Aston Villa was ruthless. Youri Tielemans (41.) and Emiliano Buendía (45.+3) hit them with a one-two punch before halftime, Morgan Rogers (58.) sealed the 0:3. Villa claimed their first European trophy in 44 years, Freiburg missed out on the first major title in their history.[1]

As painful as it is: this season was a statement. Coach Julian Schuster took the club to the DFB-Pokal semifinal and through a full Europa League campaign to the final — with a squad that shouldn't stand a chance against the money-soaked powerhouses. Top scorer Igor Matanović totaled 15 goals across all competitions.[2]

Context In the Bundesliga it was "just" 7th place (47 points). Sounds middling — it's not: the parallel European run with midweek matches drained the tank, and 7th of 18 clubs means upper half. The reward: next season Freiburg play in the Conference League Playoff, so back to Europe. Three Europa seasons in a row — extraordinary for a club this size.

Transfer Thread: Who stays?

With the record season comes the covetous reflex: other clubs want Freiburg's best. The window officially opens July 1 and runs through early September.[3] The story of the summer is Johan Manzambi.

Manzambi — the possible club record

The 20-year-old Swiss midfielder/winger is Freiburg's hottest sale prospect. His market value is quoted at €35 to 50M; Manchester United, Arsenal, Chelsea, and even Real Madrid are said to be interested. Freiburg will only sell above the current club record — €30M, which Brentford paid for Kevin Schade in 2023. A sale would automatically be a new club record. Manzambi himself is publicly cautious. (Rumor, as of 05.21 — no firm deal.)[4]

Goalkeeper question: Atubolu out, Backhaus in?

A switch looms in goal: regular starter Noah Atubolu (academy product) could leave the club, with Freiburg keen on Werder's U21 national goalkeeper Mio Backhaus (22) — transfer fee around €15M under discussion. (Rumor, as of 05.21.)[5]

Champions League: PSG defends — Havertz' heartbreak

Saturday's final (05.30) in Budapest was a thriller. PSG beat Arsenal 4:3 on penalties, after 1:1 after extra time. Kai Havertz put Arsenal ahead as early as the 6th minute — the first German to score in two different Champions League finals. Ousmane Dembélé equalized after the break from the spot (65.). In the shootout, it was Arsenal defender Gabriel who missed the decisive penalty.[10]

Back-to-back titles: Winning twice in a row — like being top of the class two years running. PSG is only the second club in the modern Champions League era to do it, after Real Madrid 2016/17. It's PSG's second Champions League title overall.

It's Monday, June first. The club season is over, and exactly ten days from now the World Cup rolls in North America. Today is less about results and more about the transition into summer: the World Cup, the transfer window, and a look back at a Freiburg season nobody will forget soon.

Let's start with Freiburg. The Sport-Club had their most successful season ever — and it still ended with tears. On May twentieth, Freiburg played in a European final for the first time, the Europa League final in Istanbul. Against Aston Villa, they lost zero to three. Tielemans and Buendía scored before halftime, Rogers sealed it after the break. It hurts: Coach Julian Schuster took the club to the cup semifinal and to the Europa final, and in the league they finished seventh. Next season Freiburg will play in the Conference League playoff — their third consecutive European season.

With success come the vultures. The transfer window officially opens July first, and the summer's story is Johan Manzambi. The twenty-year-old Swiss midfielder has a market value between thirty-five and fifty million euros, and half of Europe is knocking — from Manchester United to Real Madrid. Freiburg will only sell above their club record, which Brentford set in twenty twenty-three at thirty million for Kevin Schade. A sale would automatically be a new record. Then there's the goalkeeper question: starter Atubolu could leave, Werder's Backhaus could come in. Both are rumors so far, no firm deal.

Now to the national team, the real headline of the coming weeks. The World Cup in the USA, Canada, and Mexico is the first with forty-eight teams and one hundred four matches; it runs from June eleventh to July nineteenth. For Daniel in Brazil, it's a gift, because unlike a European World Cup, the German kickoff times fall into convenient Brazilian afternoon and early evening hours.

The German national team won their final test against Finland four to zero on the last day of May. Tomorrow, June second, the team flies to Chicago. There, on June sixth, comes the last test against host USA, at half nine at night German time, which is half four in the afternoon in Brazil. Then it's off to basecamp in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. The World Cup opener against Curaçao is June fourteenth in Houston, at seven p.m. German time, two p.m. in Brazil. In Group E there's still Côte d'Ivoire on the twentieth and Ecuador on the twenty-fifth. On paper, a manageable group.

One more thing about the Champions League: Paris Saint-Germain won the title on Saturday, four to three on penalties against Arsenal after one to one. Kai Havertz scored early for Arsenal, Dembélé equalized from the spot. It's only the second club after Real Madrid to win back to back in the modern era.

In the Bundesliga, Bayern were champions with eighty-nine points. St. Pauli and Heidenheim went down directly, Wolfsburg lost the playoff to Paderborn. In Brazil, the league rolls on: Palmeiras lead after eighteen matchdays with forty-one points ahead of Flamengo.

Tomorrow the German national team takes off for the USA — from then on it's all about the World Cup here. We'll talk soon.
