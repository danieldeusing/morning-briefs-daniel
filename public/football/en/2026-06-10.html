SC Freiburg — Transfer window heating up, a club record in goal

Off-season, but movement: Sport-Club gearing up. No Freiburg player in World Cup squad.

Backhaus for ~12 million euros — the club's most expensive signing ever

SC Freiburg signs goalkeeper Mio Backhaus from Werder Bremen. The base fee should be "in the range of twelve million euros" — a club record, with performance-based bonuses to follow.[1] For a club that has built its identity on clever, affordable transfers, this is a statement: Freiburg is investing unusually heavily in the most critical position. Backhaus, 22, is seen as the long-term number one.

Yamamoto in, Röhl out to Everton

Veteran Nicolas Höfler ended his playing career and joins the reserve team; backup keeper Elias Etringer returned to Elversberg.

Brazil — Ancelotti, Neymar, and a blockbuster opener

Group C: Brazil · Morocco · Haiti · Scotland. First match at peak BRT broadcast time.

The Seleção enters a World Cup with a foreign coach for the first time: Carlo Ancelotti has, despite an uneven season, nevertheless relied on his axis with Vinicius Jr. and surprisingly also brought Neymar (34) — the country's all-time leading scorer. The opener packs a punch: Brazil–Morocco on Saturday, 13.06., 19:00 BRT (in Europe first on Sunday 0:00 MESZ) in New Jersey. Morocco reached the 2022 semifinal — this is no warmup game, it's the group's toughest test right out of the gate. Then comes Haiti (19.06.) and Scotland (24.06.), who are seen as underdogs in the group.

DFB Squad — Warmup won, then the Karl shock

Base in Winston-Salem · Opener Sun 14.06. against Curaçao · all times MESZ / BRT.

2:1 over the USA — ninth consecutive win

In the final test before the tournament, Germany defeated host USA 2:1 on 06.06. in Chicago. Kai Havertz and Leroy Sané scored; it was the ninth competitive or warm-up win in a row.[4] The team then moved into their World Cup base, "The Graylyn Estate" in Winston-Salem (North Carolina), and trains on the Wake Forest University campus. At the first public training on Monday, around 3,000 fans showed up.

Heads up Lennart Karl is out. The Bayern prospect suffered a muscle tear in his left thigh in the last training session before the USA match and will miss the World Cup. Nagelsmann called up Assan Ouédraogo (RB Leipzig, 20) — he played his only international on November against Slovakia (6:0) and scored.[5] Tough luck for Karl, who was one of the squad's breakthrough stories.

World Cup 2026 — Framework & opening

11.06.–19.07.2026 · USA, Canada, Mexico · 48 teams, 104 matches.

For the first time, 48 teams compete for the World Cup, divided among twelve four-team groups and three host nations. The opener kicks off tomorrow, Thursday 11.06., with host Mexico facing South Africa in the legendary Azteca Stadium (21:00 MESZ / 16:00 BRT).[7] On the first two matchdays, up to four games run per day — for Daniel in Brazil, many kickoffs land conveniently in late afternoon through early evening (BRT), while some late matches from the US West Coast don't start until well after midnight MESZ.

Why the kickoff times are so scattered: Like a family pizza being baked in three kitchens at once — the World Cup runs in three countries and across several US time zones. A match on the US East Coast (e.g., New Jersey, close to BRT) starts for Daniel at prime evening time; one in Los Angeles doesn't kick off until deep in the Brazilian night. That's why every time is listed twice: MESZ for the German perspective, BRT for Daniel's living room.

It is Wednesday, June tenth. This is the calm before the big tournament. The Bundesliga and the Brazilian league are on summer break, but starting tomorrow, Thursday, host Mexico opens the World Cup at the Azteca against South Africa. For Daniel, two teams matter most: the German squad and the Seleção.

The most important news from the German camp is mixed. The good: Germany won the final warmup on June sixth in Chicago, beating host USA two-one. Kai Havertz and Leroy Sané scored, making it nine wins in a row. The bad: Lennart Karl, the young Bayern talent, tore a muscle in his thigh at the last training session before that game and will miss the World Cup. Nagelsmann brought in twenty-year-old Assan Ouédraogo from RB Leipzig as his replacement.

Germany starts on Sunday in Houston against Curaçao at seven p.m. German time, two p.m. in Brazil. Curaçao, with around one hundred fifty-eight thousand inhabitants, is the smallest nation ever to appear in a World Cup — on paper, a sure three points. The real tests come later against Côte d'Ivoire and Ecuador. The captain is Joshua Kimmich, and returning Manuel Neuer takes the number one spot in goal.

For Brazil, it gets serious right away on Saturday evening, Brazilian time. Carlo Ancelotti, the first foreign coach in Seleção World Cup history, meets star Vinicius Junior and surprisingly called-up Neymar directly against Morocco, the two-thousand-twenty-two semifinalist. This is no gentle start—it's immediately the toughest test of the group. Kickoff is seven p.m. in Brazil, midnight in Germany.

At SC Freiburg, the transfer window is hot. The club signs goalkeeper Mio Backhaus from Bremen for a base fee of around twelve million euros—a club record. Japanese midfielder Rihito Yamamoto also arrived for about seven million. On the way out, Merlin Röhl moves permanently to Everton. By the way, no Freiburg player is in the German World Cup squad; even Matthias Ginter was not called up.
