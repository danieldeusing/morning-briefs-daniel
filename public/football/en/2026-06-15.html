Germany 7:1 Curaçao — the opening-day masterclass

Seven goals, seven different scorers, a record for the ages

In Shell Energy Stadium in Houston (68,021 spectators), the German national team delivered its cleanest World Cup opener in years on Sunday: 7:1 against Curaçao. Felix Nmecha opened the scoring in the 6th minute, Comenencia headed an own goal (21'), Schlotterbeck made it 3:0 (38'), Havertz converted a penalty in first-half stoppage time (45+5'), Musiala (47'), Brown (68'), and Undav (78', Opta says 73') added more before Havertz completed his brace in the 88th.[1]

The stats underline the dominance: 3.91 xG on 26 shots (12 on target), 65% possession, plus 16 shots in the first half alone — this ties the World Cup record set by Ivory Coast against Serbia in 2006. Curaçao managed just 0.4 xG from eight attempts.[1][2]

xG (Expected Goals): A probability calculation per shot — from this position, on average, X out of 100 players would have scored. Germany's 3.91 xG means that by chance quality alone, a 4:0 result would have been the "fair" mean. Seven goals are thus not just luck, but genuine dominance — plus efficiency.

239:238 — Germany overtakes Brazil as World Cup all-time top scorer

With the seventh goal, the German national team moved ahead of Brazil to the top of the all-time World Cup goal scorers table: 239 goals in World Cup history, Brazil stands at 238. This is not mere trivia — Brazil held this record for decades. That a 7:1 against Curaçao brings the record is a small historical irony: in 2014 in Belo Horizonte, it was already about the number seven, then against Brazil itself.[3]

Coach Julian Nagelsmann assessed the victory matter-of-factly: "We really needed this convincing win. We needed this self confidence." Translation: three points plus confidence, don't read anything into the next match from this.

The direct rival: Ivory Coast 1:0 Ecuador

Parallel to Germany's opener, Ivory Coast prevailed in Philadelphia with 1:0 against Ecuador — Amad Diallo rescued his team in the 90th minute. With this, Côte d'Ivoire ended Ecuador's 19-game unbeaten run and put themselves on level terms with Germany in Group E. In other words: Saturday's duel in Toronto is the de facto group final for first place.[4]

Brazil: 1:1 against Morocco — Ancelotti concerned

Vinícius saves the point, but the match shows cracks

On Saturday (13.06.) in MetLife Stadium (80,663 spectators): Brazil 1:1 Morocco. Ismael Saibari (21') gave Morocco the lead, Vinícius Júnior (32') equalized before halftime. Statistically a note with weight: It was the first time in Vinícius' last nine goal appearances for the Seleção that Brazil did not win afterward — Vinícius goal = Brazil win was suddenly no longer a given.[6]

Coach Carlo Ancelotti assessed the opener with unusual candor: he was "a little worried", Brazil "didn't start very well" into the match, with "difficulty controlling possession in the first half". The Italian knows: Brazil didn't lose, but they squandered the opener — and the pressure starts now.

It's Monday, June fifteenth. A day after Germany's perfect World Cup opening. Seven to one against Curaçao in Houston, seven different goalscorers, and with the seventh goal, an all-time record.

Felix Nmecha opened in the sixth minute, then an own goal, then Schlotterbeck, then two from Havertz, with Musiala, Brown and Undav in between. Three point nine one expected goals from twenty-six shots, sixty-five percent possession. Sixteen shots in the first half alone — that ties the World Cup record set by Ivory Coast against Serbia in two thousand six.

The truly historic moment came with the seventh goal. Germany has now overtaken Brazil as the all-time World Cup top scorer in history. Two hundred thirty-nine goals against two hundred thirty-eight. A record Brazil held for decades. Coach Nagelsmann called it in — he said his team needed this clear win, above all for the confidence.

Brazil itself didn't have the same luck on Saturday in MetLife Stadium. One one against Morocco. Saibari scored in the twenty-first minute, Vinícius Júnior equalized in the thirty-second. Remarkable: It was the first time in Vinícius' last nine goal appearances for the Seleção that Brazil didn't win afterward. Coach Carlo Ancelotti was openly concerned — his word was: "a little worried".

In Group C, Scotland greets from the top of the table, Brazil stands as third. On Friday, a win against Haiti in Philadelphia is required, kick-off just after half-past two in the morning German time on Saturday, half-past nine Friday evening in Brazil. Otherwise, the knockout round gets shaky.

In Group E, Germany's real rival is clear: Ivory Coast, one to zero against Ecuador in Philadelphia, Amad Diallo scored in the ninetieth minute. Both teams have three points. The direct duel on Saturday in Toronto, ten p.m. German time, five p.m. in Brazil, thus decides first place in the group.

Outside the World Cup, SC Freiburg is reinforcing. Three signings for twenty-nine million euros: goalkeeper Mio Backhaus for twelve million from Werder Bremen, Japanese striker Keisuke Goto for ten million from Anderlecht, midfielder Rihito Yamamoto for seven million from Sint-Truiden. Financed through the Röhl deal: Merlin Röhl goes for twenty-five million to Everton and comes straight back on loan. Net, that leaves a loss of only three point two million euros. Sportingly, Freiburg loses little substance.

Today the World Cup continues without German or Brazilian involvement. Spain against Cape Verde at six p.m. German time, one p.m. in Brazil, in Atlanta. Belgium against Egypt at nine p.m. German time, four p.m. in Brazil, in Seattle. Then Saudi Arabia against Uruguay at midnight and Iran against New Zealand at three a.m. German time on Tuesday. Three of the four matches fall in Daniel's convenient Brazilian evening window.

Tomorrow, groups I and J sort themselves, and matchday two begins Thursday already. Things get interesting for Daniel starting Friday evening with Brazil against Haiti, Saturday evening Germany against Ivory Coast in Toronto. Until then we'll keep reading here as the groups take shape.
