SC Freiburg — the record summer

As of June 18, 2026 · Transfer window open (May–Aug)

While the World Cup dominates the headlines, Freiburg is quietly — but expensively — rebuilding behind the scenes, and the biggest move concerns a position that was thought to be secure: the goal. With Mio Backhaus (22), most recently first-choice keeper at Werder Bremen, the club is bringing in its most expensive signing ever for around €15 million (including bonuses), contract through 2031. That's a statement: Backhaus is not being brought in as a backup.[1]

The previous first-choice keeper Noah Atubolu is apparently set to leave. The club confirmed the Backhaus signing as a succession arrangement — but so far there's no paying buyer. According to bulinews (as of mid-June), after a contract standoff there were "no concrete offers"; a reported market value of €20–25 million is in circulation, and transfer rumors (including Newcastle) are unconfirmed.[2]

Context Freiburg normally sells to buy. The fact that the club is bringing in a €15M keeper before selling Atubolu is unusually bold. If the sale doesn't go through at a good price, suddenly there are two expensive goalkeepers sitting on the bench — the financial softest spot of this summer.

Offensive reinforcements are also coming: Keisuke Goto (21, striker) arrives from RSC Anderlecht (most recently on loan at Sint-Truiden) for a reported ~€13 million. Goto is even in the Japanese World Cup squad right now — Freiburg is signing a player performing on the biggest stage.[3] Additionally, Rihito Yamamoto (24, midfielder) moves from Sint-Truiden for ~€7 million; last season he made 29 starting appearances there, with 5 goals and 6 assists.[4]

Two Japanese players plus a German top keeper — the profile matches Freiburg's approach: young players with development potential rather than expensive finished stars. Around €35 million in spending is still a big deal for Sport-Club standards. The counterfinancing via the Atubolu sale is the still-open part of the equation.

Germany at the World Cup — dream start, but the opponent was Curaçao

Group E · Opener June 14 in Houston

7:1. That's a massive scoreline and Germany's biggest World Cup win in over two decades. Kai Havertz scored twice (penalty in stoppage time of the first half and in the 88th minute), with goals from Nmecha (6'), Schlotterbeck (38'), Musiala (47'), Brown (68'), and Undav (78'). Comenecia had briefly leveled it at 1:1 for Curaçao (21').[5]

Reality check Curaçao, with around 156,000 inhabitants, is the smallest nation ever to play in a World Cup — an underdog that almost any top nation could beat by double digits. The 7:1 is a morale booster and good for goal difference, but not a reliable stress test. That comes first against Ivory Coast.

That's exactly Germany's next opponent on Saturday (Sat June 20, Toronto, 10:00 p.m. CEST / 5:00 p.m. BRT). The Ivorians won their opener 1:0 and sit level with Germany at the top of the group — this will be the first real gauge of Nagelsmann's squad. The squad includes skipper Kimmich, Musiala, Wirtz, Havertz, Rüdiger, Schlotterbeck, and returning Manuel Neuer (40), who reversed his 2024 retirement from international football.[6]

Top stories from the early group matches

It's Thursday, June 18th, and the World Cup in North America is in full swing. For the German national team, the start couldn't have been better.

Germany won their opening match against Curaçao seven to one. Kai Havertz scored twice, with additional goals from Nmecha, Schlotterbeck, Musiala, Brown, and Undav. That's the biggest German World Cup win in over twenty years. But it's important to keep things in perspective: Curaçao is the smallest nation ever to play in a World Cup. The first real test comes on Saturday against Ivory Coast, kickoff at ten p.m. German time, which is five p.m. in Brazil.

Today there are four group matches. Czech Republic versus South Africa, Switzerland versus Bosnia, Canada versus Qatar, and late at night Mexico versus South Korea. At press time, all four hadn't kicked off yet, so we have just the kickoff times and no results.

Brazil had a tougher time. Just one to one against Morocco, the goal scored by Vinícius Junior. In a group where Scotland won their first match, the Seleção are already under pressure. On Friday against Haiti, a win is essential, kickoff at ten p.m. Brazil time.

Among the other favorites, there were big names. Lionel Messi scored a hat trick against Algeria to tie the World Cup goal record, now at sixteen goals, level with Miroslav Klose. England beat Croatia four to two, and France and Norway also won, with goals from Mbappé and Haaland.

At Daniel's beloved SC Freiburg, the most expensive transfer summer in club history is underway. The new goalkeeper Mio Backhaus costs around fifteen million euros, plus two Japanese players, Keisuke Goto and Rihito Yamamoto. Current first-choice keeper Atubolu is heading out but hasn't found a buyer yet. That's the financial softest spot of the summer.
