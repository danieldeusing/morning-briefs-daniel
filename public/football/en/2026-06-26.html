The German team: group winners despite Ecuador stumble

Top of Group E — and a hollow last match

Germany finishes the World Cup group stage as winners of Group E: two wins, one loss, 6 points, goal difference +6. The group title was virtually wrapped up before the final matchday, and you could see it in the result: against already-eliminated Ecuador, Julian Nagelsmann's team lost 1:2 — Sané scored early (2nd minute), then Angulo (9th) and Plata (77th) turned the game around. A result with no bearing on placement, but it raised questions about how sharp the squad would be in their first real high-pressure knockout match.[1]

Before that, the tournament had been solid: a 7:1 rout against Curaçao to open (Nmecha, Schlotterbeck, Havertz double, Musiala, Brown, Undav) and a grinding 2:1 win over Ivory Coast, both goals from Undav, the second deep into stoppage time (90+4). With ten goals scored, Germany boasts the second-best attack of the group stage — the Ecuador loss is more of a footnote than a warning sign.[1]

Round of 32 Monday: Paraguay — probable, not yet certain

The first knockout match kicks off on Monday, 29.06., at 22:30 MESZ / 17:30 BRT at Gillette Stadium in Foxborough, near Boston. The opponent: by current reckoning, Paraguay, which finished third in its group (4 points, goal difference −2). Here's the critical point — and caution is warranted: the opponent is not yet official. Nagelsmann's team will face one of the best third-place finishers, and which one depends on whether today and tomorrow's final groups (H, I, J, K, L) all wrap up. Kicker puts the Paraguay probability at 99 % citing "Football meets Data", but leaves three other scenarios open (including Scotland and Sweden).[2][3]

What matters today Only once tonight's matches and Saturday's finales are done does the opponent become official. Until then: Paraguay very likely, but "probable" — not "certain".

Nagelsmann himself remains calm: "Eventually we'll face a good team — but all the teams ahead of us are good anyway," he said after the group win. On paper, Paraguay is beatable: a compact, defensively organized South American side that advanced through third place more via discipline than attacking prowess. But a knockout match is a knockout match — an early deficit like the one against Ecuador would no longer be recoverable.[3]

Brazil: Vinícius carries the Seleção — Japan in the way

Group C winners, and a striker in top form

Brazil finish Group C undefeated as winners: two wins, one draw, 7 points, 7:1 goals. After a sluggish 1:1 draw against Morocco to start, they rattled off back-to-back 3:0 wins over Haiti and Scotland. In that final match, Neymar also returned — as a substitute, he made his first appearance for the national team since October 2023.[7][8]

But the star of the tournament from Brazil's perspective is Vinícius Jr.: 4 goals in 3 group matches (one vs. Morocco, one vs. Haiti, a brace vs. Scotland). That ties him with Haaland and Mbappé atop the scorers' list — and makes him only the fifth Brazilian ever to score in all three group matches of a World Cup. The four before him: Jairzinho 1970, Romário 1994, Ronaldo and Rivaldo 2002 — Brazil won the World Cup in each of those years. A good omen, nothing more, but under coach Ancelotti, Vinícius has taken a leap: 7 goals in 13 appearances for Brazil under Ancelotti versus 6 in 39 beforehand.[7][8]

SC Freiburg: goalkeeper guard change in the transfer window

Backhaus arrives for record fee, Atubolu wants out

The Bundesliga is on summer break — no league matches until the season starts in August 2026 — but the transfer market is alive, and Freiburg's biggest move concerns the goal. SC has signed Mio Backhaus (22) from Werder Bremen for a base fee of around €12–15 million — reportedly the most expensive transfer in club history. The reason: starting keeper Noah Atubolu is pushing to leave; Brighton and AC Milan are named as interested parties, his market value around €20 million.[10]

Context By Freiburg standards, this is a bold gambit: sign the most expensive player in club history to prepare for your starting keeper's departure. Sportily logical — Atubolu's value (~€20 million) easily finances Backhaus — but a starting-goalkeeper swap always carries risk. Backhaus must prove he can plug the gap behind a Freiburg defense that's often stretched thin.

SC has been active this summer overall: signings like Yuito Suzuki (~€10 million, Brøndby), Cyriaque Irié (~€8.5 million, Troyes), and Igor Matanovic (~€6.7 million, Frankfurt) add up to some €33 million — funded partly by selling Ritsu Doan to Eintracht Frankfurt (~€21 million). Backup keeper Jannik Huth has his contract extended; Robert Wagner is likely heading out on loan again.[11]
