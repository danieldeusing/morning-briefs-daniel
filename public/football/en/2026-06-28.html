Germany: group winners with a blemish — now Paraguay

It's the day before the first knockout match, and the DFB team arrives at the Round of 32 with mixed feelings. They won Group E soundly — six points, plus-six goal difference, top spot.[1] But the last group game was a 1:2 loss to Ecuador: Sané scored early (2'), then Angulo (9') and Plata (77') turned it around.[2] Because the group win was already secure, it was more of a B-team test than a warning sign — still, you don't like taking a loss as your last impression.

The path there was strong: a 7:1 thrashing of Curaçao to start (Havertz brace, plus Nmecha, Schlotterbeck, Musiala, Brown, Undav) and a 2:1 over Ivory Coast with two Undav goals, the winner in stoppage time.[3][4] This attacking punch is the main weapon tomorrow.

Opponents Paraguay advanced as one of the best third-place teams (3rd in Group D) — the team that never makes headlines and yet gets the job done.[5] Paraguay defends deep and compact, living off counter-attack moments. For Germany that means: patience against a deep block.

Deep block: Imagine a team lines up two rows of players in front of their own goal and defends only there, rather than pressing high. Hard to crack because there's no space behind the defense — you have to create the gap yourself instead of finding it with a long ball.

Kickoff: Monday, 06/29, 22:30 MESZ / 17:30 BRT, Gillette Stadium in Foxborough (Boston area).[6] For Daniel in Brazil that's early evening — very manageable.

Brazil: unbeaten through Group C, Vinícius in form

The Seleção does what a title favorite should do: unbeaten through the group, seven points, conceded just one goal.[7] Start was a 1:1 draw with Morocco (Vinícius Júnior leveled), then a 3:0 over Haiti (Matheus Cunha brace, plus Vinícius) and to finish a 3:0 away in Scotland — Vinícius with two goals, Cunha with the third.[8][9][10]

Vinícius Júnior is at four tournament goals, Matheus Cunha at three — that's the dual engine driving Brazil.[11] Anyone following the Premier League knows Cunha from Manchester United; at the World Cup his form is paying off.

In the Round of 32 awaits Japan — disciplined, technically clean, enormous running power. Exactly the sort of opponent that can make life difficult for a favored team if the Seleção lets the ball out of their hands too casually. Brazil is clear favorite, but no walk in the park.

Kickoff: Monday, 06/29, 19:00 MESZ / 14:00 BRT, NRG Stadium in Houston.[12] For Daniel that's early afternoon — and the match that dominates everything in the country.

World Cup format: why there's suddenly a "Round of 32"

Anyone who followed the tournament in 2022 stumbles over the knockout phase: it no longer starts with the Round of 16, but with a Round of 32 — 16 matches, 16 teams advance.[13]

Round of 32: Because the 2026 World Cup has 48 instead of 32 teams for the first time, there are twelve four-team groups. From each group the top two advance, plus the eight best third-place finishers — that's 32 teams in the first knockout round. Like a cup tournament with one extra preliminary round because more teams play.

The rest of the schedule: Round of 16 from Sat 07/04, quarterfinals 07/10–11, semifinals 07/15–16, final Sun 07/19.[13] Relevant for Daniel: If Germany and Brazil both advance, they could only meet in the final — they're in different tournament halves.

SC Freiburg: Doan sale, two Japanese players in, cash stays even

Freiburg is at the center of an active summer — and the biggest departure stings: Ritsu Doan moves to Eintracht Frankfurt for around 21 million €.[14] Doan was Freiburg's most reliable attacking force for years; replacing him is the real task of this window. Plus Merlin Röhl to Everton for 25 million € in the Premier League — the club's biggest single sale by fee.[15]

On the other side the club invests though: In goal comes Mio Backhaus (22) from Werder Bremen for 12 million €, up front Keisuke Goto (21) from RSC Anderlecht for 10 million € — Goto had 10 goals and 5 assists as a loanee at St. Truiden recently.[16][17] Plus defensive midfielder Rihito Yamamoto (24) for 7 million €.[18] Notable: Freiburg is again doubling down on the Japanese market.

Veteran Nicolas Höfler (36) switches to the reserve side on a free transfer — essentially the end of his Bundesliga career.[19]

Bottom line this window is nearly balanced so far: roughly 29 million € in, about 27 million € out, a small deficit of around 1.85 million €.[20] For a club like Freiburg that lives on smart dealing, that's a decent balance — crucial will be whether Goto and the new signings fill the Doan gap offensively.
