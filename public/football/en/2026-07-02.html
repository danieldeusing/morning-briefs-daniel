SC Freiburg — summer squad takes shape

Priority one: what happens at Dreisam's successor, Europa-Park Stadium, while the World Cup runs.

Four arrivals, five departures — measured overhaul

Freiburg works the summer methodically: targeted reinforcements rather than marquee names. In goal, Mio Backhaus from Werder Bremen (confirmed 05-23, reportedly around 12 million euros base fee plus add-ons) — a smart move with the Atubolu transfer rumors in the background: Freiburg pads the goalkeeper position before the number one potentially leaves. Adding midfielder Rihito Yamamoto (from VV Sint-Truiden, 05-27) and striker Keisuke Goto (from RSC Anderlecht, 06-11).[1]

Fee check On the Goto transfer fee, sources conflict: baden24.de lists it as "not disclosed," while a kicker.de summary cites roughly 10 million euros (as of ~06-26). Until a source confirms the figure hard: treat it as a kicker-cited estimate, not fact.

Defender Anthony Jung has also extended (05-28) — continuity at the back. On departures: Junior Adamu (to Schalke 04, 06-11), Robert Wagner (permanent to Dynamo Dresden, 06-23, with buyback option per cross-check), Elias Etringer (to SV Elversberg, 06-08), and Yann Sturm (to Eintracht Braunschweig, 06-19, contract through 2029). Merlin Röhl's Everton move was set in motion back in fall 2025 and doesn't strictly count as part of this window.[1]

Read: No wow-transfer, but a clean squad cut. Backhaus is the bet that—in a pinch—decides Freiburg's season: if Atubolu does go, the position is covered; if he stays, suddenly you have a very solid goalkeeper tandem. Exactly the kind of forward-thinking planning Freiburg is known for.

Germany — the World Cup exit no one wanted [4][5]

The national team fails at their own dream World Cup in North America in the Round of 32.

It was the penalty shootout that knocked Germany out. After a 1:1 finish even after extra time—Enciso had put Paraguay ahead in the 42nd minute; Havertz equalized after halftime—the DFB side fell to Paraguay on 06-29 in Foxborough with a 3:4 penalty shootout loss. The penalty drama: Germany botched three kicks—Havertz failed on the very first try, then Woltemade and Tah also missed; Paraguay made only two mistakes and held their nerve, José Canale converting the decisive penalty. Compounding the pain, a Tah goal in extra time was disallowed after VAR—Waldemar Anton had fouled Paraguay goalkeeper Orlando Gill in the buildup.[4]

Round of 32: This World Cup has 48 teams for the first time, so before the Round of 16 comes an extra knockout round of the last 32. The DFB calls it "Round of 16" in their statements; in the 48-team format it's actually the round before—meaning out, earlier than Germany has ever been in a World Cup.

And Nagelsmann? The national coach rules out quitting: "I'm not one to run away." His contract runs through summer 2028. DFB leadership pointedly leaves his future up in the air—President Neuendorf says he's not "simply moving on" after "such a low point," director Völler calls him "likely the right man" but insists it's not his decision alone.[5]

My take: A coaching change now would be shortsighted. The tournament was a disaster, no question—but rebuilding the national team is a multi-year project, and losing on penalties isn't a systemic indictment. DFB leadership playing "time will tell" sends the wrong signal regardless: real backing looks different.

Brazil — Seleção advances, Norway awaits

For Daniel in Brazil, consolation this summer: the Seleção is in the Round of 16.

Brazil dodged the Round of 16 trap. After falling behind early against Japan (Sano, 29th minute), the Seleção on 06-29 in Houston turned it around: Casemiro headed the equalizer in the 56th, Gabriel Martinelli scored in the 90th minute for a 2:1 victory.[6] Not a brilliant display, but an advance—and in knockout soccer that's what counts.

In the Round of 16 comes Norway, who beat Ivory Coast 2:1—Erling Haaland scored in the 86th and now has five World Cup goals in three games. Kickoff Sunday, 07-05, 10:00 PM MESZ / 05:00 PM BRT at MetLife Stadium (New Jersey).[7] A real test: Haaland against Brazil's defense is the duel this Round of 16 turns on.

It's Thursday, July second, and for a German fan in Brazil, it's a bittersweet morning. Germany is out of their own dream World Cup, Brazil is still alive, and today three more Round of 32 matches are on the slate.

Let's start with the pain. Germany fell in Foxborough on June twenty-ninth in the Round of 32 against Paraguay. One to one after extra time, then the team lost the penalty shootout three to four. Three German shooters missed: Havertz on the very first shot, then Woltemade and Tah. Paraguay kept their composure, José Canale sealed it with the decisive penalty. It's the earliest World Cup exit Germany has ever faced.

The new World Cup has forty-eight teams, so before the Round of 16 comes an extra knockout round of the last thirty-two. The DFB calls it the Round of 16 in their statements, but in the forty-eight-team format it's the round before that. However you name it, Germany is out, earlier than they've ever been.

Head coach Julian Nagelsmann is staying. He says he's not one to run away. His contract runs through summer twenty twenty-eight. The DFB leadership, though, is playing it cautiously, leaving his future deliberately unclear. My opinion: a coaching change now would be hasty, but the lukewarm backing sends the wrong signal either way.

Consolation for Daniel comes from his adopted home. Brazil won on June twenty-ninth in Houston, two to one against Japan. Casemiro equalized with his head, Martinelli scored in stoppage time for the win. No brilliant performance, but an advance—which is what matters in knockout soccer.

In the Round of 16, Norway awaits with Erling Haaland, who has already scored five World Cup goals. Kickoff is Sunday, July fifth, at ten PM Central European Summer Time, that's five PM Brazilian time, at MetLife Stadium.

Today the Round of 32 continues. Spain against Austria at nine PM Summer Time, that's four PM in Brazil. Then Portugal versus Croatia and Switzerland against Algeria, both overnight into Friday European time, but still Thursday evening and midnight in Brazil.

And SC Freiburg? Quietly assembling its roster. Four arrivals including goalkeeper Mio Backhaus from Werder Bremen, and five departures. Training starts July twelfth, then it's off to training camp in Schruns, Austria, with friendlies against Derby County and Greuther Fürth.

Tomorrow we'll see how the Round of 32 fills out, and whether there's any new movement on goalkeeper Noah Atubolu from Freiburg. Until then: chin up and fingers crossed for the Seleção on Sunday.
