World Cup 2026 · The round of 16 begins — without us

From today on, it's elimination: lose and you're out. Two matches open proceedings in the USA. At 7:00 PM CEST / 2:00 PM BRT, tournament co-favorite Canada faces Morocco in Houston; at 11:00 PM CEST / 6:00 PM BRT, France takes on Paraguay in Philadelphia.[1] And Paraguay — that's the team that knocked out Germany. For German fans, it's the bitter aftertaste of the evening: the team that beat us advances, and we're already gone.

To recap, because it still stings: Germany lost the Round of 16 — the first knockout round in this 48-team format — on 06/29 in Boston to Paraguay, 1-1 after extra time, 3-4 on penalties. Havertz equalized, a Tah header in extra time was ruled out by VAR, and from the spot, both Havertz and Tah missed.[2][3] It was the first World Cup penalty shootout loss in DFB history — a sentence that needs to be read twice.

Round of 16 instead of quarterfinals: With 48 teams for the first time in 2026, there's an extra knockout round. Thirty-two teams start in the first elimination round (the "round of 32"), and the 16 winners advance to the round of 16. Germany didn't make it past that first hurdle.

For tonight: France is a clear favorite against Paraguay, but the South Americans just proved they can go 120 minutes and a penalty shootout. Anyone writing Paraguay off hasn't watched June 29.

Brazil in the tournament · Sunday vs. Norway

Here on the ground, it's the weekend's marquee match: Brazil faces Norway on Sunday at 10:00 PM CEST / 5:00 PM BRT at MetLife Stadium near New York.[1] For Daniel in everyday Brazil, that means the 5 PM kickoff runs straight into Sunday evening — half the country is glued to the screen. Norway — with Haaland as their weapon — is no formality, but Brazil is the favorite. If the Seleção wins, they meet the winner of France–Paraguay in the quarterfinals (July 9–11).

Colombia advanced with a 1-0 win over Ghana (goal by Jhon Arias, 14th minute, on 07/03 in Kansas City) and faces Switzerland on Tuesday.[4] Canada comes in after Morocco beat the Netherlands on penalties on 06/29 (1-1 a.e.t., 3-2 on penalties) to knock them out of the tournament.[5]

SC Freiburg · The squad gets rebuilt

While the tournament runs, Freiburg is working on a new squad — and with an unusually deep wallet. The marquee arrival is at goalkeeper: Mio Backhaus (22) comes from Werder Bremen for a base fee of around €12M and is, by multiple reports, the club's most expensive signing ever.[6] A double-digit million fee for a keeper — that's a statement for Freiburg. It also suggests that first-choice keeper Noah Atubolu is heading out; no exit is confirmed yet, so we'll treat it as a logical consequence, not fact.

Up front, there's movement with Keisuke Goto (21) switching from RSC Anderlecht. The reported fee is around €10M — though numbers vary, one source cited €11M, so the soft language is warranted.[7] The Japanese forward was recently on loan at St. Truiden, where he scored double figures in the Belgian league. A young, developable striker who fits Freiburg's profile: more promise than an instant guarantee.

On the departure side: Junior Adamu is heading to Schalke 04, and Yann Sturm to second-tier Eintracht Braunschweig.[8] Both reports come from aggregators and search results, not a cleanly sourced primary source (kicker.de blocked direct access) — so read with a degree of caution until the club confirms officially.

Context Two double-digit million outflows in one summer are unusual for SC. But the pattern fits: Freiburg invests selectively and young (goalkeeper and striker), not broadly. Whether Backhaus is an immediate #1 or Goto is ready for Bundesliga right away will be decided in pre-season — both are bets on development, not finished starters.

Bundesliga · Schedule is set, but the ball is quiet

No football in Germany today — deep summer break; the 2026/27 season starts late August. What's new is that the DFL published the schedule on 07/02, and the opener on 08/28/2026 is confirmed: Bayern Munich vs. VfB Stuttgart kicks off the season.[9][10] That's roughly a week later than usual — the North American World Cup has pushed the calendar back.

Season framework calendar: This is the DFL's skeleton schedule for the whole season — when each matchday happens, when the cup and internationals fit in. Think of it like a school year's master schedule: first the rough grid, then the specific matches per week get filled in. That detailed schedule came out on July 2.

Germany's transfer window has been open since 07/01 and stays open until 08/31 — the critical deadline by which Freiburg and everyone else must finalize their rosters. Until Bundesliga kickoff, the World Cup in the USA remains the heartbeat; league matters return gradually as pre-season training ramps up.
