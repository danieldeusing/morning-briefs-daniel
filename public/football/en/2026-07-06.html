SC Freiburg — the transfer window is in full swing

Summer break, but the squad is moving. One addition up front, two departures, two extensions.

Addition: Keisuke Goto (21) from RSC Anderlecht

SC have signed Japanese international striker Keisuke Goto — officially confirmed on 06/11. The 21-year-old was most recently under contract at Anderlecht but spent last season on loan at VV Sint-Truiden, where he scored ten goals in 28 league appearances and added five assists. A young, goal-hungry forward with real upside — exactly the profile Freiburg is known for: buy early and cheap, develop patiently.[1]

Heads up — rumor A transfer fee of roughly €11 million is so far only a journalist's figure (per Sacha Tavolieri, as of 06/11) — the club has published neither an amount nor a contract length. Only the move itself is confirmed fact.
bundesliga.com · sport.sky.de

Departures: Wagner and Weißhaupt

Midfielder Robert Wagner (22) is moving permanently to second-division side Dynamo Dresden — both clubs are keeping the fee and contract length confidential. Wagner was already on loan at Dresden in the second half of last season (15 second-division appearances, helping secure promotion), so the permanent move is the logical next step. Striker Noah Weißhaupt is also leaving the club, heading to SV Darmstadt 98.[2][3]

scfreiburg.com · baden24.de

Extended: Huth and Jung

Goalkeeper Jannik Huth (32) is staying, as is left-back Anthony Jung (34), who proved his worth in 15 competitive matches last season. Two experienced anchors locked into the squad — unspectacular, but the kind of move that keeps a locker room stable.[4]

scfreiburg.com · kicker.de

Freiburg players at the World Cup

From the current squad, Yuito Suzuki is representing Japan at the 2026 World Cup (Group F alongside the Netherlands, Tunisia and Sweden) — in his first Freiburg season he made 41 appearances, scoring nine goals and adding seven assists. Alongside him, former Freiburg player Ritsu Doan (now at Eintracht Frankfurt) is also in the Japanese squad.[5]

DFB — picking up the pieces after the World Cup exit

First the historic penalty-shootout elimination, then the head coach's resignation, now the Klopp question.

1-1 against Paraguay — out on penalties

Germany was eliminated already in the Round of 32 (the round of 32 of the 48-team tournament) at the near-home World Cup in North America: 1-1 after extra time against Paraguay, then defeat on penalties on 06/29 in Foxborough — Germany's first-ever World Cup exit on penalties. Kai Havertz missed Germany's first attempt, and Jonathan Tah sent the decisive kick over the bar.[6][7]

About the number The exact penalty-shootout score is reported inconsistently: independent outlets (Sky Sports, ESPN, CBS, NBC) all agree on 3-4 for Paraguay. The DFB's own report, however, states "4-5 on penalties" — likely a different counting convention. We follow the 3-4 confirmed by multiple sources; the DFB figure could not be independently verified.
skysports.com · espn.com · dfb.de

Nagelsmann resigns

Two days after the elimination, Julian Nagelsmann drew the consequence: on Friday midday (07/03) he resigned as head coach, and the DFB boards dissolved his contract that same day. The earliest realistic end to an era that began with high expectations — and never really caught fire in the tournament.[8]

dfb.de

Klopp in talks — but still under contract with Red Bull

And the name half of Germany wanted to hear came right on cue: Jürgen Klopp said on Friday evening on MagentaTV that he's in contact with the DFB and is "ready" — in his own words: "I'm more than fired up right now. So I'm ready." The catch: Klopp is still tied to Red Bull as "Head of Global Soccer" through 2029. Nothing is signed, and any move would first require a release. So: the hottest rumor of the summer, but no deal done.[9]

Context Until a release from Red Bull and a DFB contract are in place, "Klopp becomes head coach" remains speculation — treat it as an open process, not a done deal.

World Cup 2026 — the knockout rounds without Germany and Brazil

Today the final quarterfinal tickets are decided. Two big names are already out.

Brazil out: 1-2 against Norway

Friday it was Germany, Sunday it was Brazil: Carlo Ancelotti's Seleção lost yesterday (07/05) in the Round of 16 1-2 against Norway. Erling Haaland scored twice (a header in the 79th minute, a long-range finish in the 90th), while Neymar pulled one back late for Brazil from the penalty spot. Norway is through to its first-ever World Cup quarterfinal — and for Neymar, it was in all likelihood the last World Cup match of his career.[10][11]

Brace: Two goals by the same player in the same match. Haaland headed in the first and finished the second from distance — enough to knock Brazil out of the tournament practically single-handedly.

It's Monday, July sixth, and this is a bitter football summer — for German and Brazilian fans alike. Both nations are already out of the World Cup knockout rounds, and for the German federation, it's triggered a small earthquake.

Germany was eliminated in the Round of 32, eliminated by Paraguay. The match finished one-one, then came the penalty shootout — and it ended three-four for Paraguay. It's the first World Cup penalty-shootout elimination in German history. Kai Havertz missed the first attempt, and Jonathan Tah sent the decisive kick over the bar. One quick note on the score: the German football federation itself reports four-five, but every independent outlet — Sky, E-S-P-N, C-B-S — agrees on three-four, and that's the number we're going with.

The fallout came fast. On Friday at midday, head coach Julian Nagelsmann resigned, and the federation dissolved his contract that same day. And that same evening, the name everyone wanted to hear came up: Jürgen Klopp said on television that he's ready and in touch with the federation. But hold on — nothing is signed. Klopp is still under contract with Red Bull through twenty twenty-nine, and any move would first need a release. So, the hottest rumor of the summer, but no deal done.

Brazil is out too. Yesterday, Carlo Ancelotti's Seleção lost one-two to Norway. Erling Haaland scored twice, once with his head and once from distance, while Neymar pulled one back late from the penalty spot. For Norway, it's their first-ever World Cup quarterfinal — and for Neymar, it was in all likelihood the last World Cup match of his career.

Today the last quarterfinal spots are decided. Portugal plays Spain at nine p.m. Central European time, which is four p.m. in Ribeirão Claro. Late at night, host nation U-S-A takes on Belgium. Already through are Morocco, France, Norway, and England.

At Freiburg, the transfer carousel is spinning. The club signed Japanese international striker Keisuke Goto from Anderlecht, a young, goal-hungry forward. On the other side, Robert Wagner is moving permanently to Dynamo Dresden and Noah Weißhaupt to Darmstadt. Goalkeeper Jannik Huth and left-back Anthony Jung have both extended their contracts. There's talk of a transfer fee for Goto around eleven million euros — but that's only a journalist's figure, and the club hasn't confirmed any amount.

Both leagues remain on break. The Bundesliga doesn't kick off its new season until August twenty-eighth, Bayern against Stuttgart. In Brazil, the league has been on hold since June eleventh because of the World Cup — the table has stood still since matchday eighteen, with Palmeiras comfortably in front. The federation has moved the restart up to July sixteenth.

Looking ahead: this week the World Cup keeps moving fast with the quarterfinals, and the big question remains whether and when the German football federation settles the Klopp situation. Tomorrow we'll recap who's qualified for the final eight.
