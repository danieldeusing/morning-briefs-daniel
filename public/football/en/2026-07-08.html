SC Freiburg — Manzambi's Knee [1][2][3]

Switzerland's best player at this World Cup has been sidelined since Tuesday. Argentina awaits on Saturday.

There are weeks when a Bundesliga midfielder from the Breisgau region suddenly becomes the most interesting man at a World Cup. Johan Manzambi has three goals and two assists at this World Cup, making him Switzerland's top scorer — Sportschau calls him the hero of the match against Bosnia-Herzegovina.[1] Then, in the final training session in Vancouver before the round of 16, a knee bruise. He missed the match against Colombia.[2]

Switzerland advanced anyway — 0:0, 4:3 on penalties. Now it's Argentina, Saturday evening in Kansas City. Head coach Murat Yakin doesn't sound euphoric: "The injury in the last moment of training was a shock. I don't think you get rid of a swelling like that quickly. Time-wise, I don't think it will be enough." And yet the same source reports a "small glimmer of hope" that the Freiburg player could be fit in time for the quarterfinal.[3]

My read: this is a door being deliberately left open. A bruise isn't a ligament injury — it heals through pain threshold and swelling, not over weeks. Four days between the round of 16 and the quarterfinal is tight, but not absurd. If Manzambi can run pain-free on Friday, he plays. No nation voluntarily leaves its best scorer out against Messi.

For Daniel The match kicks off in Brazil on Saturday at 22:00 BRT — in Germany it's already Sunday, 03:00 MESZ, by then. So readers of the Swiss press will find reports dating the match to "July 12." Both are correct, it's the same kickoff.

Transfer Window — Record Fee in Goal, Question Marks Up Front

Window open until 31.08. Rumors are labeled as such.

On 23.05. the Sport-Club completed the most expensive transfer in its history: goalkeeper Mio Backhaus (22) arrives from Werder Bremen. The base fee is around €12 million, and with add-ons the package climbs to as much as €15 million — more than Freiburg has ever paid for a player, Ritsu Doan included.[4][5] As part of the deal, Werder exercised the purchase option for Karl Hein (on loan from Arsenal, ~€3 million) and installed him in goal.[4] That leaves Noah Atubolu's future in Freiburg open; Sportschau expects a departure.[6]

Purchase option vs. purchase obligation: A loan can come with a right of first refusal. With an option, the club that took the loan may buy the player afterward — but doesn't have to. With an obligation, it must, as soon as an agreed condition is met (e.g., staying up, a number of appearances). Like a rental car with a buy-out clause: option means you decide at the end; obligation means once you've driven 20,000 km, it's yours and you pay.

Also in the squad picture: Keisuke Goto (from Anderlecht, 11.06.) and Rihito Yamamoto (from St. Truiden, 27.05.) are joining, and Anthony Jung extended his contract on 28.05. Confirmed departures are Junior Adamu (Schalke 04, 11.06.), Yann Sturm (Eintracht Braunschweig, 19.06.), Robert Wagner (Dynamo Dresden, permanent, 23.06.), and Elias Etringer (SV Elversberg, 08.06.).[5]

But the real story is on the pitch in Kansas City. Manzambi's market value currently stands at €50 million, his contract runs through 2030 and contains no release clause — so Freiburg doesn't have to do anything at all.[7][8] Concrete offers remain unconfirmed so far; what's circulating are interest reports from aggregators, not done-deal reports. Treat every "hot lead" as exactly that until the club confirms it.

Release clause: A price fixed in the contract at which a club must let a player go. Like a price tag the seller is no longer allowed to remove once someone puts the money down. Manzambi doesn't have one — so Freiburg alone decides at what number it starts listening. That's exactly what makes a World Cup scoring list so expensive.
Sell now

The market value is a World Cup high, not a normal state. Five scoring contributions at a global tournament are the loudest sales pitch there is. After a bruise, a weaker first half of the season, or a lost quarterfinal, nobody will be talking about €50 million anymore. And Freiburg has just tied up €15 million in goal.

Hold

Contract through 2030, no clause: the price doesn't fall on its own. The Sport-Club is coming off a Europa League final and enters the season with European ambitions — without the squad's most creative player, the first half of the season gets thin. And a transfer in the middle of the tournament would be the worst possible timing for the player himself.

My call: Hold until the tournament ends, then negotiate. The fee rises with every match Switzerland survives — and doesn't drop a cent if Freiburg stays quiet. With a contract through 2030, the negotiating position is as strong as it's ever been. Anyone who signs before 19.07. in this situation is giving money away.

Round of 16 — How We Got Here [12]

All results checked against two independent sources. As of 08.07.2026.

The round of 16 has been complete since Tuesday. Norway knocked out the Seleção in East Rutherford on 05.07. with a 2:1 win — both goals from Erling Haaland. The same day, England beat Mexico 3:2 in Mexico City. On 06.07., a late Mikel Merinos goal in Arlington ended not only Portugal's tournament (0:1) but also Cristiano Ronaldo's World Cup career; at the same time, Belgium tore apart the USA 4:1 in Seattle. On Tuesday, Argentina rallied to beat Egypt 3:2 in Atlanta amid VAR drama, and Switzerland beat Colombia in Vancouver 4:3 on penalties after a 0:0 draw. Also through: France (1:0 against Paraguay, Philadelphia) and Morocco (3:0 against Canada, Houston).[12]

What stands out about this round: hardly any favorite came through comfortably. Portugal fell to a late substitute's goal, Colombia only after a shootout, Egypt amid VAR drama — and Brazil lost to a Norway side that simply took its chances. If there's a lesson here: efficiency beats dominance when a tournament is inflated to 48 teams and legs get heavy in July.

Germany — the Aftershock [16][17]

For context, because it defines the mood of this summer: Germany was already eliminated on 29.06. — in the round of 32, against Paraguay, who stood at FIFA rank 41 at the start of the tournament (Germany: 10). Julio Enciso scored in the 42nd minute, Kai Havertz equalized in the 54th. In the penalty shootout, Havertz, Nick Woltemade, and Jonathan Tah all missed — final score 3:4. It was Germany's first-ever World Cup penalty shootout defeat.[16][17]

Sky calls it a "stunning last-32 exit," Al Jazeera "one of all-time World Cup upsets." Both are true, and neither explains anything. A 48-team tournament has an extra knockout round in which a fourth-place group finisher meets a group winner before any rhythm has developed. That raises variance — and variance eats favorites. That's exactly why Norway's win over Brazil isn't a glitch in the system, but the system itself.
