DFB — the rebuild after the exit [1][2][3]

The Nagelsmann resignation is confirmed. Everything on Klopp is negotiation status — flagged as such.

Five days after the exit, the DFB cleared the decks: Julian Nagelsmann resigned as head coach on July 3. "My top priority was always the team's success," Sportschau quotes him — a resignation, not a firing.[1] That leaves the most important job in German football open, ten and a half months before the home-Euros dress rehearsal.

Since the weekend, one name keeps coming up as successor: Jürgen Klopp. Sportschau's headline reads "Klopp on his way to the DFB," but the outlet itself notes his unveiling could still take a while.[2] The catch: Klopp is tied to Red Bull as "Head of Global Soccer" through 2029. Red Bull denies a move; reports say there's no written exit clause, but reportedly a verbal understanding to let him go if the DFB comes calling. Negotiations are said to be over a release fee in the single- to possibly double-digit millions.[3] The DFB has officially confirmed or presented nothing at all — treat every "as good as done" report accordingly.

A release fee for a coach: Paying money to buy a player out of a running contract is normal; for coaches, it's almost never done. Klopp is under contract with Red Bull — if the DFB wants him sooner, it has to compensate Red Bull, like a transfer fee, just for the man on the touchline. It would be the first time ever the DFB has paid for a head coach.

My read: A public broadcaster writing "on his way" is more than Twitter chatter — but the deal hinges on a release that another company can make pricier at any time. Until the DFB puts Klopp on a podium, it's a negotiation status, not a contract. Sporting-wise it would send a signal: a motivator and transition-obsessive for a team that's lately been missing one thing above all — energy.

The World Cup exit against Paraguay [4][5][6]

Result checked against three independent sources — including FIFA's own highlight channel.

A reminder, because it explains this summer's mood: Germany fell in the round of 32 to Paraguay on June 29, a side ranked 41st by FIFA at the tournament's start (Germany: 10th). Julio Enciso headed Paraguay in front in the 42nd minute, Kai Havertz equalized in the 54th. After 1-1 through extra time too, it went to a shootout — where Havertz, Nick Woltemade, and Jonathan Tah missed. José Canale converted in sudden death for the final 3-4. It was Germany's first-ever World Cup shootout loss.[4][5][6]

Source check The DFB's own site lists the match as a "4-5" loss and dates it to June 30. Sky Sports, ESPN, Fox, and the official FIFA highlight clip, by contrast, agree on 3-4 (Paraguay 4, Germany 3) and June 29 (US Eastern time; the match kicked off in Foxborough in the evening, past midnight in Europe). We follow the three independent sources plus FIFA, not the federation's own page.

Sky calls it a "stunning last-32 exit," Al Jazeera "one of all-time World Cup upsets." Both are true and explain nothing. A 48-team tournament has an extra knockout round in which a group's fourth-place finisher meets a group winner before any rhythm has set in — that raises variance, and variance eats favorites. Norway's win over Brazil is the same mechanism at work, not a fluke in the system.

SC Freiburg — transfer window: retooled, not sold off [8][9]

Window open through 08/31. Rumors are flagged as such.

At Sport-Club, this is already shaping up to be one of the most expensive summers in club history — on both sides of the ledger. Arrivals: goalkeeper Mio Backhaus (22, from Werder Bremen, roughly €12m) and center-forward Keisuke Goto (21, from RSC Anderlecht, €10m). Goto played for Japan at the World Cup and spent last season on loan at VV St. Truiden, scoring ten goals with five assists in 28 matches.[8]

Departures: the big one is Merlin Röhl — a permanent move to Everton for €25m, after a mandatory purchase clause kicked in once Everton avoided relegation (finishing 13th). Also gone: Junior Adamu, permanently to Schalke 04 (€0.8m), while Robert Wagner (Dynamo Dresden, €0.85m), Noah Weißhaupt (Darmstadt 98, €0.5m), and Yann Sturm (Eintracht Braunschweig, free transfer, contract through 2029) left elsewhere in the window.[8]

Mandatory purchase clause: A loan can include an option to buy. With an obligation, the borrowing club MUST buy the player outright once a condition is met — for Röhl, that was Everton avoiding relegation. Like a rental car with a fixed buyout price past a certain mileage: once you hit it, it's yours whether you want it or not. For Freiburg, that means €25m locked in, no renegotiation needed.

As a rumor — and only a rumor — Newcastle United are said to be interested in young talent Johan Manzambi (20): according to kicker, the Premier League club has already made contact.[9] That's not a done deal. My take: the rebuild looks healthy. Röhl's €25m funds two young, high-upside arrivals — exactly the Freiburg model. It only gets risky if a creative player like Manzambi also leaves without a replacement lined up.

Brazil — Norway knocks out the Seleção, Palmeiras lead [13][14][15]

Table checked today against two independent sources (lance.com.br + gazetaesportiva.com, identical).

Brazil's World Cup ended on July 5 in the round of 16: a 1-2 loss to Norway at MetLife Stadium. Erling Haaland scored twice in the final eleven minutes (his tournament goals 6 and 7), and Neymar's stoppage-time penalty came too late — Bruno Guimarães had earlier missed a penalty of his own. For Norway, it was the first World Cup in 28 years, straight to the quarterfinals; for Brazil, the weakest World Cup campaign since 1990.[13]

The domestic league has been on pause since June 11 for the World Cup; the table has stood still after matchday 18. Palmeiras lead with 41 points and just one loss — seven clear of Flamengo, who have a game in hand. At the bottom, Chapecoense (9 points) look all but doomed. Matchday 19 kicks off Thursday, July 16: Botafogo–Santos and Vitória–Vasco at 19:30 BRT / 00:30 MESZ (Fri), with the rest of the round following on the 17th, 21st, 22nd, and 23rd. Brazil's early exit, incidentally, hasn't shifted the CBF calendar.[14][15]

It's Thursday, July 9th. The World Cup quarterfinals kick off today, but the biggest German story is playing out off the pitch: the DFB is looking for a new head coach.

Julian Nagelsmann resigned on July 3rd, five days after the World Cup exit. Since the weekend, one name keeps coming up as his successor: Jürgen Klopp. German broadcaster Sportschau even wrote that Klopp is on his way to the DFB. But careful — nothing is confirmed yet. Klopp is under contract with Red Bull through 2029, and the DFB would first have to negotiate his release, possibly even paying a fee, which would be a first for a German head coach. Until Klopp is sitting on a podium, this is a negotiation, not a deal.

A quick reminder of how the exit happened: Germany lost to Paraguay on June 29th. One-one after extra time, then three-four on penalties. Havertz, Woltemade, and Tah all missed. It was Germany's first-ever World Cup shootout loss. Side note: the DFB's own website actually lists a different scoreline, but Sky, ESPN, Fox, and the official FIFA clip all agree on three-four — that's the version we're going with.

Tonight brings the first quarterfinal: France against Morocco, ten p.m. German time, five p.m. for you in Ribeirão Claro. Three more follow through Saturday. The most exciting night is Saturday, when Norway against England overlaps with Argentina against Switzerland.

Brazil is out too. On July 5th, the Seleção lost one-two to Norway. Erling Haaland scored twice in the final eleven minutes, and Neymar's stoppage-time penalty came too late. For Norway, it was their first World Cup in twenty-eight years, and they went straight to the quarterfinals. For Brazil, it's their worst World Cup since nineteen ninety.

Back home, Sport-Club's transfer window is red-hot. Mio Backhaus joins in goal for around twelve million from Werder Bremen, and Japanese forward Keisuke Goto arrives for ten million from Anderlecht. On the other side, Merlin Röhl moves permanently to Everton for twenty-five million after a mandatory purchase clause kicked in when Everton avoided relegation. Junior Adamu is heading to Schalke. It's a healthy rebuild: Röhl's fee is funding two young signings, exactly the Freiburg model.
