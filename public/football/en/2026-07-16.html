SC Freiburg — the club's most expensive summer ever

Squad planning in the open transfer window · as of 07/16/2026

For Daniel, Freiburg remains the real headline — and rarely has a summer in Breisgau been this eventful. SC finished the season seventh, lost the Europa League final, and is now funding a full squad rebuild with a record sale. Let's take it step by step.

Manzambi to Aston Villa — Freiburg's most expensive sale

The 20-year-old Swiss international Johan Manzambi is moving to Aston Villa. The figures vary by source: Fabrizio Romano cites a base fee of around €60 million, while Sky and kicker put the total package including bonuses at just under €70 million. Either way, it's by far the most expensive departure in Freiburg's club history. Ironic twist: it's Villa — the very club that beat SC 3-0 in the Europa League final in May — that's now getting its gem.

According to Romano and Florian Plettenberg (as of 07/16), Newcastle had actually reached a club-to-club agreement first — but Manzambi himself is said to have preferred Villa, which then closed the deal. This is fast-moving transfer reporting, not an official club statement; treat it as a rumor until Freiburg confirms.

Fee "including bonuses": the nearly €70 million isn't a fixed wire transfer — it's a base fee plus bonus tiers that only pay out once certain conditions are met, such as appearance or goal counts, or Villa qualifying for the Champions League. Think of it like buying a car with optional extras: the sticker price is only reached if every extra gets added on. That's why it's "~€60m base," not "€70m fixed."
footballtransfers.com

Three new arrivals: Backhaus (club record), Yamamoto, Gotō

Freiburg is reinvesting immediately. In goal comes Mio Backhaus from Werder Bremen — at roughly €15 million, the highest fee SC has ever paid for a player. Alongside him, central midfield all-rounder Rihito Yamamoto (€7m, from Belgium's St. Truiden) and, up front, Japanese striker Keisuke Gotō (~€10m, from RSC Anderlecht). On the outgoing side, two rumors are circulating alongside Manzambi: Junior Adamu is said to be leaning toward Schalke 04, and goalkeeper Noah Atubolu is reportedly on Real Sociedad's radar — both per media reports (as of 07/16), unconfirmed.

kicker.de · sportschau.de

Despite the EL final: only the Conference League playoff

One point that's easy to miss: Freiburg reached the Europa League final — and still isn't automatically in European competition for 2026/27. As the runner-up, there's no entry spot (only the winner gets that). Through the league, 7th place isn't enough for the Europa League, only for the Conference League playoff — a qualifying hurdle, not a guaranteed spot.

Why no automatic European spot? UEFA entry spots are tied to league position, not cup runs. The Europa League spot is the prize for winning that competition — reaching the final doesn't count. And 7th place in the Bundesliga happens to land exactly on the Conference League playoff tier for 2026/27: so Freiburg first has to survive a knockout tie in August just to reach the group stage. No free pass — an extra match.

DFB — the restart after the debacle

National team & the coaching question · as of 07/16/2026

The biggest story in German football right now isn't happening on the pitch, but on the bench. After a historically early World Cup exit, the DFB is regrouping — and the top job is still open.

The exit: on penalties for the first time ever

Germany fell in the round of 32 to Paraguay: 1-1 after extra time (Enciso 42', Havertz ~53.), then 3-4 on penalties — on 06/29 in Foxborough near Boston. It's the first-ever World Cup exit for a German team decided on penalties. The bitter part: in extra time, VAR ruled out a Jonathan Tah header (102') because Waldemar Anton had fouled the keeper beforehand.

Date/result follows the matching cluster of FIFA's match center, Sky Sports, and Al Jazeera (06/29, Paraguay won 4-3 on penalties). One German source cluster dated it differently, to 06/30 with a "4-5" score — we deliberately did not adopt that discrepancy.

skysports.com · aljazeera.com

Nagelsmann out — Klopp negotiating, but no signature yet

The DFB presidium parted ways with head coach Julian Nagelsmann on 07/03, terminating his contract that ran through 2028. Jürgen Klopp is seen as the preferred solution: according to Sky, there is a "basic agreement" on a contract through the 2030 World Cup — but nothing has been signed yet, explicitly.

The main obstacle: Klopp still has a contract with Red Bull as "Head of Global Soccer" through 2029. Only its termination would clear the path; talks between DFB leadership and Red Bull boss Oliver Mintzlaff are scheduled for the coming days. In short, it's a negotiation, not a done deal. Until there's a signature, "Klopp becomes head coach" officially remains open — even though most signs point that way.

Brazil was already out of this World Cup in the round of 16: 1-2 against Norway, with a brace from Erling Haaland, Neymar pulling one back late from the penalty spot — the weakest World Cup campaign since 1990. Nothing changes on the bench, though: Carlo Ancelotti had already extended his CBF contract through 2030 before the tournament and is staying on. It's Thursday, July 16th. Germany is long gone from the World Cup, the Bundesliga is on break — and yet today is a busy one. The biggest story in German football right now isn't happening on the pitch, but on the coaching bench.

The DFB parted ways with Julian Nagelsmann in early July, even though his contract ran through 2028. Jürgen Klopp is expected to take over. According to Sky, there's a basic agreement on a contract through the 2030 World Cup. But here's the catch: nothing is signed yet. Klopp still has a contract with Red Bull through 2029, and only ending that would clear the way. So this is a negotiation, not a done deal.

The World Cup exit itself still stings. Germany fell in the round of 32 to Paraguay, one-one after extra time, then three-four on penalties. It was the first World Cup exit for a German team ever decided on penalties. In extra time, VAR even wiped out a German goal. The match fell on June 29th, based on the matching accounts from FIFA, Sky Sports, and Al Jazeera.

Meanwhile in Freiburg, it's the most expensive summer in the club's history. Johan Manzambi is moving to Aston Villa for a base fee of around sixty million euros, with bonuses pushing it close to seventy. That's by far the most expensive sale SC has ever made — and, ironically, to the very club, Villa, that beat Freiburg three-nil in the Europa League final back in May.

The club is reinvesting that money right away. Mio Backhaus arrives in goal from Werder Bremen for around fifteen million, a club record for a purchase. Add to that Rihito Yamamoto in midfield and Japanese striker Keisuke Gotō up front. On the way out, there are rumors around Junior Adamu and goalkeeper Noah Atubolu, though neither is confirmed yet.

One detail that's easy to miss: despite reaching the Europa League final, Freiburg will only play in the Conference League playoff next season. A final doesn't earn a spot — only a title does — and seventh place in the league is only good enough for the smaller European door, and even that only after a qualifying match in August.
