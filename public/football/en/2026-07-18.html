World Cup 2026 — the final nobody wanted any different

Spain – Argentina, Sun. 19.07., 21:00 CEST / 16:00 BRT, MetLife Stadium

How both got to the final

Spain eliminated France 2-0 in Arlington near Dallas on Tuesday, 14.07. — Oyarzabal converted a penalty in the 22nd minute, Pedro Porro added a second in the 58th. That was no fluke win but the most mature Spanish performance of the tournament: possession as a weapon, not an end in itself.

Argentina had to suffer a lot more on Wednesday, 15.07. in Atlanta: against England, the Albiceleste trailed deep into the closing stages before Enzo Fernández equalized in the 86th minute and Lautaro Martínez headed home in the second minute of extra-time stoppage — 2-1 a.e.t. Messi assisted both goals. At 39, he's no longer the man who wins a game single-handedly; he's the man who decides it in the final ten minutes.

espn.com · en.wikipedia.org · sports.yahoo.com
a.e.t. and penalties: If a knockout match is level after 90 minutes, there are two 15-minute periods of extra time ("a.e.t." = after extra time). If the score is still tied after that, a penalty shootout decides it. Think of a board game that ends in a draw: first there's a short extra round, and if that still doesn't settle it, everyone rolls the dice five times. Argentina's 2-1 was settled in the extra round; Germany's exit on 29.06. came down to the dice.

My take: Spain is the favorite, but the wrong favorite

Spain has the better collective, the more controlled semifinal, and the calmer path here — a 2-0 win over France with no real scare is a statement. Argentina looked poor against England for 80 minutes and still won. That's exactly the problem for Spain: this Argentina side doesn't need a good match, it needs two good moments. If Spain takes the lead and takes the foot off the gas, Spain wins. If it's still 0-0 after 70 minutes, I'd back Argentina. For Daniel in Brazil, by the way, that comes with a nice side effect: an Argentine title will be topic number one here on Monday — and not a friendly one.

DFB — a shambles with a Klopp footnote

Out in the round of 32, coach gone, succession open

The exit against Paraguay, 29.06.

Germany was eliminated in the round of 32 against Paraguay on Monday, 29.06.2026: 1-1 a.e.t. (1-1, 0-1), 3-4 on penalties, played in Foxborough near Boston. That's doubly bitter — the earliest German World Cup exit since the 48-team format was introduced, and the first penalty shootout a German team has ever lost at a World Cup. The statistic German football had relied on for decades like a law of nature is gone.

de.wikipedia.org

Nagelsmann: contract terminated on 03.07.

On Friday, 03.07.2026, the DFB board terminated Julian Nagelsmann's contract, which had actually been set to run until 2028 — he himself had asked for it the evening before. That's the more honest version of a firing, and credit is due to the DFB for not dragging it out into a weeks-long saga.

de.wikipedia.org
Don't confuse this Jürgen Klopp is NOT the national team manager. As of 18.07.2026, following talks between DFB president Neuendorf and vice president Watzke with Klopp in New York, there is an agreement in principle on the key points — the goal is a contract running through the 2030 World Cup. What's still open is the decisive part: Klopp's contract as Head of Global Soccer at Red Bull runs until 2029, and a conversation with Red Bull CEO Mintzlaff was scheduled for early next week. Without that sign-off, nothing happens. Until there's a signature, this is a negotiation, not an appointment.

SC Freiburg — the most expensive departure in club history

Transfer window open until 31.08.2026 · Training camp in Schruns from 22.07.

Manzambi moves to Birmingham for a reported €60m

It became official on Friday, 17.07.2026: Johan Manzambi (20, Switzerland international) moves permanently to Aston Villa, contract until 30.06.2030. The fee has not been confirmed by the club; reported figures are a €60m base fee, with add-ons taking it up to just under €70m, and Newcastle is said to have also bid. If that's accurate, it's by far the biggest sale in Freiburg's history — and an Aston Villa club record too.

On the pitch, this is the classic Freiburg deal and still one that hurts. A 20-year-old the club developed itself is leaving at the peak of demand, not at the peak of his form. Economically it's irresistible; on the field, the club now lacks exactly the player Streich wanted to build the center of the team around. The next six weeks until 31.08. will decide whether the record sale becomes a record squad — or just a record bank balance.

bundesliga.com · sofascore.com · scfreiburg.com
Base fee and add-ons: Clubs rarely pay a transfer in one lump sum. There's a fixed amount due immediately (the base fee), plus extra payments that only kick in if something specific happens — 20 appearances, European qualification, a national-team call-up. It's like buying a car with a down payment plus installments, except the installments only come due if the car actually runs. That's why you read "€60m" and "just under €70m" side by side: one number is certain, the other is a hope.

Höfler retires, Yamamoto arrives

Nicolas Höfler is ending his professional career and joining Freiburg's second team — after more than a decade at the club, a departure that leaves a gap in the squad no single transfer can fully replace: the holding midfielder who keeps things organized when it gets chaotic.

As his replacement in central midfield, Rihito Yamamoto (24, Japan) arrived from Belgian top-flight side VV St. Truiden; per a kicker report, the fee is around €7m. In the past Jupiler Pro League season he was a regular starter at Sint-Truiden and scored five goals — the one figure that multiple stats portals agree on. Finer numbers (starts, assists) vary by a unit or two between sources and can't be reliably pinned down today without a single, cleanly dated stats provider. Overall, the deal fits the Freiburg profile: not a big name, but a bet on playing time and maturity.

It's Saturday, July eighteenth, and this is the biggest football day of the cycle. Tonight at eleven o'clock German time — six in the evening here in Brazil — France and England play for third place in Miami Gardens. And tomorrow, Sunday, at nine in the evening German time, four in the afternoon Brazilian time, a champion will be crowned at MetLife Stadium near New York: Spain against Argentina.

The two teams got there very differently. Spain beat France two-nil in Arlington near Dallas on Tuesday. Oyarzabal converted a penalty in the twenty-second minute, and Pedro Porro sealed it in the fifty-eighth. It was controlled, composed, without a real scare.

Argentina, on the other hand, had to sweat it out in Atlanta on Wednesday. Against England, they trailed deep into the match. Enzo Fernández didn't equalize until the eighty-sixth minute, and then Lautaro Martínez headed home the winner in the second minute of stoppage time in extra time — two-one. Messi set up both goals. At thirty-nine, he doesn't win games over ninety minutes anymore — he wins them over the last ten.

My take: Spain is the favorite, but the wrong favorite. This Argentina side doesn't need a good match — it needs two good moments. If Spain gets an early lead, Spain wins. If it's still scoreless after seventy minutes, I'd bet on Argentina.

From a German perspective, this tournament was a disaster. On June twenty-ninth, Germany went out in the round of thirty-two against Paraguay: one-one after extra time, then three-four on penalties. It was the first penalty shootout a German team has ever lost at a World Cup. On July third, Julian Nagelsmann's contract was terminated, at his own request.

One important thing that keeps getting reported wrong: Jürgen Klopp is not the national team manager. Following talks in New York, there's an agreement in principle on the key points, with a contract through the twenty-thirty World Cup as the goal. But Klopp is still under contract with Red Bull until twenty twenty-nine, and a conversation with Red Bull CEO Mintzlaff wasn't even scheduled until early next week. Until there's a signature, this is a negotiation, not an appointment.

Meanwhile at SC Freiburg, there was the biggest sale in club history. Johan Manzambi, twenty years old, is moving permanently to Aston Villa, contract until June thirtieth, twenty thirty. Reported figures are sixty million euros as a base fee, with bonuses pushing it toward just under seventy million. The club hasn't confirmed the amount. On top of that, Nicolas Höfler is retiring from professional football and joining the second team, and twenty-four-year-old Japanese midfielder Rihito Yamamoto has arrived from Belgium's St. Truiden. Freiburg heads to its training camp in Schruns on July twenty-second, with the first friendly on Friday against Derby County.

The Bundesliga itself is still asleep. Because of the World Cup, the season doesn't start until August twenty-eighth, with Bayern hosting Stuttgart at eight-thirty in the evening German time, three-thirty here. The table accordingly sits at zero matches and zero points for all eighteen clubs.
