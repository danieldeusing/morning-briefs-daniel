World Cup 2026 — Germany's short tournament

Out on June 29, coach gone July 3, successor still open today

A shootout against Paraguay, and that was it

It's the third straight World Cup that ends for Germany before the quarterfinals — except this time it happened a round earlier still. Kickoff was Mon, June 29 at 22:30 CEST; the penalty shootout didn't finish until the early hours of June 30. Julio Enciso headed Paraguay into the lead before halftime, Kai Havertz equalized in the 54th minute and dragged the match into extra time. Then the shootout: 3-4. Germany slipped from 9th to 12th in the FIFA rankings.[1]

Round of 32: At a World Cup with 48 teams instead of 32, so many teams advance that an extra round is needed before the round of 16 — 32 teams, 16 matches. Picture a tournament with so many kids signed up that an extra qualifying round is needed before the actual knockout matches begin. For Germany, that means the team didn't fail in the round of 16 — it failed one stage earlier.

My take: the penalty shootout is the convenient excuse. The real story is that a German team managed only one goal in 120 minutes against Paraguay, and needed that goal just to equalize after falling behind. A tournament exit on penalties can be bad luck — this one wasn't.

Nagelsmann leaves — and the DFB is in a hurry

On July 3, the DFB board terminated Julian Nagelsmann's contract, which ran through 2028, at his own request. That leaves the head coach position vacant in the middle of summer, with the next international break not until the window starting September 24.[2]

Rumor, not a report sportschau.de names Jürgen Klopp as the favorite for the succession, including "a fundamental willingness" — according to sportschau.de, as of July 20, unconfirmed. There is no confirmation from the DFB, no signature, no contract. Anyone reading that as settled today is reading a rumor.

What interests me more than the name: for the first time in years, the DFB is searching for a coach without a home tournament dictating the timeline. That's a chance to think long-term instead of hiring a figurehead. Whether the federation takes that chance will show in how long it takes.

[2] dfb.de · zdfheute.de

Spain take the title, Brazil crash out earlier than at any point since 1990

Last night's final at MetLife Stadium was decided by Ferran Torres in the 106th minute — 1-0 after extra time against Argentina. It's Spain's second World Cup title since 2010 and makes the country the first nation to hold the men's and women's world titles at the same time.[3]

For Daniel's adopted home, the tournament had already ended two weeks earlier: on Sun, July 5, Brazil lost 1-2 to Norway in the round of 16, also at MetLife Stadium. What stands out is how long it stayed 0-0 — 79 minutes. Erling Haaland headed Norway ahead in the 79th minute and doubled the lead in the 90th with a low long-range strike; both goals came in the final eleven minutes. Neymar's penalty in the 90th+10 was pure cosmetics. Bruno Guimarães had missed a penalty against goalkeeper Nyland early in the match. It's the weakest Seleção campaign since 1990.[4]

My take: this wasn't a collapse from the start — it was a match Brazil didn't lose for 79 minutes and then gave away in eleven. A team that keeps a game level that long and still loses wasn't unlucky — it lacked a goal of its own.

SC Freiburg — €70 million from all the wrong people

Transfer window open through August 31 · training camp in Schruns July 22–31

Manzambi joins the club that won the final in May

Johan Manzambi, Swiss international and one of this World Cup's breakout players, is moving to Aston Villa for £59.5m — roughly €70m including add-ons. For Villa, that's a club record, bigger than the roughly £50m paid for Amadou Onana. Newcastle were in the running with a £49m bid and got outbid.[5]

Add-ons: Part of a transfer fee isn't paid right away — only once something happens: appearance numbers, titles, international caps. It's like buying a used car where part of the price is only due once the car makes it two years without a repair-shop visit. The "€70 million" is therefore a ceiling, not the sum that lands in Freiburg's account tomorrow.

The irony is right there on the invoice: on Wed, May 20, Freiburg lost the Europa League final in Istanbul 0-3 to this very Aston Villa — Tielemans (41'), Buendía (45+3'), and Rogers (58') ended Villa's 30-year trophy drought.[6] Two months later, the same opponent buys Freiburg's best player.

My verdict: this is a good deal, and I say that as someone who would have liked to keep Manzambi. At a fee this size, a club like Freiburg doesn't say no — the only question is whether the money goes into squad depth or just sits on the balance sheet. Four incoming signings so far point to the former.

It's Monday, July twentieth. The World Cup wrapped up last night, and for your two teams, it was a short one. Germany is out, Brazil is out, and Spain are the new world champions.

Germany fell in the round of thirty-two to Paraguay. With this year's forty-eight-team format, that's the round just before the round of sixteen — an extra qualifying round because so many teams make it through. Kickoff was Monday, June twenty-ninth, at ten-thirty at night Central European Summer Time, and the shootout didn't wrap up until the early hours of the thirtieth. Paraguay went ahead on a header from Julio Enciso, Kai Havertz equalized in the fifty-fourth minute, and the shootout ended three to four. Germany dropped from ninth to twelfth in the FIFA rankings.

Four days later, Julian Nagelsmann was no longer the head coach. The federation tore up his contract, which ran through twenty twenty-eight, on July third, at his own request. There's still no successor. Sportschau names Jürgen Klopp as the favorite and reports a general willingness on his part — but that's a rumor, not a done deal. Nothing is signed. The federation has until the international break starting September twenty-fourth to figure it out.

Last night's final at MetLife Stadium was settled by Ferran Torres in the hundred-and-sixth minute — one-nil after extra time over Argentina. It's Spain's second World Cup title since twenty ten, and it makes them the first nation ever to hold both the men's and women's world titles at the same time. Brazil, meanwhile, had already been home for two weeks: on Sunday, July fifth, the Seleção lost one-two to Norway in the round of sixteen. The match stayed scoreless for seventy-nine minutes. Then Erling Haaland headed Norway ahead in the seventy-ninth minute and struck again in the ninetieth — both goals in the final eleven minutes. Neymar's penalty deep into stoppage time was just window dressing. This wasn't an early collapse — it was a match Brazil held level almost the whole way and then gave away in the final stretch. It's Brazil's worst World Cup showing since nineteen ninety.

In Freiburg, all of that is overshadowed by a transfer. Johan Manzambi, one of this tournament's breakout players, is moving to Aston Villa for fifty-nine point five million pounds. That's roughly seventy million euros with bonuses included, and it's a club record for Villa. Newcastle bid forty-nine million pounds and got outbid.

Here's the twist: it's the same club that knocked Freiburg out of the Europa League final in Istanbul on May twentieth, three-nil. First the trophy, now the best player. I still think it's the right deal — at that price, a club like Freiburg doesn't say no. What matters is whether the money goes into squad depth.

On the new signings, I'm being careful. Goalkeeper Mio Backhaus, Rihito Yamamoto, and Keisuke Goto are in, but each of those reported fees comes from a single outlet and hasn't been cross-checked. What is confirmed is the return of Yannik Engelhardt from Como. On Philipp Lienhart, there's only speculation — no club, no fee.

The calendar ahead: from Wednesday through July thirty-first, Freiburg trains in Schruns, in Austria's Vorarlberg region. On Friday, July twenty-fourth, they play a friendly against Derby County — four in the afternoon Central European Summer Time, eleven in the morning your time, in Imst, in Tirol. The next competitive match is the first DFB Cup round away at Fortuna Düsseldorf on Sunday, August twenty-third, at six in the evening Central European Summer Time, one in the afternoon your time, at the Merkur Spiel-Arena. The Bundesliga itself doesn't start until Friday, August twenty-eighth, with Bayern hosting Stuttgart at eight-thirty in the evening Central European Summer Time, three-thirty your time. Freiburg finished last season seventh, with forty-seven points, and will play the Conference League playoff.

In Brazil, nothing happens today. Round nineteen is stretched across the whole week because the weekend was cleared for the World Cup final. The next match is Atlético Mineiro against Bahia on Tuesday, with no confirmed kickoff time yet.
