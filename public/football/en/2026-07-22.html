SC Freiburg — record sale, new squad, camp starts today

Transfer window open (May–Aug) · fees per media reports, transfermarkt.de and kicker.de unavailable today (403)

Manzambi brings in the record fee

The sale of Johan Manzambi to Aston Villa is official (announced Fri, Jul 17, 2026). The club itself has not named a fee — media reports range from around €60m (SofaScore) to €70m (OneFootball). Whatever the final figure, it's the biggest sale in club history.[1] Manzambi made 58 competitive appearances for Freiburg (9 goals, 11 assists) and was voted the Europa League's best young player last season. A loss that stings on the pitch — but frees up money that gives the squad planning some breathing room.

Heads up The €60–70m range is a media estimate, not a club figure. Until Freiburg or Villa confirm a number, it stands as "per reports."
bundesliga.com · sofascore.com · onefootball.com

New faces, new backbone

Value for the sale proceeds is already coming in: according to reports, Mio Backhaus (from Werder Bremen, ~€15m, up to €18m with add-ons — reportedly Freiburg's new club transfer record, surpassing Ritsu Doan's ~€10.4m) takes over in goal as the new number one, joined by Rihito Yamamoto from St. Truiden (~€7m) for midfield. The returnee is Yannick Engelhardt, whom Freiburg is buying back from Como for around €7.5m — he spent the 2025/26 season on loan at Borussia Mönchengladbach.[2] The figures are media estimates; the primary sources (transfermarkt, kicker) were unreachable today, which is why we deliberately flag them as "per reports."

Bundesliga — summer break, 2025/26 final table

Season ended May 16, 2026 · final table source: de.wikipedia.org (Bayern figures cross-checked against ad-hoc-news.de; kicker.de/bundesliga.com 403 today)

Champions once again are FC Bayern — with 89 points and a goal difference of +86, they'd already wrapped up the title on Apr 19, four matchdays before the end. At the other end, VfL Wolfsburg, finishing 16th, went down after losing the relegation playoff against SC Paderborn (0-0, 1-2 AET), while Heidenheim and St. Pauli were relegated directly. Freiburg finished the season in 7th place — enough for the Conference League playoff, but a mixed year with a negative goal difference.

Conference League playoff: Freiburg isn't in Europe yet — they still have to qualify for the Conference League's league phase (Europe's third-tier club competition) over a two-legged tie. Think of it like a make-up exam for the European ticket: pass it, and the continental season continues.

DFB & World Cup — Klopp on the verge of signing, Spain are world champions

2026 World Cup concluded · Germany–Paraguay match date per FIFA/ESPN (Mon, Jun 29); dfb.de/ZDF date it Jun 30 (German calendar day after midnight)

The coaching job: Klopp has "basically agreed" — but hasn't signed

After the World Cup exit, the DFB terminated Julian Nagelsmann's contract on Jul 3 — at his own request, following the disappointing tournament run.[6] The search for a successor has been on ever since, and all signs point to Jürgen Klopp: Tagesspiegel and Sky report a basic agreement has been reached, with a contract through the 2030 World Cup reportedly under discussion and the signature said to be planned for Friday. Klopp himself is quoted as saying, "Nothing is final, but the talks have been going well and everything is moving in the right direction."[7]

As of Jul 22 This is a well-founded rumor, not a confirmed report. Until the DFB confirms it officially, Klopp remains a candidate, not the head coach.
dfb.de · tagesspiegel.de

The exit: undone at the penalty spot

Germany fell to Paraguay in the round of 32: 1-1 after extra time (Enciso 42', Havertz 54'), then 3-4 on penalties — on Monday, Jun 29, in Foxborough.[8] It was, according to Al Jazeera, Germany's first-ever World Cup defeat in a penalty shootout — and the exit already in the round of 32 is considered one of the tournament's biggest upsets.

Round of 32: Because the 2026 World Cup was played with 48 teams instead of 32 for the first time, there's one more knockout round than before — the round of 32 ahead of the round of 16. Think of it as an extra first step on the staircase: Germany stumbled right on it.
zdfheute.de · espn.com · aljazeera.com

World champions Spain

Spain claimed the title: 1-0 after extra time against Argentina, with substitute Ferran Torres scoring the decisive goal in the 106th minute. The final took place on Sunday, Jul 19, at 21:00 CEST / 16:00 BRT at MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford, New Jersey. It's Spain's second World Cup title since 2010.[9]

It's Wednesday, July twenty-second. For SC Freiburg, today marks the start of the training camp in Schruns, Austria — and the squad taking the field there looks very different from the one that finished the season back in May.

At the center of it all is a record sale: Johan Manzambi has moved to Aston Villa. The club hasn't confirmed the fee itself, but media reports put it somewhere between sixty and seventy million euros — either way, the biggest sale in club history. In return, reports say the club is bringing in goalkeeper Mio Backhaus from Bremen, Rihito Yamamoto from Belgium, and returning player Yannick Engelhardt from Como. Those figures are media estimates, not confirmed.

Freiburg stays in the Montafon through the end of July. Two friendlies are set: on Friday against English second-division side Derby County, at four in the afternoon our time, eleven in the morning in Brazil, and on the thirty-first against Greuther Fürth. After that come Racing Strasbourg and Crystal Palace, both at home.

At the German Football Association, everything revolves around the coaching job. Julian Nagelsmann left the federation after the World Cup exit, with his contract terminated in early July. Jürgen Klopp is seen as the successor: Tagesspiegel and Sky report a basic agreement has been reached, with the signature reportedly planned for Friday. But a word of caution — nothing is confirmed. Klopp himself says nothing is final yet. Until the DFB makes it official, he remains a candidate, not the head coach.

The World Cup itself has been decided. Germany was knocked out as early as the round of thirty-two, undone at the penalty spot against Paraguay, losing three to four after a one-one draw in extra time. According to Al Jazeera, it was Germany's first-ever World Cup loss in a penalty shootout. The new world champion is Spain: a one-nil win over Argentina after extra time, with the decisive goal coming from substitute Ferran Torres in the hundred-and-sixth minute. It's Spain's second title since two thousand ten.

The Bundesliga is deep in its summer break. Champions of the season just ended are once again Bayern, with eighty-nine points, while Freiburg finished seventh and now has to fight through the playoff for a Conference League spot. The new season kicks off on August twenty-eighth with Bayern against Stuttgart, and before that comes the first round of the cup.

In Brazil, Série A is back. Tonight Coritiba takes on league leaders Palmeiras, at half past midnight our time, half past seven in the evening in Brazil — the first competitive match for both since the World Cup break. Palmeiras leads clearly with forty-one points. A note on the standings: positions sixteen through twenty conflict between sources today, so we're deliberately not presenting them as confirmed.

Tomorrow will likely be all about Freiburg's preseason — and whether the Klopp rumor turns into a real, confirmed story by Friday.
