The market: from seller's to buyer's market

The Freelancer-Kompass 2026 (annual freelancermap study, this year n=5,412 DACH self-employed, ~60 questions) delivers the harshest market picture in years. The average hourly rate dipped slightly to 103 €/h (from 104 € in 2025) — nominal stagnation that is in real terms a real-wage loss. More dramatic is monthly income: −21 % from 8,432 € (2025) to 6,653 € (2026).[1]

The reason is utilization, not rate: 43 % of freelancers have no secured project pipeline, and although weekly hours rose from 40 to 42, revenues are falling — more work for less money.[1][2] Price expectations are correspondingly defensive: 62 % plan no rate increase for 2026, 9 % want to actively cut. The study itself calls it the flip "from seller's to buyer's market."[2]

Buyer's market: When more freelancers are chasing projects than there are projects available, the client holds the stronger hand — like a farmers' market just before closing time, where the buyer pushes the price down because the vendor needs to move inventory. For Daniel that means: rate negotiation in 2026 is about defending the band, not adding a markup.

It's Saturday, June 20th. The job board is well stocked today — ninety-six listings, with the highest match score at eighty-eight out of a hundred. But the market underneath those numbers is cool, and that's the real story.

The Freelancer-Kompass 2026, a survey of more than five thousand four hundred self-employed workers across the DACH region, paints the harshest picture in years. The average hourly rate sits at one hundred and three euros, barely moved from last year. But monthly income has fallen twenty-one percent — from a little over eight thousand to six thousand six hundred and fifty-three euros. Forty-three percent of freelancers have no secured pipeline, and even though people are working more hours, revenues are falling. The study itself calls it the flip from a seller's to a buyer's market.

For Daniel, that means: the dense wall of Java and Spring listings on the board is real — sixty listings carry that stack — but rate negotiation this year is about defense, not pushing for a premium. Where a markup can still stick is in the niche. Today it shows up twice: a smart-home project with Z-Wave and MQTT, and an automotive role with CAN-Bus, both at one hundred ten to one hundred forty euros per hour. Exactly Daniel's rare, high-paying segment.

At the top of the board sits Aleph Alpha, the Heidelberg AI lab, with a senior Java role for an industrial IoT platform — stack match and mission appeal in one listing. In the Intriguing tier, JetBrains dominates with ten AI-native developer roles, joined by Anthropic, Mistral, and Helsing.
