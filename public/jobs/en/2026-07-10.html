Market signal: the freelance rate falls for the first time [1][2][3]

The Freelancer Compass 2026 from freelancermap — the largest annual DACH freelancer survey — reports an average hourly rate of €103 for 2026. That sounds unremarkable, but it's a turning point: it's the first decline since the survey began; the prior-year average was still €104.[1] More dramatic is the income side: average monthly income fell from €8,432 to €6,653 — a drop of roughly 21%.[1] The survey ran in four waves between 11/17/2025 and 02/08/2026 with a base of 5,412 self-employed respondents — it's not a real-time figure, but it's the most solid annual reference available for this market segment.

Hourly rate vs. income: The hourly rate is the price per hour of work; monthly income is what actually comes in at the end of the month. That the rate falls only about one percent while income falls 21% means: it's not the price per hour that's collapsing, but utilization — there are simply fewer paid hours. Like a taxi driver whose per-kilometer fare stays almost the same, but who gets far fewer rides.

That's exactly what Daniel's board reflects: the volume is there (49 listings, 36 of them freelance), prices sit within the band — but the market is visibly squeezing. As a backdrop, German industry is cutting broadly. Volkswagen, per personalwirtschaft.de, is discussing a restructuring course involving up to 100,000 positions worldwide and four affected plants;[2] Porsche, per finanzen.net (as of 07/06), is said to be cutting up to 4,000 additional positions, with a future package expected by the end of July.[3] These figures are aggregated press reports, not primarily confirmed individual numbers — they set the mood, they aren't the story.

Translated for today's board: enterprise Java/Spring remains the most in-demand segment and availability stays consistently high — but the lever is clearly selection and utilization, not the markup. Where Daniel can still command a premium are the rare corners: automotive safety, telematics, IoT, and — on the employee side — the Swiss agentic-AI roles.

Minimum annual revenue & reference gate: Public contracting authorities often require, as proof of "economic capacity," a minimum annual revenue (typically double the contract value) and several comparable, already-delivered reference projects.
