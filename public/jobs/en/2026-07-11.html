Market signal: two surveys agree on ~€90–102/h [1][2][3][4]

For the rate band, there are two independent pieces of evidence this year that say practically the same thing. The Freelancer Compass 2026 from freelancermap reports a median of €95/h for IT freelancers in the DACH region; by specialization, Software & Web Development at €90/h is the lowest segment, SAP/ERP at €120/h the highest.[1] The Freelancer Study 2026 from freelance.de, drawing on a separate survey, arrives at a mean of €101.98/h IT-wide, with a clear experience gradient (20+ years ≈ €107/h, under 5 years ≈ €83/h).[2]

Median vs. mean: The median is the value exactly in the middle — one half earns more, the other less. The mean (average) adds up all the rates and divides by the count, which is why a few very high rates pull it upward. That the mean (€102) sits above the median (€95) is therefore normal and not a contradiction — it just means the top earners stretch the field upward. For Daniel's Java/Spring core, the reliable expectation is the band ~€90–102/h.

That's exactly what the board reflects: the 60 freelance listings come in, researched, at €85–130/h*, the standard Java/Spring core tightly around €90–120/h*. As a macroeconomic backdrop, the Federal Employment Agency (Bundesagentur für Arbeit) holds the unemployment rate in June at 6.2% (2.936M, −15,000 versus May),[3] and the broad hiring freeze continues to concentrate in auto, machinery, chemicals, and bank back-office; software/IT shows a mixed picture.[4] Deliberately not cited: the "SAP cuts X jobs in 2026" figure in circulation — the source behind it is a 2023 SAP restructuring, not a current number.

Minimum annual revenue & reference gate: Public contracting authorities often require, as proof of "economic and financial standing," a minimum annual revenue (typically around twice the contract value) and several comparable, already-delivered reference projects in the matching sector. This is rarely a competence problem but a size problem: a solo GmbH like twiceD has the stack, but not necessarily the documented revenue or the public-sector reference — exactly the gap a partner or bidding consortium fills.

It's Saturday, July eleventh. Today's real story isn't sitting on Daniel's job board — it's buried in the European public tender database. Five fresh, technically relevant contracts from German public agencies and hospitals have surfaced, with deadlines stretching from July twentieth to August third. Here's the twist: not one of them can be bid on by twiceD alone. Every single one carries at least one mandatory requirement that sits above what a solo shop can show.

The clearest case is a warehouse-logistics digitization project at the GSI Helmholtz Center for heavy-ion research and the FAIR accelerator project, with a deadline of July twenty-third. The actual core of the work — a paperless workflow application using text recognition for incoming goods — would be well within twiceD's reach to build. But the system is required to be fully integrated into the SAP S slash four HANA enterprise platform, or to talk to SAP's asset module through a certified interface. That's not a size problem, it's a genuine gap in skills and certification. And to make it tougher than the rest, the ISO twenty-seven-thousand-one information-security certification has to be in hand at bid time, not just by contract award.

The remaining four are either size problems or product problems. The Cologne municipal hospitals require a minimum annual revenue of one million euros net for their software-controlling project — twiceD sits at roughly half a million. At least a bidding consortium is explicitly allowed, so twiceD could ride along as a partner. Germany's Federal Ministry of Justice is looking for developers for the German Patent and Trademark Office's specialist systems, but depending on the lot, it wants an average revenue of between two and a half and four and a half million euros — realistically, twiceD could only show up here as a subcontractor to a large prime contractor.

Two more sit in the gray zone. The district of Schwaben wants ready-made software-as-a-service licenses for ninety-three social-psychiatric services — that's a product purchase, not a development contract, and it demands two references with at least a hundred fifty licenses each in the social sector. And SBK is looking for a treasury management system to replace an Excel-based setup. On paper, this one has the lowest bar, since the structured field only showed the standard exclusion-grounds declaration. But not finding something isn't the same as it not existing — the full criteria are probably sitting in procurement documents that weren't pulled here, so this one stays explicitly flagged with a caveat.
