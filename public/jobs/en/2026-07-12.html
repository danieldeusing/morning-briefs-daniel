Market signal: first rate decline, and the AI premium shifts [1][2][3][4][5]

The Freelancer-Kompass 2026 from freelancermap (n ≈ 5,400, survey period 11/17/2025–02/08/2026) reports the most notable data point of the year: the average hourly rate has declined for the first time ever — to €103/h, down from €104 last year.[2] By specialization, software & web development is lowest at €90/h (it's the largest and most heterogeneous group), with consulting & management at €121/h — nearly 20% above average — the highest.[1][3]

The study's second claim is the more relevant one for Daniel. The plain "I use AI tools" premium is dead: 53% of all freelancers use AI tools daily, another 23% weekly — mere tool use is now baseline, no longer a differentiator. The market still pays a real premium (20–30%) only for specialized AI-infrastructure expertise: ML in production, vector databases, cloud architecture, compliance-ready setups.[3]

AI-tool use vs. AI infrastructure: A tradesperson who uses a power drill instead of a screwdriver doesn't charge a premium for it anymore — the tool has become standard. What gets paid is whoever builds the workshop: RAG pipelines, vector databases, running models in production. That's exactly the line the study draws with "20–30% only for specialized AI-infra knowledge" — not for "I use ChatGPT." For twiceD, that's the direction the premium is shifting toward, away from plain coding-tool use.

The demand trend fits: the number of AI project postings in the DACH freelance market rose more than 530% between 2023 and 2025,[4] while overall IT project volume, per the same analysis, fell by roughly 23%. As a macroeconomic backdrop, SAP (as of 07/06) imposed an almost company-wide hiring freeze across ~110,000 employees, exempting only "business-critical AI roles"; CEO Christian Klein is signaling a "very, very different" workforce within 2–3 years.[5]

Public tenders (TED EU)

Today, a single stack-adjacent candidate, presented as a case study for the partner gap — not as an immediately actionable deadline. The contracting authority is public and the subject matter matches Daniel's core stack exactly, but one of the eligibility requirements sits far above solo/two-person capacity. What a partner would need to bring is spelled out below.

Reference threshold with bidder share: Public contracting authorities often require, as proof of "technical capability," a previously delivered reference project of a certain minimum size — here, a budget of at least €3.2M. The additional condition "≥ 80% bidder share" means the bidder itself must have delivered the lion's share of that reference project, not merely taken part as a minor subcontractor. That's rarely a capability problem — it's a scale problem: a solo GmbH like twiceD has the Java stack, but no government project of this size in its history with a majority stake of its own — exactly the gap a partner or bidding consortium fills.

It's Sunday, July twelfth. Today's real story isn't on the job board — it's in two fresh studies and a single government tender out of Bavaria.

For the first time ever, the average freelance hourly rate across the German-speaking region has fallen. The Freelancer-Kompass twenty twenty-six puts the average at one hundred three euros an hour, down one euro from last year. Software and web development sits at the low end at ninety euros — exactly the segment where Daniel's Java and Spring work lives.

The study's second finding is the more important one. The plain premium for simply using AI tools is dead. Fifty-three percent of freelancers now use them daily — that's become standard, not an edge. A real premium of twenty to thirty percent still gets paid, but only for specialized infrastructure knowledge: running models in production, vector databases, cloud architecture. Not for opening ChatGPT.

Against that backdrop, the software giant SAP is freezing hiring almost company-wide. Roughly one hundred ten thousand jobs are under a freeze, with only business-critical AI roles exempt. At the same time, the number of AI project postings jumped more than five hundred thirty percent between twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-five. Demand is shifting, not disappearing.

The board itself is calm and continuous. Ninety-five listings, seventy-four carried over unchanged from yesterday and twenty-one new. The same three-way tie as on prior days shares the top score of eighty-eight points: iSAX, BridgingIT, and Keil, all Java, Spring, and Angular, all strongly remote. The average fit score sits at sixty-eight.

Among the new arrivals, telematics infrastructure leads — two Java healthcare roles from VARIUS and TRIBAR, both scoring eighty. The niche of IoT, automotive, and building automation holds steady, led by an automotive role at HENNING in Ingolstadt scoring eighty-six. On the employee side, today's clear contrast shows up: where agentic AI is in demand — at ELCA, Camunda, and AXA in Switzerland — the premium holds. In the broad Java freelance stream, it's gone.

There's also a single EU tender, presented as a case study. The Bavarian State Tax Office is looking for Java development and a web frontend for identity and access management — a perfect stack match. But eligibility requires a reference project worth at least three point two million euros, with the bidder itself accounting for at least eighty percent of it.
