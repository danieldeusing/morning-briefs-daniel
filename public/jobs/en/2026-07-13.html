Market Signal: The Skilled-Worker Paradox and Three Freezes [3][4][5][6][7]

Today's data point is a contradiction. Bitkom still counts around 109,000 unfilled IT-skilled-worker positions in the German economy — the figure comes from the August 2025 study and remains the most recently published one to date (no newer Bitkom figure for 2026). At the same time, an analysis from 07/09/2026 reports that the number of IT job postings has fallen 22% year-over-year — the source itself explicitly calls it a "paradox."

Why this isn't a contradiction: Two different things are being measured. The 109,000 is the structural need — positions companies would love to fill because the skilled workers simply aren't there. The −22% is the number of actively posted reqs — and that's shrinking because companies freeze hiring during shaky economic conditions instead of posting new roles. Picture a company that needs ten people but, given an uncertain order book, only posts three openings: the gap stays at seven, the visible listings drop to three. For a freelancer, that means the need is there, but it shows up less often as an open posting in the shop window — network and existing clients beat the job board.

In Daniel's segment, that plays out through three freezes. IKEA is shutting down its IT Germany GmbH entirely — 233 jobs by end of 2026, 170 of them at IKEA Purchasing Services Germany.[5] BASF is planning a "massive workforce reduction" in its ~8,500-strong IT department, has raised its savings target to €2.3B, and is building a Digital Hub in Hyderabad, while Global Digital Services is also being cut in Ludwigshafen through 2030 (the exact cut number isn't published — only the direction and department size are documented).[6] As a counterexample: Siemens is reorganizing under "One Tech Company" — 20,000 of 87,000 German employees affected, but explicitly without planned layoffs and with investment in retraining rather than cuts; not a freeze, but a restructuring.

On the rate side, the floor holds: senior Java backend runs, per the rate studies, at €90–120/h (€115–130/h in regulated industries like banking/insurance),[1] and the IT freelancer average sits at €101.98/h versus €100.49 outside IT.[2] So the premium isn't shifting upward — it's shifting toward rare skills, agentic AI, cloud architecture — while the broad standard stream stays tight within the band.

Project Tenders (TED EU)

Two stack-adjacent candidates today — and they form a clean contrast pair for the partner-gap analysis. In one, the gaps are fillable through a partner (certifications, references, a named lead); in the other, the eligibility thresholds are so high that any realistic partner model fails — the difference between ⚠️ Partner and ✗ Skip.

Maximum-care hospital, HIS & ISO trio: A "maximum-care hospital" (Krankenhaus der Maximalversorgung) is the highest level of care — a university hospital or large central hospital with every specialty department. The contracting authority requires proof that the bidder has already delivered a comparable project there, including integration with the HIS (hospital information system — the central software that manages patient data, admissions, and discharges). On top of that, three ISO certifications: 27001 (information security), 9001 (quality management), 14001 (environmental). That's rarely a capability problem — twiceD can build the cloud/AI platform — it's a documentation-and-scale problem: a solo GmbH doesn't have the hospital references and the three certifications; a partner brings them.
