Market Signal: Freezes at the Corporates — Not in the Freelance Market [1][3][4][5][6]

The most important mistake to avoid today is an equivalence. The freeze headlines are real: SAP imposed a nearly company-wide hiring freeze on 07/06 — only "business-critical AI roles" are exempt, and the savings flow into AI investment.[3] VW's supervisory board discussed up to 100,000 jobs worldwide (~16% of roughly 630,000 employees) and four German plants on 07/09 — with no immediate decision.[4] Bosch is cutting roughly 22,000 German jobs in its mobility division, ~6,000 already gone (as of February 2026).[5]

Why this isn't Daniel's market: A hiring freeze at SAP means: SAP posts fewer permanent positions. A plant cut at VW or Bosch means: fewer factory/automotive jobs. Both affect large employers and the auto industry. The board here is something else: 54 freelance projects, posted by SMEs, consultancies, and staffing agencies (iSAX, BridgingIT, Keil, teclift …) looking for a Java/Angular developer for a concrete 6–48-month engagement. When a corporation freezes internally, demand often even shifts into the external project market — a freeze at SAP is not a demand signal against a freelance Spring Boot developer at a mid-sized firm. That's why the board stays full while the corporate floor cuts.

On the rate side, the floor holds, but low: the Freelancer Compass 2026 (published 07/02) puts software/web development at €90/h as the lowest specialization, below the DACH IT median of €95/h and well below SAP/ERP (€120/h) and data & analytics (€100/h).[1] Daniel's standard Java/Spring core thus sits, by research, within the band, not above it — the premium sits with the rare skills (agentic AI, cloud architecture), not in the volume stream.

Deliberately not carried as a signal: the often-cited 109,000 open IT positions come from the Bitkom study from August 2025 — eleven months old, no 2026 refresh found.[6] A structural gap from a year ago says nothing about today's project demand; it appears here only as a completeness footnote, not a day's finding.

Project Tenders (TED EU)

Two stack-adjacent tenders today — and they form a clean contrast pair for the partner-gap analysis. In one, twiceD would be the technical bidder and the partner fills point gaps (certifications, references, a named lead). In the other, the eligibility thresholds are the metrics of a mid-sized company — poolable via a bidding consortium, but only in a way where twiceD rides along as a junior consortium partner rather than leading. Both are ⚠️ Partner, but they're two different partner models.

Maximum-care hospital, HIS & the ISO trio: A "maximum-care hospital" (Krankenhaus der Maximalversorgung) is the highest level of care — a university hospital or large central hospital with every specialty department. The contracting authority requires proof that the bidder has already delivered a comparable project there, including integration with the HIS (hospital information system — the central software for patient data, admissions, and discharges). On top of that, three ISO certifications: 27001 (information security), 9001 (quality management), 14001 (environmental). Rarely a capability problem — twiceD can build the cloud/AI platform — but a documentation-and-scale problem: a solo GmbH doesn't have the hospital references and the three certifications; a partner brings them.
Bidding consortium, "specific revenue" & FTE: A bidding consortium (Bietergemeinschaft) is a group of multiple companies submitting a joint bid — and the contracting authority allows their figures to be added together (pooled). "Specific revenue" means: not total revenue counts, only the revenue in the tender's field (here: AI software). FTE stands for full-time equivalents of permanently employed staff. Why this makes the difference: if a tender requires ≥ €5M AI revenue per year and 100 AI developers, a bidding consortium can pool that — but whoever contributes 100 FTE is itself a mid-sized company. twiceD (a solo/two-person GmbH) can't reach either threshold on its own; it can only ride along, contributing its share (applied AI, LLM integration, EU AI Act analysis) to a consortium led by a qualifying partner.
