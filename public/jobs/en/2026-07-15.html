New for the BR remote setup: KIRA and the 2026 sham self-employment audit

A signal that this time comes not from the board but from the compliance side — and touches Daniel's specific setup (German client, contractor resident in Brazil) directly. Germany's statutory pension insurer has been rolling out an AI-assisted audit tool called KIRA since early 2025; nationwide deployment is reportedly planned for 2026. It scans wage and reporting data filed since January 2023 for patterns of sham self-employment — and the audit approach also applies (per this single source) to contractors resident abroad, when the client is a German company.[5]

Sham self-employment & KIRA: "Sham self-employment" (Scheinselbstständigkeit) means: someone bills as a freelancer but in practice works like an employee — fully embedded in a client's operation, subject to instructions, usually with just one client. Picture someone who wears no company uniform and writes their own invoices, yet sits at the same desk every day as the permanent staff and does exactly their work — the state then says: this is in truth a hidden employment relationship, and social-security contributions are missing. KIRA is the pension insurer's new AI tool that automatically scans reporting data for such patterns. For twiceD, the GmbH structure itself (a real company, multiple clients, its own equipment) is the protective wall — but the foreign-residence detail means: "I'm based in Brazil" is not an automatic exemption from the audit if the client is German.

In parallel, the EU Platform Work Directive (in force since 01.12.2024) must be transposed into German national law by 02.12.2026 — it tends to shift the burden of proof toward platforms/clients. This isn't a board signal and no reason to panic: twiceD bids through its own GmbH with multiple project clients, which is structurally the right side to be on. It's the framework worth knowing in 2026 before a German client raises the question — not after. Note: the source's byline date wasn't visible at retrieval; the facts cited (KIRA rollout 2026, directive deadline 02.12.2026) are each explicitly tied to those dates. The foreign-residence scope rests on thenetworkschool.de alone and is not confirmed on the official DRV pages.

Market signal: corporate freezes — not the freelance market [1][3][4][6]

The main thinking error to avoid today is an equivalence. The restructuring headlines are real, but at the corporate level: VW's supervisory board, at its meeting on 07/09, rejected CEO Blume's plan to close four German plants (Emden, Hanover, Zwickau, Audi Neckarsulm) as part of the up-to-100,000-jobs initiative — no plant closure was decided; the next formal item is the half-year report on 07/23.[3] At SAP, the restructuring originally around ~8,000 positions is reported to have been widened to 9,000–10,000 — though this is only synthesized secondhand from search snippets, without an individually opened primary source, and is therefore deliberately kept soft here as "reportedly," not a hard figure.[4]

Why this isn't Daniel's market: Job cuts at VW or SAP affect permanent employment and factory/corporate jobs. The board here is something different: 60 freelance projects posted by SMEs, consultancies, and staffing agencies (iSAX, BridgingIT, Keil, teclift …) looking for a Java/Angular developer on a temporary basis for a specific 6–48-month effort. When a corporation cuts internally, demand often even shifts into the external project market. A cut at SAP is therefore not a negative demand signal for a freelance Spring Boot developer at a mid-sized company — the floors are separate.

Deliberately not treated as a signal: the often-cited 109,000 open IT positions come from the Bitkom study from August 2025 (repeated verbatim in the January 2026 PDF) — roughly 11 months old, no fresher 2026 figure found.[6] A structural gap from nearly a year ago says nothing about today's project demand; it's included here only as a completeness footnote, not as today's finding.

Project tenders (TED EU)

Three stack-adjacent tenders today — and they cover the whole spectrum of the partner-gap analysis. The third is the exception that hasn't happened in days — a notice whose visible eligibility hurdle is so low that twiceD could bid on it alone. But on this third one specifically, I'm staying cautious: the TED API returned only a single eligibility clause for it, and that could be truncation. A possible ✅ direct fit — not yet confirmed.

Eligibility criteria & why a solo GmbH rarely meets them alone: In public tenders, the bidder must formally prove it can handle the contract — not just professionally, but as a business. Typical proof points: a minimum annual revenue (shows the company is financially viable), a minimum headcount in full-time equivalents (FTE — shows enough people are available), reference projects (has delivered something comparable before, often for public clients), professional liability insurance above a minimum sum, and sometimes certificates like ISO 27001 (information security). twiceD is a real GmbH, but at solo/two-person scale: it can deliver the technical build, but doesn't reach "≥ 10 permanent full-time staff" or "≥ €1.5M annual revenue" on its own. That's why a matching contract turns into a ⚠️ partner case: a bigger partner contributes the revenue proof, FTE count, references, or certificate, twiceD the technical share.
