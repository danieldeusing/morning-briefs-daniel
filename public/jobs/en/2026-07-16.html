Market signal: rate turns, employment cools — but which market? [1][2][3][4]

For the first time in years, the rate trend points down. The Freelancer-Kompass 2026 (freelancermap, n=1,310) puts the average DACH hourly rate at €103 — down from €104 in 2025, and the first decline in an otherwise unbroken run: €96 (2022) → €100 (2023) → €102 (2024) → €104 (2025) → €103 (2026). freelancermap itself cites "tougher market conditions" as the reason; the monthly average from project work sits at €6,653 (median €5,000).[1] In parallel, the ifo employment barometer fell to 92.3 points in June (May: 93.9) — companies are planning layoffs more than hiring.[2] And Bosch confirms the automotive headwind: roughly 22,000 DE jobs by 2030, 6,000 of them already cut in 2025.[3]

ifo employment barometer: Every month, the ifo Institute asks thousands of companies whether they plan to hire more or fewer people over the coming months, and compresses the answers into a single number. Think of it as a fever thermometer for the economy's hiring mood: 100 is the neutral baseline — above it, companies plan to hire on balance; below it, they plan to cut. 92.3 means the majority is planning cuts, not growth — a cool market, not a feverish one.

The key mistake would be to conflate all this. A falling all-freelancer average, a weak employment barometer, and a Bosch cutback all sit at the level of permanent employment, the average across all freelancer types, and the automotive corporate tier. The board here is something else: 76 freelance projects, issued by SMEs, consultancies, and staffing agencies (iSAX, BridgingIT, Keil, teclift …) looking for a senior Java/Angular developer for a specific 3–48-month engagement — in the upper IT band, which the survey itself prices above the overall average. When a corporation cuts internally, demand often shifts into the external project market instead. The tiers are separate; the cooling signal is context, not a board alarm.

Project tenders (TED EU)

Two stack-relevant tenders today plus one borderline case — and all three are partner cases: the stack fits (weakly, for the third one), but staffing, reference, or revenue thresholds exceed twiceD's solo capacity. There's no possible solo bid today. Where the TED API only returned the eligibility threshold as a category without a figure, it's explicitly marked below as unconfirmed/presumed — no invented numbers; the exact values sit in the eForm PDF, which wasn't opened for this triage.

CPV, person-days (PD) & eligibility criteria: A CPV code is the EU's shopping-basket key for a tender — 72* stands for IT services, and that's what we filter by. A person-day (PD) is one working day for one person; "≥ 200 PD/year" means the reference project delivered at least 200 person-days per year — roughly a role staffed by one full-time person on an ongoing basis. And eligibility criteria are the formal proof that a bidder can handle the contract as a company: minimum annual revenue, minimum headcount (FTE), reference projects (often for public clients), professional liability insurance, sometimes certificates like ISO 27001. twiceD is a real GmbH, but at solo/two-person scale — it can deliver the technical build, but not "three public references, each ≥ 200 PD/year" or a seven-role team strength on its own. That's what turns an otherwise fitting tender into a ⚠️ partner case.
