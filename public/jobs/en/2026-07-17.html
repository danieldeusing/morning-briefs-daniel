Market Signal: The Bottleneck Sits with the Bidder, Not the Contract

Yesterday's cooling signal came from a different tier — corporate employment and an average across all freelancer types. Today it's Daniel's own. The Freelancer Compass 2026 measures in its first wave (surveyed November 17 – December 3, 2025): 43% of respondents "currently have no secured workload for the coming months," and in the IT/software segment, 23% report fewer contracts than the previous year.[2] This isn't a corporate statistic — this is the project market itself.

Secured workload: This refers to whether a freelancer already has a signed contract for the coming months that fills their working hours. Picture a tradesperson whose calendar for next month is still completely empty: they're not unemployed, but they also don't know if they'll be earning money next week. "43% without secured workload" therefore means: almost every other freelancer currently has no confirmed follow-on contract on the calendar — writing proposals instead of doing billable work.

And yet the volume side is calmer than the mood suggests. The Compass reports roughly 90,000 registered project tenders for 2025 — "in 2025 the market stabilizes with around 90,000 projects, back at 2020 levels," after the roughly 132,000-project peak in 2022.[1] About a third below peak, yes — but the wording deliberately says "stabilizes," not "collapses": this is a plateau at pre-boom levels, not an ongoing slide. Read together, both pieces produce a precise picture: the number of contracts has normalized, the number of providers hasn't. The 2022 boom pulled more freelancers into the market than the 2025 market employs — hence 43% idle time alongside plenty of open tenders. For Daniel, concretely, that means: filling the board is trivial (104 today), the award is the work. And the Compass even confirms the board's structure from the outside: "SAP, Java, and DevOps dominate the freelancer search in IT for 2025"[1] — on the board, Java 47×, DevOps 17×.

The three DACH layoff headlines of the past weeks belong alongside this, not inside it. Volkswagen plans to cut up to 100,000 of its currently roughly 657,000 group jobs; the Hannover, Zwickau, and Emden sites plus the Audi plant in Neckarsulm face closure (as of June 26, 2026).[3] The Commerzbank is cutting roughly 3,000 further full-time positions in its takeover fight with UniCredit (May 8, 2026).[4] Zalando is closing its Erfurt logistics center with 2,700 employees at the end of September.[5] That's full-time-employment tier in automotive, banking, and logistics — and none of these companies is putting out the Java projects that fill this board. The honest connection is indirect and runs both ways: corporate restructuring often shifts demand into the external project market (fewer internal heads, same software), but at the same time increases the number of released developers bidding there too. That's exactly the 43% effect in slow motion.

Project Tenders (TED EU)

Two stack-adjacent tenders today, both partner cases — and one of them has the narrowest gap in days: BG Kliniken's Lot 1 requires only three full-time developers and €250,000 in performance-specific revenue. That's not a solo effort, but it's the first threshold in weeks that a two-person shop could reach with one small partner, instead of a corporation. Bitter by contrast: the best content match of the day — govdigital's AI development framework contract — clearly falls into the skip tier at its eligibility threshold and therefore sits below in the skip line, not in the table.

CPV, Lots & Eligibility Criteria: A CPV code is the EU procurement-category key for a tender — 72* stands for IT services, and that's what we filter on. A lot is a separable sub-package of a contract: the contracting authority splits it up so smaller providers can also bid on a part instead of all-or-nothing. And eligibility criteria are the formal proof that a bidder can handle the contract as a business: minimum annual revenue, minimum headcount (FTE), reference projects in the right sector, sometimes certificates like ISO 27001. twiceD is a real GmbH, but at solo-/two-person scale — it can deliver the technical build, but not "100 employees" or "three references from a maximum-care hospital" on its own strength. A content-wise fitting contract thereby becomes a ⚠️ Partner needed case.
