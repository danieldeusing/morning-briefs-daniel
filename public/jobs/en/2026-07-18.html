Rate cross-check: two studies, one corridor

For the first time in weeks, the in-house rate band can be checked against two independent DACH surveys fielded in 2026 — and they don't contradict each other. The Freelancer Compass 2026 from freelancermap (over 5,400 respondents, published July 2, 2026) puts the hourly-rate median at €95/h for IT freelancers in the DACH region and €90/h for the "software & web development" subcategory.[1] The freelance.de Freelancer Study 2026 (over 3,300 respondents, fieldwork January 27 – March 2, 2026, published March 13, 2026) measures an IT average of €101.98/h, against €101.70/h across all fields and €100.55/h the prior year.[2]

Median vs. average: The two figures don't measure the same thing, so the gap isn't a contradiction. The median is the middle value when you line up every respondent in order — half fall below it, half above. The average divides the sum of all rates by the count. Ten freelancers, nine at €90 and one at €300: median €90, average €111. A few expensive specialists pull the average up but not the median — that's exactly what explains the gap between €95 (freelancermap) and €102 (freelance.de).

Cross-check verdict: consistent. Two independent samples, two metrics, one corridor of €90 to 102/h — matching the €90–120/h reference band for DE senior Java remote, whose upper third reflects the seniority and niche premiums that neither study breaks out separately. In practice: the €90–130/h* at the top of the board is well supported, but it's the upper edge — not the norm. And the four stated rates below the median floor stay board-visible but are never a pitch: Right People Group (€70/h), Optimus Search (from €70/h), CIMPCO (€80/h), FERCHAU (€50–65/h).

Project tenders (TED EU)

Six open, stack-matching tenders — the biggest track-3 catch since the section launched. More important than the count: all six list their eligibility criteria in full, which produces a rankable gap ladder. It runs from "three references" (Leibniz-IWT) through "one certificate" (CISPA) and "one public-sector reference plus proof of insurance" (Rhineland-Palatinate) to revenue thresholds of €1 to 2.3 million (ITZBund), and finally a 250-employee wall (DGUV). The table follows exactly this order — the easiest case first.

Segment-specific minimum revenue: Public contracting authorities almost always require proof that a bidder is financially large enough to carry the contract. "Segment-specific" tightens that: what counts isn't the company's total revenue, but only the revenue from comparable services. Like a bakery applying for a big pretzel order: selling a lot of cake doesn't help — what's asked for is pretzel revenue. For twiceD as a two-person GmbH, this is the most common gap trigger: the technical build is doable, the revenue proof isn't. A partner supplies exactly that proof (as a bidding consortium or borrowed capacity), twiceD supplies the technical delivery.

It's Saturday, July eighteenth. Today the board itself takes a back seat — the real news is on the tender page.

Six open EU tenders line up with the stack today, with deadlines between July thirtieth and August eleventh. That's the biggest haul this section has ever landed. What matters more than the count, though, is that all six list their eligibility criteria in full — which means, for the first time, the partner gap can be ranked from easiest to hardest.

At the very bottom of that ladder sits the Leibniz Institute for Materials Engineering in Bremen. It's looking for modular components for data refinement, built around ontologies and knowledge graphs and the automation of experimental workflows — applied AI in the narrow sense, not model research. The eligibility clause that came back requires only a self-declaration and at least three references for comparable work over the last five years. No minimum revenue, no headcount, no certificate. That would make it the first tender twiceD could bid on solo. There's an honest caveat, though: on another notice, it became clear the interface truncates long criteria text. So a hard threshold could be sitting in the full award document and simply not showing up here. A targeted refetch failed today with a four-hundred error. That's why it's marked tentative rather than a flat direct hit. Next step is to pull the full procurement documents from the platform and read them in the original.

One rung up sits the most interesting case of the day. CISPA, the Helmholtz Center for Information Security in Saarbrücken, is procuring a research information system. The only hard requirement in the notice is a certificate under ISO twenty-seven thousand one — or, literally, comparable evidence. No revenue figure, no headcount. That's not a capacity gap, it's an evidence gap, and that exact word "or" is the question worth clarifying. The move is to ask the contracting authority in writing before July thirtieth what counts as comparable. The deadline, though, at twelve days out, is the tightest in the field.

Then there's Rhineland-Palatinate. The state's finance ministry is tendering further development of its data and information platform — the best content match of the bunch, Java on the back end, Angular up front. Surprisingly, there's no numbered revenue threshold. What's required is liability insurance covering five hundred thousand euros for personal injury and property damage and two hundred fifty thousand for financial loss, plus three references, at least one of them from a public-sector client. The insurance is just a policy question. The public reference is the real sticking point — and for that, a small partner is enough, an agency with a state or municipal reference, not a systems house.

From there, the ladder gets steep fast. The federal government's IT center, ITZBund, wants a million euros in segment-specific revenue for its six-year framework contract on the real-estate information system, plus a standing commitment of three staff positions over seventy-two months — the staffing lock-in is the real problem there, not the money. For its KESS specialist system, the same agency wants two point three million euros in annual revenue, plus two references specifically from tax-fraud enforcement work. A bidding consortium can pool revenue; a domain reference has to have actually been delivered by somebody. And right at the top sits Germany's statutory accident insurance body, DGUV, with a tender for OpenText development: a million euros in average revenue over three years and at least two hundred fifty employees. That's not a gap a partner closes anymore — it's a different class of company. Realistically, the only role left there is subcontractor to a large integrator.

On the rate side, there's something rare today: two independent DACH studies, both fielded in twenty twenty-six, that confirm each other. The Freelancer Compass from freelancermap, published July second with more than fifty-four hundred respondents, puts the median at ninety-five euros an hour for IT and ninety euros for software and web development. The freelance-dot-de study, published in March with more than thirty-three hundred respondents, measures an IT average of one hundred one point nine eight euros. Median and average aren't measuring the same thing — a handful of expensive specialists pull the average up without moving the median. So the gap isn't a contradiction, it's the same market seen from two angles. The reliable corridor is ninety to one hundred two euros an hour. The researched ninety to one hundred thirty euros at the top of the board sits at the upper end of that, plus a seniority premium.
