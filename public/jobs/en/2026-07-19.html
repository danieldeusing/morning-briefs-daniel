The honest caveat first: these six are not a random sample. Their Fit scores range from 48 to 66 — mostly frontend and standard backend slots, not the Java-21-plus-Kafka-plus-AWS projects at the top. The top listings with a Fit of 84–88 state no rate at all. So what's observable is the lower half of the board; the upper half remains an assumption.

That's exactly why the external anchor matters. Malt's rate barometer sets the day rate for Java developers with 8–15 years of experience at €699 (data from the "last 3 months," retrieved July 19, 2026), tiered from €340 (0–2 yrs) through €526 (3–7 yrs) to €720 (15+ yrs); regionally, Frankfurt €764, Munich €676.[1] Converted to eight hours — the same conversion the ProTIP listing itself makes with €680/day ≈ €85/h — that comes to €87/h for the senior average, €90/h for the 15-plus tier, and €95/h for Frankfurt. The board's top rates are listed at €90–130/h*: that is, at the upper edge of Malt's band and beyond.

Day rate vs. hourly rate: German project boards sometimes quote in hours, sometimes in days — and both describe the same money, just sliced differently. Think of a price tag listed either "per slice" or "per loaf": to compare, you have to divide by the number of slices. The usual divisor is eight hours per project day; the ProTIP listing on the board does that math itself with €680/day ≈ €85/h. What matters is the direction of the error: if you quietly work ten hours under a day-rate contract, your real hourly rate drops by a fifth — even though the number in the contract never changes.

Verdict: The defensible corridor for the standard Java track is €85–100/h — the Malt senior average as the floor, the Freelancer Compass median (€95/h)[2] in the middle, the Frankfurt premium at the top. A target of ~€100/h is therefore not unrealistic, but it's the upper edge of the market, not its middle. Anyone aiming to land there isn't negotiating the rate itself, but whatever carries the premium: niche (IoT/automotive, listed on the board at €100–140/h*), contract length (the top listings run 24–48 months), or scope of responsibility. On the plain Java track alone, that number is no longer attainable.

The skills gap, recalculated

When a rate is under pressure, the first reflex is to point to the skills shortage. The figure everyone cites for that doesn't hold up under scrutiny. The 728,000 missing skilled workers by 2027 comes from a release by the German Economic Institute (IW) — dated August 9, 2024, so nearly two years old. It covers all professions. The ranking there is led by sales with about 37,000 unfilled positions, followed by childcare (27,600), social work (22,200), and nursing (20,300). IT specialists rank fifth with 19,000 — literally: "for IT specialists it would be 19,000."[3]

A more recent look confirms the direction. The MINT Spring Report 2026 (published May 5, 2026) puts the total MINT skills gap in March 2026 at 133,900 people, of which 44,200 are in academic expert roles; the biggest bottlenecks are in energy/electrical trades (48,900), metalworking (26,500), and construction (26,400) — not software development.[4] A Bitkom figure circulating in search results — 149,000 missing IT workers, up 12% — could not be cross-checked this round and is therefore not used here.

What that means in practice: the narrative of a dramatic IT shortage no longer works as a negotiating argument in 2026 — it's either outdated or refers to other professions. That fits with the observed €70–85/h rates and the first decline in the DACH average in years. The bottleneck hasn't disappeared, it has just become specific: Kubernetes experts with DevSecOps experience are consistently described as the scarcest profile in the German IT market — a qualitative claim, without a solid figure.[6] Kubernetes appears 29× on the board, AWS 27×, AI integration 19×. That's the direction in which a premium is still defensible today.

Three regulatory clocks are running at once

While the price gives way, risk rises on the other side of the ledger. Three dates belong on the calendar — all three touch on the same question: when is an IT freelancer actually considered self-employed in Germany's eyes?

Bogus self-employment (Scheinselbstständigkeit): the German state checks whether someone who invoices as a freelancer on paper is actually working like an employee — fixed hours, a single client, instructions like a team member. Like a "rental car" that sits in the same spot every morning, has the same driver, and never gets returned: eventually someone asks whether that isn't just a company car. If that's found to be the case, it's usually the client who has to pay back social security contributions — retroactively, for up to four years. That's why this topic is less a tax risk for Daniel than a placeability risk: end clients afraid of back payments shorten projects, insist on service contracts with a clearly defined deliverable — or move straight to agency-placement (ANÜ) models, which are practically unworkable for a Brazil-based GmbH.

First, KIRA. AI-driven audit software that analyzes payroll and accounting data, which companies have been automatically submitting since January 2023 as part of the electronically supported payroll tax audit. In a test phase since January 2025, with nationwide rollout planned for 2026. The difference from previous practice isn't legal but statistical: suspicious patterns are no longer found by spot-checking, but systematically.[7]

Second, the EU Platform Work Directive. Germany must transpose it into national law by December 2, 2026. Its core principle is a reversal of the burden of proof: until now, the freelancer had to prove they were self-employed — going forward, the platform must prove that no employment relationship exists.[7] What matters is the scope, and that's still open today: the directive targets platform work, not classic B2B project contracts. Whether and how far the German transposition text also covers intermediary project boards and staffing agencies won't be decided until the draft legislation appears. That's the actual date to watch for — not December 2 itself.

Third, the AÜG (Temporary Employment Act). The Federal Employment Agency's technical directive on the Temporary Employment Act carries an effective date of July 1, 2026; the PDF's full text couldn't be extracted this round, but the title and date are confirmed.[8] A secondary source also cites legislative changes dated December 22, 2025, taking effect on December 30, 2025 and June 29, 2026 — the primary source wasn't checked, so no content is summarized here.[9] Practical relevance to the board: eight listings today carry the ANÜ tag. They don't run through the GmbH and aren't structured as a service contract — visible on the board, but practically not an option.
