Business Vocabulary: Invoice, Interest Rate, Exchange Rate

Monday is business day. Twelve words that come up every month in EUR-BRL workflow — from the invoice you send to the DE company, to the tax you pay on both sides, to the boleto, the Brazilian payment slip that European languages simply don't have a word for. Four verbs with full conjugation (six persons, five tenses, four languages), three BR-PT specifics, and a classic false friend that still trips up fifteen-year EN speakers now and then.

  • FALSE FRIEND bekommen ≠ to become — DE "bekommen" means EN "to receive"; EN "to become" is DE "werden". Lifelong classic.
  • FALSE FRIEND actual ≠ aktuell — EN "actual" = DE "tatsächlich", not "current". The DE word for "current" is "aktuell".
  • BR-PT o boleto — the Brazilian payment slip has no direct DE/ES equivalent. Note: in ES-MX, "boleto" means "ticket" instead (transport/event).
  • BR-PT nota fiscal vs. fatura — nota fiscal is the formal tax document; fatura is closer to a credit card statement or a general invoice.
  • CONJUGATION überweisen is a strong verb: überweisen → überwies → überwiesen. Never *überweisste*.
  • CONJUGATION ES transferir is stem-changing (e>ie): yo transfiero, not *transfero*. PT changes differently: eu transfiro (e>i only in the yo form).
  • GENDER die Steuer (f) but der Steuersatz (m) — German compounds take the gender of the final element.
  • PHRASE "to issue an invoice" in DE is "eine Rechnung stellen", literally "to put an invoice". Never *eine Rechnung machen*.
  • ES-MX la tasa de interés (MX, Latin-American) instead of el tipo de interés (ES). But: el tipo de cambio is also the Banxico standard in MX.
  • PHRASE PT "pagar a conta" covers both "pay the bill" and "face the consequences" — same double meaning as in EN.

It is Monday, May eighteenth. Business day on the languages brief. Today, twelve vocabulary items that come up every month in EUR-BRL business workflow — four verbs with full conjugation, eight nouns, all of them side-by-side in German, English, Brazilian Portuguese, and Mexican Spanish.

The four verbs are überweisen, bezahlen, unterschreiben, and bekommen. Überweisen is a strong verb in German — ich überweise, ich überwies, ich habe überwiesen. In Brazilian Portuguese it is transferir, with a stem change in the eu form: eu transfiro. In Mexican Spanish the stem change is stronger: yo transfiero. These three first-person forms look similar but are not identical — eu transfiro in Portuguese, yo transfiero in Spanish.

Bezahlen and unterschreiben are the easier verbs. Pagar and assinar in Portuguese, pagar and firmar in Spanish, all regular. In German, unterschreiben follows the same strong pattern as schreiben — ich unterschreibe, ich unterschrieb, ich habe unterschrieben.

The fourth verb is the day's main prize: bekommen. Bekommen does not mean to become. Classic false friend. Bekommen means to receive — what you bekommen comes to you. To become would be werden in German. In Portuguese it is receber, in Spanish recibir. Both regular.

On the noun side, three places where the languages diverge. First: invoice. In Brazilian Portuguese, two words split the meaning — nota fiscal is the formal tax document that the tax office cares about. Fatura is closer to a credit-card statement or a general invoice without fiscal function.

Second: the interest rate. In Mexican Spanish it is tasa de interés. In Spain people say tipo de interés. But careful: with exchange rate it flips. In Mexico it is tipo de cambio — the standard term at the Mexican central bank.

Third: the word of the day, boleto. Boleto in Brazilian Portuguese is a payment slip — a barcoded document used to pay electricity bills, taxes, anything at a counter or in the banking app. This concept does not really exist in the German-speaking world. Important: the same word boleto means something completely different in Mexican Spanish — there it is a ticket, for a flight or an event. If you say boleto in Mexico, people think of travel, not money.
