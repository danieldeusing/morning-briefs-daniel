Software Vocabulary: to compile, interface, vulnerability, library

Tuesday is software day. Twelve words from your daily workflow — four verbs with full conjugation, eight nouns — laid out across all four languages side by side. The interesting part today isn't the vocabulary itself, it's the traps: a livraria in PT-BR isn't a software library (it's a bookstore), eventually in EN doesn't mean "eventuell" in DE, and BR-PT keeps the anglicism "o mouse" while Mexican Spanish translates to "el ratón". Plus a whole family of German anglicism-verbs (deployen, pushen, committen) that get conjugated as regular weak verbs.

  • FALSE FRIEND livraria (PT) ≠ library — livraria = bookstore. A software library in PT is biblioteca. If you say "instalei a livraria", you've installed a bookstore.
  • FALSE FRIEND eventually (EN) ≠ eventuell (DE) — EN "eventually" = sooner or later, in the end; DE "eventuell" = maybe / possibly. "We'll eventually fix it" means "we will fix it (at some point)", not "we might fix it". Classic meeting trap.
  • BR-PT o arquivo (BR) vs. o ficheiro (EU-PT) — Brazilians say "arquivo" for file, Portuguese say "ficheiro". In Brazil, always "arquivo".
  • BR-PT o mouse (anglicism kept) vs. ES-MX el ratón (translated) — typical pattern: BR-PT often leaves English terms in place, ES-MX translates them more conservatively (even though "el mouse" is heard colloquially).
  • CONJUGATION DE herunterladen is strong AND separable: "ich lade herunter, du lädst herunter (umlaut!), ich lud herunter, ich habe heruntergeladen". The umlaut in the du/er form is the main stumbling block.
  • CONJUGATION ES desplegar (to deploy) is stem-changing e>ie: yo despliego, not *desplego. Preterite yo-form with g→gu: desplegué.
  • CONJUGATION DE abstürzen forms the perfect with sein, not haben: "Der Server ist abgestürzt." Motion / state-change verbs → sein.
  • ANGLICISM DE devs conjugate English verbs as regular weak verbs: deployen → deployte → hat gedeployt; pushen → pushte → hat gepusht; committen → committete → hat committet. Even in formal documentation.
  • GENDER Software nouns in DE: die Datei, die Schnittstelle, die Bibliothek (all fem.) — but der Server, der Browser, der Quellcode (all masc.). Anglicisms usually take the gender of the nearest DE equivalent or default to "der".
  • PHRASE ES-MX la carpeta for "folder" (a filesystem directory); "el directorio" exists too but "carpeta" is the everyday term. PT-BR is analogous: a pasta.

It's Tuesday, May nineteenth. Software day in the language brief. Today, twelve developer-life words laid out across four languages side by side: German, English, Brazilian Portuguese, and Mexican Spanish. Four verbs with full conjugation, eight nouns.

The four verbs are kompilieren, bereitstellen, abstürzen, and herunterladen. Kompilieren — to compile — is a German -ieren verb, perfectly regular, no ge-prefix in the participle: ich kompiliere, ich kompilierte, ich habe kompiliert. In Portuguese and Spanish it's just a regular -ar verb: compilo, compilei, compilarei.

Bereitstellen is the formal German word for "to deploy", and it's separable: ich stelle die neue Version bereit. In Brazilian Portuguese it becomes implantar — eu implanto a nova versão. In Mexican Spanish, desplegar — and pay attention: that's a stem-changing verb. Yo despliego, with the diphthong, not desplego. And in the preterite the g shifts to gu before the e: yo desplegué.

Abstürzen — to crash — is separable, and it forms the perfect tense with sein, not haben: der Server ist abgestürzt, not hat abgestürzt. That's the typical sein-construction for state-change verbs. In Portuguese, travar — o servidor travou. In Spanish, fallar — falló dos veces anoche. Both regular.

Herunterladen is the most interesting verb of the day: strong AND separable. Strong, because laden changes its stem vowel — ich lade, ich lud, ich habe geladen. Separable, because herunter detaches as a prefix. And in the du and er forms, the a takes an umlaut: du lädst herunter, er lädt herunter. Three traps in one verb. Portuguese and Spanish are much easier: baixar in Brazilian Portuguese, descargar in Spanish, both regular.

On the noun side, there are two spots where the languages diverge. First: the file. In Brazilian Portuguese it's arquivo. That matters — Portugal says ficheiro, Brazil says arquivo. Saying ficheiro in Brazil immediately marks you as European Portuguese.

Second: the mouse. This is the classic axis between the two Romance languages: Brazilian Portuguese just keeps the anglicism "o mouse", while Mexican Spanish translates and says "el ratón". You'll hear "el mouse" in Mexico too, but ratón is standard.

And then the word of the day: Bibliothek — in English, library. In Portuguese, ALWAYS biblioteca, NEVER livraria. Livraria is a bookstore. If you type "instalei a livraria React" into Slack, you've installed a bookstore. That's the false friend every Latin-American learner makes at least once. Spanish is analogous: biblioteca is the academic word, librería means bookstore. Some Mexican tech forums do use librería for software libraries, but that's slang.

Two bonus observations for later. The German word "eventuell" does NOT mean "eventually" — classic meeting trap. Eventuell means "maybe", eventually means "in the end". And German devs just conjugate English verbs with German endings: ich deploye, ich pushe, ich committe. Sounds odd at first, but it's standard even in formal documentation.
