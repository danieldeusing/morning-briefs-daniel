Family & Everyday Vocabulary: the big kids' day

Wednesday is family day — and a big one today: thirty-three words around the toddler, the household, illness and the playground, twelve of them verbs with full conjugation, every word with at least two example sentences across the four languages. Four refreshers from yesterday (to pick up, to sleep, tired, to throw a tantrum) come back as review. Today's payoff sits in two patterns: the Romance stem-changing verbs (PT/ES vestir-se e→i, ES despertarse/consolar diphthongize o/e→ue/ie) and the BR-vs-MX lexical splits (mesada vs. el domingo, carrinho vs. carriola, trocar a fralda vs. cambiar el pañal).

  • BR ≠ MX mesada (BR) vs. el domingo (MX) — the allowance. Brazil thinks monthly (mês → mesada), Mexico from the Sunday payout day. Spain: la paga.
  • BR ≠ MX carrinho (BR) vs. carriola (MX) — the stroller. Spain: el cochecito. Three words, one ride.
  • CONJUGATION to comfort = ES consolar diphthongizes o→ue: „yo consuelo", „que yo consuele" — but „nosotros consolamos". PT consolar stays regular (eu consolo). Same pattern as dormir and despertar.
  • CONJUGATION to get dressed → PT vestir-se / ES vestirse shift e→i (eu me visto, yo me visto, él se vistió). German stays separable.
  • CONJUGATION to wake up takes sein in German („ich bin aufgewacht"). PT acordar regular, ES despertar(se) e→ie (yo me despierto, but nosotros despertamos).
  • BR-PT Spelling traps: brigar g→gu (briguei), trocar c→qu (troquei) — before e. Like buscar yesterday.
  • CHANGING to change a diaper — BR trocar a fralda, MX cambiar el pañal. Two completely different verbs for the same job.
  • GENDER baby food (der Brei) (m, no plural) → PT a papinha (f), ES la papilla (f). grandparents (die Großeltern) (plural-only) → os avós / los abuelos.
  • SER/ESTAR sick/tired/full with estar (temporary): „está doente / cansado / satisfeito". ES agrees (cansado/cansada).
  • GENDER pediatrician (der Kinderarzt) = PT/ES pediatra — identical for male and female („o/a pediatra", „el/la pediatra"). Never *pediatro.
  • FALSE FRIEND impfen ≠ EN „to imp"; EN „to vaccinate". German noun „die Spritze" = EN „shot/injection", PT „a injeção", ES „la inyección".
  • REVIEW refreshed from yesterday: to pick up (buscar≠buscar), to sleep (triple irregular), tired, to throw a tantrum (fazer birra / hacer un berrinche).

It's Wednesday, May twentieth — and a big family day in the languages brief. Thirty-three words around the toddler, the household, illness and the playground, twelve of them verbs with full conjugation. Every word today has at least two example sentences, parallel in German, English, Brazilian Portuguese and Mexican Spanish.

The most important pattern first: the Spanish stem-changing verbs. To comfort is consolar in Spanish — and like dormir and despertar, the vowel jumps under stress: yo consuelo, with ue. But as soon as the stress moves away, the stem stays calm: nosotros consolamos. In Portuguese, by contrast, consolar is perfectly regular: eu consolo. If you internalize this one pattern, dormir, despertarse and consolar all fall into place together.

The second pattern is the spelling traps before e. In Brazilian Portuguese the g in brigar becomes gu — eu briguei. The c in trocar becomes qu — eu troquei. And the c in buscar from yesterday becomes qu too — eu busquei. Three verbs, one rule: before e the spelling is adjusted to save the pronunciation.

Then the lovely differences between Brazil and Mexico. The allowance is a mesada in Brazil, from the month — in Mexico el domingo, from Sunday. The stroller is o carrinho here, la carriola there. The swing is o balanço versus el columpio. The playground o parquinho versus el parque. And changing a diaper is trocar a fralda in Brazil, cambiar el pañal in Mexico — two completely different verbs for the same job.

Among the baby verbs it's worth a look at the reflexive forms. To get dressed is vestir-se and vestirse, both with e to i: eu me visto, yo me visto. To wake up takes the auxiliary sein in German — ich bin aufgewacht — while in Spanish despertarse diphthongizes: yo me despierto.

The care verbs are fortunately mostly regular. To feed, to bathe, to cry, to vaccinate and to breastfeed follow the -ar pattern in Portuguese and Spanish: alimentar, banhar, chorar, vacinar, amamentar. Only to bathe splits in Portuguese: dar banho when you bathe someone, tomar banho when you bathe yourself.

Among the nouns, a few gender traps. The spoon is masculine in German, but a colher and la cuchara are feminine. Baby food is masculine, but a papinha and la papilla feminine. And the pediatrician is pediatra — the same for a man and a woman, only the article changes.

From the illness corner, three words with a Mexican touch: a cold is la gripa in Mexico, not la gripe like in Spain. Fever is a febre and la fiebre. And the shot is a injeção and la inyección — the vaccination itself a vacina and la vacuna.

Finally the review words from yesterday: to pick up, which is buscar in Brazilian Portuguese and recoger in Mexican Spanish — never buscar, because that would be to search. To sleep, triple irregular. Tired. And the kid melting down at the supermarket throws birra in Brazil and un berrinche in Mexico. That was Wednesday, family day.
