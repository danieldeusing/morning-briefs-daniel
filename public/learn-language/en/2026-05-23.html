Food & Restaurant Vocabulary: Ordering, Spicy, the Bill

Saturday is kitchen-and-restaurant day — and hardly any topic divides Brazil and Mexico as cleanly as food does. Today thirty-six words around the menu, the stove and the tip, twelve of them verbs with full conjugation, each word with at least two parallel example sentences across all four languages. Three refreshers return in culinary dress (füttern at the table, der Löffel for the soup, satt after the meal). Today's payoff sits in three bundles: the Spanish stem-changing verbs (probar o→ue, revolver o→ue, recomendar e→ie, pedir/servir e→i, plus the irregular freír), the dense BR-vs-MX food lexicon (garçom/mesero, cardápio/menú, gostoso/rico, picoso/apimentado, fogão/estufa) and two nasty false friends (esquisito ≠ exquisito, PT pasta ≠ noodles).

  • BR ≠ MX o garçom (BR) vs. el mesero (MX) — the waiter. Spain says el camarero, Portugal o empregado de mesa. Three words, one waiter.
  • BR ≠ MX o cardápio (BR) vs. el menú (MX) — the menu. Portugal: a ementa. And the dish: BR o prato, MX el platillo.
  • BR ≠ MX el plato fuerte (MX) vs. o prato principal (BR) — the main course. In Mexico the main course is „strong", not „main".
  • FALSE FRIEND esquisito (PT) ≠ exquisito (ES) — PT esquisito means weird/strange, ES exquisito means delicious. EN exquisite. One letter, opposite meaning.
  • FALSE FRIEND pasta (PT) ≠ noodles — in Portuguese pasta is the folder / binder. Noodles are a massa. (ES la pasta = noodles, as expected.)
  • FALSE FRIEND la estufa (MX) is the stove — but in Spain estufa is the heater / greenhouse. Spanish stove = la cocina.
  • FALSE FRIEND Rezept (DE) means cooking recipe and medical prescription; PT receita / ES receta too. EN recipe ≠ receipt.
  • CONJUGATION ES stem changes: probar (yo pruebo), revolver (revuelvo) jump o→ue, recomendar (recomiendo) e→ie — but nosotros stays regular (probamos, revolvemos, recomendamos).
  • CONJUGATION pedir / servir e→i in both languages: PT eu peço / sirvo, ES yo pido / sirvo; in ES the 3rd-person preterite jumps too (pidió, sirvió).
  • CONJUGATION pagar g→gu before e (PT eu paguei, ES yo pagué) · freír irregular (yo frío, 3rd-person pret. frió).
  • BR ≠ MX gostoso (BR) vs. ¡qué rico! (MX) — delicious. And spicy: BR apimentado/picante, MX picoso. Taste carries a passport.
  • REVIEW back in culinary dress: füttern (at the table, alimentar), der Löffel (for the soup, a colher/la cuchara), satt (after the meal, satisfeito/satisfecho).

It's Saturday, the twenty-third of May — and today is kitchen-and-restaurant day in the languages brief. Thirty-six words around the menu, the stove and the tip, twelve of them verbs with full conjugation. Every word has at least two example sentences, parallel in German, English, Brazilian Portuguese and Mexican Spanish.

The most important pattern first: the Spanish stem-changing verbs. To taste is probar in Spanish — and under stress the vowel jumps: yo pruebo, with ue. Likewise revuelvo for revolver, to stir. Recomendar, to recommend, jumps e to ie: yo recomiendo. And pedir and servir, to order and to serve, raise the e to i: yo pido, yo sirvo. But as soon as the stress moves away, the stem settles down again: nosotros probamos, revolvemos, recomendamos, pedimos, servimos. In Portuguese, provar and recomendar are regular, while pedir and servir also raise in the first person: eu peço, eu sirvo, with the subjunctives peça and sirva.

The second pattern is two special cases in the kitchen. To pay is pagar — and before e the g protects itself with a u: in Portuguese eu paguei, in Spanish yo pagué. And to fry is freír in Spanish, a genuinely irregular verb: yo frío with an accent on the i, fríes, fríe, but nosotros freímos; in the preterite freí and frió. In Portuguese, fritar stays perfectly regular.

Then the lovely differences between Brazil and Mexico, which are especially dense around food. The waiter is o garçom in Brazil, el mesero in Mexico — and in Spain el camarero. The menu is o cardápio versus el menú; in Portugal a ementa. The main course is o prato principal in Brazil, but el plato fuerte in Mexico — the „strong" dish. The dish itself is o prato versus el platillo. The stove is o fogão versus la estufa, the pan a frigideira versus el sartén. And when praising food: delicious is gostoso in Brazil, in Mexico people say ¡qué rico! Spicy is apimentado or picante in Brazil, picoso in Mexico.

With the false friends, special care is needed today — three of them. In Portuguese esquisito does not mean exquisite, but weird, strange: comida esquisita is weird food, not delicious food. In Spanish, by contrast, exquisito really does mean delicious. Second: Portuguese pasta is not noodles, but the folder or binder — noodles are a massa. Third: estufa is the stove in Mexico, but the heater or greenhouse in Spain. And the word Rezept means, in German as in Portuguese and Spanish, both a cooking recipe and a medical prescription.

Today's review words came back in culinary dress: füttern, to feed, at the table, which is alimentar in Portuguese and Spanish. Der Löffel, the spoon, for the soup, a colher and la cuchara. And satt, full, after the meal — in Portuguese estou satisfeito, in Spanish estoy satisfecho, always with estar, because it's a temporary state.

Finally, two expressions from the table. Before eating you wish a good appetite — bom apetite, buen provecho. And when you have to deal with an unpleasant mess, in German that's die Suppe auslöffeln, to spoon out the soup; in Brazilian Portuguese descascar o abacaxi, to peel the pineapple; and in Mexican Spanish pagar los platos rotos, to pay for the broken plates.
