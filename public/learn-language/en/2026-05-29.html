Vocabulary Travel II: Border, Rodoviária, Connection

Friday is travel day again — but today one level deeper than a week ago. Last Friday it was airport, train station, and gas station. Today the travel routine when something doesn't go smoothly: at the border, at customs, at the departures board, when you miss the connection and a booking has to be cancelled. Thirty-five words in four clusters — verbs (fourteen with full conjugation, three of them reviews), nouns around borders and bus terminals, booking adjectives, and one idiom when plans go sideways. Pay special attention today to two dense bundles: the Spanish stem changes in perder (e→ie), mostrar (o→ue), and cargar (g→gu), plus the irregular classic salir (yo salgo, saldré), and the BR-vs-MX travel lexicon on the ground: rodoviária/central de autobuses, ônibus/autobús (camión in MX slang), cobrador/conductor (beware: false friend), pedágio/caseta.

  • FALSE FRIEND el conductor (ES) ≠ conductor (EN train) — in Spanish el conductor is the driver. The train/bus conductor is el cobrador or el revisor. Wrong address on the bus = embarrassing.
  • BR ≠ MX a rodoviária (BR) vs. la central de autobuses (MX) — the central bus terminal. In Tijuana also central camionera. The long-distance bus itself: BR o ônibus interestadual, MX el autobús foráneo (everyday also just "el camión").
  • BR ≠ MX o pedágio (BR) vs. la caseta de cobro (MX) — the toll booth. "Pedágio" in BR Portuguese means both the fee and the booth; on the MX-200D you pay at the caseta.
  • BR-PT fazer baldeação (BR) — to transfer (bus/train). In Portugal more fazer transbordo; in MX hacer transbordo. At the airport you switch conexão even in BR.
  • CONJUGATION salir (ES) is doubly irregular: yo salgo in present, saldré in future, saldría in conditional. Imperative tú: sal. Portuguese sair has one stumbling block: eu saio (with i!).
  • CONJUGATION perder (ES) diphthongizes e→ie under stress: yo pierdo, tú pierdes, ellos pierden — but nosotros stays well-behaved (perdemos). Also irregular in Portuguese 1st person: eu perco, que eu perca.
  • CONJUGATION mostrar (ES) is o→ue under stress: yo muestro, tú muestras — but nosotros mostramos. PT mostrar stays regular (eu mostro).
  • CONJUGATION Spelling before e/o: PT embarcar/desembarcar c→qu (eu embarquei, desembarquei), ES buscar c→qu (yo busqué), ES cargar g→gu (yo cargué), ES aterrizar z→c (yo aterricé).
  • PHRASE einsteigen / aussteigen / umsteigen (board / disembark / transfer) — all three take sein (not haben) in the German perfect and are separable, so the prefix migrates to the end of the main clause: "Wir steigen in Curitiba um."
  • GENDER der Anschluss (m, pl. Anschlüsse) → a conexão (f) / la conexión (f). Masculine in German, feminine in PT/ES. And: el enlace is the more official MX variant (on rail tickets).
  • GENDER die Quittung (f) → o recibo (m) / el recibo (m). Feminine in German, masculine in PT/ES. Important for the customs experience: no receipt means no proof of value.
  • REVIEW miss · fill up · land return today — in the connection context (missing the bus), at the auto-caseta (filling up before the border), and in the emergency landing scenario (landing in Curitiba instead of Londrina).

It's Friday, May twenty-ninth — and today the second travel day in the languages brief. A week ago it was airport, train station, and gas station. Today one level deeper: what happens when something doesn't go smoothly — at the border, at customs, at the departures board, when the connection is missed and a booking has to be cancelled. Thirty-five words in four clusters: fourteen verbs with full conjugation, sixteen nouns around border and bus terminal, four booking adjectives, and one idiom.

The first major pattern: the separable German verbs with "be". Board, disembark, transfer, enter country, exit country — all movement verbs, all "I have boarded, exited". In main clause the prefix flies to the end: we transfer in Curitiba over. In subordinate clause and infinitive it stays front: so we can transfer. In Portuguese it works differently — embark and disembark carry the spelling pitfall before e: eu embarquei, not embarcei. In Spanish, board and get down are straightforwardly regular.

The second pattern: the Spanish stem and spelling changes, densely bundled. Lose diphthongizes e→ie under stress: yo pierdo, tú pierdes, ellos pierden — but nosotros stays well-behaved, perdemos. Show diphthongizes o→ue: yo muestro, he shows the passport — and here too nosotros mostramos. Search becomes busqué before e in the simple past and busque in the subjunctive, because the k-sound must be preserved. Load becomes cargué, arrived-logic. Land becomes aterricé before e, the z yields to c. And the classic leave is doubly irregular: yo salgo in present, saldré in future, saldría in conditional, imperative just sal.

The third pattern: BR-vs-MX ground-transport lexicon. The central bus terminal is in Brazil a rodoviária — literally "the road-station" — in Mexico la central de autobuses or colloquially la central camionera. The intercity bus is o ônibus interestadual versus el autobús foráneo, in Mexican everyday often just "el camión". The toll booth is o pedágio in Brazil — where "pedágio" means both the fee and the booth — in Mexico la caseta de cobro, and in Spain el peaje. The conductor is o cobrador in Brazil and el cobrador in Mexico — and right here lurks a classic false friend.

Because in Spanish el conductor is not the conductor but the driver. Who checks tickets on the bus is el cobrador or el revisor. Conductor means conductor in English, driver in Spanish — mix that up and you're talking to the wrong person. A thing worth Daniel remembering, especially on Mexico trips.

With booking adjectives a fine German distinction: cancelled and cancelled are both "cancelled" in English — but in German they distinguish who cancelled. A booking is cancelled when the traveler cancels. A bus or flight is cancelled when the provider cancels it — strike, mechanical damage, weather. In Portuguese and Spanish both are cancelar and cancelado, context decides. And sold out is esgotado in Brazil, agotado in Mexico — literally both "exhausted", so strongly as "full" used.

With nouns gender wanders through the languages. The connection is masculine in German, but a conexão and la conexión are feminine. The emergency exit is masculine, but a saída de emergência and la salida de emergencia are feminine. The receipt is feminine, but o recibo and el recibo are masculine. And the cancellation is feminine in German, la cancelación in Spanish — but o cancelamento in Portuguese, masculine. Three languages, three genders for the same thing: that's language-learning everyday life.

The three reviews came back today in connection context. Miss, lose, lose — missing the connection in São Paulo is no drama but costs a night. Fill up, supply, load gasoline — before the border in Foz, because diesel in Paraguay is pricier than in Brazil, unlike most goods. And land, land, land — because of fog in Curitiba instead of Londrina; that happens on the north-pioneer route in May and June regularly.

At the end the idiom of the day: throw a wrench in someone's plans — when a strike, closed border, or mechanical breakdown throws travel awry. In Portuguese you say spoil the plans, in Spanish spoil the plans to someone. Word-for-word translating gets nowhere; the image "wrench in plans" lives only in German.
