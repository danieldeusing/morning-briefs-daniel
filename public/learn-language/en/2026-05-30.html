Kitchen Vocabulary II: Breakfast, Grill, Allergies

Saturday, kitchen day — but one layer deeper than a week ago. On May 23rd we covered the menu, the waiter, and the three core verbs (eat, cook, order). Today the real day-to-day of eating in Norte Pioneiro: breakfast at the padaria, the churrasco on Saturday afternoon, and the situations that get awkward when you lack the words — announcing an allergy or lactose intolerance, swapping a side dish, splitting the bill. Thirty-four words in four clusters: fourteen verbs (with full conjugation, three of them review from last week), nouns around breakfast and the grill, adjectives for the state of food, and one idiom. Two dense spots today: first a whole bundle of Spanish stem changes in cooking — verter (e→ie), calentar (e→ie), moler (o→ue), probar (o→ue), servir (e→i), and the doubly irregular freír (yo frío, part. frito) — and second the BR-vs-MX food lexicon: padaria/panadería, garçom/mesero, churrasco/asada, feijão/frijoles, conta/cuenta.

  • FALSE FRIEND el menú (MX) ≠ Menü (DE) — in Mexico el menú is the menu/bill of fare. The German "Menü" (multi-course set meal) is MX la comida corrida, BR o prato feito / o PF. Ordering "un menú" means asking to see the card, not requesting a three-course meal.
  • BR ≠ MX a padaria (BR) vs. la panadería (MX) — the bakery. In Brazil it also doubles as a breakfast spot: pão na chapa (toasted bread with butter) + pingado (milky coffee). The standard roll: BR o pão francês, MX el bolillo.
  • BR ≠ MX o garçom / a garçonete (BR) vs. el mesero / la mesera (MX) — the waiter/waitress. "Garçom" comes from French but is perfectly standard BR-PT. In Spain it would be el camarero — never in Mexico, where it is always mesero.
  • BR-PT o churrasco — the grill as Saturday ritual. The verb: BR fazer churrasco, MX hacer una carne asada (or simply una asada). The grill itself: BR a churrasqueira, MX el asador.
  • CONJUGATION freír (ES) is doubly irregular: yo frío (with accent), él frió, ellos frieron — and the participle is usually frito (rarely freído). PT fritar, by contrast, is perfectly regular: eu frito, fritei.
  • CONJUGATION verter / calentar (ES) both diphthongize e→ie under stress: yo vierto, yo caliento — but nosotros stays regular (vertemos, calentamos). moler / probar shift o→ue: yo muelo, yo pruebo — nosotros molemos, probamos.
  • CONJUGATION servir (PT & ES) raises the stem vowel e→i in the 1st person: eu sirvo / yo sirvo, and throughout the subjunctive: que eu sirva. Also in the PT preterite 3rd person: ele serviu (no shift), but ES él sirvió, ellos sirvieron.
  • CONJUGATION Spelling before e: PT descascar c→qu (eu descasquei, que eu descasque), ES recoger g→j before o/a (yo recojo, que yo recoja — not *recogo). Important for clearing the table.
  • CONJUGATION backen / braten / gießen (DE) — three German pitfalls: backen is now mostly regular (backte, hat gebacken; the old buk is archaic), braten is strong with umlaut (du brätst, er brät, briet, gebraten), gießen strong (goss, gegossen, gösse). And mahlen is a mixed verb: regular preterite mahlte, but strong participle gemahlen.
  • GENDER die Bohne (f, usually pl. die Bohnen) → o feijão (m) / el frijol (m, pl. los frijoles). Feminine in German, masculine in PT/ES. In Brazil the daily arroz e feijão (rice and beans) — staple food, not a side-dish luxury.
  • PHRASE stating an allergy / intolerance — vital in a restaurant: "I am lactose-intolerant" = BR tenho intolerância à lactose, MX soy intolerante a la lactosa. "Nut allergy" = BR alergia a castanhas/nozes, MX alergia a las nueces.
  • IDIOM das Wasser läuft mir im Mund zusammen → BR dar água na boca, MX se me hace agua la boca. Literally "makes water in my mouth" — the image is the same in all three languages, only the sentence structure rotates.
  • REVIEW probieren · schneiden · braten return from the kitchen lesson of May 23rd — today in the breakfast and grill context (tasting the scrambled eggs, cutting the roll, frying the meat instead of grilling).

It is Saturday, the thirtieth of May — and kitchen day in the language brief again, but one layer deeper than a week ago. On the twenty-third of May we covered the menu, the waiter, and the core verbs. Today the real day-to-day of eating in Norte Pioneiro: breakfast at the padaria, the churrasco on Saturday afternoon, and the situations that get awkward when you lack the words — announcing an allergy, swapping a side dish, splitting the bill. Thirty-four words in four clusters: fourteen verbs with full conjugation, three of them review.

The first major pattern: the Spanish cooking stem changes, particularly dense today. Verter and calentar shift e to ie under stress: yo vierto, yo caliento. Moler and probar shift o to ue: yo muelo, yo pruebo. And in all four the first-person plural stays regular: vertemos, calentamos, molemos, probamos. That is the rule to remember — the shift only happens when the stem is stressed, and for nosotros the stress falls on the ending.

Servir raises the stem vowel differently: e becomes i, not ie. Yo sirvo, eu sirvo, and throughout the subjunctive: que … sirva. In Spanish this carries into the third person of the past tense too: él sirvió, ellos sirvieron. In Portuguese the past tense stays calm: ele serviu, no shift.

The hard case of the day is freír, to fry in German or English. Yo frío with accent on the i in the present. Él frió and ellos frieron in the past. And the participle is usually frito, not freído — huevos fritos, fried eggs. The Portuguese fritar is perfectly well-behaved by comparison: eu frito, eu fritei. Learning them side by side makes the contrast clearest.

The second pattern: the German cooking verbs with hidden pitfalls. Backen is now mostly regular: ich backte, ich habe gebacken. The old strong buk is almost never heard, it appears only as a variant in the dictionary. Braten by contrast is strong with umlaut: du brätst, er brät, past tense briet, participle gebraten. Gießen is strong: goss, gegossen, subjunctive gösse. And mahlen is the real trap — a mixed verb: regular preterite mahlte, but strong participle gemahlen. Freshly ground pepper is frisch gemahlener Pfeffer, not gemahlt.

Two spelling traps lurk when clearing the table. In Spanish it is recoger la mesa — and recoger shifts g to j before o and a: yo recojo, not recogo, subjunctive que yo recoja. In Portuguese, for peeling, descascar shifts c to qu before e: eu descasquei in the past, que eu descasque in the subjunctive. The sound stays the same; the spelling adapts.

The third pattern: the food lexicon divides Brazil and Mexico. The bakery in Brazil is a padaria — and there it doubles as a breakfast spot, with pão na chapa and cafezinho. In Mexico la panadería, more of a place to buy bread. The standard roll: in Brazil o pão francês, in Mexico el bolillo. The waiter: in Brazil o garçom, borrowed from French but perfectly standard Portuguese. Never in Mexico — always el mesero there. And the grill: in Brazil o churrasco, the sacred Saturday ritual, in Mexico la carne asada or simply una asada.

Rice and beans in Brazil are not a side-dish luxury — they are daily: arroz e feijão. Worth noting: die Bohne is feminine in German, but o feijão in Portuguese and el frijol in Spanish are masculine, and in Spanish usually in the plural — los frijoles. Three languages, the same plate, different genders.

The false friend at the end, and it can be costly: el menú in Mexico is the menu/bill of fare, not a multi-course meal. Ordering un menú means asking to see the card. The German Menü, meaning several courses at a set price, is la comida corrida in Mexico and o prato do dia or o PF in Brazil. And when the smell of the grill makes your mouth water: in Brazil dá água na boca, in Mexico se me hace agua la boca.
