At the Doctor & At the Office: Symptoms, Pharmacy, Bureaucracy

Sunday, the rotating topic is Politics, Law, Health — but today a different layer than exactly one week ago. On May 24, we covered the institutions: elections, government, constitution, courts, judges, core health vocabulary. To avoid redundant learning, today we focus on practical encounters — the moments when newcomers to Norte Pioneiro really run short of words: at the doctor's (naming symptoms, booking an appointment), at the pharmacy (getting a remedy, bandaging a wound), and at the office (registering, filling out forms, applying for documents). Plus civic life: voting, demonstrating. Thirty-two entries across four clusters: fifteen verbs with full conjugation (three from last Sunday), nouns around body, clinic, and bureaucracy; adjectives for "how are you feeling"; and one idiom. Today two tough spots: first, the German strong health verbs — schwellen (you swell, I have swollen), genesen (I have recovered) and verbinden (bandaged, bandaged) — all three with traps in stem and auxiliary — and second, the Brazil-vs-Mexico terminology of health systems and bureaucracy: posto de saúde/centro de salud, atestado/incapacidad, cadastro/trámite.

  • False friend constipado (ES) ≠ constipated (EN) — in Mexican Spanish, estar constipado means "to have a cold" (stuffy nose!), not "to be constipated." Constipation is MX estreñimiento. In BR-PT, constipado is ambiguous (cold or constipation depending on region) — when in doubt, say estar resfriado for a cold.
  • BR ≠ MX the health center: o posto de saúde / UBS (BR) vs. el centro de salud (MX) — primary care facility. In Brazil, the SUS (Sistema Único de Saúde), entry point is the UBS (Unidade Básica de Saúde) or informally postinho. In Mexico, centro de salud or the IMSS system.
  • BR ≠ MX to book an appointment: marcar (uma) consulta (BR) vs. hacer una cita / agendar (MX) — make a doctor appointment. BR marcar consulta, MX sacar/hacer una cita. The appointment itself: BR a consulta, MX la cita.
  • BR ≠ MX medical certificate: o atestado (médico) (BR) vs. la incapacidad / el justificante (MX) — doctor note for missing work. BR atestado médico, MX la incapacidad (via IMSS) or generally el justificante.
  • Conjugation schwellen (DE) is strong and takes sein: you schwillst, he schwillt (e→i), simple past I schwoll, perfect I bin geschwollen. The ankle swells — not "has swollen."
  • Conjugation genesen (DE) also strong with sein: I genas, I bin genesen, subj. II I genäse. PT/ES regular: eu me recupero / yo me recupero.
  • Conjugation verbinden (DE) strong i–a–u: I verband, have verbunden — with haben, unlike the other two. To bandage a wound: PT enfaixar / fazer um curativo, MX vendar / hacer una curación.
  • Conjugation tossir (PT) shifts stem vowel o→u in 1st person: I tusso, and in subjunctive throughout: that I tussa. ES toser by contrast is regular: I toso.
  • Conjugation manifestar (ES) diphthongizes e→ie under stress (like pensar): I manifiesto, that I manifieste — but nosotros stays regular manifestamos. PT manifestar is regular: I manifesto.
  • Gender the injection (f) → BR a injeção (f) / MX la inyección (f). But "the vaccine" is BR a vacina, MX la vacuna — and the "prick" itself BR a picada, MX el piquete.
  • Phrase to fill out a form — unavoidable at the office: BR preencher um formulário, MX llenar un formulario. "To register": BR se cadastrar, MX registrarse / darse de alta.
  • Idiom to get back on one's feet = to recover → BR ficar de pé / se recuperar, MX ponerse de pie / reponerse. Figuratively, to get back in business after illness or crisis — all three languages use the image of "standing."
  • Review to vaccinate · to examine · to cure return from the Politics/Law/Health lesson of May 24 — today in concrete practical context (having the child vaccinated, the doctor examining the wound, the cold healing by itself).

It's Sunday, the thirty-first of May. The rotating topic is Politics, Law, and Health — but today a different layer than exactly one week ago. On May twenty-fourth, we covered the big institutions: elections, government, courts. To avoid redundant learning, today focuses on practical encounters — the moments when newcomers to Norte Pioneiro really run out of words.

Three settings. First, at the doctor's: booking an appointment, naming a symptom — bleeding, swelling, coughing. Second, at the pharmacy: getting medication, bandaging a wound, recovering. And third, at the office: registering, filling out a form, requesting a certificate.

Thirty-two words in four groups, fifteen of them verbs with complete conjugation. Three you already know from last Sunday — vaccinating, examining, and healing return as review, this time in concrete clinical context.

Today two tricky spots. First, the German strong verbs around health. Schwellen means: you swell, and in the past tense with be — the ankle is swollen, not has swollen. Genesen the same way with be: I have recovered. But verbinden takes have: I have bandaged the wound. Three strong verbs, two different auxiliaries — that's where the trap lies.

Second, the terminology of health systems and bureaucracy that diverges between Brazil and Mexico. In Brazil you go to the posto de saúde, the state Sistema Único de Saúde; in Mexico to the centro de salud. A sick note is atestado in Brazil, incapacidad in Mexico. And you book an appointment with marcar consulta in Brazil, hacer una cita in Mexico.

A false friend to remember: constipado in Mexican Spanish means having a cold — so a runny nose, not constipation. If you mean constipation, you say estreñimiento. When in doubt, just say resfriado for a cold.
