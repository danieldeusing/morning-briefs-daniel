Staying overnight on the road: Check-in, Pousada, front desk

Friday, June 5th — this week's theme is Travel & Transportation, and it's Festa Junina season. In May we covered the basics of travel (driving, flying, luggage) and transport junctions (border, customs, bus, transfers). Today we move one step further: where you sleep on the road. You book a pousada for a Festa weekend, check in, ask if breakfast is included, complain about the broken air conditioning, and rest up. These are exactly the verbs and words you'll need at every Brazilian front desk. 34 entries, 14 verbs with full conjugation, three reviews from May.

  • FALSE FRIEND ES rentar ≠ EN to rent (only) — in Mexico, rentar is the normal word for "to rent" (un carro, un cuarto); in Spain they say alquilar, and rentar means more like "to yield interest".
  • FALSE FRIEND EN to assist ≠ ES asistir — the receptionist wants to help you (ayudar / atender); asistir a means "to attend/be present", not "to assist". At the desk: "¿En qué le puedo ayudar?"
  • IDIOM BR pé na estrada = "to hit the road", literally "foot on the street" — the typical Brazilian expression for setting off.
  • BR-PT a pousada — the small, family-run inn is called a pousada in Brazil (not "a pensão"); it's the standard on a Festa weekend in the interior.
  • BR-PT fazer o check-in / a diária — "to check in" in everyday Brazilian is fazer o check-in, and the room price per night is a diária (in MX la tarifa por noche).
  • CONJUGATION ES/PT incluir shows i→y shift or i-stem: yo incluyo, él incluyó (ES); eu incluo, ele incluiu (PT) — never *inclue.
  • CONJUGATION ES ofrecer / PT oferecer: ES c→zc (yo ofrezco, Subj. ofrezca), PT c→ç before o/a (eu ofereço, Subj. ofereça).
  • CONJUGATION ES traer / PT trazer are strongly irregular: yo traigo, traje, traiga (ES); eu trago, trouxe, traga (PT).
  • CONJUGATION ES encontrarse shows o→ue: me encuentro — but nosotros keeps o (nos encontramos).
  • CONJUGATION DE contain / offer / unlock are strong: enthält, enthielt, hat enthalten; bot an, hat angeboten; schloss auf, hat aufgeschlossen.
  • PHRASE PT o café da manhã está incluso? = "Is breakfast included?" — the sentence that starts everything at a Brazilian front desk.

It's Friday, June fifth, and this week's theme is travel and transportation. It's Festa Junina season in Brazil, so today's vocabulary is about where you sleep on the road: hotels, pousadas, front desk, rooms. Thirty-two entries, fourteen verbs with full conjugation, three reviews from May.

The most important aha-moment for you is in Mexican Spanish. To rent is rentar in Mexico, not alquilar like in Spain. You rent a car, you rent a room. If you say alquilar, everyone understands you, but rentar is the word you hear at a Mexican counter.

With the verbs, full attention is worthwhile at three points. Trazer in Portuguese and traer in Spanish are both strongly irregular. It's eu trago and yo traigo in the present, and trouxe and traje in the past. You have to memorize this one, there's no pattern to help.

Incluir, that is include, is the second trap. In Spanish a y slides in: yo incluyo, él incluyó. In Portuguese it's eu incluo and in the third person ele inclui, never inclue. And ofrecer in Spanish becomes ofrezco in the I-form, the c changes to zc.

On the German side, there are three strong verbs today: enthalten becomes enthielt and hat enthalten, anbieten becomes bot an and hat angeboten, aufschließen becomes schloss auf and hat aufgeschlossen. Words you need at a front desk.

Today's practice assignment: call a nearby pousada and book a room for the Festa weekend. Ask for the nightly rate, the diária, and whether breakfast is included. In Portuguese: o café da manhã está incluso?
