Home Vocabulary: watering, painting, the garden

Wednesday means Family & Everyday Life — and after recent family-themed days covering babies (May 20), household & school (May 27), and errands (June 3), today is all about the home itself: house, garden, and the small repairs that never run out in a house in the Norte Pioneiro. The vocabulary for mowing the lawn, watering flowers, painting walls, drilling, hanging things up, and moving house — verbs with full conjugations plus terms for rooms, garden, and tools, in all four languages side by side.

  • False friend DE "die Decke" has two meanings — the blanket (PT o cobertor / ES la cobija) and the ceiling (PT o teto / ES el techo). One of them you can paint — the other you cannot.
  • False friend ES "renovar" ≠ DE "renovieren" 1:1 — in BR-PT, the word for renovating a house is reformar (doing a reforma), not renovar; renovar in PT means rather "to renew/extend" (a contract, a passport).
  • BR-PT cortar / aparar a grama = to mow the lawn. A grama is the lawn in BR; in Mexico one says el pasto or el césped, "mowing" = podar / cortar el pasto.
  • BR-PT a furadeira = the drill, furar = to drill. In Mexico: el taladro (the tool), taladrar (to drill). Tool names vary widely by language.
  • Conjugation ES regar (to water) has two stem changes: e→ie (yo riego) and g→gu before e (yo regué, subj. riegue). PT regar, by contrast, has no diphthong: eu rego (never *riego), only eu reguei / regue.
  • Conjugation ES colgar (to hang up) likewise double: o→ue (yo cuelgo) + g→gu before e (yo colgué, subj. cuelgue). In nosotros the stem stays plain: colgamos.
  • Conjugation ES renovar (to renovate) o→ue: yo renuevo, but nosotros renovamos without the diphthong — the change only affects stressed forms.
  • Conjugation PT/ES trocar/cambiar (to replace) — PT trocar spells c→qu before e: eu troquei, subj. troque. ES prefers cambiar here (regular).
  • Conjugation DE gießen is strong: ich gieße · goss · habe gegossen · gösse (ablaut ie-o-o, auxiliary haben). streichen is likewise strong: strich · gestrichen.
  • Conjugation DE umziehen ("to move house") forms the perfect with sein: wir sind letztes Jahr umgezogen — not *haben umgezogen. Separable & strong: zieht um, zog um, ist umgezogen.
  • Gender DE "der Garten" (m) ↔ PT "o jardim" (m) ↔ ES "el jardín" (m) — all the same for once. But "das Werkzeug" (n) ↔ PT "a ferramenta" (f) ↔ ES "la herramienta" (f): gender jumps again.
  • Review Today we revisit gießen (from May 30, now in the garden instead of the kitchen), der Eimer · der Besen (from May 27), and die Decke (from May 20, today in the sense of "ceiling").

It's Wednesday, June tenth, and the theme is Family and Everyday Life. After recent family days covering babies, household and school, and running errands, today is all about the home itself: house, garden, and the small repairs that never seem to end.

Today's brief covers twelve verbs with full conjugations and more than thirty entries in total, including three reviews: bucket and broom from the household day on May twenty-seventh, and the word for ceiling/blanket from May twentieth.

For the verbs, the one that deserves the most attention first is gießen — to water plants. In both Spanish and Portuguese that's regar, but watch out: it's a trap. In Spanish the e becomes ie in stressed forms: yo riego, riegas, riega. In Portuguese that diphthong does not happen: you say eu rego, never riego. In both languages, though, g spells as g-u before e: yo regué, eu reguei.

Another Spanish verb with a stem change is colgar, to hang up. The o becomes ue: yo cuelgo, and before e the g becomes g-u: yo colgué. In nosotros the stem stays plain: colgamos. The same applies to renovar: yo renuevo, but nosotros renovamos.

An important lexical trap: in Brazilian Portuguese, renovating a house is reformar, not renovar. Doing a reforma means renovating. Renovar in Portuguese means rather to renew or extend something like a contract or a passport.

For German strong verbs: gießen — ich gieße, goss, habe gegossen. Streichen, to paint a wall — ich streiche, strich, habe gestrichen. And the biggest grammar trap of the day: umziehen, to move house, takes sein in the perfect. You say wir sind letztes Jahr umgezogen, not haben umgezogen.

Among the nouns, the German word Decke is a beautiful false friend. It means two things: the blanket — o cobertor in Portuguese — and the ceiling — o teto. You can paint one of them; the other you cannot. Other key words: a grama is the lawn, a furadeira the drill, o parafuso the screw, and o telhado the tiled roof.
