World Cup vocabulary: anfeuern, vergeben, das Achtelfinale

Thursday means Football & Sport — and tonight the topic fits perfectly: host Mexico opens against South Africa at the Azteca Stadium in World Cup 2026 (21:00 CEST / 16:00 BRT, prime living-room time for Daniel), with Brazil kicking off Saturday against Morocco. The last football day (May 28) covered the basics — das Tor, der Schiedsrichter, der Elfmeter. Today we go one step further: the vocabulary you need to talk about a match — anfeuern and mitfiebern, missing a chance, making a substitution, the round of 16, stoppage time, the score. Full verb conjugations plus tournament terms, four languages side by side.

  • FALSE FRIEND PT „torcer“ ≠ DE „turnen“ — in football, torcer means “to cheer / to be a fan” (eu torço pelo Brasil = I support Brazil), literally “to twist”. Nothing to do with gymnastics.
  • FALSE FRIEND EN „to assist" ≠ DE „assistieren" in football — the goal pass is EN an assist, but in German you say „die Vorlage / das Zuspiel", not „die Assistenz". PT a assistência and ES la asistencia work fine.
  • FALSE FRIEND ES „el campeonato“ vs. „el campo“ — campeonato = the championship/tournament, campo = the pitch. One letter, two different worlds. Same in PT: o campeonato ↔ o campo.
  • BR-PT a torcida = the fans / fanbase (collective noun), o torcedor = an individual fan. In Mexico: la afición (the fanbase), el aficionado / el hincha (an individual fan).
  • BR-PT o goleiro = the goalkeeper (Brazil), in Portugal o guarda-redes. In Mexico: el portero (also el arquero in South America). Three words, one man in goal.
  • CONJUGATION ES jugar (to play) is doubly irregular: u→ue in the stem (yo juego) and g→gu before e (yo jugué, subj. juegue) — the only u→ue verb in Spanish.
  • CONJUGATION ES perder (to lose) e→ie only in stressed forms: yo pierdo, but nosotros perdemos with the bare stem. defender (to defend) works the same way: yo defiendo · nosotros defendemos.
  • CONJUGATION PT torcer / vencer spell c→ç before o/a: eu torço · eu venço, subj. torça · vença — otherwise the c would sound like /s/ instead of /k/.
  • CONJUGATION DE vergeben (to miss a chance) is strong: vergibt · vergab · hat vergeben · vergäbe. pfeifen likewise: pfiff · gepfiffen. übertragen (to broadcast): überträgt · übertrug · hat übertragen, inseparable.
  • CONJUGATION DE ausscheiden & antreten form the perfect tense with sein: Deutschland ist ausgeschieden · Brasilien ist angetreten — never *hat ausgeschieden. Both are strong & separable.
  • GENDER DE „das Tor“ (n) ↔ PT „o gol“ (m) ↔ ES „el gol“ (m). But „das Halbfinale“ (n) ↔ PT „a semifinal“ (f) ↔ ES „la semifinal“ (f): grammatical gender shifts across languages.
  • REVIEW Today we revisit gewinnen · verlieren · schießen (from May 28) — this time in a World Cup context rather than as bare vocabulary basics.

Practice today Watch the opening match (Mexico–South Africa, 21:00 CEST / 16:00 BRT) and then describe in Portuguese what happened: quem ganhou? qual foi o placar? quem marcou o gol? a torcida comemorou? Three or four sentences are enough — today's terms will line up almost naturally.

It's Thursday, June 11th. The World Cup starts tonight — and since Thursday is normally football & sports day anyway, the timing is perfect. Host Mexico opens against South Africa at the Azteca Stadium, kickoff at nine p.m. CEST, which is four p.m. for Daniel. Perfect living-room time to hear vocabulary in practice.

The last football day, at the end of May, covered the basics — das Tor, der Schiedsrichter, der Elfmeter. Today we go a step further: the vocabulary you need to talk about a match, rather than just naming what's on the pitch.

In the pitch verbs, spicy conjugations lurk. Play is jugar in Spanish — the only verb with the u→ue stem change, so yo juego, and also you spell it jugué with u in the past. Lose is perder in Spanish, with e→ie, so yo pierdo, but we lose stays nosotros perdemos, without the diphthong. The exact same logic applies to defend, defender.

In German, many of these verbs are strong. To miss a chance: vergibt, vergab, hat vergeben. The referee whistles: pfeift, pfiff, hat gepfiffen. And a broadcaster airs the match — that's inseparable despite the über- prefix, so hat übertragen.

Two German verbs form the perfect tense with sein instead of haben. Be eliminated: Deutschland ist ausgeschieden. Take the field: Brasilien ist gegen Marokko angetreten. Both are often mixed up with haben — close attention pays off here.

As a fan in front of the TV, comes the day's best false friend. In Portuguese, to cheer is torcer — eu torço pelo Brasil, I support Brazil. Nothing to do with gymnastics. In Mexico you say echarle porras a alguien or simply apoyar.

With tournament terms, grammatical gender bounces happily between languages. The round of 16 is plural in Portuguese and Spanish: as oitavas, los octavos. And in Brazilian Portuguese the knockout stage is wonderfully vivid: o mata-mata — literally "kill-kill", because the loser flies out immediately.

Three or four more words for the periphery wrap up the day: die Vorlage, der Kommentator, die Übertragung. And for practice, there's a task: watch the opening match and then tell in Portuguese who won, what the score was, and whether the crowd celebrated.

Tomorrow the topic shifts to travel & transport — fitting for a World Cup where teams crisscross three countries. Until then: enjoy the whistle.
