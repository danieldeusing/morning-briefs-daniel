Restaurant Vocabulary: reserving, ordering, complaining, paying

Saturday is food day — and today we're going out to eat instead of cooking. The full restaurant arc: booking a table, reading the menu, ordering, lodging a complaint ("this is not what I ordered"), and paying with a tip and a split bill. Ten verbs with full conjugation, plus around 23 nouns, adjectives and phrases — three of them review items — in DE, EN, PT-BR and ES-MX side by side. The focus is on Brazilian restaurant register (o cardápio, o garçom, a conta, a gorjeta), with Mexican contrast (el mesero, la cuenta, la propina). Conjugation traps abound: PT "peço" (d→ç), ES "pido" (e→i), the highly irregular siblings trazer / traer, and the g→gu shift in "paguei / pagué".

  • FALSE FRIEND a entrada (PT/ES) means starter/appetizer in a restaurant — but the same word also means entrance/admission ticket. Context decides: "de entrada, uma salada" = a salad as a starter.
  • FALSE FRIEND reklamieren (DE) ≠ reclamar (PT) — DE "reklamieren" = lodging a specific complaint; PT "reclamar" = complaining/grumbling in general. In ES-MX the preferred verb is "quejarse".
  • CONJUGATION pedir: PT "eu peço" (d→ç, not *pedo!), subjunctive "que eu peça"; ES "yo pido", he/she "pidió" (e→i).
  • CONJUGATION trazer (PT) / traer (ES) highly irregular: "eu trago / eu trouxe" — "yo traigo / yo traje / ellos trajeron".
  • CONJUGATION probar (ES) is o→ue: "yo pruebo", but "nosotros probamos" (no shift). PT "provar" is regular.
  • CONJUGATION pagar: before -e, g becomes gu — PT "eu paguei", ES "yo pagué" (never *pagei / *pagé).
  • CONJUGATION empfehlen (DE) is strong: "er empfiehlt", past empfahl, perfect "hat empfohlen" — and ES "recomendar" diphthongizes: "yo recomiendo".
  • BR-PT o cardápio = the menu (typically Brazilian; Portugal says "a ementa"). And "o garçom / a garçonete" for waiter/waitress.
  • BR-PT a conta = the bill, "a gorjeta" = the tip (in BR usually the waiter's 10%, optional). Standard phrase: "A conta, por favor".
  • PHRASE el mesero / la mesera (ES-MX) = the waiter — not "camarero" (Spain). "La cuenta, por favor" for the bill.
  • GENDER o cardápio (m!) — the -io ending looks feminine, but it's masculine. And "a xícara" (f, BR) = cup vs. "o copo" (m) = glass.
  • PHRASE Está frio / frío — the most common complaint: "o prato está frio" / "está frío" ("the dish is cold").

Mini-Grammar: the d→ç in "peço" and the e→i in "pido"

The key verb of the day — to order — is irregular in both Romance languages, but in different ways. This is the classic stumbling block: learners studying PT and ES in parallel tend to carry one form over to the other — and get it wrong.

pedir, two paths: In Portuguese only the spelling of the first person changes: "eu peço" (d becomes ç to preserve the soft s-sound) and throughout the subjunctive "que eu peça". The stem vowel e stays! In Spanish, by contrast, the stem vowel itself shifts: "yo pido", "tú pides", he/she "pidió" (e→i), but "nosotros pedimos" keeps the e. Mnemonic: "Portuguese swaps the letter, Spanish swaps the vowel."
pedir → peço (PT, d→ç)≠pedir → pido (ES, e→i)

Related, but different again: empfehlen (to recommend). ES "recomendar" diphthongizes under stress ("yo recomiendo", but "nosotros recomendamos" without the shift — like "pensar → pienso"); PT "recomendar" stays fully regular ("eu recomendo"). And "probar" (ES) shifts o→ue: "yo pruebo", "nosotros probamos".

ELI5: why "trazer" and "traer" look so wild

The waiter is constantly bringing something — which makes "trazer"/"traer" the most-used verb at the table. Both are highly irregular, and the stem changes completely depending on tense. Think of it as three building blocks and you won't get tangled up.

trazer / traer (three stems): Three drawers. Present: PT trag- ("eu trago"), ES traig- ("yo traigo"). Past: PT troux- ("eu trouxe"), ES traj- ("yo traje, ellos trajeron"). Future: PT shortens to trar- ("trarei"), ES regular trae- ("traeré"). The subjunctive follows the present: "que eu traga" / "que yo traiga".

Practical at the table: "Você traz mais um copo de água?" (Can you bring another glass of water?) and "O garçom já trouxe a conta" (The waiter already brought the bill).

False Friend & BR-PT: "reclamar" and "a entrada"

Today's false friend is a verb you genuinely need in a restaurant. The German reklamieren is narrow — it means "to flag a specific defect." The Portuguese reclamar is broad: any grumbling, complaining, or griping.

Not like this: treating "reclamar" as only for formal complaints. PT "ele reclama de tudo" = "he complains about everything" — completely everyday.
Instead: For a polite restaurant complaint in ES-MX, use "quejarse": "Quiero quejarme con el mesero". In PT, "reclamar com o garçom" is enough.

And a second classic: a entrada / la entrada means starter in a restaurant — but the same word also means entrance and admission ticket. "De entrada, uma salada" is the appetizer; "a entrada do restaurante" is the door. Context saves you.

BR-PT vs. ES-MX: the same restaurant, different words

When dining out, Brazilian Portuguese and Mexican Spanish often diverge precisely where European varieties would use yet another word:

  • Menu: BR "o cardápio" (Portugal "a ementa"), ES-MX "el menú / la carta".
  • Waiter: BR "o garçom / a garçonete", ES-MX "el mesero / la mesera" — not "camarero" (Spain).
  • Bill: BR "a conta", ES-MX "la cuenta" — neither "a fatura / el cheque" in a restaurant.
  • Tip: BR "a gorjeta" (often the waiter's 10%), ES-MX "la propina".
  • Dish/food item: BR "o prato", ES-MX "el platillo" — not "el plato" (which is more "the plate" in MX).

For Daniel in Norte Pioneiro this means: when booking a table, "o cardápio" and "a conta" come up almost every time, and the service charge ("o serviço", the 10%) is usually already on the bill — legally optional in Brazil.
