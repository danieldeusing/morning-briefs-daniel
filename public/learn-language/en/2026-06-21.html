Vocabulary: World of Work — quit, strike, negotiate

Sunday is the "Law & Rights" day — and after elections, bureaucracy, social security, and the environment, the rotation wheel lands today on Work & Labor Law. Right in Daniel's world: freelance developer in Brazil, contracts in four languages, navigating German permanent-employment logic and the Brazilian CLT (carteira assinada, FGTS, aviso prévio). Twelve verbs with full conjugation — hire, quit, lay off, strike, negotiate, demand — plus around thirteen nouns (salary, union, severance) and seven adjectives, opposite pairs, and phrases, four of them review items, in DE, EN, PT-BR, and ES-MX side by side. Conjugation traps abound: the strong verb "entlassen" (er entlässt, entließ, hat entlassen), the g→j in ES/PT "exijo", the e→i in "pido/despidió" — and the separable verbs "stellt ein" / "rechnet ab".

  • FALSE FRIEND der Kollege (DE) ≠ college (EN) — Kollege = colleague / coworker, not "college" (= university). PT "colega (de trabalho)", ES "colega / compañero de trabajo".
  • FALSE FRIEND a demissão (PT) is ambiguous: depending on context it means dismissal by the employer OR resigning yourself. "pedir demissão" = to resign; "ser demitido" = to be fired.
  • CONJUGATION entlassen is strong: "er entlässt", simple past entließ, present perfect "hat entlassen", subjunctive II entließe — never *entlasste!
  • CONJUGATION exigir (ES/PT): g→j only before o/a — "yo/eu exijo", subjunctive "exija", but "tú exiges" keeps the g.
  • CONJUGATION despedir (ES) shifts e→i: "yo despido", he/she "despidió", but "nosotros despedimos" keeps the e.
  • CONJUGATION einstellen / abrechnen are separable: "ich stelle ihn ein", "hat ihn eingestellt"; "ich rechne ab", "hat abgerechnet".
  • BR-PT streiken has no verb equivalent in PT/ES — it's a phrase: "fazer greve / entrar em greve" (PT), "hacer huelga / ir a la huelga" (ES). The noun: a greve / la huelga.
  • BR-PT a CLT = Brazil's labor code; "carteira assinada" = formally employed with a signed work booklet (CTPS); "autônomo / MEI" = self-employed.
  • GENDER das Gehalt (n) vs. "o salário (m)" (PT) / "el sueldo (m)" (ES) — gender differs; and "die Überstunden" is plural-only ("as horas extras", "las horas extra").
  • PHRASE sich beschweren (über) — reflexive in DE: "ich beschwere mich beim Betriebsrat". PT "reclamar (com)", ES "quejarse (con)".
  • PHRASE eine Stelle ausschreiben = "abrir / anunciar uma vaga" (PT), "publicar una vacante" (ES). Severance pay: a indenização (PT) / la liquidación (ES).
  • IDIOM auf großem Fuß leben = to spend more than you earn: "viver acima das posses" (PT), "vivir por encima de sus posibilidades" (ES).

Mini Grammar: the strong verb "entlassen" and the g→j in "exijo"

The trickiest verb of the day is entlassen. It looks like a weak "-lassen word", but it is in fact a strong verb (ablaut class 7) — and that is exactly why learners keep producing wrong forms like *entlasste.

entlassen — three things to remember: First, in the present tense the stem vowel shifts to an umlaut: "er entlässt" (not *entlasst). Second, the simple past is entließ with a long ß sound (not *entlasste). Third, the past participle matches the infinitive: "hat entlassen" — and the subjunctive II is the synthetic form entließe, not "würde entlassen". The auxiliary is always haben.

On the Romance side, a spelling trap lurks in exigir (to demand). In both Spanish and Portuguese: g changes to j only when followed by o or a — to preserve the soft sound.

exig- + o → exijoaberexig- + e → exiges (g bleibt)

Specifically: "yo/eu exijo" and the whole subjunctive "exija, exijas, exijamos" switch to j; "tú exiges", "exigimos", and the preterite "exigí/exigi" keep the g because e or i follows. The same pattern as "recoger → recojo".

ELI5: why "streiken" has no verb form in PT/ES

In German, streiken is a clean verb — you can conjugate it ("ich streike, du streikst"). In Portuguese and Spanish, no such verb exists: you make a strike.

fazer greve / hacer huelga: Think of "strike" (a greve / la huelga) as an object you do. What gets conjugated is the auxiliary "to make" (fazer / hacer), while the noun stays fixed: "eu faço greve", "nós fazemos greve" — "yo hago huelga", "nosotros hacemos huelga". The same goes for "entrar em greve" / "ir a la huelga" (to go on strike).

In practice: you need to master "fazer" and "hacer" (both highly irregular — "fiz / hice" in the preterite), but the word for "strike" itself never changes. One fewer verb to memorize, one irregular auxiliary more to practice.

False Friend & BR-PT: "a demissão" and "der Kollege"

The most dangerous false friend today is the Portuguese a demissão. Unlike the German "Entlassung", it is ambiguous: it can mean dismissal by the employer or resigning yourself — the direction is determined by the verb in front of it.

Trap: "ele pediu demissão" does not mean "he was fired" — it means "he resigned". "pedir demissão" = to ask for your own dismissal = to leave voluntarily.
Correct: Being fired is "ser demitido" or "ser dispensado". Resigning is "pedir demissão". In ES-MX the same split: "me despidieron" (fired) vs. "renuncié" (resigned).

And the classic for every office day: der Kollege is not the English "college" (= university), but "colleague / coworker". PT "o colega (de trabalho)", ES "el colega / el compañero de trabajo" — in Latin America you hear "compañero" more often on the job.

BR-PT vs. DE: same termination, different legal world

For Daniel as a developer in Brazil, it is worth noting how differently the words reflect their legal world — the Brazilian CLT system and German permanent employment often do not map onto each other at all:

  • Permanent employment: DE "festangestellt", BR "carteira assinada" (= with a registered CTPS work booklet), ES-MX "de planta".
  • Self-employed: DE "freiberuflich / selbstständig", BR "autônomo / MEI / PJ", ES-MX "freelance / autónomo".
  • Severance: DE "die Abfindung", BR "a indenização" plus "FGTS" (mandatory savings fund), ES-MX "la liquidación".
  • Notice period: DE "die Kündigungsfrist", BR "o aviso prévio" (min. 30 days), ES-MX "el aviso".
  • Probation: DE up to 6 months, BR "período de experiência" — under the CLT two phases of 45 days each, totaling at most 90.

In practice: anyone signing a contract in Brazil should know the difference between "carteira assinada" and "PJ" (pessoa jurídica = billing as a company entity) — that choice determines entitlement to FGTS, aviso prévio, and indenização if the contract ends.

It is Sunday, June twenty-first — the Law and Rights day in this language brief. After elections, bureaucracy, social security, and the environment, the rotation wheel lands today on Work and Labor Law. That is Daniel's daily reality: freelance developer in Brazil, contracts in four languages, moving between German permanent-employment logic and the Brazilian CLT with carteira assinada, FGTS, and aviso prévio. Twelve verbs with full conjugation, around thirteen nouns, and seven adjectives, opposite pairs, and phrases — four of them review items — in German, English, Brazilian Portuguese, and Mexican Spanish, side by side.

The trickiest verb of the day is entlassen, to lay off. It looks harmless but it is a strong verb. Three things to remember: in the present tense the stem vowel shifts to an umlaut, so "er entlässt", not "entlasst". The simple past is "entließ", not "entlasste". And the past participle stays the same as the infinitive: "hat entlassen". The auxiliary is always haben.

On the Romance side, a spelling trap hides in exigir, to demand. In both Spanish and Portuguese the g changes to j only when followed by an o or an a — to keep the soft consonant sound intact. So "yo exijo", "eu exijo", and the entire subjunctive "exija". But "tú exiges" and "exigimos" keep the g because an e or i follows. The exact same pattern as recoger to recojo.

One neat quirk: the German verb streiken has no verb equivalent in Portuguese or Spanish. Instead, you make a strike. What gets conjugated is the auxiliary to make — "eu faço greve", "nós fazemos greve", and "yo hago huelga", "nosotros hacemos huelga". The word for strike itself, a greve or la huelga, never changes. One fewer verb to memorize, but you do need to master fazer and hacer.

Today's false friend is a demissão. Unlike the German Entlassung, it is ambiguous: it can mean being fired by the boss or resigning yourself. "Pedir demissão" does not mean being laid off — it means quitting. Being fired is "ser demitido". Mexican Spanish works the same way: "me despidieron" for being fired, "renuncié" for resigning. And the classic office mix-up: der Kollege is not the English "college", meaning university — it means colleague or coworker, Portuguese colega, Spanish often compañero.

The vocabulary section is also a tour of two very different labor law systems. In Brazil, permanent employment is "carteira assinada", being self-employed is "autônomo" or "PJ", severance pay is the indenização plus the mandatory savings fund FGTS, and the notice period is the aviso prévio of at least thirty days. The probation period, the período de experiência, runs under the CLT in two phases of forty-five days each, capped at ninety total.
