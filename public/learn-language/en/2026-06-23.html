Vocabulary Contracts & Trade: conclude, liable, terminate

Monday, June 23 — the fifth and final round of this week's Economics & Finance theme. After fundamentals, work, taxes, and banking, today we focus on contract law: concluding a contract, negotiating a clause, a deadline expires, someone is liable for damages. Exactly the vocabulary a self-employed person in Brazil needs — service contracts, suppliers, the nota fiscal (tax invoice), and the ubiquitous procuração at the cartório (notary). 32 new entries, 12 verbs with full conjugation, 3 reviews from tax week (8 days ago — perfect SRS rhythm).

  • FALSE FRIEND EN actual ≠ DE aktuell — EN actual = really/actually, not "current". DE aktuell = current, up to date. PT atual = current (also ≠ EN actual).
  • FALSE FRIEND PT compromisso ≠ EN compromise — compromisso = appointment/obligation; EN compromise (Kompromiss) = PT acordo / concessão. Classic trap.
  • FALSE FRIEND PT/ES vencer in contract context = expire (o prazo vence = the deadline expires), not "win". Same word, two completely separate meanings.
  • CONJUGATION DE abschließen is strong and separable: ich schließe ab, preterite ich schloss ab, perfect ich habe den Vertrag abgeschlossen (ie-o-o ablaut, like schließen).
  • CONJUGATION PT redigir has a spelling change g→j: eu redijo (not "redigo"), subjunctive que eu redija. Systematic for all -gir verbs (dirigir, exigir).
  • CONJUGATION PT/ES vencer has c→ç/c→z before a/o: PT eu venço, ES yo venzo; otherwise regular (vence, venceu / venció).
  • GENDER the contract (m), the clause (f), the offer (n) — three genders in one topic. PT: o contrato (m), a cláusula (f), o orçamento (m).
  • BR-PT nota fiscal / NF-e — in Brazil every B2B transaction requires an Nota Fiscal eletrônica: a government-mandated e-invoice with CNPJ and chave de acesso. "the invoice / la factura" is the umbrella term; a nota fiscal is the BR legal instrument.
  • BR-PT a procuração = power of attorney. Brazil has strong notary culture: almost every formal act requires a procuração reconhecida em cartório (notarized). ES: el poder notarial.
  • IDIOM fechar um negócio (PT) = to seal a deal, literally "close a business". ES (MX): cerrar el trato (trato = arrangement, ≠ contrato, the written contract).
  • IDIOM to wrap something up (EN) = to conclude binding / signed & sealed. PT fechar / acertar, ES dejar cerrado.
  • PHRASE The contract enters into force = o contrato entra em vigor = el contrato entra en vigor. Opposite: the contract is void/invalid = nulo / nulo.

Pay special attention today to one German verb: abschließen. It's strong and separable. In the present it's ich schließe ab, in the past ich schloss ab, and in the perfect ich habe den Vertrag abgeschlossen. Never abgeschließt — the stem shifts from ie to o, just like the root word schließen.

In Portuguese there's a tricky spelling rule in the verb redigir, meaning to draft a document. In the first-person form, the g becomes j: eu redijo, not redigo. And in the subjunctive the same: que eu redija. This applies systematically to all -gir verbs, like dirigir or exigir.

And a second conjugation trick that Portuguese and Spanish share: vencer, in contract context meaning to expire. Before a and o the c shifts: in Portuguese eu venço with cedilla, in Spanish yo venzo with z. Otherwise everything stays regular: vence, venceu, venció.

Three false friends for today. First, the classic: the English word actual means really/actually, not current — current in English is actually "current". Second: the Portuguese compromisso is an appointment or an obligation, not a compromise; the compromise is called acordo. Third: vencer in a contract means to expire, not to win — the same word-body, two separate worlds.

Two distinctly Brazilian terms you need constantly in daily life. The Nota Fiscal, short NF-e, is the government-mandated electronic invoice with CNPJ — without it, a B2B deal in Brazil simply isn't legal. And the procuração is power of attorney; Brazil has a strong notary culture, almost every formal act requires a procuração, certified at the cartório.

Finally, two idioms for business: in German etwas unter Dach und Fach bringen, in Brazilian Portuguese fechar um negócio, in Mexican Spanish cerrar el trato — all three mean to seal a deal.
