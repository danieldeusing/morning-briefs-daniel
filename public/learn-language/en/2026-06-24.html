Vocabulary Networks: connect, send, the packet

Tuesday, June 24 — round five of the week's Software & Technology theme. After fundamentals, bugs & deployments, IT security, and AI tools, today we tackle the wiring underneath: Networks, Web & Protocols. How data connects, transmits, sends, receives — and the vocabulary you need to talk in PT-BR about a latência, a banda larga, o pacote and a nuvem when the fiber in Norte Pioneiro acts up again. Ties into today's software headline (CVSS 10.0 at PTC Windchill — a network vulnerability). 32 new entries, 12 verbs with full conjugation, 3 repeats from the tech series (network, server, error).

  • FALSE FRIEND EN actual ≠ DE aktuell — EN actual = real/genuine, not "current/today's". DE aktuell = current; PT atual = current (also ≠ EN actual). The classic false friend holds.
  • FALSE FRIEND EN to load ≠ DE laden only partially: DE laden covers load (data) and "charge/invite". For the battery it's PT carregar, ES cargar — the same verb for "loading a file" and "charging the phone".
  • Conjugation DE laden is strong with umlaut: ich lade, but du lädst / er lädt; simple past lud, perfect habe geladen. Same pattern for herunterladen / hochladen (lud herunter, hat hochgeladen).
  • Conjugation PT carregar / ES cargar have the spelling shift g→gu before e: eu carreguei (not "carregei"), subjunctive carregue; ES yo cargué, subjunctive cargue. Preserves the soft g sound.
  • Conjugation ES enviar takes a written accent on the stressed i in the present: yo envío, tú envías, él envía, ellos envían — but nosotros enviamos (no accent). PT enviar has no such accent: eu envio.
  • Conjugation DE verbinden is strong (i–a–u): verbinde / verband / habe verbunden. empfangen a–i–a with umlaut: empfange, but du empfängst / er empfängt; simple past empfing.
  • Gender das Paket (n), die Verbindung (f), der Router (m) — three genders. PT: o pacote (m), a conexão (f), o roteador (m); ES: el paquete, la conexión, el router.
  • BR-PT baixar / fazer download — Brazilians say baixar um arquivo (to download), not the European descarregar. Upload = subir or fazer upload.
  • BR-PT a net caiu = "the network is down". Colloquially a net (f.) means the internet, and o wi-fi is masculine in BR (pronounced "uái-fái"). ES (MX): se cayó el internet.
  • Phrase die Verbindung trennen = desconectar = desconectar. Counterpart to sich verbinden (conectar-se / conectarse).
  • Idiom am seidenen Faden hängen (DE, for a shaky connection) ≈ PT por um fio, ES de un hilo — "a conexão está por um fio".
  • Phrase Die Anfrage läuft in ein Timeout. = A requisição expira (dá timeout). = La solicitud expira (caduca). Useful when the API does not respond.

Vocabulary: Network Verbs

32 new entries + 3 repeats · 12 verbs with full conjugation · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Protocol: the fixed rules two computers follow when talking — who says what when and how they respond (HTTP for the web, TCP/IP for routing). Think of a radio code: "Over" tells the other side it's their turn. Latency is the delay until the reply comes back (the "ping"); bandwidth is how much can pass through at once (pipe diameter), throughput is how much actually arrives. High bandwidth + low latency = smooth; that's the goal.

Today's brief has thirty-two new entries and twelve verbs with full conjugation across all four languages, plus three repeats from the tech series: the network from May twenty-sixth, the server from June ninth, and the error from June second — all words that fit right into today's networking theme.

One German verb to watch today: laden. It's a strong verb with an umlaut in the present tense. First person is ich lade, but second is du lädst and third is er lädt. The simple past is ich lud, the perfect ich habe geladen. The same pattern applies to herunterladen and hochladen — ich lud herunter, ich habe hochgeladen.

In Portuguese and Spanish, the word for "load" hides a spelling rule. Portuguese carregar, Spanish cargar — before an e, the g becomes g-u to keep the soft sound. So in the preterite it's eu carreguei and yo cargué, in the subjunctive carregue and cargue. Never carregei or cargé.

A second conjugation trick: the Spanish enviar, meaning "to send." Under stress, the i takes a written accent. Yo envío, tú envías, él envía, ellos envían — but nosotros enviamos with no accent. In Portuguese the accent disappears entirely: eu envio.

Two false friends for today. First, the classic: English actual means real or genuine, not current — current in English is current. Second, a partial false friend: German laden covers both English load and "charge the battery" — and in Portuguese both meanings share the same verb, carregar.

A few characteristically Brazilian expressions worth knowing. Download in Brazilian Portuguese is baixar, not the European descarregar; upload is subir or fazer upload. And when the internet goes down, Brazilians simply say: a net caiu. The word wi-fi is masculine in Brazil and pronounced "uái-fái."
