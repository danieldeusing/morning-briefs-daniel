Vocabulary: Leadership — liderar, das Ziel, yo dirijo

Monday, June 30 — Weekly theme Business & Finance, today Part VI: Corporate Leadership & Management. The vocabulary you need to run a team, set a strategy, and track your numbers: leading a team (liderar), delegating tasks, defining a strategy, measuring goals, and knowing whether you're on track. 31 entries, 11 verbs with full conjugation — including the notorious spelling traps deleguei / delegué (g→gu) and yo dirijo (g→j) — plus 3 refreshers from the Freelancer Monday of June 1 and the Investment Monday of June 15 (gründen, diversifizieren, der Chef).

  • FALSE FRIEND DE Chef ≠ EN chef — the German Chef is your boss or manager, not a cook. EN chef = DE der Küchenchef / PT o chef de cozinha / ES el chef. PT for "boss" = o chefe, ES = el jefe.
  • FALSE FRIEND DE Kontrolle ≠ EN control — the German Kontrolle usually means a check or inspection. EN control in the sense of "steering/command" is DE die Steuerung. The business term Controlling blends both meanings.
  • FALSE FRIEND EN to assist ≠ "assistir" — PT assistir mostly means "to watch / attend" (assistir à reunião = to attend/watch the meeting). "To help someone" is PT ajudar, ES ayudar. EN to assist = to help.
  • BR-PT gerir vs. liderar/administrar — "to run a company" in BR-PT is gerir or administrar; to "lead" a team is liderar. Dirigir means both "to manage/steer" and "to drive a car" — context decides.
  • CONJUGATION PT/ES delegar → deleguei / delegué — spelling trap: the preterite 1st person inserts a u to keep the g hard (g→gu). Never *delegei / *delegé. In the subjunctive: delegue / delegue.
  • CONJUGATION PT/ES dirigir → eu dirijo / yo dirijo — before o/a, g→j to preserve the soft sound: 1st-person present dirijo, full subjunctive dirija. Você dirige keeps the g.
  • CONJUGATION PT/ES medir → eu meço / yo mido — two different traps: PT has c→ç (eu meço, que eu meça), ES has a stem change e→i (yo mido, él midió, but nosotros medimos).
  • CONJUGATION DE scheitern → ich bin gescheitert — motion/state-change verb, perfect with sein, never *habe gescheitert. "Die Verhandlung ist gescheitert", "Das Projekt ist an den Kosten gescheitert".
  • CONJUGATION DE -ieren → no "ge-" — verbs ending in -ieren form the past participle without ge-: delegiert (not *gedelegiert), kommuniziert, diversifiziert. Compare: führen → geführt (with ge-).
  • CONJUGATION DE messen → du misst, ich maß — strong verb with e→i: du misst, er misst (not *messt), preterite maß, subjunctive II mäße, participle gemessen.
  • GENDER die Führung (f), das Ziel (n), das Controlling (n) — three genders. PT: a liderança (f), a meta (f), o controle (m); ES-MX: el liderazgo (m), la meta (f), el control (m). Don't guess gender — it diverges across languages.
  • PHRASE estar no caminho certo (PT) = "to be on track" = ES vamos por buen camino. The opposite: não atingir a meta / no alcanzar la meta — to miss the target.

Leadership Verbs: to lead, to delegate, to measure

11 verbs with full conjugation + refreshers · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Conjugation: adapting a verb to match the person and tense — like a garment tailored to its wearer ("ich führe, du führst, ich führte"). Spelling changes (PT/ES): the written form adjusts to preserve the spoken sound. With delegar, an inserted u keeps the g hard (deleguei / delegué); with dirigir, before o/a the g becomes j (dirijo, dirija) to keep the soft sound. Stem-vowel change: in ES medir, the stressed syllable shifts e→i (yo mido) — but with nosotros the stem stays plain (medimos). Perfect with "sein" (DE): motion/change verbs like scheitern form the past with sein instead of haben — ich bin gescheitert, never *ich habe gescheitert. And -ieren verbs (DE, borrowed from Latin) drop the ge- in the participle: delegiert, not *gedelegiert.

Grammar of the Day DE -ieren verbs — past participle without "ge-". Verbs ending in -ieren form their past participle without the ge- prefix: delegieren → delegiert, kommunizieren → kommuniziert, diversifizieren → diversifiziert, investieren → investiert. Compare: führen → geführt (with ge-, because it is not a -ieren verb). Why? The -ieren verbs are borrowed from French/Latin — the ge- is a Germanic past-tense marker that these Romance loanwords do not take. Memory hook: if the infinitive ends in -ieren, drop the ge-.
Idiom "das Ruder in der Hand behalten" — to keep the helm. Literally: das Ruder is a boat's steering oar; in der Hand behalten means not letting it go. Figuratively: to stay in control, not to let the situation slip away — especially when external pressure mounts. "Auch in der Krise hat sie das Ruder in der Hand behalten." EN to keep the helm / stay at the wheel · PT manter o leme / ter as rédeas nas mãos · ES mantener el timón / llevar las riendas.

It's Monday, June 30. This week's theme is Business and Finance, and today we're focusing on the part that touches every manager: leading a team, delegating tasks, setting a strategy, and measuring your own numbers.

The centerpiece is the leadership verbs. Liderar means to lead a team; dirigir or gerir means to run an organization. And with dirigir comes the first classic pitfall: in the first-person form, the g becomes a j — eu dirijo and yo dirijo — to preserve the soft sound. The same j appears across the full subjunctive: dirija.

Delegating brings the next trap. In the preterite, first person, Portuguese spells it de-le-guei and Spanish spells it de-le-gué — an inserted u to keep the g hard before the e. Never delegei or delegé.

The verb "to measure" is tricky in three different ways. In German it's a strong verb: du misst, ich maß, ich habe gemessen. In Portuguese the first-person form is meço, with a cedilla. And in Spanish the stem vowel shifts from e to i — yo mido — but at nosotros the stem stays plain: medimos.

On the German side, the most important verb to get right is scheitern, meaning to fail. It's a change-of-state verb and takes sein in the perfect, not haben. The correct form is ich bin gescheitert — never ich habe gescheitert. Connected to this is today's grammar rule: verbs that end in i-e-r-e-n drop the ge in the past participle. It's delegiert, kommuniziert, diversifiziert — never gedelegiert.

Two false friends are especially worth noting today. The German Chef is your boss or manager, not a cook. And the German Kontrolle usually means a check or inspection, not "control" in the steering sense. The business term Controlling happens to blend both meanings.

Three refreshers return from earlier weeks: gründen from June first, diversifizieren from June fifteenth, and der Chef, also from June first. All three fit naturally into today's leadership theme.
