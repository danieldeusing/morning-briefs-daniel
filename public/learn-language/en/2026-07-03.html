Vocabulary Behind the Wheel: refueling, overtaking, breaking down

Friday, July 3rd — this week's topic is Travel & Transportation, focused today on driving, renting a car & roadside breakdowns. Earlier travel days covered airports, borders, buses, lodging, and online booking; today you're behind the wheel yourself: picking up a rental car at the car rental agency, refueling, paying the toll at the toll plaza, overtaking safely — and knowing what to say if you get a flat tire or your car breaks down. 31 entries, 12 verbs with full conjugations (including 3 refreshers from June's travel day), plus Brazilian toll vocabulary and the Mexican term for a flat tire.

  • FALSE FRIEND EN to park ≠ DE parken, but watch out for „das Parkett" — and more importantly: PT estacionar = to park, but a estação means "station," not "parking lot." The parking lot is o estacionamento (BR) / el estacionamiento (ES-MX).
  • FALSE FRIEND ES rebasar ≠ DE „rasen" (to speed) — in Mexico, rebasar means "to overtake," not "to speed." And rebasar ≠ rebosar ("to overflow") — one letter, two different worlds.
  • BR-PT o pedágio — in Brazil the toll is called pedágio (EP: portagem). On Daniel's routes (BR-153, BR-369, PR-151), EPR Litoral Pioneiro operates the praças de pedágio (toll plazas); the booths accept PIX, cash, and card.
  • BR-PT o estepe — in Brazil the spare tire is colloquially called estepe (from English "stepney"), not pneu suplente as in Portugal. "To change a tire" = trocar o pneu, to grab the spare = pegar o estepe.
  • BR-PT furar / se ponchó — "to get a flat" is BR furar o pneu (o pneu furou), ES-MX poncharse la llanta (se ponchó la llanta). In Mexico, ponchar is standard; pinchar is more Spain/South America.
  • CONJUGATION DE liegenbleiben → perfect tense with „sein" — when the car stalls: ich bin liegengeblieben, not *habe liegengeblieben. But anhalten (to deliberately stop) takes haben: ich habe angehalten. Two similar verbs, two different auxiliaries.
  • CONJUGATION DE abbiegen → „sein", strong verb: ich bog ab, ich bin abgebogen. abschleppen → „haben", weak verb: ich habe abgeschleppt. Movement with a change of direction = sein; towing an object = haben.
  • CONJUGATION PT alugar (to rent) has the g→gu trap: 1st person perfect aluguei, subjunctive alugue — the u keeps the g hard. Same with verificar and its c→qu trap: verifiquei, verifique.
  • CONJUGATION PT abastecer (to refuel) has the c→ç trap before a/o: eu abasteço, subjunctive abasteça — the ç keeps the soft s-sound.
  • GENDER der Mietwagen (m), die Reifenpanne (f), das Reserverad (n) — three genders. PT: o carro alugado (m), o pneu furado (m), o estepe (m); ES-MX: el carro rentado (m), la llanta ponchada (f), la llanta de refacción (f). Don't guess the gender.
  • PHRASE Mein Auto ist liegengeblieben. = PT Meu carro quebrou / deu pane. = ES-MX Mi carro se descompuso. The sentence you'll need on the shoulder of the road.

Vocabulary: Verbs Behind the Wheel

9 fresh verbs + 3 refreshers, each with full conjugations · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Present perfect with „sein" (the 'to be' auxiliary) (DE): verbs of motion or change of state form the past tense with sein instead of haben — ich bin abgebogen, ich bin liegengeblieben, not *ich habe …. Verbs with a direct object (stopping something, towing a car) stick with haben. Separable Verb (DE): the prefix moves to the end of the main clause — ich halte an, ich biege ab — in the perfect tense the ge- sits in the middle: an·ge·halten, ab·ge·bogen. Spelling Changes (PT): the spelling adapts to preserve the sound — alugar becomes aluguei in the perfect (the u keeps the g hard), verificar becomes verifiquei (the qu keeps the k-sound), abastecer becomes abasteço (the ç keeps the s-sound).

Pay special attention today to German auxiliary verbs in the perfect tense. Movement and change of state verbs take "be": I turned, I drove, I broke down. But if you stop consciously or tow a car, you use "have": I stopped, I towed. Two nearly identical verbs, break down and stop, with two different auxiliary verbs.

In Portuguese, there are three spelling traps right away. Renting is alugar, and in the first-person past tense it becomes aluguei with an inserted u to keep the g hard. Checking is verificar, and it becomes verifiquei with qu to maintain the k sound. And refueling is abastecer, with c-cedilla: eu abasteço.

Two important words for Brazil and Mexico. In Brazil, the toll is pedágio, and on Daniel's routes, the BR-153, BR-369, and PR-151, EPR Litoral Pioneiro operates the toll plazas; you pay with PIX, cash, or card. The spare tire is Brazilian estepe, not pneu suplente.

For a flat tire, Brazil and Mexico diverge. In Brazil you say o pneu furou, the verb is furar. In Mexico you say se ponchó la llanta, the verb is poncharse — the Spanish pinchar is almost never heard in Mexico; it's more of a Spain and South American word.
