Vocab: Frontend & QA — testen, corregir, der Button

Tuesday, July 7 — weekly theme Software & Technology, today Part VII: Frontend, UI & Quality Assurance (Testing/QA). The vocabulary for clicking and scrolling an interface, filling in an input field, checking a test case, fixing a bug, and seeing whether the test is green or fails. 33 new entries, 12 verbs with full conjugation — including the notorious Spanish traps yo corrijo (g→j) and yo introduzco (c→zc) — plus 3 refreshers from earlier software Tuesdays. Grammar focus: why German überprüfen gets no ge- in the participle while eingeben does, and the neat triad of PT corrijo, PT insiro, and ES introduzco, where Portuguese and Spanish solve the same idea with different letters.

  • FALSE FRIEND DE Kontrolle ≠ EN control — EN control means "to steer/regulate" (a control element in a UI), while DE Kontrolle means "inspection/check." A form control is a UI element, not a "control" in the German sense. PT o controle (BR) covers both meanings, as does ES el control — only German draws a strict line.
  • FALSE FRIEND PT realizar ≠ EN to realize — PT realizar means "to carry out/perform" (realizar um teste = to run a test), not "to become aware of." EN to realize in everyday use means "to notice." For "to implement" in software: EN implement, PT implementar, ES implementar.
  • CONJUGATION ES corregir → yo corrijo — a double trap: the g→j spelling shift before o/a (corrijo, never *corrigo; subjunctive corrija) plus the e→i stem change (corriges, corrige). PT corrigir has only the g→j trap: eu corrijo, but the equivalent of "corriges" stays corrige.
  • CONJUGATION ES introducir → yo introduzco — c→zc before o/a (introduzco, subjunctive introduzca) AND an irregular preterite on -duje: yo introduje, él introdujo (never *introdució). One of the most irregular -ir verbs.
  • CONJUGATION PT inserir → eu insiro — an e→i stem change in the 1st person: eu insiro (never *insero), but você insere. Subjunctive insira. The same pattern as ES introducir, just with a different letter.
  • CONJUGATION DE überprüfen → ich überprüfe — inseparable: über- stays fixed to the stem. Participle überprüft — without ge-. Compare the separable eingeben → eingegeben, which slots the ge- into the middle.
  • CONJUGATION DE fehlschlagen → ist fehlgeschlagen — separable and strong: der Test schlägt fehl, schlug fehl, perfect tense with sein (change of state): der Build ist fehlgeschlagen. Subjunctive II schlüge fehl.
  • GENDER der Button (m), das Eingabefeld (n), die Oberfläche (f) — three grammatical genders side by side. PT o botão (m), o campo (m), a interface (f); ES el botón (m), el campo (m), la interfaz (f). "die interface" doesn't exist in German — it's die Schnittstelle (f) or the loanword das Interface (n).
  • BR-PT printar / tirar um print — "to take a screenshot" in everyday Brazilian tech speak is printar or tirar um print (from EN print screen); standard PT would be fazer uma captura de tela. In ES-MX: tomar una captura (de pantalla). An anglicism, but ubiquitous on BR WhatsApp/Slack.
  • BR-PT o mouse / clicar vs. o rato — the mouse is o mouse in Brazil (an anglicism, universal), and o rato in European PT. "To click" is clicar: clicar no botão. ES-MX el ratón / hacer clic.
  • IDIOM Der Test ist grün / rot. = PT O teste passou / falhou. = ES La prueba pasó / falló. — in everyday QA talk, "green" means passed and "red" means failed; the neutral form is der Test besteht / schlägt fehl.
  • PHRASE auf den Button klicken und das Formular absenden = PT clicar no botão e enviar o formulário = ES hacer clic en el botón y enviar el formulario. "The layout is broken on the phone" = O layout está quebrado no celular = El diseño está roto en el celular.

Testing, checking & fixing — the QA verbs

12 verbs with full conjugation + refreshers · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Separable vs. inseparable (DE): a German prefix is like a trailer hitch — some detach in the sentence, some are welded on for good. eingeben is separable: ich gebe den Code ein, and the participle slots the ge- into the middle: eingegeben. überprüfen is inseparable: the über- stays put — ich überprüfe — and the participle gets no ge- at all: überprüft. g→j trap (ES/PT): before an o or a, a g would sound "hard" like a k — to keep the soft sound, it's written as j. That's why corregir/corrigir becomes corrijo (1st person) and, in the subjunctive, corrija. c→zc trap (ES): verbs ending in -ducir like introducir insert a z in the 1st person: introduzco — and their preterite is irregular, ending in -duje: yo introduje.

It's Tuesday, July seventh. This week's theme is software and technology, and today we're covering the front end, the interface, and quality assurance: operating an app, checking a test case, fixing a bug, and seeing whether the test passes or fails.

In German, the spotlight falls again on separable versus inseparable verbs. The verb eingeben, meaning to enter or input, is separable and also strong: ich gebe den Code ein, in the past tense gab ich ein, and the participle tucks the ge right into the middle: eingegeben. The verb überprüfen, meaning to check or verify, is inseparable: ich überprüfe, and its participle gets no ge at all: überprüft.

A second German verb deserves a close look: fehlschlagen, to fail. It's separable and strong, and it forms the present perfect with sein because it describes a change of state. So you say der Build ist fehlgeschlagen, the build has failed, never hat fehlgeschlagen.

Today's trickiest traps are hiding in Spanish and Portuguese. The verb corregir, to correct, stacks two changes at once: before an o or a, the g becomes j, so yo corrijo, and on top of that the stem shifts from e to i, giving corriges and corrige. In Portuguese, corrigir only has the first trap: eu corrijo, but você corrige.

Even more irregular is the Spanish introducir, to enter or insert. In the first person it inserts a z: yo introduzco. And in the past tense it's fully irregular: yo introduje, él introdujo, never introdució. In Portuguese, inserir expresses the same idea with a stem change: eu insiro, but você insere.

Among the nouns, it's worth noting the genders. The button is masculine, der Button; the input field is neuter, das Eingabefeld; the interface is feminine, die Oberfläche. And there's a false friend: the English word control corresponds to the German steuern, to steer or operate, not to Kontrolle, which means an inspection or check. A UI control is really ein Bedienelement, an operating element.

From everyday Brazilian usage come two nice words. Taking a screenshot is printar or tirar um print. And the mouse is o mouse in Brazil, not o rato as in Portugal. To click is clicar: clicar no botão.

For review, three words return: the verbs optimieren and generieren from AI Tools Tuesday, and the noun der Fehler, the error, from Networks Tuesday. All three fit perfectly with today's front-end theme.
