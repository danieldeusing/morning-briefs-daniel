Vocab: Sports — trainieren, malhar, el dolor muscular

Thursday, July 9 — the weekly rotation technically calls for Football & Sports today, but football vocabulary was already covered a week ago (07/02, World Cup knockout round). So today's sports slot takes a different lens: general fitness and training vocabulary — Fitnessstudio instead of stadium, Muskelkater instead of penalty kick. 35 entries, 12 of them verbs with full conjugation (10 new, 2 refreshed). Grammar highlight: a three-language comparison built around "it hurts" — German conjugates wehtun completely normally through every person (perfect tense with haben), Portuguese allows doer only in the 3rd person (a defective verb), and Spanish doler shifts its stem vowel in the present tense (duele, but dolemos). Plus laufen, the only one of the three focus verbs with a sein-perfect.

  • GENDER das Fitnessstudio / a academia / el gimnasio — DE neuter, PT feminine, ES masculine: the same building, three genders.
  • FALSE FRIEND ES agujetas ≠ dolor muscular (MX) — in Spain, "agujetas" means sore muscles, but in Mexico the word still means "shoelaces." For sore muscles in Mexico, say dolor muscular. quora.com
  • CONJUGATION DE laufen: perfect tense with SEIN — ich bin gelaufen, not *ich habe gelaufen. Motion verbs take sein. verbformen.de
  • CONJUGATION DE wehtun: perfect tense with HABEN — despite being about the body: es hat mir wehgetan, not *ist wehgetan. The counterpart to laufen in this same brief. verbformen.de
  • CONJUGATION PT doer is defective — "to hurt" exists in Portuguese only in the 3rd person (dói/doem); eu dôo doesn't exist. conjugacao.com.br
  • CONJUGATION ES doler: duele, but dolemos — the o→ue stem change only happens under stress; the unstressed nosotros form keeps the "o." reverso.net
  • CONJUGATION ES esforzarse: me esforcé, not *esforzé — before "e," "z" becomes "c" (preterite + subjunctive), plus its own o→ue stem change in the present tense (me esfuerzo).
  • BR-PT malhar — Brazilian slang for "hitting the gym, doing strength training," more casual than the formal treinar. drogariasantoremedio.com.br
  • PHRASE der innere Schweinehund — no one-word equivalent in PT/ES; both paraphrase it as "beating laziness": vencer a preguiça / vencer la flojera.
  • GENDER ES-MX los pants ≠ Spain's el chándal — the same tracksuit, two words depending on the continent; in Mexico, an English loanword.
  • FALSE FRIEND REVIEW: das Gymnasium ≠ EN gym — the German Gymnasium is a secondary school, not a fitness studio. Back from 25.06, refreshed today in a sports context.

Trainieren, laufen, wehtun — sports vocabulary

35 entries, 12 of them verbs with full conjugation (10 new, 2 refreshers) · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

sein or haben? (DE): German motion verbs that express a change of place or state form the perfect tense with sein — like a train ticket that only works "from A to B": ich bin gelaufen, ich bin geschwommen (for directed movement). wehtun, on the other hand, doesn't move anyone from one place to another — it just describes a sensation, which is why it takes haben: es hat mir wehgetan, not *ist wehgetan.

When it hurts: three languages, three strategies: German treats pain like any other verb — full personal conjugation, even though everyday speech mostly uses the 3rd person with a dative (es tut mir weh). Portuguese goes further: doer is defective — the 1st/2nd person doesn't grammatically exist at all; the pain always "belongs" to the body part, never directly to the person (a perna dói, not *eu dôo). Spanish takes a middle path: doler conjugates fully, but with a stem change (o→ue) only in the stressed forms — exactly like the related esforzarse (to push oneself), except there the spelling also comes into play (z→c before e, as with organizar/organizar yesterday).

Show conjugation

DE wehtun: separable, participle wehgetan, perfect tense with haben (not a motion verb, just a sensation). The full personal conjugation (ich tue dir nicht weh) describes "inflicting pain on someone"; everyday speech is dominated by the 3rd-person dative construction es tut mir weh. PT doer is defective — only the 3rd person singular/plural exists, agreeing with the body part that hurts (a perna dói / as pernas doem); the reflexive form doer-se does conjugate fully but is rarely used. ES doler conjugates fully, with the o→ue stem change only in the stressed forms: duele, but dolemos (unstressed). The preterite of doler is completely regular, since -er verbs show no stem change there. (verbformen.de · conjugacao.com.br · conjugator.reverso.net)

It's Thursday, July ninth. The weekly rotation technically calls for football and sports today, but we already covered football vocabulary a week ago, for the World Cup knockout round. So today's brief takes a broader lens: general fitness and training vocabulary, from the gym to sore muscles.

At the center are three verbs that together add up to a small grammar lesson: laufen, to run; wehtun, to hurt; and their Portuguese and Spanish siblings doer and doler. Laufen is a motion verb, so it forms the perfect tense with sein: ich bin gelaufen, I have run. Wehtun, on the other hand, doesn't move anyone from one place to another, so it takes haben instead: es hat mir wehgetan, it hurt me.

Portuguese takes pain even further. Doer is a defective verb — grammatically, it only exists in the third person, singular and plural. You say a perna dói, the leg hurts, but never eu dôo. The pain always belongs to the body part, never directly to the person.

Spanish takes a middle path. Doler conjugates fully through every person, but with a stem change: yo duelo, tú dueles, yet nosotros dolemos keeps the plain o, because the stress doesn't fall on the stem there. The exact same pattern shows up in the verb esforzarse, to push yourself — me esfuerzo, but nos esforzamos. And in the preterite, spelling joins in too: me esforcé, with a c instead of a z, to keep the sound before the e.

Two false friends round out the day. In Mexico, agujetas still means shoelaces, not sore muscles like in Spain — there, sore muscles are dolor muscular. And for a refresher, the German word Gymnasium makes a comeback: it's a secondary school, not a gym. If you want to work out, you go to the Fitnessstudio, not the Gymnasium.

From Brazil comes malhar today, the casual slang for strength training, more relaxed than the formal treinar. And from German, der innere Schweinehund, the inner couch potato — a phrase neither Portuguese nor Spanish has a single word for; both paraphrase it as vencer a preguiça or vencer la flojera, beating laziness.

Thirty-five entries in total today, twelve of them verbs with full conjugation, ten of those new, two refreshers: atmen and anfeuern.

Tomorrow it's travel and transportation. Until then: ich bin gelaufen takes sein, es hat wehgetan takes haben, and a perna dói — only in the third person.
