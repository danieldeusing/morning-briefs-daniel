Vocab: Churrasco — Fire, Meat & Sides

Saturday, July 11 — this week's theme is Food & Cooking, today at the fire: the churrasco, Brazil's barbecue tradition, versus Mexican carne asada. Earlier food days covered the restaurant, kitchen technique, and Festa Junina sweets; today you're at the grill grate: lighting the charcoal, skewering and turning the meat, cutting it against the grain and letting it rest, plus farofa and vinagrete — and knowing why picanha stays a loanword and why doneness is counted differently in Brazil and Mexico. 34 entries, 11 verbs with full conjugation (1 of them a review), plus the Brazilian churrasco words and today's false friend.

  • FALSE FRIEND esquisito (PT) ≠ exquisite — PT esquisito means "weird / off," not "exquisite." "A carne está esquisita" = the meat tastes off, not delicious.
  • BR-PT churrasco — in Brazil the whole social barbecue event; in Chile a steak sandwich, in Argentina a tender tenderloin cut. One word, four meanings.
  • BR-PT carne asada — the social barbecue is called carne asada in Mexico, not churrasco (MX only knows "churrasco" as a single cut of meat).
  • CONJUGATION ES encender diphthongizes e→ie — to light the fire: enciendo, enciende, but encendemos (nosotros stays without the diphthong).
  • CONJUGATION PT & ES servir e→i — eu sirvo / yo sirvo, but nós servimos / nosotros servimos. The vowel raises only under stress.
  • CONJUGATION DE wenden has TWO pasts — turning meat: wendete / gewendet (weak); turning to someone, sich an jdn. wenden: wandte / gewandt (strong). At the grill it's always the e-forms.
  • CONJUGATION mitbringen · trazer · traer — all three irregular: brachte mit, trouxe, traje. Everyone brings something to the churrasco.
  • CONJUGATION ES probar diphthongizes o→ue (pruebo) — but PT provar stays regular (provo). Same root, two patterns.
  • CONJUGATION DE ruhen lassen → double infinitive — "ich habe das Fleisch ruhen lassen," not *ruhen gelassen (double infinitive in the perfect tense).
  • PHRASE Everyone brings something — the potluck principle at a barbecue: BR cada um leva alguma coisa, MX cada quien lleva algo.
  • GENDER picanha · maminha · farofa stay loanwords — Mexico butchers the cow differently; there's no exact MX word. On the grill there, you're more likely to find arrachera.
  • REVIEW probieren · lecker/köstlich · die Beilage — refreshed from the restaurant day on 06/20, all three fit right in at a churrasco.

Vocab: Churrasco & Grill Culture

11 verbs with full conjugation (1 of them a review), plus equipment, cuts, doneness levels, sides, a false friend, and two grilling phrases · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Diphthong stem change (ES): under stress, the stem vowel jumps — encender → enciendo (e→ie), probar → pruebo (o→ue) — but the nosotros form stays at the base form (encendemos, probamos). Important: Portuguese does not do this — provar stays eu provo, never *eu prubo. e→i raising (PT & ES): servir → eu sirvo / yo sirvo, but nós servimos / nosotros servimos. The wenden trap (DE): the same verb has two pasts depending on meaning — turning wenden meat is weak (wendete / gewendet), sich an jemanden wenden ("to turn to someone") is strong (wandte / gewandt). At the grill it's always the e-forms. Double infinitive (DE): lassen + infinitive forms the perfect tense with a double infinitive — "ich habe das Fleisch ruhen lassen," not *ruhen gelassen. Strong vs. weak (DE): schneiden is strong (schnitt / geschnitten), grillen is weak (grillte / gegrillt). And a small spelling trap in ES: ahumar adds an accent on the u in stressed forms (ahúmo) so the hiatus is preserved.

The wenden trap: for the physical act of turning food, wenden is weak in standard modern German — wendete / hat gewendet. The strong forms wandte / gewandt belong to the abstract, mostly reflexive meaning ("to turn to someone"). So at the grill, never *wandte das Fleisch. PT virar / ES voltear are both regular (-ar). Doneness · BR scale vs. MX scale Brazil's churrasco uses three standard levels by internal temperature: mal passada 52–55 °C, ao ponto 60–65 °C, bem passada 70–77 °C. Mexico grades carne asada more finely into five: rojo (50–54 °C), término medio (55–60 °C), tres cuartos (61–68 °C), bien cocido (>69 °C), plus sellado. So it's not 1:1 — the Brazilian ao ponto falls between the Mexican rojo and término medio. fischer.com.br · elhorizonte.mx

It's Saturday, July eleventh. This week's theme is food and cooking, and today it's all about the fire: the churrasco, Brazil's barbecue tradition, versus Mexican carne asada. This is the vocabulary for lighting the charcoal, skewering and turning the meat, cutting it against the grain, letting it rest, and serving it with farofa and vinaigrette.

There are thirty-four entries today: eleven verbs with full conjugation across all four languages, one of them a review. Coming back for a refresher are probar, to taste, tasty or delicious, and the side dish — all three from the restaurant day on June twentieth, and all three fit a churrasco perfectly.

Today's false friend is a nice Portuguese trap. Esquisito does not mean exquisite — it means weird or off. If someone says a carne está esquisita, the meat tastes off; it's not a compliment.

Among the verbs, pay attention to the Spanish stem changes. Encender, to light the fire, diphthongizes e to ie: yo enciendo, but nosotros encendemos. And probar, to taste, diphthongizes o to ue: yo pruebo, but nosotros probamos. Portuguese doesn't follow suit: provar stays eu provo, never pruvo.

Servir raises its stem vowel from e to i in both languages: eu sirvo and yo sirvo, but nós servimos and nosotros servimos.

German has two traps here. Wenden has two different pasts: turning meat is weak, wendete and gewendet, while turning to someone — sich an jemanden wenden — is strong, wandte and gewandt. At the grill it's always the e-forms. And ruhen lassen forms its perfect tense with a double infinitive: ich habe das Fleisch ruhen lassen, not ruhen gelassen.

Mitbringen, trazer, and traer are all three irregular: brachte mit, eu trouxe, yo traje. Fitting, since everyone brings something to a churrasco — cada um leva alguma coisa in Portuguese, cada quien lleva algo in Mexican Spanish.

And a cultural note: picanha, maminha, and farofa all stay loanwords, because Mexico butchers the cow differently. Doneness is counted differently too: Brazil uses three levels, Mexico five. And the word churrasco itself means something different depending on the country — a sandwich in Chile, a cut of meat in Argentina, and the whole barbecue event in Brazil.
