Agile Vocabulary: der Sprint, priorisieren, das Code-Review

Tuesday, July 14 — this week's theme is Software & Technology, today part VIII: Agile Working — Scrum, Sprints & Code Reviews on the Team. The vocabulary for prioritizing a backlog, splitting a user story into subtasks, naming your blocker in the daily, assigning a ticket to a colleague, committing your code, and reflecting on the sprint in the retrospective. 32 entries, 11 verbs with full conjugation — including the lovely traps eu reflito (PT e→i), yo prioricé / refactoricé (ES z→c), and the separable-strong zugewiesen — plus 3 refreshers from earlier Software Tuesdays. Grammar focus: why so many -ieren verbs take no ge- in the participle (priorisiert, not *gepriorisiert), yet committen and zuweisen do — in very different places.

  • FALSE FRIEND DE der Termin ≠ EN term — DE Termin means "appointment/deadline" (a Termin with the client, the Termin for delivery). EN term means "concept/technical word" (also "semester," "contract term") — never a calendar entry. PT o compromisso / o prazo, ES la cita / el plazo.
  • CONJUGATION PT refletir → eu reflito — stem raise e→i in the 1st person: eu reflito (never *reflete for "I"), but você reflete. Subjunctive reflita. The preterite stays regular: eu refleti.
  • CONJUGATION ES priorizar → yo prioricé — spelling z→c before e: preterite yo prioricé, the whole subjunctive with -ce (priorice, prioricemos). The exact same rule applies to refactorizar → yo refactoricé.
  • CONJUGATION DE zuweisen → zugewiesen — separable and strong: ich weise zu, ich wies zu, participle zugewiesen (the ge- slots between prefix and stem). Subjunctive II wiese zu.
  • CONJUGATION DE committen → gecommittet — as an anglicism it's a weak verb, participle with ge-…-et: ich habe gecommittet (per Duden). In ES-MX there's no clean single verb — people say it periphrastically with hacer commit: yo hago commit.
  • CONJUGATION DE -ieren without ge- — priorisieren, dokumentieren, iterieren, reflektieren, blockieren, moderieren, refaktorisieren, eskalieren form their participle without ge-: priorisiert, dokumentiert, iteriert … (never *gepriorisiert).
  • GENDER der Sprint (m), das Backlog (n), die Retrospektive (f) — three genders side by side. The English loanwords are mostly masculine in PT/ES: o sprint, o backlog, o épico — el sprint, el backlog — same pattern as o software.
  • BR-PT codar — in everyday Brazilian dev talk, "to program/write code" is often codar (from EN to code): eu codo o dia todo. Standard PT would be programar. Dictionary-attested at dicionarioinformal.com.br.
  • BR-PT a daily — the daily stand-up in Brazilian Scrum teams is simply a daily (feminine!): na daily cada um fala. More neutral: a reunião diária. ES-MX: la reunión diaria or el daily.
  • IDIOM im Sprint hinterherhinken = to be behind schedule. PT estar atrasado no sprint, ES ir retrasado en el sprint. "Green / red" also means passing / failing in CI here.
  • PHRASE "Let's split up the ticket." = DE „Lass uns das Ticket aufteilen." = PT „Vamos dividir o ticket." = ES „Vamos a dividir el ticket." · "What's the status of the sprint?" = DE „Wie ist der Stand im Sprint?" = PT „Como está o andamento do sprint?" = ES „¿Cómo va el sprint?"
  • CONJUGATION PT atribuir → eu atribuo — an -uir verb with a hiatus: eu atribuo, você atribui, nós atribuímos (with the accent on the í). The German counterpart zuweisen is strong, while the Spanish asignar is well-behaved and regular.

The Sprint Verbs — Planning, Working, Improving

11 verbs with full conjugation + BR slang · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

-ieren without ge- (DE): German verbs ending in -ieren are almost always loanwords (from Latin/French) and form their participle without the usual ge-: priorisieren → priorisiert, dokumentieren → dokumentiert — never *gepriorisiert. Think of it like a customs rule: imported verbs don't pay the ge- toll. Separable (DE): a prefix like zu- or auf- detaches in the sentence — ich weise dir das Ticket zu — and in the participle the ge- slots into the middle: zugewiesen, aufgeteilt. z→c trap (ES): Spanish verbs ending in -zar swap z for c before an e, so the hissing sound stays the same: priorizar → yo prioricé, subjunctive priorice. e→i trap (PT): verbs like refletir raise the stem e to i in the 1st person: eu reflito, but você reflete.

Gender with tech anglicisms: English words have no grammatical gender, but as soon as they migrate into German, Portuguese, or Spanish, they need to get one. Rule of thumb: in PT and ES, tech loanwords are almost always masculine — o sprint, o backlog, o épico / el sprint, el backlog (like o software, el software). In German, the nearest native German word often decides: der Sprint (der Lauf, "the run"), das Backlog (das Protokoll/die Liste, "the log/list," neuter), die Retrospektive (ending -ive → feminine). One exception you'll hear in everyday BR usage: a daily is feminine (after a reunião, "the meeting").

It's Tuesday, July fourteenth. This week's theme is software and technology, and today it's about agile working: Scrum, sprints, and code reviews on the team. The vocabulary for prioritizing a backlog, splitting a user story into subtasks, naming your blocker in the daily, and reflecting on the last sprint in the retrospective.

In German, today's headline rule is this: verbs ending in i e r e n form their participle without ge. It's priorisiert, dokumentiert, iteriert, reflektiert — never gepriorisiert. Think of it like a customs rule: imported verbs don't pay the ge toll.

Two German verbs break that pattern because they're separable. Zuweisen is separable and strong at the same time: ich weise dir das Ticket zu, yesterday I said wies ich es zu, and the participle is zugewiesen — the ge slides in between the prefix and the stem. The same goes for aufteilen: aufgeteilt.

Today's prettiest trap sits in Portuguese. The verb refletir raises the e to an i in the first person: eu reflito means I reflect, but você reflete keeps the e. In the preterite everything's normal again: eu refleti.

In Spanish, watch for the z-to-c rule. Verbs ending in z a r swap the z for a c before an e. That's why it's yo prioricé, and in the subjunctive, priorice. Same story with refactorizar: yo refactoricé. It's not a mistake — it's pure spelling, so the hissing sound stays the same.

One special case is committen. In German it's a weak anglicism verb, and per Duden the participle is gecommittet: ich habe meinen Code gecommittet. Mexican Spanish has no clean single verb for it — people just say hacer commit and conjugate the irregular hacer: yo hago commit, ayer hice commit.

Among the nouns, gender is worth a look. Der Sprint is masculine, das Backlog is neuter, die Retrospektive is feminine. In Portuguese and Spanish, tech loanwords are almost always masculine: o sprint, o backlog, el backlog. And a false friend worth remembering: the German der Termin is a calendar entry or a deadline, never the English term, which means a concept or technical word.
