World Cup Final Vocabulary: der Pokal, krönen, das frühe Aus

Thursday, July 16 — this week's theme is Football & Sports, and this week brings the biggest occasion of the year: on Sunday (7/19) comes the 2026 World Cup final, Argentina vs. Spain at MetLife Stadium (East Rutherford, NJ), kickoff 4:00 PM BRT / 3:00 PM ET (wikipedia.org). The day before, on Saturday, is the third-place match: France–England in Miami, 6:00 PM BRT (worldsoccertalk.com). Spain was fully convincing in the semifinal, beating France 2-0 (aljazeera.com), while Argentina turned a 0-1 deficit against England into a 2-1 win with two late goals (aljazeera.com). And Brazil? Already eliminated in the round of 16 against Norway (1-2, a Haaland brace), the dream of the Hexa, the sixth title, has burst once again (espn.com). That's exactly the vocabulary for today: crowning the world champion, losing to the favorite, shining, trembling, celebrating — and mourning with the runner-up. 32 entries, 11 verbs with full conjugation, plus 3 refreshers (anfeuern, erschöpft, der Krampf).

  • FALSE FRIEND PT time ≠ EN time — o time [ˈtʃi.mi] means die Mannschaft, the team (borrowed from English team) — not the English word time (German die Zeit). "Time" in PT is o tempo.
  • BR-PT a virada = die Aufholjagd, the comeback (Argentina's 0-1 → 2-1). Literally "the turn"; in ES la remontada.
  • BR-PT o azarão = der Außenseiter (the underdog) — exactly what Norway was against Brazil. ES tends to say el no favorito / la sorpresa.
  • BR-PT o vice = der Vize-(Welt)meister, the runner-up — Brazilians shorten it to "o vice," and being runner-up there is almost an insult.
  • CONJUGATION DE unterliegen → perfect tense with sein — Brasilien ist Norwegen unterlegen (never *hat unterlegen). Strong: unterlag, participle unterlegen (no ge-). Verified at verbformen.de.
  • CONJUGATION ES stem change under stress — perder → pierdo, temblar → tiemblo, despedirse → me despido. But nosotros stays plain: perdemos, temblamos, nos despedimos.
  • CONJUGATION convencer — a spelling mirror PT ↔ ES — eu convenço (PT) vs. yo convenzo (ES). Both change the letter so the c stays soft.
  • CONJUGATION PT despedir-se → eu me despeço (d→ç, like pedir → peço); PT homenagear → eu homenageio (-ear inserts an i). Verified at conjugacao.com.br.
  • IDIOM mit einem Bein im Finale stehen — PT estar com um pé na final, ES tener un pie en la final. Exactly England's position after the 1-0.
  • PHRASE Die Nerven liegen blank. — PT os nervos estão à flor da pele, ES los nervios están a flor de piel.
  • GENDER das Finale (n) ↔ a final / la final (f) — in German das Endspiel is neuter, but in PT and ES final is feminine. Same thing, different gender.

The World Cup Final Verbs — Crowning, Losing, Trembling

11 verbs with full conjugation (incl. 1 review) · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Perfect tense with sein (DE) — even for unterliegen: Most verbs form the perfect tense with haben. But verbs of change of state take sein — and "unterliegen" (to lose to / be defeated) counts as one: Brasilien ist Norwegen unterlegen (Brazil lost to Norway), never *hat unterlegen. unterliegen is also strong (unterliegen – unterlag – unterlegen); the participle carries no ge-, because the inseparable prefix unter- sits right in front of it. The Romance stem change (ES): In Spanish, stressed stem vowels break open — perder → pierdo, temblar → tiemblo (e→ie), despedirse → me despido (e→i). Remember: the break only happens where the syllable is stressed — that's why nosotros always stays plain: perdemos, temblamos, nos despedimos. The soft c trick (PT/ES): convencer would have to end in -co in the "I" form — but that would sound hard, like "k." To keep the c soft, PT writes eu convenço and ES writes yo convenzo: two countries, two letters, the same soft sound.

Los calambres son comunes en un partido con drama al final.

It's Thursday, July sixteenth. This week's theme is football and sports, and this week brings the biggest occasion of the year: on Sunday, the two thousand twenty-six World Cup final kicks off — Argentina against Spain, at four in the afternoon Brazil time, at MetLife Stadium near New York. The day before, France and England play for third place, at six in the evening in Miami.

The road to the final was dramatic. Spain cruised through their semifinal, beating France two to nil, and had one foot in the final early on. Argentina, on the other hand, trailed England one to nil — until Enzo Fernández equalized in the eighty-fifth minute, and Lautaro Martínez scored the winner in the ninety-second. A genuine last-minute goal, a genuine comeback. In Brazilian Portuguese that's called a virada; in Spanish, la remontada.

And Brazil? For the sentimental host favorite, the exit came as early as the round of sixteen. Norway won two to one — Erling Haaland scored twice, and Neymar only pulled one back from the penalty spot in stoppage time. So the dream of the hexa, the sixth World Cup title, has burst once again — a sore spot for every Brazilian fan.

That's exactly why today we're learning eleven verbs with full conjugation. The most important one is unterliegen, to lose to someone, because it forms the perfect tense with sein: Brasilien ist Norwegen unterlegen — Brazil lost to Norway — never hat unterlegen. It's also a strong verb — unterliegen, unterlag, unterlegen — and it takes the dative. Alongside it come krönen, to crown; glänzen, to shine; zittern, to tremble; feiern, to celebrate; ehren, to honor; and trauern, to mourn.

Spanish has several stem changes lying in wait. Perder becomes pierdo, temblar becomes tiemblo, and the reflexive despedirse becomes me despido. But watch out: the break only happens under stress. With nosotros the stem stays plain — perdemos, temblamos, nos despedimos.

A nice spelling mirror is convencer, to convince. To keep the soft c sound, Portuguese writes eu convenço with a cedilla, and Spanish writes yo convenzo with a z. And in Portuguese, despedir-se becomes eu me despeço, with that same cedilla trick as pedir, eu peço.

Today's false friend sits between Portuguese and English. O time, pronounced "chee-mee," means the team — from the English word team. It's not the English word time, in the sense of the clock. Time, as in the hour, is o tempo in Portuguese. Plus three very Brazilian words: a virada for the comeback, o azarão for the underdog, and o vice for the runner-up — which in Brazil is almost an insult.

As a refresher, two words return from fitness day, both fitting for final-match drama: anfeuern, to cheer on — in Brazilian Portuguese torcer, which is also where a torcida, the fans, comes from — plus erschöpft, exhausted, and der Krampf, the cramp, because after a hundred and twenty minutes, legs get heavy.

Tomorrow it's all about travel and transportation. Until then: remember, Brasilien ist Norwegen unterlegen takes sein, it's yo convenzo but eu convenço, and o time means the team, not the time.
