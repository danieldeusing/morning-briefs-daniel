Long-Distance Bus Vocabulary: Rodoviária, Poltrona, Night Journey

Friday, July 17 — this week's theme Travel & Transportation, part six. After booking (May 22), the border (May 29), lodging (June 12), driving (July 3) and flying (July 10), today it's the mode of transport that really matters in Brazil: the long-distance bus. You don't just "drive" from Ribeirão Claro to Curitiba or São Paulo — you take the bus — and there's a whole vocabulary for that which German simply has no equivalent for. A German long-distance coach has a seat; a Brazilian one has a poltrona, and it comes as semi-leito, leito, and leito-cama — comfort tiers for which German only has "Liegesitz" (reclining seat) (aguiabranca.com.br). But today's real showstopper is a false friend that pits Daniel's two target languages against each other: PT caminhão = truck, ES-MX camión = bus. Same Latin root, two neighboring countries in every sense — and in Mexico you climb into exactly the vehicle you'd overtake in Brazil. Plus three idioms that all run on rails: pegar o bonde andando, perder el tren, and aufs Abstellgleis. 32 entries, 10 verbs with full conjugation, plus 3 review items (umsteigen, der Aufenthalt, die Mautstelle).

  • FALSE FRIEND PT caminhão ≠ ES-MX camión — o caminhão always means truck. Per the pan-Hispanic RAE, el camión likewise means truck ("vehículo de cuatro o más ruedas que se usa para transportar grandes cargas"), but in Mexico it simply means bus — the RAE entry tags that sense with the label "Méx. autobús" (dle.rae.es, page itself 403s, definition evidenced via the search index).
  • BR-PT a poltrona — on the bus, the seat isn't called assento, but poltrona ("armchair"). The comfort ladder: convencional/executivo → semi-leito → leito → leito-cama, which one industry source says "simulates a real bed" (motorshow.com.br). German has just one word for all of that: Liegesitz.
  • BR-PT o bagageiro = the luggage hold under the bus — not to be confused with a bagagem (the luggage itself). Operators' rule of thumb: roughly 30 kg and up to 1 m in length per passenger (aguiabranca.com.br).
  • BR-PT a baldeação ≠ a integração — baldeação is only the physical transfer from one vehicle to another; integração is the fare concept that lets you transfer within a set window without paying a second time (gestaosat.com).
  • IDIOM pegar o bonde andando (PT) — literally "to hop on a moving streetcar." Watch out, there's a meaning gap: in Brazil it means "to catch half a conversation without knowing what it's about" — not the "jump on the moving train" freeloader sense the German equivalent implies (dicionarioinformal.com.br).
  • IDIOM perder el tren (ES) / perder o bonde (PT) = to let a one-time chance slip by. German: der Zug ist abgefahren ("the train has left"). The logic is the same everywhere — a train has never once waited for anyone.
  • IDIOM aufs Abstellgleis schieben (DE) — an Abstellgleis is, per Duden, a "dead-end track where railcars are parked"; figuratively, professional sidelining (duden.de). PT says jogar para escanteio (straight out of soccer!), ES dejar en vía muerta.
  • CONJUGATION PT embarcar → eu embarquei — never *embarcei. The c turns into qu before e so the k-sound is preserved. Same trick with trocar → troquei.
  • CONJUGATION PT descer → eu desço — never *desco. The mirror image of embarcar: here the c needs to stay soft, hence the cedilla. And ES recargar → yo recargué, never *recargé.
  • CONJUGATION PT conferir → eu confiro — stem change e→i in the eu form; você confere stays regular. ES dormitar, by contrast, is perfectly regular: yo dormito, never *duermito — even though dormir → duermo.
  • CONJUGATION Motion takes sein, everything else takes haben — ich bin eingestiegen / ausgestiegen / umgestiegen / losgefahren / eingenickt, but ich habe aufgeladen / entwertet / kontrolliert / gedrängelt. einnicken takes sein because, like einschlafen, it's a change of state.
  • GENDER die Fahrkarte (f) ↔ a passagem (f) ↔ el boleto (m) — same thing, masculine in Spanish. And the station flips entirely: der Busbahnhof (m) ↔ a rodoviária (f) ↔ la central camionera (f).

The Long-Distance Bus Verbs — einsteigen, umsteigen, einnicken

10 verbs with full conjugation (incl. 1 review) · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

The sein test (German): How do you know whether a German verb forms its present perfect with sein or with haben? Rule of thumb: if something moves from A to B, or a state changes, it's sein. That's why ich bin eingestiegen, ausgestiegen, umgestiegen, losgefahren — and also ich bin eingenickt, because awake→asleep is a change of state just like einschlafen. Everything else takes haben: ich habe die Karte aufgeladen, entwertet, kontrolliert, gedrängelt. The letter swap (PT/ES): Romance languages spell things the way they sound — and when an ending would break that sound, the letter gets swapped. embarcar would have produced *embarcei in the eu past tense, pronounced with a soft s-sound; so instead eu embarquei. It's the exact opposite with descer: *desco would sound hard, like "k," so eu desço with a cedilla. And in Spanish: recargar → yo recargué, so the g stays hard. Remember: the sound wins, the spelling gives way. Trap for the advanced: dormitar (ES, to doze) looks like a relative of dormir → duermo, but it's a plain, ordinary -ar verb: yo dormito, never *duermito. Resemblance is no proof of irregularity.

On Board — Poltrona, Leito & Bagageiro

The Brazilian seat hierarchy: A German long-distance coach has one type of seat. A Brazilian coach has a poltrona — literally "armchair" — and it comes in tiers. Convencional / executivo is the standard seat with air conditioning and WiFi. Semi-leito reclines further and has more legroom. Leito is the business class of the highway: wide seats, footrest, blanket, pillow, bottled water — operators advertise recline angles around 150–170°. And leito-cama goes up to about 180° and "simulates a real bed." Careful with the degree figures: the sources disagree (Águia Branca cites 170° for the plain leito already, Motor Show says 150° for leito and 180° for leito-cama) — there's no binding standard, the number depends on the operator. Take it as a ballpark figure, not a spec. Mexico's counterpart is the premium class of the long-distance bus lines, such as ADO Platino with „asientos ergonómicos… con reclinación amplia para viajar prácticamente acostado". aguiabranca.com.br · motorshow.com.br · blog.ado.com.mx

It's Friday, July seventeenth. This week's theme is travel and transportation, and today's focus is the mode of transport that truly matters in Brazil: the long-distance bus. After booking, borders, lodging, driving, and flying, this is the sixth installment in the series. Thirty-two entries, ten fully conjugated verbs, plus three review words.

Today's false friend pits Daniel's two target languages against each other. In Portuguese, o caminhão always means truck. In Spanish, according to the Real Academia Española, el camión also means truck — a vehicle with four or more wheels built to haul heavy loads. In Mexico, though, camión simply means bus. So much so that the bus station there is called la central camionera. Say in Mexico City that you're taking the camión, and you're boarding a bus. Say the same thing in Madrid, and it sounds like you're hijacking a forty-ton truck.

That brings us to Brazilian bus vocabulary, for which German has no real equivalent. A German long-distance bus has a seat. A Brazilian one has a poltrona, literally an armchair — and it comes in tiers. Convencional is the standard seat. Semi-leito reclines further back. Leito is the business class of the highway, with a footrest, blanket, pillow, and bottled water. And leito-cama, according to operators, simulates an actual bed. The recline angles quoted range somewhere between one hundred fifty and one hundred eighty degrees — but be careful: sources disagree, there's no fixed standard, and the number depends on the operator.

Two more Brazilian words worth knowing. O bagageiro is the luggage compartment under the bus, not to be confused with a bagagem, the luggage itself. It usually allows around thirty kilos per person. And a baldeação is the physical transfer from one vehicle to the next — not to be confused with a integração, the fare concept that lets you transfer within a set time window without paying a second fare.

With the ten verbs, the key rule is the old German auxiliary-verb question. If something moves, or a state changes, the perfect tense takes sein. So: ich bin eingestiegen — I boarded, ausgestiegen — got off, umgestiegen — transferred, losgefahren — set off. And also: ich bin eingenickt — I dozed off, because going from awake to asleep is a change of state, just like einschlafen. Everything else takes haben: ich habe die Karte aufgeladen — I topped up the card, entwertet — validated it, kontrolliert — checked it, gedrängelt — pushed my way through.

In Portuguese and Spanish, a single principle rules today: the sound wins, and the spelling adapts to protect it. Embarcar becomes eu embarquei in the first-person past, with a qu, so the k sound is preserved. The opposite happens with descer: eu desço, with a cedilla, so the c stays soft. And in Spanish, recargar becomes yo recargué, so the g stays hard. Never recargé.

Two extra traps. Portuguese conferir raises its stem vowel only in the eu form: eu confiro, but você confere. And Spanish dormitar looks like a relative of dormir, which becomes duermo — but it's actually a perfectly regular -ar verb. It's yo dormito, never duermito. Resemblance, it turns out, is no proof of irregularity.

Three idioms ride the rails today. Pegar o bonde andando literally means jumping onto a moving streetcar — but in Brazilian usage it means catching a conversation halfway through without knowing what it's about. That's not quite the German free-rider image, so watch out. Perder el tren in Spanish and perder o bonde in Portuguese both mean letting a one-time chance slip by — in German, der Zug ist abgefahren, the train has left. And someone who gets pushed aufs Abstellgleis, according to Duden, ends up on a dead-end track — sidelined. Portuguese calls this jogar para escanteio, borrowed from football, while Spanish says dejar en vía muerta.
