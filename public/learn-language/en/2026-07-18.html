Lanchonete Vocabulary: Salgado, Açaí & Motoboy

Saturday, July 18 — weekly theme Food, Cooking, Restaurant, part seven. After the basics (05/23), Restaurant II (05/30), Festa Junina (06/06), "In the Restaurant" III (06/20), Kitchen at Home (07/04), and Churrasco (07/11), exactly one level is still missing — the one where Brazilians eat most often: not the restaurant, not the grill, but the lanchonete — the corner counter where you eat a salgado standing up and wash it down with a guaraná. German has almost no equivalents for this register, and, fittingly, today's most useful words are precisely the ones that don't even exist in European Portuguese. Two dated hooks frame the day: since Lei 15.330 of 01/08/2026, açaí is by law Brazil's fruta nacional — alongside cupuaçu, which has held that status since 2008 (senado.leg.br). And on Dia da Pizza (07/10), iFood reported 50 million pizza orders in the first half of 2026 — the equivalent of 195 per minute (centraldovarejo.com.br). The motoboy, then, is not a marginal word but infrastructure. Grammar-wise, today is a spelling day: PT adoçar → adocei versus ES endulzar → endulcé — two endings that look alike and behave in exactly opposite ways. 30 entries, 11 verbs with full conjugation, of which 3 are refreshers (the waiter, the portion, to complain).

  • FALSE FRIEND die Marmelade ≠ marmalade — in German, Marmelade is the umbrella term for any cooked fruit spread, strawberry as much as apricot. English marmalade means exclusively the citrus spread, practically always orange; everything else is called jam. Anyone who says "strawberry marmalade" in English sounds like someone ordering red wine as white wine. PT says a geleia, ES-MX la mermelada — both again without the citrus restriction.
  • BR-PT a lanchonete — the snack-bar counter. The word is a Brazilian re-spelling of luncheonette; in European Portuguese you'd more likely say snack-bar or pastelaria. (Editorial register assessment, not a dictionary source.)
  • BR-PT o salgado — umbrella term for everything savory in the display case: coxinha, pastel, empada, quibe, esfirra, pão de queijo. German has no single word for this — "Teigtasche" (pastry pocket) only covers half of it. iFood's partner blog calls the coxinha "sem dúvidas, o salgado mais famoso do Brasil" ("without a doubt, Brazil's most famous salgado") (blog-parceiros.ifood.com.br).
  • BR-PT o motoboy — the motorcycle courier, an English loanword with a Brazilian meaning. A single number shows how central he is: iFood logged 50 million pizza orders in the first half of 2026, an average of 195 per minute (iFood press figures for Dia da Pizza, 07/10/2026, centraldovarejo.com.br).
  • LAW açaí has been fruta nacional since 01/08/2026 — enshrined in Lei 15.330/2026, published in the Diário Oficial da União. The law amended Lei 11.675 from 2008, "que já reconhecia o cupuaçu como fruta nacional" ("which had already recognized cupuaçu as a national fruit") — Brazil now has two official national fruits (senado.leg.br).
  • CONJUGATION PT adoçar → eu adocei, ES endulzar → yo endulcé — today's trap. Both endings look related but behave in opposite directions: in Portuguese, the cedilla disappears before e (the c is already soft there anyway); in Spanish, z becomes c. Never *adoquei, never *endulzé.
  • CONJUGATION DE -ieren verbs take no ge- in the past participle — ich habe frittiert / paniert / reklamiert, never *gefrittiert. Rule of thumb: loan verbs ending in -ieren are stressed on the final syllable, and German ge- only attaches before an unstressed first syllable.
  • CONJUGATION ES freír → yo frío, participle frito — a stem change e→í like reír, plus an irregular participle you eat every day: papas fritas. Likewise envolver → yo envuelvo, participle envuelto — never *envolvido.
  • IDIOM eine Extrawurst wollen (DE) — literally "to demand an extra sausage," meaning, per Duden, "informally, special treatment, an exception, or the fulfillment of an individual request" (duden.de).
  • IDIOM pagar o pato (PT-BR) — "to pay for the duck" = to take the blame for something someone else messed up, "muitas vezes de forma injusta" ("often unfairly") (dicio.com.br). Closest English equivalent: to be left holding the bag.
  • IDIOM me vale un cacahuate (ES-MX) — "it's worth a peanut to me" = I couldn't care less. A Mexicanism; the peanut stands for the lowest possible value. Emphatic variant: me vale un reverendo cacahuate (languagetool.org).
  • PHRASE "For here or to go?" — PT „Para comer aqui ou para levar?", ES-MX „¿Para aquí o para llevar?". The single most important sentence at the counter, and shortest in Spanish: the verb disappears entirely.

The Snack Verbs — Frying, Filling, Snacking

11 verbs with full conjugation (incl. 1 review) · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Today's rule — the sound wins: Romance languages spell things so the sound stays correct. When an ending would break the sound, the letter gets swapped — and because Portuguese and Spanish use different tools for this, they end up going in opposite directions on verbs that look almost identical. Portuguese has the cedilla (ç), which forces a soft s-sound before a/o/u. But before e, c is already soft — the cedilla is redundant there and drops: adoçar → eu adocei. Spanish has no cedilla, but it does have z; and because Spanish traditionally doesn't write z before e, it becomes c instead: endulzar → yo endulcé. Two verbs with the same meaning ("to sweeten"), an almost identical ending, and mirror-opposite rules. The hard-consonant guard: same logic, opposite goal — here a hard sound needs to survive. beliscar → eu belisquei, picar → yo piqué, entregar → eu entreguei / yo entregué. Without the inserted letter you'd read *belis-sei and *entre-hei. And the reverse, before a/o: oferecer → eu ofereço (PT needs the cedilla to keep it soft), ofrecer → yo ofrezco (ES inserts a whole -zc-, as in conocer → conozco). German side rule: verbs ending in -ieren never get a ge- in the past participle: frittiert, paniert, reklamiert. The reason is stress — ge- only attaches before an unstressed first syllable, and -ieren verbs are stressed at the end.

Lanchonete, Salgado & Açaí — the vocabulary of the counter

19 entries (incl. 2 reviews) · Nouns, adjectives, phrases, idioms · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Today's false friend Marmelade proves that a word can share the same origin in two languages and still cover different ground. German Marmelade is the umbrella term: strawberry, cherry, apricot jam — all fair game. English marmalade, by contrast, is narrow — it almost always means the citrus spread, classically bitter orange with bits of peel; anything else is called jam. "Strawberry marmalade" sounds about as wrong to an English ear as "strawberry sauerkraut" would in German. PT (a geleia) and ES-MX (la mermelada) follow the broad German logic. Memory aid: the narrower the word looks, the narrower it is — in English.

Two news items frame the day, and both have a date. Since January eighth of this year, açaí has by law been Brazil's national fruit. That's written into law number fifteen thousand three hundred thirty, published in the Diário Oficial da União. The law amended an older one from two thousand eight, which had already declared cupuaçu the national fruit — so Brazil has had two national fruits since then, both from the Amazon region.

That's why motoboy makes today's vocabulary list — he's not a bit player, he's infrastructure. The word sounds English, but it doesn't actually exist in English; it's a Brazilian coinage.

Grammar-wise, today is a spelling day, and the trap is a good one. Portuguese adoçar and Spanish endulzar both mean "to sweeten." The two endings look related. But in Portuguese, the cedilla before the e simply drops — eu adocei, with a plain c. In Spanish, the z turns into a c — yo endulcé. Two almost-identical endings, two opposite rules. Never adoquei, never endulzé.

Three more conjugation notes to remember. German verbs ending in -ieren never take a ge- prefix in the participle: ich habe frittiert, paniert, reklamiert. Spanish freír is irregular — yo frío, and its participle is frito, which you eat every day as papas fritas. And envolver, to wrap, becomes envuelto, never envolvido.

Today's false friend sits between German and English. In German, Marmelade is the umbrella term for any fruit spread. English marmalade, by contrast, means exclusively the citrus spread, practically always bitter orange. Everything else is jam. A fun aside: Marmelade comes from the Portuguese marmelo, the quince.

And in Mexican Spanish, me vale un cacahuate, "it's worth a peanut to me" — which simply means you don't care. Colloquial to crude; the neutral variant is me da igual.
