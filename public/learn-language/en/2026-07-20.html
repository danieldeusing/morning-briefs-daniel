Tariff Vocabulary: 25% Starting Wednesday, and the Words for It

Monday, July 20 — this week's theme is Economy & Finance, today part nine: Tariffs & Foreign Trade. The occasion is on the calendar: on Wednesday, July 22, the new US tariffs of 25% on most Brazilian goods take effect — coffee, beef, and avocados are exempt. Brasília isn't responding with countertariffs, but through the Reciprocity Law and a WTO complaint. This is exactly the vocabulary you hear on every newscast right now — and that you need when a client in São Paulo asks what it means for their supply chain. 35 entries, 12 verbs with full conjugation (two of them refreshers), plus the five most common conjugation traps of the day and two false friends that fall right into this topic.

  • FALSE FRIEND Tarif ≠ tariff — the English tariff is an import duty; the German Tarif is a price or wage schedule (Handytarif, Tarifverhandlung). The German word for tariff is der Zoll. In PT/ES the family lines up again: a tarifa / el arancel.
  • FALSE FRIEND kontrollieren ≠ to control — German kontrollieren means to check, to inspect (Zollkontrolle = your luggage gets searched). English to control means to steer, to command. "The customs officer controlled my luggage" sounds to English speakers as if the officer remote-controlled the suitcase — the right words are checked / inspected.
  • CONJUGATION PT impor is a pôr verb — eu imponho, você impõe, vocês impõem, and in the simple past eu impus. Treat it like a regular -or verb and you get *eu imporo — which doesn't exist. Same pattern in Spanish: imponer follows poner (yo impongo, yo impuse, yo impondré).
  • CONJUGATION PT reagir → eu reajo — before o and a the g becomes a j, to keep the soft sound. Throughout the present subjunctive: que eu reaja, que nós reajamos. Same logic as Spanish recoger → recojo.
  • CONJUGATION Spelling shift before "e" — PT ameaçar loses the cedilla: eu ameacei, subjunctive ameace. ES amenazar swaps z for c: yo amenacé, subjunctive amenace. The sound stays the same — only the spelling follows the rule.
  • CONJUGATION ES transferir has two stem changes — yo transfiero (e→ie in the present), but él transfirió (e→i in the preterite) and nosotros transferimos with no diphthong. PT transferir only does the raising: eu transfiro, otherwise você transfere.
  • CONJUGATION treten takes sein, aufheben and ausnehmen take haben — das Gesetz ist in Kraft getreten, but die Regierung hat den Zoll aufgehoben and hat Kaffee ausgenommen. All three are strong verbs, yet the auxiliary differs.
  • GENDER der Zoll has two meanings — the duty (25% Zoll) and the agency (der Zoll hat das Paket einbehalten). PT and ES keep those cleanly separate: duty = a tarifa / el arancel, agency = a alfândega / la aduana.
  • BR-PT o despachante aduaneiro — in Brazil, the importer itself almost never handles customs clearance; a licensed customs agent does it instead. A profession with its own name that doesn't really exist in everyday German life (closest equivalent: der Zollagent or Spediteur).
  • BR-PT Pix — Brazil's instant payment system run by the central bank, free and available around the clock. An interesting detail tied to today's topic: the US complaint list specifically names Pix as one of its criticisms of the Brazilian market.
  • IDIOM mit gleicher Münze heimzahlen — PT pagar na mesma moeda, ES pagar con la misma moneda. For once, literally identical across all three languages — and exactly what Brazil says it is not doing.
  • REVIEW überweisen · verhandeln · die Rechnung — refreshed from Banking Day (June 22) and Contracts Day (June 23). All three are core vocabulary today: den Zoll überweisen, Ausnahmen verhandeln, and the nota fiscal is exactly the invoice that counts at the border.

The Occasion: 25% Starting Wednesday

Why tariff vocabulary is today's focus — and what the news is really saying

Starting Wednesday, July 22, 2026, the United States is imposing a 25% tariff on most Brazilian goods. The legal basis is Section 301 of the 1974 US Trade Act — the tool Washington uses to respond to other countries' allegedly unfair trade practices; the underlying investigation has been running since July 2025.[1][3] Exempt are, among others, coffee, beef, avocados, Brazil nuts, petroleum products, and aircraft parts.[2]

Lula calls the measure "illegal and arbitrarily imposed." Brazil is not responding with countertariffs, but through its own Reciprocity Law (Lei da Reciprocidade) and a complaint filed with the WTO.[4]

Not Yet in Effect A second Section 301 investigation — into forced labor in supply chains — concludes on July 24 and could add another 12.5 percentage points (37.5% total). That is a possible outcome, not a rate in force. Anyone saying "37.5%" today is saying something wrong.[5]
What a tariff actually is: Imagine someone standing at the shop door, taking a quarter of the value of every crate that comes in from outside as an entry fee. That's not paid by the sender in Brazil, but by the importer in the United States — who usually passes it on to the sales price. That's why the news often says tariffs ultimately hit a country's own consumers. It still works against Brazil, though: if US customers have to pay 25% more, they buy less — Brazilian goods get more expensive on the shelf than goods from a country with no tariff. That's why exporters fight so hard for exactly one thing: getting onto the exemption list.
Language Note The three languages form "to impose a tariff" in completely different ways. German uses a verb from legal language: einen Zoll verhängen — the same verb used for a penalty or a state of emergency. Portuguese says impor uma tarifa ("to impose"), Spanish imponer un arancel. English headlines, on the other hand, like to reach for a boxing-ring image: to slap a tariff on. That's exactly the phrase in the CNBC headline for this story.

12 verbs with full conjugation (2 of them refreshers), plus tariffs & authorities, trade balance, Brazilian practice, two false friends, and two idioms · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

PT impor / ES imponer: children of pôr and poner — neither is its own pattern; both inherit the complete (irregular) paradigm of their root verb. PT: eu imponho like eu ponho, você impõe like você põe, vocês impõem, and in the simple past eu impus like eu pus. ES: yo impongo like yo pongo, yo impuse like yo puse, future impondré like pondré. If you know pôr/poner, you also know impor, compor, supor, disponer — same family. PT reagir: g→j before o and a — in Portuguese, g sounds soft before e/i but hard before a/o/u. To keep reagir sounding the same throughout, a j is written before o and a: eu reajo, and throughout the present subjunctive que eu reaja … que nós reajamos. Otherwise it stays g: você reage, eu reagi. Same rule as Spanish recoger → recojo. Spelling shift before "e" — PT ameaçar: the cedilla only appears before a/o/u; before e a plain c is enough, so eu ameacei and subjunctive ameace. ES amenazar: you don't write z before e or i, so yo amenacé and subjunctive amenace. Both are purely spelling rules — the pronunciation doesn't change. ES transferir: e→ie and e→i in the same verb — in the present tense it diphthongizes under stress (yo transfiero, tú transfieres, ustedes transfieren), but not in nosotros transferimos, because the stress falls on the ending. In the third person of the preterite and throughout the subjunctive, the stem instead raises to i: él transfirió, ellos transfirieron, que nosotros transfiramos. It follows sentir and preferir here, not the simpler pensar. Portuguese only does the raising, and only in the first person: eu transfiro, but você transfere. DE: sein or haben, separable or not — in Kraft treten takes sein (a change of state): das Gesetz ist in Kraft getreten. aufheben and ausnehmen are likewise strong and separable, but take haben: hat aufgehoben, hat ausgenommen — the ge- slots in between the prefix and the stem. verhängen, besteuern, verhandeln are inseparable or prefix-less and take no ge-: verhängt, besteuert, verhandelt. And all verbs ending in -ieren — exportieren, importieren, reagieren — form the participle without ge- as a rule: exportiert, importiert, reagiert.
