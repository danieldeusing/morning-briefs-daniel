Vocabulary Security Vulnerability: from Zero-Day to Patch

Tuesday, July 21 — weekly theme Software & Technology, today part nine: Vulnerabilities, Zero-Days & Patch Day. The hook is right there in today's software brief: two npm compromises in four days, plus an actively exploited SharePoint zero-day flaw (CVE-2026-58644, CVSS 9.8) whose federal patching deadline expired on July 19. That's the whole life cycle of a vulnerability, told in verbs: someone compromises a package, an attacker exploits the flaw and breaks into the server, a researcher discloses it, the vendor closes it and rolls out the patch. 36 entries, 13 of them verbs with full conjugation; three nouns are refreshers (bug, pipeline, server). Plus today's five nastiest conjugation traps and a false friend that fits the theme perfectly.

  • FALSE FRIEND sensibel ≠ sensible — German sensibel means English sensitive (delicate, needing protection). English sensible means vernünftig ("reasonable"). PT sensível and ES sensible line up with the German meaning — English is the odd one out here.
  • CONJUGATION sein or haben? — eindringen ("to break in") and durchsickern ("to leak") take sein (motion/change of state): "der Angreifer ist eingedrungen" (the attacker has broken in), "die Daten sind durchgesickert" (the data has leaked). zurückziehen ("to withdraw") and schließen ("to close") take haben, even though both are strong verbs: "hat den Patch zurückgezogen" (has withdrawn the patch), "hat die Lücke geschlossen" (has closed the flaw).
  • CONJUGATION ES cerrar diphthongizes — cierro, cierras, cierra, cierran, but cerramos keeps the plain stem (the stress falls on the ending there). Same pattern in the subjunctive: cierre … cerremos.
  • CONJUGATION ES aislar shifts the accent — aíslo, aíslas, aísla, aíslan carry an accented í, only aislamos doesn't. The accent just marks where the stress falls — the stem itself doesn't change.
  • CONJUGATION the trap before "e" — ES lanzar → yo lancé (z→c), publicar → publiqué (c→qu), divulgar → divulgué (g→gu). Same in PT: publiquei, divulguei. But PT lançar → lancei is not a real trap — the cedilla just drops, no new letter is added.
  • CONJUGATION PT corrigir → eu corrijo — before o and a, the g becomes a j to keep the soft sound. Throughout the subjunctive: que eu corrija, que nós corrijamos. Same logic as Spanish recoger → recojo.
  • GENDER die Sandbox — feminine in German (die Box), a loanword. PT says a sandbox or o ambiente isolado, ES el sandbox or la zona de pruebas. So the gender travels with the language.
  • BR-PT vazar — Brazil's everyday verb for "to leak": "os dados vazaram" (the data leaked). Noticeably more informal than the formal ser divulgado — exactly the phrase you'll hear in Brazilian tech news.
  • IDIOM let the cat out of the bag — PT/ES have no cat imagery here: PT abrir o jogo, ES descubrir el pastel ("uncover the cake"). A literal translation misfires here — go by meaning instead.
  • PHRASE to exploit a zero-day flaw — zero-day = a flaw for which no patch exists on the day of the first attack. The vendor had "zero days" to close it.
  • REFRESHER der Bug · die Pipeline · der Server — refreshed from the software days (07/07, 07/14, 06/24). Today's core vocab: the bug becomes the exploit, the patch runs through the pipeline, the attacker breaks into the server.

The Hook: Patch Day and Two npm Compromises

Why security vocabulary is up today — the life cycle of a vulnerability

Today's software brief revolves almost entirely around supply chain and active exploitation. Within four days, the npm supply chain was compromised twice over — in the AsyncAPI organization's case, the malicious code fires right at import time, bypassing the classic --ignore-scripts protection.[1] In parallel, CISA added the SharePoint flaw CVE-2026-58644 (CVSS 9.8) to its catalog of actively exploited vulnerabilities on July 16; the deadline for US federal agencies to patch had already passed on July 19 — meaning the flaw was exploited before it was closed.[2]

The good news is buried in the middle of it all: npm v12 disables install scripts by default — a direct response to the wave of attacks.[3] For the vocabulary, this is ideal: attack, disclosure, reporting, and patch are all anchored today in real, dated events.

What a security vulnerability is: Picture a house with a window that doesn't close properly. As long as nobody notices, nothing happens. A security researcher is the neighbor who spots the window and tells the homeowner (to report). A threat actor is the burglar who finds it first and climbs in (to exploit, to break in). A patch is the handyman who fixes the window (to close). And a zero-day is the worst case: the burglar is already inside while the homeowner still has no idea the window is broken.
Linguistically interesting "To close a vulnerability" — the four languages split into a door/window image and a repair image: DE die Lücke schließen, ES cerrar la vulnerabilidad — both "shut." PT and EN take the repair route: PT corrigir a falha ("to correct"), EN to patch (like patching a bicycle tire). That's exactly why the English loanword Patch literally means "flicken" (patch/mend) in German.

Vocabulary: Vulnerabilities, Zero-Days & Patch Day

13 verbs with full conjugation, plus the flaw & the attacker, patch & disclosure, three adjectives, three idioms, and three refreshers · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

DE: sein or haben, separable or not — eindringen (from "dringen": drang/gedrungen) and durchsickern take sein because they describe motion or a change of state: der Angreifer ist eingedrungen (the attacker has broken in), das Geheimnis ist durchgesickert (the secret has leaked). zurückziehen (from "ziehen": ie→o→o, zog/gezogen) and schließen (schloss/geschlossen) are also strong verbs but take haben: hat zurückgezogen, hat geschlossen. And the past-participle rule: -ieren verbs (kompromittieren, isolieren) and the ver- verb veröffentlichen get no ge- prefix (kompromittiert, isoliert, veröffentlicht), while separable verbs like ausnutzen, offenlegen, ausrollen, aufspüren insert it in the middle (ausgenutzt, offengelegt, ausgerollt, aufgespürt). ES cerrar: e→ie under stress — cierro/cierras/cierra/cierran diphthongize because the stress falls on the stem; with cerramos it falls on the ending, so the stem stays plain. The subjunctive follows suit: cierre … cerremos. ES aislar: accent shift — here no letter changes, only the stress becomes visible in writing: aíslo/aíslas/aísla/aíslan with an accent, aislamos without. In the subjunctive: aísle … aislemos. Spelling changes before "e" — to keep the sound the same, the spelling shifts before e: ES lanzar → lancé (z→c), publicar → publiqué (c→qu), divulgar → divulgué (g→gu); PT publicar → publiquei, divulgar → divulguei. Watch out: PT lançar → lancei is not such a trap — before e, the cedilla simply drops, because c already sounds soft there. PT corrigir: g→j before o and a — eu corrijo and throughout the subjunctive corrija … corrijamos, otherwise the g stays: você corrige, eu corrigi. Same rule as Spanish recoger → recojo.

The three words people confuse: the vulnerability/flaw is the weak point itself (the window that won't close). the exploit is the finished tool that opens exactly that window (the matching lockpick). the patch is the repair that seals the window shut again. In Portuguese: a falha/vulnerabilidade · o exploit · o patch/a correção. In Spanish: la vulnerabilidad · el exploit · el parche. The most common mistake: equating bug and exploit — the bug is the flaw, the exploit is the attack that abuses it.

It's Tuesday, July twenty-first. This week's theme is software and technology, and today the newsletter draws straight from our own Software Brief: security vulnerabilities, zero-days, and patch day. In just four days, the npm supply chain was compromised twice — at the AsyncAPI organization, malicious code fires the moment you import the package, slipping past the usual safeguards.

Meanwhile, a SharePoint zero-day is running hot. It carries the designation CVE two thousand twenty-six, fifty-eight, six hundred forty-four, with a CVSS score of nine point eight — about as bad as it gets. CISA added it to its catalog of actively exploited vulnerabilities on July sixteenth, and the deadline for U.S. federal agencies to patch had already expired on July nineteenth. In other words, the flaw was exploited before it was fixed — which is exactly what "zero-day" means: the vendor had zero days to respond.

The whole day can be told as the life story of a vulnerability, verb by verb. Someone compromises a package, an attacker exploits the flaw and breaks into a server, a researcher tracks down the malicious code, reports it, and discloses it, the vendor closes the vulnerability, and the team rolls out the patch. Thirteen verbs with full conjugations, plus thirty-six entries in all.

With the German verbs, the first trap is the auxiliary verb. Eindringen (to break in) and durchsickern (to leak) take sein, because they describe motion: der Angreifer ist eingedrungen — the attacker broke in; die Daten sind durchgesickert — the data leaked. Zurückziehen (to withdraw) and schließen (to close) are also strong verbs, but take haben: er hat den Patch zurückgezogen — he withdrew the patch; Microsoft hat die Lücke geschlossen — Microsoft closed the vulnerability.

Spanish brings two nice stem changes today. Cerrar diphthongizes under stress: cierro, cierras, cierra, cierran — but nosotros cerramos keeps the plain stem. And aislar only shifts the accent: aíslo, aíslas, aísla, aíslan, with the stress on the i, except for aislamos, which has none. The accent mark simply shows where the stress falls.

Then there's the trap before the letter e. In Spanish, lanzar becomes yo lancé, publicar becomes publiqué, and divulgar becomes divulgué. Portuguese works the same way: publiquei, divulguei. But watch out — Portuguese lançar isn't a real trap. Here, only the cedilla drops before e, giving simply eu lancei, with no new letter added. And Portuguese corrigir becomes eu corrijo, because g turns into j before o and a, to keep the soft sound.

Today's false friend fits the theme perfectly. German sensibel means English sensitive — private, something that needs protecting. English sensible, on the other hand, means reasonable. Here's the curious part: Portuguese sensível and Spanish sensible both match the German meaning. English is the only outlier here. If you want to say "sensible data," what you actually mean is sensitive data.

One more word from Brazil: vazar. It's the everyday word for leaking — os dados vazaram, the data leaked — much more casual than the formal ser divulgado, and exactly the phrase you'll hear in Brazilian tech news. Today also brings back three old friends: bug, pipeline, and server.

Finally, two idioms. "Letting the cat out of the bag" doesn't involve a cat in Portuguese or Spanish: they say abrir o jogo or descubrir el pastel — literally, uncovering the cake. And "a race against time" becomes corrida contra o tempo or carrera contra el reloj — exactly what defenders run after every disclosure.
