Back-to-School Vocabulary: einschulen, der Schulranzen, aufgeregt

Wednesday, July 22 — this week's theme is Family & Everyday Life, with a concrete occasion today: in Paraná, the July school recess is winding down. The state schools (rede estadual) were off from July 13 to 24/26 and start the second half-year block on Monday, July 27, 2026 (diariodosudoeste.com.br, bemparana.com.br). So today it's five days until school starts — exactly the time when you pack the Schulranzen, get the Schulmaterial beschriftet done, push Schlafenszeit earlier again, and get the kids (half aufgeregt, half übermüdet) vorbereitet for the first day of school. 39 new entries, 12 verbs with full conjugation — including the Spanish stem changes sonar (o→ue) and despertar (e→ie), the c→qu trap clasificar/classificar, and the 1st-person trap conozco/conheço — plus 3 refreshers (die Schultasche, die Hausaufgaben, der Lehrer).

  • FALSE FRIEND die Note ≠ EN note — DE Note means a school grade, in English grade/mark. The English word note is a memo, a slip of paper, or a banknote. Curiously, PT a nota means both — the school grade and the banknote.
  • BR-PT a apostila — the typically Brazilian, slim course booklet used by the private school systems (COC, Positivo, Objetivo), distinct from the livro didático, the classic ISBN textbook (r5ensino.com.br). German has no exact word for it.
  • CONJUGATION ES klingeln = sonar (o→ue) — el timbre suena ("the bell rings"), but nosotros sonamos keeps the bare stem. The diphthong appears only under stress.
  • CONJUGATION ES wecken = despertar (e→ie) — yo despierto, tú despiertas, but nosotros despertamos. PT acordar, by contrast, is dutifully regular.
  • CONJUGATION sortieren → ES clasificar / PT classificar — 1st person preterite clasifiqué / classifiquei (c→qu before -e), never *clasificé. Same pattern with PT toquei (tocar = klingeln).
  • CONJUGATION kennenlernen → yo conozco / eu conheço — the 1st person singular present is irregular: ES c→zc (conozco), PT c→ç (conheço) — never *conoco / *conheco.
  • GENDER der Schulranzen · die Federmappe · das Lineal — three genders in a single pencil case. You have to learn the grammatical gender word by word.
  • BR-PT der Schulranzen → a mochila — the stiff German-style satchel (Tornister) doesn't exist in Brazil; here all kids carry a soft mochila (backpack), and es-MX likewise uses la mochila.
  • BR-PT die Brotdose — BR a marmita / a lancheira, es-MX la lonchera (from English lunch). And school supplies are called los útiles escolares in Mexico.
  • IDIOM mit dem falschen Fuß aufstehen = to start the day in a bad mood. PT acordar com o pé esquerdo, ES levantarse con el pie izquierdo — here the foot shifts to the left.
  • PHRASE „Hast du alles eingepackt?" — the morning phrase before heading out the door: BR „Você guardou tudo na mochila?", es-MX „¿Ya guardaste todo?"

Start-of-School Verbs — anmelden, wecken, sich einleben

12 verbs with full conjugation · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX

Stem changes in Spanish (o→ue, e→ie): Some Spanish verbs change their stem vowel — but only where the stress falls on it. sonar (klingeln) becomes suena, despertar (wecken) becomes despierto. In the nosotros form, the stress shifts away, so the bare stem stays: sonamos, despertamos. Think of it like an accordion: under pressure (stress) the vowel opens up, without pressure it collapses back. c→qu / g→gu before -e: In PT and ES, the hard /k/ or /g/ sound is preserved by inserting a u before an -e: classificar → classifiquei, tocar → toquei, empolgar → empolguei. Without the u, you'd get /s/ or /ʒ/ instead. Reflexive verbs: sich eingewöhnen, sich gewöhnen, sich freuen need a reflexive pronoun (mich/dich/sich). In PT and ES, a clitic does the same job: eu me acostumo, yo me acostumbro — "I get myself used to it".

All About the Start of School — Supplies, Nerves & Follow-Up

Nouns, adjectives, idioms & everyday sentences · incl. false friend, BR-PT & 3 refreshers

Learning gender along with the word (der/die/das): German nouns have a grammatical gender that you can't tell from the word itself — der Schulranzen (the school satchel), die Federmappe (the pencil case), das Lineal (the ruler). It's best to learn the article together with the word, as one fixed vocabulary unit. PT and ES have only two genders (m/f); the neuter drops out. False friends: words that look identical in two languages but mean different things. Today's focus: DE Note (school grade) versus EN note (a memo). Same letters, different world. BR-PT vs. European PT: Brazil has its own everyday words — a apostila (workbook), o apontador (pencil sharpener; in Portugal afia), a mochila (backpack) instead of a stiff satchel. We consistently use the Brazilian variant.

It's Wednesday, July twenty-second. This week's theme is family and everyday life, and today there's a concrete reason for it: in Paraná, the July school recess is winding down. State schools were off from the thirteenth through around the twenty-fourth of July and start the second semester next Monday, the twenty-seventh. That leaves just five days until school starts back up — exactly the window for packing the school bag, labeling supplies, and easing bedtime back earlier.

Today's verbs mostly hide Spanish stem changes. "To ring" is sonar, and the vowel breaks open under stress: el timbre suena, the bell rings. But in the "we" form the stress shifts away, so it's nosotros sonamos, with no diphthong at all. The same happens with "to wake up," Spanish despertar, e turning to ie: yo despierto, but nosotros despertamos. Portuguese acordar, by contrast, stays nicely regular.

The second trap is a spelling one. To keep the hard k-sound before an e, c becomes qu. "To sort" is clasificar in Spanish, and the first-person past tense is clasifiqué, not clasificé. In Portuguese, classificar becomes classifiquei, and tocar, which also means "to ring," becomes toquei. The same principle, with g turning into gu, shows up in me empolguei, "I got excited."

A classic case is the first person of "to get to know." In Spanish conocer, it's yo conozco, with an inserted z, and in Portuguese conhecer becomes eu conheço, with a cedilla c. Never conoco or conheco. And since there was a typo in the source notes: the reflexive "to get used to" in Portuguese is acostumar-se, with an r and no b — eu me acostumo. Spanish acostumbrarse, on the other hand, does carry the b.

Today's false friend fits the school theme perfectly. The German Note is a school grade, in English grade or mark. The English word note, meanwhile, means a memo or a banknote. Curiously, Portuguese a nota means both — the school grade and the banknote, as in uma nota de cem reais, a hundred-real bill.

The pencil sharpener is an apontador in Brazil and a sacapuntas in Mexico. And the lunch box is la lonchera in Mexican Spanish, straight from the English word lunch.
