Thursday, July 23 — this week's theme is Football & Sports. The World Cup is done and dusted; we covered the tournament vocabulary on July 16. What's up now is the year's second football season: the personnel season. Tomorrow, Friday, July 24, 2026, at 11:00, at DFB headquarters in Frankfurt, Jürgen Klopp will be unveiled as Julian Nagelsmann's successor (sportschau.de, July 23); Nagelsmann had resigned on July 3 after the round-of-16 exit. In parallel, the transfer window is red hot — Johan Manzambi is moving from SC Freiburg to Aston Villa for a club-record £59.5 million (skysports.com, July 17) — and the Brasileirão has been back underway since July 19, with Palmeiras topping the table. That's exactly the vocabulary you need for Globo Esporte in the evening: renunciar, o sucessor, a coletiva, o reforço, fichar. 35 entries, 12 of them verbs with full conjugation — including the sein-perfect trap in zurücktreten, the mixed ernennen–ernannte–ernannt, the ES stem change volver → vuelvo/vuelto, and the PT -ear pattern nomear → eu nomeio — plus 3 refreshers (der Trainer, der Favorit, der Gegner).

  • FALSE FRIEND der Manager ≠ EN the manager — in German football, the Manager is the person in charge of the business side (squad, transfers, contracts — today usually titled Sportdirektor, sporting director). In British English, by contrast, the manager is exactly the head coach: "Klopp was Liverpool's manager." Mix the two up, and you've turned the coach into an accountant.
  • BR-PT o técnico — in Brazil, the head coach is called o técnico at every level, club and national team alike. European Portuguese's o treinador is understood, but in the Norte-Pioneiro region it sounds like stiff newspaper prose.
  • BR-PT a coletiva (de imprensa) = the press conference — the short, everyday Brazilian form. European PT says conferência de imprensa, es-MX la conferencia de prensa (the Iberian rueda de prensa is rarely heard in Mexico).
  • CONJUGATION zurücktreten needs sein — "Nagelsmann ist zurückgetreten" (has resigned), never *hat zurückgetreten. The same holds for nachfolgen ("ist nachgefolgt") and zurückkehren ("ist zurückgekehrt"). And: du trittst zurück, er tritt zurück — a strong e→i shift.
  • CONJUGATION ernennen–ernannte–ernannt — a mixed verb like nennen/kennen/brennen: the endings are weak, but the stem vowel still jumps e→a. So der DFB ernannte ihn (the DFB appointed him), never *ernennte in the simple past.
  • CONJUGATION unterschreiben & unterbrechen are inseparable — which is why there's no ge- in the participle: hat unterschrieben, hat unterbrochen (not *geunterschrieben). unterbrechen is also strong: du unterbrichst, er unterbrach, hat unterbrochen.
  • CONJUGATION ES volver (to return): o→ue + irregular participle vuelto — yo vuelvo, tú vuelves, él vuelve, but nosotros volvemos (stress shifts away, diphthong disappears). The participle is irregular: ha vuelto, never *volvido.
  • CONJUGATION PT nomear (to appoint): eu nomeio, eles nomeiam — -ear verbs insert an i when the stem is stressed, but not in the "we" form: nós nomeamos. Simple past: eu nomeei.
  • CONJUGATION PT trocar (to switch): eu troquei — c→qu before -e, so the /k/ sound stays put. Don't get *troquei backwards: present eu troco, simple past eu troquei, subjunctive que eu troque.
  • NO 1:1 MATCH wechseln ↔ verpflichten ↔ ES fichar — German splits the perspective (the player wechselt [moves] to X ↔ the club verpflichtet [signs] Y). Spanish uses one verb for both directions: el jugador fichó por el Aston Villa and el Aston Villa fichó a Manzambi. So don't force the same sentence structure in ES that you'd use in DE.
  • NO 1:1 MATCH der Bundestrainer has no one-word equivalent in PT/ES — it always needs a paraphrase: o técnico da seleção (alemã) / el director técnico (DT) de la selección. The Iberian el seleccionador is uncommon in Mexico.
  • IDIOM in jemandes Fußstapfen treten = to step into a predecessor's shoes. PT seguir os passos de alguém, ES seguir los pasos de alguien — the same image three times, with a different verb each time (treten ↔ seguir).

The Verbs of the Handover

12 verbs with full conjugation · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX · cross-checked against verbformen.de, conjugacao.com.br, conjugator.reverso.net

Separable or inseparable? German prefixes fall into two kinds. Separable ones (zurück-, nach-, vor-, an-, fort-) are stressed and fly to the end of the main clause: er tritt zurück, sie führt an. In the participle, the ge- slides into the middle: zurückgetreten. Inseparable ones (unter- in unterschreiben, be-, ver-, er-) stay stuck and get no ge-: hat unterschrieben, hat bestätigt. Think of it like a trailer hitch: the stressed prefix is coupled on and can be unhitched, the unstressed one is welded on. Perfect tense with sein: Verbs describing a movement or a change of state form the perfect tense with sein instead of haben. A resignation is exactly that kind of change of state — from office into no-office: er ist zurückgetreten. Same for nachfolgen and zurückkehren. And wechseln can go either way: a change of place ⇒ ist gewechselt, a change of object ("das Trikot wechseln", to change jerseys) ⇒ hat gewechselt. Why ES fichar replaces two German verbs: fichar comes from la ficha, the index card — to "file someone into the record." That's why it works from both directions: the club signs them up (fichar a alguien), and the player gets signed (fichar por un club). German needs two separate verbs to say the same thing.
