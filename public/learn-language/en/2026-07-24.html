Vocabulary: Severe Weather Behind the Wheel: schleudern, ausweichen, die Rutschgefahr

Friday, July 24 — weekly theme Travel & Traffic, seventh installment. This time it's not the airport or the long-distance bus, but something that's literally on the doorstep today: INMET has had a yellow thunderstorm advisory in effect since yesterday at 6:00 p.m. (No. 27882, "Tempestade") for around 350 municipalities in Paraná — including Ribeirão Claro and Jacarezinho —, valid until today at 11:59 p.m. BRT, with 20–30 mm of rain per hour, gusts of 40–60 km/h, and hail (apiprevmet3.inmet.gov.br, as of July 24). This is the vocabulary you need on the PR-431 when the road surface is glistening: derrapar, alagar, interditar, o pisca-alerta, a aquaplanagem. Grammatically, everything today hinges on one question — sein or haben in the perfect tense. Verbs of motion take sein (ich bin gerutscht), verbs with an object take haben (der Regen hat die Straße überflutet) — and schleudern stands with one foot in each camp. 34 entries, 14 of them verbs with full conjugation, plus 4 refreshers (bremsen, überholen, liegenbleiben, der Stau) from the driving edition of July 3.

  • FALSE FRIEND blenden ≠ EN to blend — German blenden means "to block someone's view with light": der Gegenverkehr blendet mich. English to blend means to mix. Whoever says "the headlights blended me" has puréed the headlights. Correct: the headlights dazzled me.
  • BR-PT a carona = a ride, a lift — Priberam explicitly marks this meaning as Brazilian and names the European Portuguese counterpart: „Acto de transportar ou de ser transportado gratuitamente num veículo. (Equivalente no português de Portugal: boleia.)" In Mexico the same thing is called el aventón.
  • CONJUGATION rutschen and ausweichen need sein — ich bin gerutscht, er ist dem Ast ausgewichen. Both are motion through space, and in German that takes sein. *Ich habe gerutscht doesn't exist.
  • CONJUGATION schleudern takes haben — verbformen.de lists hat geschleudert as the main form and additionally notes a sein variant (for motion with direction: der Wagen ist von der Fahrbahn geschleudert). When in doubt: with an object or without direction ⇒ haben.
  • CONJUGATION überfluten, überqueren, überholen are inseparable — the prefix über- is unstressed here, sticks to the verb, and blocks the ge-: hat überflutet, hat überquert, hat überholt. With umleiten, by contrast, um- is stressed and separable: er leitet um, hat umgeleitet.
  • CONJUGATION PT frear (bremsen): eu freio — the -ear verbs insert an i under stress: eu freio, você freia, vocês freiam — but nós freamos without it. The preterite is nonetheless regular: eu freei.
  • CONJUGATION ES desviar (umleiten): yo desvío with an accent — like enviar: the stressed ending pulls the accent onto the í (desvío, desvías, desvía, desvían), only nosotros desviamos stays unaccented. Preterite: yo desvié.
  • CONJUGATION ES cruzar (überqueren): yo crucé — z→c before e, otherwise you'd get the misspelled *cruzé. Same family as -car → -qué and -gar → -gué. Subjunctive: que yo cruce.
  • CONJUGATION PT alagar / escorregar: eu alaguei, eu escorreguei — g→gu before e, to keep the hard /g/. And ofuscar (blenden) does the same with c→qu: eu ofusquei.
  • NO 1:1 MATCH ausweichen governs the dative, PT desviar the preposition de, ES esquivar nothing at all — ich weiche dem Ast aus ↔ desvio do galho ↔ esquivo la rama (direct object, no preposition). Three languages, three different hookups to the same image.
  • GENDER die Warnblinkanlage ↔ o pisca-alerta ↔ las intermitentes — German feminine singular, Portuguese masculine singular, Mexican Spanish feminine plural. A word-for-word transfer loses the gender first and then the number.
  • IDIOM es gießt wie aus Eimern — PT-BR says está chovendo canivete ("it's raining pocketknives"), es-MX está lloviendo a cántaros ("from jugs"). Three languages, the same weather, three different vessels — and one of them isn't a vessel at all, but a blade.

The Verbs of Wet Asphalt

14 verbs with full conjugation · DE · EN · PT-BR · ES-MX · cross-checked against verbformen.de, conjugacao.com.br, conjugator.reverso.net

Perfect tense with sein or with haben? German has two auxiliary verbs for the perfect tense, and the choice isn't a matter of taste. Rule of thumb: haben takes any verb that can have an object (you can ask "what?") — der Regen hat die Straße überflutet. sein takes verbs where the subject moves through space or changes state — ich bin gerutscht, er ist ausgewichen, das Auto ist liegengeblieben. Think of a map: if my point on it changes, it's sein; if I'm just doing something to an object, it's haben. Portuguese and Spanish don't have this problem at all — there everything runs through ter or haber respectively. Separable or inseparable? If the prefix is stressed (um-leiten, aus-weichen), it flies to the end of the main clause (er leitet den Verkehr um) and the ge- slots into the middle in the participle: umgeleitet. If it's unstressed (überfluten, überqueren, überholen), it sticks fast and the ge- disappears entirely: hat überflutet. Like a trailer versus a welded-on part: one you can uncouple, the other you can't. Aquaplaning means a continuous film of water sits between the tire and the road. The tire can no longer push the water aside and instead floats on top of it — like a water ski. In that moment steering, braking, and accelerating are all useless, because nothing has contact with the road anymore. In Portuguese a aquaplanagem, in Mexican Spanish el acuaplaneo.

Grammar-wise, today comes down to one question: sein or haben in the perfect tense. The rule of thumb is simpler than it sounds. If you're moving through space or changing your state, you take sein — Ich bin gerutscht, Er ist dem Ast ausgewichen, Das Auto ist liegengeblieben. But if you're doing something to an object, you take haben — Der Regen hat die Straße überflutet.

One verb keeps a foot in each camp. Verbformen.de lists schleudern with haben — hat geschleudert — but also flags a sein variant for movement with a direction: der Wagen ist von der Fahrbahn geschleudert. When in doubt: with an object, or without a clear direction, go with haben.

The second German trap is prefixes. Überfluten, überqueren, and überholen are inseparable, because the über is unstressed. That means it stays glued to the verb and the participle gets no ge-: hat überflutet, hat überquert, hat überholt. With umleiten, on the other hand, the um is stressed, so it's separable: er leitet den Verkehr um, hat umgeleitet.

In Portuguese, the star of the review is bremsen — frear. Frear belongs to the -ear verbs that insert an i under stress: eu freio, você freia, vocês freiam — but nós freamos, without the i, because the stress there falls on the ending. The preterite stays well-behaved: eu freei. Then there are the spelling traps: eu alaguei with a u, eu escorreguei with a u, eu ofusquei with qu.

In Spanish there are three things to flag. Desviar takes an accent: yo desvío, tú desvías, ellos desvían — only nosotros desviamos stays unaccented. Cruzar is spelled crucé in the preterite, with a c, not a z. And advertir has two separate stem changes: yo advierto in the present, but él advirtió in the preterite.
