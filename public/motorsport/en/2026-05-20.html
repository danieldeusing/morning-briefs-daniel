Formula 1 is back. Formula 1 · Canada preview

First race in five weeks — and it's a sprint

Formula 1 is coming off an unusually long break. After the Miami Grand Prix on May 3, a five-week hole opened in the calendar — created when the rounds scheduled for mid-April in Bahrain and Saudi Arabia were cancelled over the Iran war. What was meant to be 24 races became 22. On Sunday the season resumes at the Circuit Gilles Villeneuve in Montreal — and as a sprint weekend, no less.

The race start is set for 4:00 PM local time — that's 5:00 PM BRT for Daniel in Ribeirão Claro and 10:00 PM CEST in Germany. The unusually late start is deliberate: it's meant to ease the timing clash with the Indianapolis 500, running the same day, so fans can watch both classics.

Sprint weekend: instead of just qualifying plus the race, there's an extra short race on Saturday (about a third of the distance, its own points for the top 8) plus a dedicated Sprint Qualifying on Friday. Like an appetizer contest before the main course — it brings extra points, but also a second helping of risk: on Montreal's concrete-walled circuit (the "Wall of Champions"), a small mistake quickly ends your day.

Antonelli, 19, and a Mercedes stranglehold

The face of the 2026 season is Andrea Kimi Antonelli. The 19-year-old Italian missed the opening win in Australia (his teammate George Russell took it), but then won China, Japan and Miami in a row. In Miami he held Lando Norris behind him in a controlled drive and pushed his championship lead out to 20 points. With that win — already at his first victory in China — he became the second-youngest grand prix winner in history, behind only Verstappen (18 in Spain in 2016).

Behind it lies a simple fact: Mercedes has won all four races and locked out the front row in each of the four qualifying sessions. In the 2026 regulation reset — when everyone starts from zero — the Brackley squad has made the best start. Helmut Marko (Red Bull) has already predicted the title will go to Mercedes; the only open question, he says, is whether Antonelli beats his more experienced teammate Russell.

Verstappen's hardest year — and it isn't the engine

While Mercedes pulls away, four-time world champion Max Verstappen is enduring the most difficult season of his career. With just 26 points he sits seventh; his best result from four races is a fifth place, and Red Bull scored only twelve points combined across the opening two rounds — partly because of technical retirements. Team boss Laurent Mekies dampened expectations early: "Bear with us in the first months."

The cause is the interesting part. Many had flagged the new, in-house Ford power unit as the 2026 risk — yet technical analysis shows the engine is surprisingly competitive for a newcomer. The real problem is the chassis: the RB22 has balance weaknesses through virtually every type of corner, which overloads the tyres and wears them faster than rivals'. A car problem is generally more fixable over a season than an engine problem — cold comfort in Montreal.

Power unit vs. chassis: the "power unit" is the drivetrain (combustion engine + electric motor), the "chassis" is the rest of the car (structure, suspension, aerodynamics). Picture a bicycle: a strong rider (engine) helps little if the frame is bent and wobbles in every corner (chassis). At Red Bull it's the frame that's the problem right now, not the legs.

Bortoleto: Brazil's return to Formula 1

Especially relevant for Daniel: Gabriel Bortoleto is, as of this season, the first full-time Brazilian driver since Felipe Massa in 2017 — and he races for Audi, newly arrived as a works team (grown out of the former Sauber/Stake outfit). At the season opener in Australia he banked two points from tenth on the grid with a smart tyre strategy (P9), giving Audi a score in its very first race. The 21-year-old currently sits on 2 championship points.

Notably, Bortoleto spent almost all of January in the simulator to master the complex energy-recovery systems of the new 2026 cars — and, by several observers' accounts, handled them better than some far more experienced rivals. In Montreal, a track where he has little prior experience, we'll see whether Audi can keep touch with the midfield.

Why the 2026 cars are different

2026 is the biggest technical reset year in over a decade. Three things changed fundamentally, and they explain why the pecking order has been reshuffled:

New power unit: the electrical share of the drivetrain was massively upgraded — the energy recovery unit (MGU-K) may now deliver roughly three times the power it used to, and the heat-based MGU-H component was scrapped. On top of that, F1 runs on sustainable fuel. Like a hybrid car where the electric motor is suddenly almost as important as the gas engine.
Active aerodynamics: instead of a single flap (DRS), front and rear wings can now actively change their angle — flat on the straights for top speed, steep in the corners for grip. Like a sail that trims itself as needed.

Add to that narrower and lighter cars (shorter wheelbase, ~30 kg less minimum weight). The net effect is cars that are slightly slower per lap than the previous generation — but the field bunches up tighter as soon as one team nails the new concept. That is exactly what Mercedes has done in 2026.

Endurance · Nürburgring, Le Mans & WEC

Nürburgring 24h: Mercedes-AMG wins, Verstappen's debut heartbreak

Last weekend (May 16–17) the Eifel hosted what may be the toughest endurance race in the world: the 24 Hours of the Nürburgring on the 25 km combination of the Nordschleife and the Grand Prix circuit. Overall victory went to the Mercedes-AMG GT3 #80 run by Winward Racing with Maro Engel, Luca Stolz, Fabian Schiller and Maxime Martin — Mercedes' first N24 overall win in ten years (last in 2016).

But the headline belonged to someone else: Max Verstappen contested his first-ever Nürburgring 24 Hours — in his own, self-entered Mercedes-AMG #3 — and ran in a winning position for a long time before a broken driveshaft stopped him hours from the flag. A bitter ending that stands as a symbol of Verstappen's 2026 so far: fast, but stopped by bad luck (and the machinery).

Green Hell: the nickname for the Nürburgring Nordschleife — over 20 km, 73 corners, ever-changing conditions, often rain in one section while the sun shines in another. A driving error or a technical failure over 24 hours is almost inevitable; reliability counts as much as speed.

Le Mans draws closer — and São Paulo after it

The next big endurance highlight is the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans on June 13–14 at the Circuit de la Sarthe — also the third round of the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC). The race traditionally starts on Saturday at 4:00 PM local (CEST) — for Daniel, 11:00 AM BRT. The official Test Day (Journée Test) is on May 31. In the Hypercar top class the works programs of Toyota, Ferrari, BMW, Porsche, Cadillac and others go head to head.

Of particular interest to Daniel: four weeks after Le Mans, on July 12, the WEC visits Interlagos for the 6 Hours of São Paulo — the only WEC home round in Brazil, and so a must-cover date in this newsletter as it approaches.

Hypercar: the fastest class in endurance racing such as Le Mans. Manufacturers build prototypes under a cost-capped rule set (LMH/LMDh) that are equalized via Balance of Performance — targeted weight and power adjustments, like a handicap in golf — so different concepts run at similar speeds and the racing stays close.
