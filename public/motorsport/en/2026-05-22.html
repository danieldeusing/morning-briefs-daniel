Formula 1 · Canada weekend

Sprint Friday: what counts today

Formula 1 returns after five race-free weeks — the gap arose because the races planned for mid-April in Bahrain and Saudi Arabia were canceled due to the war in the region; what was 24 planned races became 22. It resumes today at the Circuit Gilles Villeneuve, and right away with two sessions that count: Free Practice 1 (12:30 EDT / 13:30 BRT / 18:30 MESZ) and Sprint Qualifying (16:30 EDT / 17:30 BRT / 22:30 MESZ), which sets the grid for Saturday's sprint race.

The start of Sunday's main race is set for 16:00 local time — 17:00 BRT for Daniel, 22:00 MESZ in Germany. The unusually late start time is deliberate: it eases the clash with the simultaneously running Indianapolis 500, so fans can watch both classics.

Sprint weekend: instead of just qualifying plus race, there's an extra short race on Saturday (about a third of the distance, its own points for the top 8) plus a dedicated Sprint Qualifying on Friday. Like an appetizer contest before the main course — extra points, but also a second dose of risk: on Montreal's concrete-walled track ("Mur des Champions"), a small mistake quickly ends in the wall.

The upgrade arms race: Montreal becomes a technical battlefield

The real story of this weekend plays out in the garages. Three teams bring substantial developments to Montreal — and all aim at the same question: can the Mercedes dominance still be reeled in before the season is decided?

Mercedes brings the first update package of the year for the W17. Team boss Toto Wolff makes the case head-on: "Our rivals made a step in Miami, and we have to answer that." Notably, the championship leader is upgrading rather than resting on its lead. McLaren counters with an exceptionally broad package for the MCL40 — updates across seven areas (floor, chassis, front and rear wings, bodywork, halo, roll hoop) after Norris won the Miami sprint. Red Bull, following the big Miami overhaul (sidepods, lighter floor, active rear wing), this time brings smaller, balance-oriented updates.

Why upgrades count for so much in 2026: in a regulation-reset year, everyone starts with new, barely understood cars. Whoever "cracks" the new concept first pulls away — but the learning curve is steep, and a good update package can bring several tenths per lap. Like with a new programming language: the first to find the right patterns builds far faster. Exactly this race for the fastest learning curve is underway in Montreal.

Antonelli, 19, chases a historic record

The face of the 2026 season is Andrea Kimi Antonelli. The 19-year-old Italian missed the opening win in Australia (teammate George Russell took that one) but then won China, Japan and Miami in a row and leads the championship by 20 points over Russell. In Montreal he can make history: no driver has ever won the first four races of a career in a row — a fourth straight win to start a career would be exactly that.

Behind it lies a simple fact: Mercedes has won all four races and locked out the entire front row in each of the four qualifying sessions. In the 2026 regulation reset — when everyone starts from zero — the Brackley works team made the best start. That is precisely why the chasers' update package is so explosive.

Verstappen's hardest year — engine or chassis?

While Mercedes pulls away, four-time world champion Max Verstappen is going through his toughest season yet. With just 26 points he sits seventh; his best result from four races is a fifth place in Miami, and Red Bull scored only twelve points combined across the first two races. Experts argue over the cause: team boss Laurent Mekies says they don't have "the best engine" and want to improve the power unit built in-house with Ford. Technical analyses (gpblog, RacingNews365) disagree: the power unit is competitive for a newcomer — the real problem is the RB22's chassis, whose balance weaknesses overload the tires.

Power Unit vs. chassis: the "power unit" is the drivetrain (combustion engine plus electric motor), the "chassis" the rest of the car (structure, suspension, aerodynamics). Picture a bicycle: a strong rider (engine) is little use if the frame is bent and wobbles in every corner (chassis). A chassis problem is easier to fix over the season than an engine problem — cold comfort in Montreal.

Bortoleto: Brazil's man in the Audi works team

Especially relevant for Daniel: Gabriel Bortoleto has been, since this season, the first Brazilian full-time driver since Felipe Massa in 2017 — and drives for Audi, newly entered as a works team (grown out of the former Sauber/Stake team). Already at the season opener in Australia he gathered two points (P9) from tenth on the grid with smart tire strategy — Audi's first championship points ever. In Miami he finished twelfth. The 21-year-old currently sits on 2 championship points; in Montreal, a track he has little prior experience on, the goal is to reconnect with the midfield.

Endurance · WEC, Le Mans & São Paulo

WEC: BMW leads after Spa one-two

In the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC), two of eight rounds have been run. After Imola (Toyota win) and Spa (BMW one-two), BMW leads the manufacturers' standings for the first time — the Spa success was BMW's first Hypercar win ever. In the drivers' standings, the BMW pairing René Rast / Robin Frijns leads with 35 points, ahead of Toyota's Buemi/Hartley/Hirakawa.

Hypercar: the fastest class in endurance races like Le Mans. Manufacturers build prototypes under a cost-capped rule set (LMH/LMDh) that are equalized via Balance of Performance — targeted weight and power adjustments, like a handicap in golf — so different concepts run at similar speeds.

Nürburgring 24h: Mercedes-AMG wins, Verstappen's debut drama

Last weekend (May 16–17) the Mercedes-AMG GT3 #80 of Winward Racing/Team Ravenol won the 24 Hours of the Nürburgring with Maro Engel, Luca Stolz, Fabian Schiller and Maxime Martin — Mercedes's first overall N24 win in ten years. But the headline was written by Max Verstappen: he contested his first 24h race at the Nürburgring in his own Mercedes-AMG #3, led for a long time — and was stopped roughly three and a half hours from the end by a broken driveshaft. A bitter symbol of his 2026 so far.
