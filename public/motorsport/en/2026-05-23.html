Formula 1 · Canada weekend

What counts today: Sprint race, then Qualifying

Race day is tomorrow — but Saturday in Montreal is the real double-header. At 12:00 EDT / 13:00 BRT / 18:00 CEST the Sprint race runs over roughly a third of the race distance; it awards points to the first eight (8–7–6–5–4–3–2–1). Four hours later, at 16:00 EDT / 17:00 BRT / 22:00 CEST, comes Qualifying for Sunday's main race.

The Sprint starts from yesterday's Sprint-qualifying order — Russell in front, Antonelli alongside. Sunday's main race starts at 16:00 local (17:00 BRT, 22:00 CEST), deliberately two hours later than usual to ease the clash with the simultaneous Indianapolis 500.

Sprint weekend: instead of just Qualifying + race, there's an extra short race on Saturday with its own points for the top eight — like a starter contest before the main course. Extra points, but a second helping of risk too: on Montreal's concrete-walled circuit ("Wall of Champions") a small mistake quickly ends in the barriers.

Russell strikes back — Mercedes lockout number five

The big question coming into the weekend was whether the chasers' upgrade packages would crack Mercedes' dominance. The answer from Sprint qualifying: no — and it was George Russell who underlined it. With a 1:12.965 he pushed team-mate Antonelli (+0.068) to second and gave Mercedes its fifth complete front row in five events of 2026. In morning practice Antonelli had been quickest (1:13.402 ahead of Russell) — by the afternoon Russell had flipped the internal order.

Notably: the Mercedes update was meant to protect the advantage — and it held it. McLaren brought its broad MCL40 package and took the second row with Norris (P3) and Piastri (P4), but sat over three tenths behind Russell. Ferrari follow on row three (Hamilton P5, Leclerc P6), Red Bull behind (Verstappen P7, Hadjar P8). The verdict: the chasers got closer, but not close enough.

Front-row lockout: when both cars from one team take the front row — positions 1 and 2. In each of the five 2026 events a Silver Arrows one-two has headed the grid; proof of how clearly Mercedes has understood the new rules best so far.

Antonelli's record bid starts to wobble

Until Montreal the story was clear-cut: Andrea Kimi Antonelli, 19, missed the opener (winner: Russell) then won China, Japan and Miami in a row, and leads the title by 20 points. In Canada he can make history — no driver has ever won the first four races of their career consecutively. But this weekend, for the first time, the team-mate is faster: Russell on Sprint pole, Antonelli 0.068 behind.

That makes Sunday's race the key. If Antonelli doesn't win, the historic shot at four straight wins from a career start ends — and the intra-Mercedes title fight gains a new edge. Today's Sprint race is already a pointer: whichever Silver Arrow shows the better race pace carries the psychological advantage into Qualifying.

Friday chaos: a groundhog, two crashes, an engine change

Friday in Montreal was unusually turbulent. In practice Alexander Albon collided with a groundhog on track — the impact damaged his Williams so badly that he couldn't take part in Sprint qualifying at all; he starts the Sprint from P21. Liam Lawson (Racing Bulls) was sidelined by a hydraulic failure and starts 22nd; Esteban Ocon lost his front wing in a bizarre brush with the wall, and Franco Colapinto needed an engine change. In Sprint qualifying itself Fernando Alonso brought out a red flag in SQ1 with a hit at Turn 3; he starts the Sprint from P16.

Why a groundhog makes headlines: the Circuit Gilles Villeneuve sits on an island in the St Lawrence River (Île Notre-Dame) — wildlife on track is a known, almost annual occurrence there. At 300 km/h even a small animal is a hard obstacle: the impact can wreck suspension or floor, as it did for Albon.

Bortoleto: Audi mid-pack, a points chance in the Sprint

Of particular relevance for Daniel: Gabriel Bortoleto, the first Brazilian full-time driver since Felipe Massa in 2017, starts the Sprint from P12 — right behind Audi team-mate Nico Hülkenberg (P11). Both Audis are stuck in the midfield but within striking distance of the points: in a turbulent Sprint on the wall-lined circuit, a jump into the top eight isn't out of the question. Bortoleto sits on 2 points in the standings (P9 on his Australia debut, Audi's first points ever); Montreal is largely new ground for him.

Endurance · WEC, Le Mans & São Paulo

WEC: BMW leads after the Spa one-two

In the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC), two of eight rounds are done. After Imola (Toyota win) and Spa (BMW one-two), BMW leads the manufacturers' table for the first time — the Spa result was BMW's first Hypercar win and its first outright endurance victory since Le Mans 1999. In the drivers' standings the BMW crew René Rast / Sheldon van der Linde / Robin Frijns leads, ahead of the Toyota crews.

Hypercar: the fastest class in endurance racing such as Le Mans. Manufacturers build prototypes under a cost-capped rule set (LMH/LMDh), balanced via Balance of Performance — targeted weight and power adjustments, like a handicap in golf — so different concepts run at similar pace.

Le Mans draws closer — and São Paulo after it

The next big endurance highlight is the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans in race week from 10–14 June at the Circuit de la Sarthe. The race traditionally starts on Saturday, 13 June at 16:00 local (CEST) — 11:00 BRT for Daniel. Le Mans is the season's third actually-run WEC round (listed as round 4 on the original calendar, because Qatar was postponed). In the Hypercar top class, Toyota, Ferrari, BMW, Porsche, Cadillac, Aston Martin and others go head-to-head.

Of particular interest for Daniel: four weeks after Le Mans, on 12 July, the WEC visits Interlagos with the 6 Hours of São Paulo — the only WEC home round in Brazil, and therefore a fixture in this newsletter as it draws nearer.
