Formula 1 · Canada weekend

What matters today: the race — and the rain

The Canadian Grand Prix starts today at 16:00 EDT / 17:00 BRT / 22:00 MESZ over the full distance, round 5 of 22. The grid is set: Russell on pole, Antonelli alongside. The start is deliberately two hours later than usual to avoid clashing with the simultaneous Indianapolis 500 — and the date is unusual too: this is the first Canadian GP in May rather than its traditional June slot.

The big unknown is the weather. Race day brings a 60–70% chance of precipitation, plus a chilly ~12 °C and gusts to 36 km/h. It would be the first wet race in which the all-new 2026 generation meets rain in competition — nobody has reliable wet data on these cars. Tyre choice, pit timing and a well-judged switch to intermediates can flip the result; on the wall-lined island circuit, a small mistake ends the race.

Intermediates: rain tyres for a wet but not flooded track — the tread clears water without sacrificing as much grip as the extreme full-wet tyre. In changeable rain, the timing of the switch often decides the whole race: too early and the tyre overheats on a drying track, too late and you lose seconds in the wet.

Russell's perfect Saturday: sprint win and pole

Who wins the upgrade arms race was the question of the weekend — the answer is still Mercedes, and it's George Russell putting down the exclamation mark. First he won the sprint race (finishing time 28:50.951) ahead of Norris (+1.272) and Antonelli (+1.843), then he took pole for the main race with a 1:12.578. Both times he beat Antonelli by exactly 0.068 seconds — the same gap as in Friday's sprint qualifying. It's the fifth Mercedes front-row lockout in five events in 2026.

Behind them the gap has shrunk but not closed: McLaren (Norris P3, Piastri P4) and Ferrari (Hamilton P5, Leclerc P8) are tightly packed, with Red Bull in the mix (Verstappen P6, Hadjar P7). Just four tenths covered the qualifying top 8 — close, but Mercedes stays in front.

Front-row lockout: when both of a team's cars take the front row — P1 and P2. In every one of the five 2026 events a Silver Arrows one-two has stood at the front; proof of how clearly Mercedes has best understood the new regulations so far.

The crash that puts Antonelli's record chase in doubt

The sprint produced the first real intra-Mercedes flashpoint. On lap 5, Antonelli tried to pass around the outside at Turns 1/2; the two Silver Arrows touched, Antonelli was forced off track, cut the corner and dropped back — and Norris seized on the scrap to take P2. On the radio Antonelli raged, called Russell's defence "very naughty" and demanded a penalty. The stewards saw no case: no investigation into Russell.

That makes today's race the key. Andrea Kimi Antonelli, 19, won China, Japan and Miami back-to-back after missing out in the opener, and now leads the title with 106 points. Today he can make history — no driver has ever won the first four races of his career in a row (the sprint counts separately, so the GP streak is formally still intact). But Russell was faster three times this weekend, starts from pole, and has cut the title gap from 20 to 18 points. In the rain, where grid position matters less, everything is open.

Penalty lottery: Hamilton clear, Hülkenberg +10s

Two similar scenes, two different verdicts. Nico Hülkenberg (Audi) picked up a 10-second penalty in the sprint for leaving the track and gaining an advantage on Liam Lawson. Lewis Hamilton (Ferrari) cut the final chicane (Turn 13) late on and stayed ahead of Piastri — but was investigated after the race and not penalised. The difference, per the stewards: Hülkenberg was actively defending a position, whereas Piastri was never in a position to pass Hamilton — so no "lasting advantage" under the driving standards guidelines. Hamilton keeps sprint P6, with no knock-on to today's grid.

Track limits & "lasting advantage": the white lines mark the permitted track width. Run all four wheels beyond them and gain time or a position, and you risk a penalty. What matters is whether a real advantage resulted: force a chaser off or keep ahead of an attacker, yes — drift wide briefly with no rival duel, often no.

Bortoleto: Audi in the midfield, P13 into the race

Especially relevant for Daniel: Gabriel Bortoleto, the first Brazilian full-time driver since Felipe Massa, had a mixed weekend. He finished the sprint 12th (outside the points), and in main-race qualifying he was knocked out in Q2 as 13th — this time behind Audi team-mate Hülkenberg (P11), who flipped the intra-team duel. Both Audis sit in the midfield, within striking distance of the points. In the expected rain a well-timed tyre stop can shuffle the order — Bortoleto's best shot at more than his current 2 championship points.

Endurance · WEC, Le Mans & São Paulo

WEC: BMW leads going into the Le Mans build-up

Two rounds of the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC) have been run. After Imola (Toyota win) and Spa (BMW one-two), BMW leads the manufacturers' table — the Spa result was BMW's first Hypercar win and its first overall win in global endurance racing since Le Mans 1999. In the drivers' standings the BMW crew René Rast / Robin Frijns (#20) leads with 35 points, ahead of the Toyota crews. No race is run until Le Mans — the standings are frozen.

Hypercar: the fastest class at endurance races like Le Mans. Manufacturers build prototypes to a cost-capped ruleset (LMH/LMDh) that are equalised via Balance of Performance — targeted weight and power adjustments, like a handicap in golf — so different concepts run at similar pace.

Le Mans draws closer — and São Paulo after that

The next big endurance highlight is the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans, race week 10–14 June at the Circuit de la Sarthe.
