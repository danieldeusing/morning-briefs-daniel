Formula 1 · The sibling fight at Mercedes

Wolff threatens team orders — "not a millimetre hesitant"

The intra-Mercedes duel between Kimi Antonelli and George Russell boiled over in Canada: the two Silver Arrows touched once in Saturday's sprint and once in Sunday's main race, before Russell's engine failure ended the fight on its own. Team principal Toto Wolff has now made clear he won't watch indefinitely. "More than ever, this fight is on," he said — but added the warning that if the team's points were at risk of being lost, or too much time were being lost to the competition, "we would not be a millimetre hesitant of putting the handbrake on".

Team orders: a team's instruction to its two drivers not to race each other — for example letting the faster one through, or freezing positions. Like two siblings in the same jersey, where the coach says: "Pass the ball to each other instead of stealing it." Legal, but contentious with fans because it smothers real competition.

Russell: "The title is Antonelli's to lose"

Remarkably candid, George Russell reads the situation himself. After the Montreal zero — an engine failure out of the lead on lap 30, having won the sprint and pole — he admits the championship is now "Antonelli's to lose". The 43-point lead his 19-year-old team-mate holds after four wins in a row is a deficit that weighs heavily in an already-shortened comeback. It is the honest assessment of a driver who knows a single failure has cost him half a season's cushion.

Verstappen's exit clause is ticking

While Mercedes runs out front, contractual pressure is building at Red Bull. Max Verstappen is tied down through 2028, but his deal contains an exit option: if he sits outside the top two of the drivers' standings at the summer-break cutoff — Monday, July 27 — he is free to test the market. Right now he is only seventh on 43 points, a full 45 behind Russell in 2nd. Red Bull's package, struggling in the 2026 regulation reset, makes the clause a real threat — Canada (P3) was their best weekend of the year, and even that barely moves the maths.

Hamilton within reach — and Bortoleto's Monaco chance

The quiet climber is still Lewis Hamilton: after his hard-fought P2 in Canada (72 points), he trails Ferrari team-mate Charles Leclerc (75) by just 3 points — making the intra-Ferrari duel as tight as the one at Mercedes. Relevant for Daniel: Gabriel Bortoleto (Audi), the first full-time Brazilian driver since Felipe Massa, sits on 2 championship points (P15). In Monaco, where a single clean qualifying lap counts, the tight street circuit forgives the Audis' pace deficit more readily than a fast track — his next real chance.

MotoGP · Italian GP at Mugello

An Aprilia one-two races into the Ducati den

With Formula 1 on a break, MotoGP delivers the only major race this weekend: the Italian Grand Prix at Mugello (round 7 of 22). Surprisingly, Aprilia leads the table — Marco Bezzecchi (142 points) ahead of brand-mate Jorge Martín (127). Only then comes Fabio Di Giannantonio (VR46 Ducati, 116), who two weeks ago won the chaotic Barcelona race — marked by two red flags and three race starts — for his first MotoGP win since 2023. The best KTM rider is Pedro Acosta (92); the factory Ducatis of Bagnaia (63) and Marc Márquez (57) are surprisingly off the pace.

Mugello is Ducati's home track and traditionally high-speed terrain — the weekend is a litmus test of whether the Bologna marque strikes back on home soil or Aprilia cements its lead. The main race runs Sunday, May 31, at 14:00 CEST / 09:00 BRT over 23 laps; Saturday's sprint (15:00 CEST / 10:00 BRT) hands out half points.

Sprint (MotoGP): since 2023 MotoGP runs a short race every Saturday over half the distance — half points for the top nine. Like a halftime mini-game that already counts: there are real points twice a weekend, not just on Sunday. The grid for both the sprint and the main race is set by the same Saturday-morning qualifying.

Endurance · WEC, Le Mans & São Paulo

WEC: BMW still ahead — standings frozen until Le Mans

In the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC), only two rounds have run. After Imola (Toyota win) and Spa (BMW one-two), BMW leads the manufacturers' standings on 59 points — the Spa result was BMW's first-ever Hypercar win. Toyota (52) trail by just seven, Ferrari (42) within reach. With no round until Le Mans (Jun 13/14), the standings are unchanged since Spa.

Hypercar: the fastest class in endurance races like Le Mans. Manufacturers build prototypes under a cost-capped rule set (LMH/LMDh) that are equalised via Balance of Performance — targeted weight and power adjustments, like a handicap in golf — so different concepts run at similar pace.
