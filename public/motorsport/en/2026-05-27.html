The Mercedes year — and the rule tweak ahead [1][2][3]

Compression ratio: How much the engine compresses the gasoline-air mixture before ignition. Picture an air pump you squeeze down hard — the harder you press, the more powerful the "pop." Higher compression = more power, but the 2026 rule caps it at 16:1 (down from 18:1) so all manufacturers are treated equally.

The 2026 regulation reset — new chassis, new power units — was supposed to mix up the field. Instead it catapulted Mercedes to the top, much like the hybrid shift in 2014. Kimi Antonelli won in Canada for the fourth straight time and leads the drivers' championship with 131 points; Mercedes sits atop the constructors' standings with 219 to Ferrari's 147.[1]

The dispute underneath: rivals suspect Mercedes meets the 16:1 limit only in the cold engine and gains more compression — and thus more power — under hot running. The FIA has announced a compromise: starting June 1, 2026, both cold and 130 °C readings will be taken; from 2027 onward only hot. This falls right before the Monaco weekend, forcing all teams to make engines compliant by then.[2] Estimates put a Mercedes advantage at around half a second per lap — the tweak could chip at it, but likely won't erase it.[3]

Context Important: it's not a retroactive disqualification, but a tightening of the measurement method going forward. Antonelli's past wins stand. What's interesting is whether Mercedes stays just as quick from Monaco onward.

It's Wednesday, May 27. Formula 1 takes a week off after Canada, but motorsport stays busy — and one question hangs over everything: how fast is Mercedes really?

The big 2026 regulation reset has a clear winner. Rookie Kimi Antonelli won in Montreal for the fourth straight time and leads the drivers' championship with 131 points. Mercedes also dominates the constructors' standings well ahead of Ferrari. According to formula1.com, this echoes the hybrid shift of 2014, when Mercedes broke away for a whole era.

But the advantage is controversial. The competition suspects Mercedes is exploiting a loophole in the new engine rule — more power in hot conditions than the rules technically allow. The FIA responded: starting June 1, the engine gets measured both cold and hot, as ESPN reports. That's right before Monaco on June 7. Key point: it's not a penalty, Antonelli's wins stand. The real question is whether Mercedes can still run this fast after the tweak.

In Canada itself, Max Verstappen salvaged third place — his best result in an otherwise rough season. Red Bull is struggling: Verstappen is only seventh in points and says his car is undrivable. Analysts at GP-Blog see the problem less in the engine than the chassis balance.

But this weekend belongs to motorcycles. MotoGP races at Mugello in Italy. Aprilia is the surprise: Marco Bezzecchi leads with 142 points ahead of teammate Jorge Martín. Record world champion Marc Márquez is down in ninth. For Daniel in Brazil: the race Sunday starts at nine in the morning local time, the sprint Saturday at ten, and qualifying that morning at 5:50.

In long-distance racing, BMW scored a double in Spa — the first Hypercar win since coming back to the top class and the first factory win since 1999. BMW now leads just ahead of Toyota and Ferrari. The next event is the season's crown jewel: the 24 Hours of Le Mans in mid-June.

Tomorrow and the next day are all about Mugello and the race. Then comes Monaco — and the question of whether the regulation tweak really slows Mercedes. Until then.
