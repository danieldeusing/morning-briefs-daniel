FP1 Mugello: Di Giannantonio leads, Márquez back at P15 [1][2]

Italy's MotoGP free practice opener is in the books — on a damp track after morning rain, air 22 °C, asphalt 23 °C. Fabio Di Giannantonio (VR46-Ducati), fresh off a Barcelona win, posted 1:46.242 as the quickest time, followed by Jorge Martín (Aprilia, +0.369) and Ai Ogura (Trackhouse-Aprilia). Crutchlow fills in for Zarco — three years out, "the hardest thing I've ever done" [3][4]

This weekend at Honda LCR, a 40-year-old is sitting where MotoGP hasn't seen him since the 2023 Japan GP: Cal Crutchlow, three wins in his active career, latterly a Yamaha test driver. He's replacing badly injured Johann Zarco and says himself it will be "probably the hardest thing I've ever done".[3] At the Misano IRTA manufacturer test on Wednesday, he checked his physical condition before LCR confirmed the deal on Thursday.

Zarco's injury bill from the Barcelona restart crash is severe: both ACL and PCL ruptured, inner meniscus damaged, small fibula fracture on his left ankle. Surgery must wait until the knee de-swells first; LCR boss Lucio Cecchinello expects "a couple of months" out — "Mugello and Hungary are basically impossible".[4]

ACL+PCL+meniscus: Think of your knee as a door hinge — a band in front and back, plus a shock-absorbing cartridge disc. Tear both bands and the cartridge is damaged too, and the hinge is completely open. Surgery has to wait because the joint must de-swell first — otherwise the new band won't hold. Four to six months of rehab is realistic if all goes smoothly.

F1 on pause: the ADUO powder dries [8][9]

While F1 takes a week between Canada and Monaco, the spring's most critical regulation decision comes due in the background. ADUO — the relief rule for engine manufacturers 2% or 4% behind the leader on the performance index — delivers its first decision within two weeks of Canada.[8] That window is running right now.

ADUO (Additional Development and Upgrade Opportunities): Imagine a lower-league football club with the oldest training ground gets to hire an extra elite coach for a season, so the league doesn't run the same way every year. That's roughly how ADUO works with engines: whoever sits ≥ 2% behind the best on the dyno can take an extra upgrade; from 4% onward, two of them. It's meant to stop one team (like Mercedes 2014) from being locked in for five years straight.

The surprise twist of recent days: Red Bull itself is leaning toward not taking the upgrade even if approved. Team principal Laurent Mekies publicly expects the Honda/RBPT power unit to cross the threshold — but his team is inclined toward the strategic move of saving resources for the 2027 engine (Red Bull + Ford building one for the first time together).[9] The calculus: six months of catch-up racing in 2026 costs dearly and eats into 2027 prep — and Mercedes dominance is already so entrenched in 2026 that an ICE upgrade won't flip it anyway.

Context If approval comes AND Red Bull actually declines, that's the season's most honest 2027 message: the team admits the year is lost and pivots to the reset. Strategically clean, competitively risky — Verstappen's patience has had limits in the past.

Monaco weekend — what to expect [10]

A week away: what's likely the slowest, tactically most complex race of the year. Track 3.337 km, 78 laps, ~35 overtakes total on average — and the result is usually decided in qualifying. For Mercedes, Monaco is the first test of the announced team orders line (Wolff: "back off a step if you're breathing down each other's necks"). Antonelli leads by 43 pts over Russell — and in Monaco there's no "sorted in the pits" scenario that won't cost the pole straight into Sunday.

For Charles Leclerc (Ferrari) it's a home race — last win there in 2024. Ferrari trails Mercedes by 72 pts — one win changes nothing statistically but would polish the year's burnished shame. Verstappen: P7 in standings, Red Bull P4 in constructors — Monaco's history rarely delivers statistical miracles to teams without the pole.

WEC: Le Mans build-up — new Hyperpole format [11]

The 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans runs 13–14.06, the official test day already Sun 08.06 — parallel to Monaco's F1 Sunday. Entered: 62 vehicles total, including 18 Hypercars (3× Ferrari 499P as defending champs, 3× Toyota GR010, 2× BMW M Hybrid V8, plus Cadillac, Peugeot, Aston Martin, Alpine, Genesis).

New Hyperpole format: per class, the 8 fastest cars qualify for a 30-min shoot-out for pole — uncompromising, traffic-free. This replaces the old "Hyperpole for top Hypercars only" and finally gives LMP2/GT3 their own stage. BMW starts as manufacturer leader with 59 pts, just ahead of Toyota (52) and Ferrari (42), unbeaten at Le Mans since 2023.
