Quali Mugello: Bezzecchi makes history — 1:43 pole, Aprilia front row [1]

That's the answer to yesterday's pressure narrative. Marco Bezzecchi posted 1:43,921 in Mugello qualifying — the first sub-1:44 lap in track history — a new track record that clinched pole in front of 50,000+ Aprilia supporters. Behind him, Raúl Fernández (Trackhouse-Aprilia, +0.224) and Jorge Martín (Aprilia, +0.363) complete a pure Aprilia front row — on the very track where the marque has never won a MotoGP race.[1]

Row two is the real shocker: Marc Márquez qualifies at P4 (+0.373) — best Ducati, just behind the Aprilia wall. Fermín Aldeguer (Gresini-Ducati, P5) and Pecco Bagnaia (P6) follow. Friday's pacesetter Di Giannantonio starts P7 — qualifying pace belonged to the Aprilias.

Context Pole does not equal victory, least of all in a sprint. But Bezzecchi converted the pressure he created himself — his public "dream come true" statement — into the one session where it matters: against the clock, no traffic. Yesterday's statistics curse (Aprilia without a Mugello win, Bezzecchi without a podium there) now faces its own challenge against the pole he just claimed.

Márquez: from FP1-P15 to Quali-P4 — comeback holds [2][3]

P15 yesterday with 1.2 seconds adrift in comeback FP1, best Ducati at P4 today. Marc Márquez delivered the fastest possible return after his double surgery (right foot, fifth metatarsal + long-troubled right shoulder, both operated day after his Le Mans sprint crash on 9 May) — direct Q2 entry via Friday practice (P6) and then a qualifying lap that puts him just 0.373 seconds from pole and ahead of both factory Ducatis.[2]

The crucial detail isn't on the timing sheet: Márquez passed his final medical clearance Friday morning and is approved for the full weekend — not just FP1.[3] His pre-weekend messaging was damage control ("not maximum intensity, but pushing through"). P4 in qualifying turns that into a podium option, if shoulder and foot hold over sprint and race distance.

Q2 direct entry: Think of qualifying like a two-stage casting call. The top 10 from Friday's practice skip the first round (Q1) and go straight to the final (Q2) to battle for the first twelve grid spots. Anyone outside the top 10 must get through Q1 — and can only reach a maximum of position 11 or 12 from there. Márquez secured his direct entry Friday; Acosta did not.

Friday Practice: Diggia double, three red flags, Acosta in Q1 [4]

Friday belonged to Fabio Di Giannantonio at first: P1 double across both sessions, the decisive combined practice in 1:44,808 — just 0.091 seconds ahead of Bagnaia. He advanced directly to Q2 alongside Bagnaia, Bastianini, Morbidelli, Aldeguer, and Márquez.[4] Jorge Martín secured Q2 entry on a "lung-busting" final lap at P8.

Most notable loser: Pedro Acosta (KTM). A late-lap mistake dropped him to P13 — detour through Q1, from which he clawed back only to P10 on Saturday. Plus three red flags in practice: Quartararo crashed at Turn 4, Miller at Turn 1, Binder had a technical issue at pit exit.

F1: Break — Monaco weekend ahead [6]

F1 on break between Canada (24.05.) and Monaco — no new race standings. The 5–7.06. event is likely the slowest, tactically trickiest race of the year: 3.337 km, 78 laps, result almost always decided in qualifying. For Mercedes, it's the first real test of the announced team-orders protocol — Antonelli leads Russell by 43 points, and at Monaco there's no "pit box to sort it" scenario that can still defuse an expensive pole order on Sunday.

For Charles Leclerc (Ferrari), Monaco is home (last win there 2024). Ferrari trails Mercedes by 72 points — a single victory won't close that gap, but it would polish the season narrative. Verstappen: P7 in the championship, Red Bull P4 among constructors — Monaco rarely rewards come-from-behind efforts when pole eludes you.

WEC: Le Mans build-up — new Hyperpole format [7]

The 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans is 13–14.06., with the official test day already on Sun 08.06. — parallel to Monaco F1 Sunday. Entries: 62 cars, including 18 Hypercars (3× Ferrari 499P as defending champions, 3× Toyota GR010, 2× BMW M Hybrid V8, plus Cadillac, Peugeot, Aston Martin, Alpine, Genesis).

New Hyperpole format: per class, the 8 quickest cars advance to a 30-minute shootout for pole — zero traffic. BMW enters as manufacturer table-leader (59 pts), just ahead of Toyota (52) and Ferrari (42), unbeaten at Le Mans since 2023.

Brazil watch: quiet week before Monaco

F1 side is quiet. Franco Colapinto (Alpine, P11 championship, 15 pts) simulator work after his career-best P6 in Canada — Alpine is talking their best season start ever. Gabriel Bortoleto (Audi, P15 championship, 2 pts) remains sole full-time Brazilian F1 driver since Massa (2017); his Canada diagnosis ("no quick fix" in Audi's tire strategy) faces another test at Monaco. In MotoGP, Diogo Moreira (LCR-Honda) posts his rookie weekend at P8 in Mugello qualifying — Honda's best result and strongest qualifying finish for the Brazilian. WEC-side no Brazilian in Hypercar, but Augusto Farfus (BMW M-Team WRT) in LMGT3.

It's Saturday, May thirtieth. In Tuscany, the Mugello weekend hits its critical day — and qualifying just rewrote yesterday's entire pressure script.

Marco Bezzecchi, the championship leader on Aprilia, has posted the first one-forty-three lap in Mugello history: one forty-three point nine two one, a new track record, claiming pole in front of his Italian Aprilia fans. Behind him, Raúl Fernández and Jorge Martín — a complete Aprilia front row. And on the very track where Aprilia has never won a MotoGP race. Bezzecchi opened the weekend saying "a dream come true" — and delivered in the one session that matters: against the clock, solo.

The second big story is Marc Márquez again. Fifteenth in comeback practice yesterday, double surgery on foot and shoulder — today he qualifies fourth, best Ducati, just three tenths off pole. His final medical clearance passed Friday morning; he races the full weekend. What looked like damage control has become a podium shot.

Friday itself belonged first to Fabio Di Giannantonio, leading both practice sessions and advancing with five others straight into the qualifying final. Bad news for Pedro Acosta: a late mistake dropped him to thirteenth and forced a detour through Q1. Three red flags too — Quartararo, Miller, and Binder.

For Daniel in Brazil, the brutal part was early this morning with qualifying at five fifty. The sprint runs today at ten Brasília time — not yet run as this brief was made — and the race proper runs tomorrow morning at nine o'clock for twenty-three laps. Sprint results in tomorrow's brief.

Formula One stays on break before Monaco. Standings unchanged since Canada: Kimi Antonelli leads George Russell by forty-three points; Russell suffered an engine failure in Montreal. Mercedes leads the constructors' table by seventy-two over Ferrari. Monaco from June fifth to seventh will be Mercedes' first real test of their team-orders line — and on this track, qualifying decides almost everything because overtaking barely happens.

In endurance racing, Le Mans is coming closer. Ninety-fourth running, June thirteenth and fourteenth, test day a week before. Eighteen Hypercars, new Hyperpole format, and BMW heads in as table-leader ahead of Toyota and Ferrari, unbeaten here since twenty twenty-three.

And one Brazil note from Mugello: rookie Diogo Moreira on the LCR Honda qualified eighth — Honda's best result and his best qualifying finish. Sprint this morning, race tomorrow early. Until then.
