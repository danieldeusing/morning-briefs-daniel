Mugello sprint: Fernández wins, Bezzecchi squanders pole [1][2]

The pole from Saturday morning (Bezzecchi with a track record) evaporated Saturday afternoon. Marco Bezzecchi was caught off-guard in turn 1 by charging rookie Diogo Moreira, fell from P1 to P6, and fought back over the eleven laps only to P4.[2] The sprint was won by his Aprilia brand teammate Raúl Fernández (Trackhouse) — his first MotoGP sprint win, achieved while dealing with gastroenteritis, with a 1.289 s margin over Jorge Martín (P2). Local favorite Fabio Di Giannantonio (VR46-Ducati) completed the podium.[1]

One tire call made all the difference: only Fernández and Martín chose the medium rear-tire compound — and those two broke away. Marc Márquez was P5 (+9,055 s) one week after his double surgery, Aldeguer P6, Bagnaia only P7 after a poor start in front of his home crowd. Three DNFs: Mir, Bastianini, Morbidelli.

Analysis Pole means nothing in a sprint if the start fails — Mugello's long braking zone into turn 1 punishes hesitation brutally. Bezzecchi didn't convert his own "dream come true" pressure into points; instead of extending his lead, he let Martín claw back 3 points. For today's main race, this matters: in warm-up Bezzecchi was immediately fastest again — he has the pace, what he needs now is a clean getaway.

IndyCar: Detroit today — Palou third straight pole [4][5]

Today IndyCar visits the Detroit city circuit (Chevrolet Detroit Grand Prix, 1.645 mi, nine turns, 100 laps) — start 13:30 BRT / 12:30 EDT.[4] Leading from pole is Álex Palou (Chip Ganassi, Honda): his third straight pole (1:01,901) and his 16th career pole — across three completely different track types (IMS road course, Indy 500 oval, now city circuit). Behind him Will Power, Scott McLaughlin, and Scott Dixon.[5]

IndyCar: North America's top single-seater series — kind of the US equivalent to F1, but with standard chassis (Dallara) and a mixed calendar of ovals, street circuits, and traditional road courses. more evenly matched than F1, because aerodynamics and chassis are identical for all; the driver makes the bigger difference.

Palou leads the season comfortably after Indianapolis 500: 273 pts, 37 pts ahead of David Malukas. The 2025 regular-season champion is well on his way to repeating that dominance — despite a post-race penalty at Indy that didn't dent his lead.

Brazil watch: Moreira shines, F1 on hold

In Mugello, rookie Diogo Moreira (LCR-Honda) delivered his best weekend yet: P8 in qualifying (best Honda result) and in the sprint even up to P3 early — he was the one who caught Bezzecchi off-guard in turn 1 — before dropping back to P10 after an overly aggressive move. Mature pace, youthful ambition: exactly the mix of a promising rookie. In F1, Franco Colapinto (Alpine, P11 championship, 15 pts) and Gabriel Bortoleto (Audi, P15 championship, 2 pts) prepare for Monaco during the break — Bortoleto remains, since Massa (2017), the only full-time Brazilian F1 driver. In today's IndyCar and NASCAR, no Brazilian top favorite.

It is Sunday, May thirty-first — and for Daniel a triple-header race day: MotoGP in Mugello in the morning, IndyCar in Detroit at midday, NASCAR in Nashville tonight.

The main story comes from Tuscany, and it flipped overnight. In Saturday's sprint, Marco Bezzecchi squandered his track-record pole: in the first turn, he was caught off-guard by Brazilian rookie Diogo Moreira, dropped to sixth, and fought back only to fourth. His Aprilia teammate Raúl Fernández took the win — his first sprint victory in the premier class, achieved while battling gastroenteritis. Second was Jorge Martín.

That shrinks Bezzecchi's championship lead from fifteen to twelve points. In numbers: one hundred forty-eight to one hundred thirty-six. Marc Márquez came fifth one week after his double surgery — and was able to take notes afterward, a good sign for the nerve.

The main race runs today at nine in the morning Brazilian time, twenty-three laps, and has not yet been contested at briefing time. In the morning warm-up, Bezzecchi was immediately fastest again — he has the pace, now he just needs to nail the start at turn one. Race results will be in tomorrow's brief.

In parallel, IndyCar visits Detroit, start at one thirty Brazilian time. On pole is Álex Palou — his third straight pole, across three completely different track types. Palou leads the season comfortably with thirty-seven points in hand. The context: a week ago, Felix Rosenqvist won the Indianapolis five hundred with a pass on the final lap — margin zero point zero two three three seconds, one of the closest finishes in history.

In Formula One, racing remains on hold ahead of Monaco. Standings are unchanged since Canada: Kimi Antonelli leads by forty-three points over George Russell, Mercedes seventy-two points clear of Ferrari. Monaco from the fifth to seventh of June will be the first real test of Mercedes' team-orders approach — and on this track, qualifying decides almost everything.

In long-distance racing, Le Mans draws closer: test day June eighth, the twenty-four-hour race June thirteenth and fourteenth. BMW enters as title leader — the Spa win was BMW's first world endurance win since Le Mans nineteen ninety-nine.

And the Brazil watch: Diogo Moreira was the discovery of Mugello weekend — best Honda result, briefly even third in the sprint. Race this morning, Detroit at midday. See you tomorrow.
