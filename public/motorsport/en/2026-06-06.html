MotoGP: Márquez takes pole — despite crash [1][2]

The first headline of the racing weekend comes from Hungary: Marc Márquez has secured pole position at Balaton Park in qualifying — with 1:36.785 and only 0.053 s ahead of Pedro Acosta (KTM).[1] Remarkable: Márquez crashed in his first flying lap at the freshly paved (and slippery) turn 1, but came back and turned the tables in the closing minutes. Fermín Aldeguer (Gresini Ducati, +0.340) completes a purely Spanish front row.[2]

Pole position: the first starting place, awarded in qualifying to the fastest driver in a single lap — like the inside lane start in the 100-meter dash. On tight, overtake-poor circuits, pole is half the race win.

The special thing about today: Márquez is racing his second race back after double surgery (foot + shoulder) — and appears hardly hindered by it; at Balaton he looked stronger than last week in Mugello. In the championship he remains at P8 (71 points), far behind the two Aprilia factory drivers. A pole doesn't change the table; it changes expectations for the Sprint and race.

For the championship leaders, the grid is less ideal: Bezzecchi (leading, 173) starts only from P6, his only real rival Martín (156) even from P8 — both behind the Ducati trio Bagnaia (P5), Di Giannantonio (P4). In today's 13-lap Sprint, the 17-point gap can certainly shift.

F1: Monaco qualifying decides — right now at 11:00 BRT [3][4]

In Formula 1, the most important part of the day is still to come: qualifying at 16:00 MESZ / 11:00 BRT (at the time of writing results were still pending).[3] Nowhere does Saturday count as much as here: 3.337 km, 78 laps, a result that almost always falls in qualifying because overtaking in Monaco is nearly impossible. Before that, FP3 runs (12:30 MESZ / 07:30 BRT).

Monaco is more than just a single GP this time: it opens a phase of six races in eight weeks — and is the first real test of Mercedes team management.[4] After the tough duel in Canada, team principal Toto Wolff has reaffirmed the red line: the fight goes on, but if team points are at risk, they will "apply the brake." Sky commentator David Croft explicitly advises against it.

Team orders: when two drivers from the same team are battling for positions, the team can order who gets to drive up front — like a coach stopping two strikers from both going for the same goal, so neither runs the other down. Sensitive, because both also want their own championship.

Antonelli leads with 131 points, 43 ahead of Russell (88) — the gap grew in Canada through Russell's engine failure. For Charles Leclerc (Ferrari, P3 with 75) it's the home race; Lewis Hamilton (P4, 72) is only 3 points behind him. Verstappen (P7, 43) struggles on a circuit that rarely rewards comebacks without pole — today every thousandth in qualifying counts.

WEC: Le Mans gets a new Hyperpole format [5][6][7]

The 94th edition of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (race 3 of the WEC season) starts Sat 13.06. at 16:00 MESZ / 11:00 BRT, finish Sunday afternoon.[5] Before that, the race week runs: practice and qualifying start Wed 10.06., the Hyperpole Thursday evening. The test day tomorrow (07.06.) is the first real pace check.

New this year: a revamped Hyperpole format.[6] Instead of one session per class, there are now two stages, and two more cars per class advance.

Hyperpole (new 2026): an intensified qualifying shootout for the best starting spots. First a regular qualifying per class — the fastest 15 move on. In Hyperpole 1 (20 min.) positions 11–15 lock in their starting spot; the top 10 advance to Hyperpole 2 (15 min.) and race there in isolation for pole. More drama, every lap counts.

62 cars are entered, including 18 in the top Hypercar class — including a trio of Ferrari 499P (defending champions, undefeated at Le Mans since 2023).[7] New: Genesis (Hyundai's luxury brand) is the first Korean Hypercar team; Porsche is absent after factory withdrawal. For F1 fans: returning drivers Logan Sargeant (Proton) and Jack Doohan (Nielsen) in the field.

IndyCar: Bommarito 500 tomorrow in primetime [8][9]

IndyCar races the Bommarito Automotive Group 500 on Sunday at the Gateway Oval (World Wide Technology Raceway near St. Louis) — the 10th edition, again under the lights and in TV primetime.[8] Scheduled start 20:00 CDT on FOX, so 22:00 BRT for Daniel on Sunday evening (the green flag falls slightly later in the evening after the pre-race show) — the night shift of the weekend. 260 laps on a 1.25-mile oval.

Álex Palou (Chip Ganassi, Honda) arrives as a commanding championship leader: 327 points, +62 ahead of Kyle Kirkwood (265, defending race winner), ahead of David Malukas (248).[9] Palou has won four of the first eight season races — on an oval where track position and fuel strategy are everything, a single mistake can shrink the championship gap faster than on a street circuit.
