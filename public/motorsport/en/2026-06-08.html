F1: Antonelli wins a chaotic Monaco — fifth straight win [1][2]

Monaco 2026 will be remembered as one of the wildest races of the season: seven retirements, two late safety cars, a red flag, and a standing restart. Through it all, Kimi Antonelli charged unbothered to his fifth win in six races — ahead of Lewis Hamilton (+6.271) and surprise third-placer Isack Hadjar (Red Bull, +23.394).[1]

The loser list reads like half the grid: Max Verstappen botched the start from P2, limped to the pits with a mechanical failure, and retired first. Charles Leclerc — the hometown driver starting from P4 — crashed into the wall at the Anthony Noghes corner on the restart, at the same time as Lance Stroll; that triggered the red flag. Lando Norris also failed to see the chequered flag. George Russell picked up two penalties (speeding in the pit lane, then a drive-through for not serving the first correctly) and dropped to P13, out of the points.[2]

Standing restart: after a red flag (race stoppage), the race doesn't resume behind the safety car with a rolling start—instead it restarts like the original start from a standstill at the starting line. Like a 100-meter sprint back into the blocks — old gaps disappear, reaction time wins.

For the championship: Antonelli runs away with it. With 156 points, he now leads by 66 over Hamilton (90), who thanks to his P2 and Russell's penalties climbs into second for the first time. Russell slips to P3 with 88 — a bitter day for Mercedes despite having the winner.

MotoGP: Márquez's 100th win — and an Aprilia disaster in Turn 1 [3][4]

Marc Márquez has claimed his 100th Grand Prix win in Hungary — in a bumper-to-bumper duel with Pedro Acosta, finishing 1.343 s clear; Francesco Bagnaia completed a Ducati day in third (+11.632).[3] It's only his second win after shoulder surgery and his first full race win of the season.

But the race was decided in Turn 1: Jorge Martín lost control under braking and wiped out his own Aprilia teammate Marco Bezzecchi — both out. In the same chaos, Fabio Di Giannantonio (fought back to P12), Raúl Fernández, and Fermín Aldeguer were caught up.[4] A self-destructive moment that erased half the championship top in one swoop.

Turn 1 braking zone: the first corner after the main straight, where drivers brake hard from top speed. Braking points bunch up tightest here — miss your marker or lock the front wheel, you run straight and often take neighbors down in the packed field.

Curiously for the championship: the lead stays unchanged — Bezzecchi (180) ahead of Martín (160), +20 — because both came away empty. But Márquez took 25 points from the leader with his win and climbs to P5 (108). Behind the top two it's tight: Di Giannantonio (138) sits just 6 points clear of Acosta (132).

Brazil watch: Moreira's breakthrough [3]

The day clearly belongs to MotoGP: Rookie Diogo Moreira (LCR Honda) finished race day P6 — his best result to date, ahead of experienced factory riders like Lecuona, Miller, and Bastianini. On the young Balaton circuit, a clear heads-up for the only Brazilian on the grid.

For Brazil's two F1 drivers, the day was quiet: Gabriel Bortoleto (Audi) and Franco Colapinto (Alpine, P12 in the championship with 15 pts) didn't score in Monaco's chaos. At Le Mans there's no Brazilian top favorite among the Hypercar frontrunners.

IndyCar: Newgarden wins Gateway — Palou runs out of fuel [5][6]

Josef Newgarden has won the Bommarito 500 at Gateway Oval — his sixth win at this track and his second of the season. At the finish he held off Marcus Ericsson by 0.7 s; Christian Rasmussen was a strong third (charged from P19 to the podium).[5]

The story of the evening, though, was Álex Palou: The championship leader started from pole, led 49 laps — then stalled in the pit box with no fuel. That dropped him to P17 (two laps down).[6] His cushion over Kyle Kirkwood (who scored valuable points in P6) shrunk from +62 to +49.

Pit stop fuel loss: IndyCar teams calculate fuel range down to the lap to minimize stops. Miss the math, and you run dry on the final meters — right as you're rolling toward the pit box, where you have to build pressure again. Seconds that can cost an entire race.

For the championship it's a shot across the bow: 49 points is still nearly a full race win's worth of lead, but the season's dominant force just made his first real mistake. Next race is Road America mid-June after a week off.

WEC: The Le Mans week begins — 94th edition Saturday [7][8]

Starting Wednesday the classic rolls: the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (Round 3 of the WEC season) on the 13.626 km Circuit de la Sarthe. At the start a full field: 18 Hypercars, 19 LMP2s, and 25 LMGT3s.[7] Race start falls Sat 13.06. at 16:00 CEST / 11:00 BRT, the finish exactly 24 hours later Sunday afternoon.[8]

New this year is the two-stage Hyperpole format during race week: Qualifying from Wed 10.06. (Hypercar 19:30 CEST / 14:30 BRT), Hyperpole Thu 11.06. evening (Hypercar 21:05 CEST / 16:05 BRT). In the top class a Ferrari 499P trio defends the Le Mans title (undefeated in Le Mans since 2023), against a strong BMW (WEC championship leader) and Toyota; Genesis (Hyundai's luxury brand) makes its Le Mans debut, Porsche absent after works withdrawal.

Hyperpole (new 2026): a sharpened qualifying shootout for the best starting slots. First a regular qualifying session per class — the fastest advance, then in two stages the top spots run solo for pole position. More drama, every lap counts.

It's Monday, June eighth — a day without racing, but we're still processing one of the wildest race Sundays of the season. Three series ran yesterday, and all three served up drama.

In Formula One, Kimi Antonelli won a chaotic Monaco — his fifth win in six races. Half the field tore itself apart around him: seven cars retired, Max Verstappen an early exit with a mechanical, hometown driver Charles Leclerc crashed into the wall on the restart. Lewis Hamilton claimed second and climbed to second in the championship, Isack Hadjar grabbed a surprise third. Antonelli now leads with one hundred fifty-six points, sixty-six clear — he's running away with it.

In MotoGP, Marc Márquez claimed his hundredth Grand Prix win, a fierce battle all the way to the checkered with Pedro Acosta. But the race was decided in Turn One: Jorge Martín lost the brakes and took out his own Aprilia teammate Marco Bezzecchi — plus three others. Oddly for the standings, the lead stays put because both top runners came away empty. Bezzecchi holds twenty points over Martín.

A Brazil note: rookie Diogo Moreira posted a strong sixth place, his career best.

In IndyCar, Josef Newgarden won at Gateway, his sixth victory there. The story was Álex Palou: championship leader started from pole, led nearly fifty laps, then ran dry in the pit box and dropped to seventeenth. His lead shrunk from sixty-two points down to forty-nine — the season's dominant force just made his first real mistake.

And now comes the highlight of the week: a double weekend. Saturday and Sunday bring the twenty-four hours of Le Mans and Formula One racing in Barcelona running side by side. Le Mans fires up Saturday at eleven in Brazilian time, with practice and qualifying sessions starting Wednesday. Ferrari's hunting a title they've held in Le Mans for three years straight.
