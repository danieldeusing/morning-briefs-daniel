WEC: Aston Martin shocks the test day—Ferrari slips [1][2]

The official Le Mans Test Day on Sunday (07.06.) is the first real pace indicator before the race week—and it flipped the hierarchy. Fastest was not three-time defending champion Ferrari, but the #007 Aston Martin Valkyrie: Tom Gamble set the best time with 3:26.293, well into the three-hour afternoon session.[1] Behind it, the #8 Toyota (Brendon Hartley) was only around 0.1 s back.

But the real headline is how tight it was: all eight manufacturers in the Hypercar class were within one second, with 19 drivers hitting Gamble's mark within a second.[2] The reigning Le Mans champion Ferrari (#51) showed up in the top 10 only at P9, and the second factory Toyota (#7) at P10—an unexpected slip into the middle of the field.

Test day at Le Mans: a single official practice day about a week before the race, where all teams drive together on the full 13.6 km track for the first time (half of which is closed public roads, sealed only for the race). Like a dress rehearsal: the times don't count toward the result, but they show who's quick.

Two incidents marked the day: in the #8 Toyota, Ryō Hirakawa collided with LMP2 driver Jake Hughes in the Goodyear chicane that morning—the car was rebuilt in a few hours and returned in the afternoon. The #51 Ferrari tapped the wall lightly at Indianapolis in the afternoon but kept going.[1] Watch out for test-day pace: nobody shows their hand before the race; fuel load and tire program distort the times. But the sheer closeness speaks to the tightest Hypercar field in years.

WEC: 24 Hours of Le Mans—94th edition, double weekend [3][4]

Starting Wednesday, the classic rolls: the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (round 3 of the WEC season) on the 13.626 km Circuit de la Sarthe. Record entry: 62 cars, 186 drivers—18 Hypercars from eight manufacturers (Aston Martin, Toyota, Cadillac, BMW, Genesis, Alpine, Ferrari, Peugeot), 19 LMP2, and 25 LMGT3.[3] Genesis (Hyundai's luxury brand) makes its Le Mans debut; Porsche absent after its factory withdrawal.

The race starts Sat 13.06. at 16:00 CEST / 11:00 BRT, the finish exactly 24 hours later on Sunday afternoon. Before that, the two-stage qualifying: regular qualifying Wed 10.06. (Hypercar 19:30 CEST / 14:30 BRT), then the Hyperpole Thu 11.06. evening (Hypercar stage 1 from 21:05 CEST / 16:05 BRT, stage 2 from 21:40).[4] WEC manufacturers' championship leader is BMW (59) ahead of Toyota (52) and Ferrari (42)—but Le Mans counts double and can turn the season around.

Hyperpole: a sharpened qualifying shoot-out for the best grid spots. First, a regular qualifying per class—the fastest advance, then the top spots fight it out in two stages for pole. More drama, every lap counts.

F1: Barcelona double strike—and the FIA hands out engine wildcards [6][7]

The next F1 round is the inaugural Barcelona-Catalunya GP—no longer "Spain," as that name moved to Madrid in 2026. The weekend runs parallel to Le Mans: FP1 Fri 12.06. 13:30 CEST / 08:30 BRT, quali Sat 13.06. 16:00 / 11:00 BRT (simultaneous with the Le Mans start!), race Sun 14.06. 15:00 / 10:00 BRT over 66 laps.[6] Barcelona is pure aero with a long straight and flowing corners: if you're quick here, you're quick almost everywhere—so teams traditionally bring their biggest upgrades here.

That's why it's spicy—the FIA just handed out its engine upgrade allocations for 2026. Under the ADUO system, manufacturers trailing the benchmark get development wildcards, scaled by deficit: Mercedes (2–4% back) gets one upgrade, Ferrari, Audi, and Honda (each >4% back) get two apiece.[7] The catch: Red Bull builds the benchmark power unit—so they get zero upgrades.

ADUO (Additional Development & Upgrade Opportunities): a catch-up mechanism in the new 2026 regulations. Power units freeze over the season—but if you measure far enough behind the class best, you get to catch up so the field doesn't blow apart. Like a handicap in golf: the weaker player gets strokes.

The kicker: Red Bull's motor is the best—yet Verstappen sits only at WM position 7 (43 points). So the problem isn't the PU; it's the whole package (chassis/aero integration in a reset year). Mercedes is "only" 2–4% off on the motor but clearly has the best car with Antonelli. Ferrari is adding a front wing package in Barcelona that theoretically brings up to two tenths.

It's Tuesday, June ninth—a day off from racing, but the calm before the storm. Starting Wednesday, the biggest weekend of the year unfolds: the twenty-four hours of Le Mans and the Formula One race in Barcelona run on the same Saturday and Sunday.

The Le Mans test day on Sunday already delivered the first shock. Fastest was not three-time defending champion Ferrari, but the Aston Martin Valkyrie: Tom Gamble set the best lap, three minutes twenty-six point two nine three. Behind it, a Toyota was only a tenth of a second back. And the most striking thing: all eight manufacturers were within a single second—the top-class field has never been this tight. Ferrari unexpectedly slipped to ninth place.

At the actual race starting Saturday, sixty-two cars and one hundred eighty-six drivers will be on the grid. Eighteen of them in the top class, with eight manufacturers—the luxury brand Genesis is even making its Le Mans debut. The start is Saturday at eleven o'clock Brazil time; before that, qualifying and the two-stage Hyperpole begin Wednesday.

Formula One runs in parallel in Barcelona—the first time the race is called Barcelona-Catalunya Grand Prix, because the Spain name moved to Madrid this year. Qualifying on Saturday happens right at the Le Mans start, and the race is Sunday at ten o'clock Brazil time.

Barcelona is also spicy because of engines. The FIA has distributed its upgrade allocations: Mercedes gets one upgrade, Ferrari, Audi, and Honda each get two, because they're more than four percent behind the best power unit. And the best power unit is built by Red Bull—which is why they don't get to upgrade at all. The odd thing: Verstappen is still only seventh in the championship. So the problem isn't the engine, but the whole package. Kimi Antonelli meanwhile leads with a sixty-six-point advantage and is chasing the record as the youngest world champion.

In the other series, it's a pause: no races since race Sunday, all tables unchanged. In MotoGP, the Aprilia duo holds the lead, and racing resumes in Brno. In IndyCar, Palou leads comfortably despite his fuel mistake, and the next race is at Road America.
