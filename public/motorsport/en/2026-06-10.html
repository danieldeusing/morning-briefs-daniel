WEC: Qualifying starts today — the tightest hypercar field ever [1][2]

Le Mans' intensive phase gets underway today (Wed 10.06.). After the first free practice starting at 14:00 CEST / 09:00 BRT, the first qualifying session follows tonight — Hypercar from 19:30 CEST / 14:30 BRT — from which the fastest 15 Hypercars advance to the Hyperpole on Thursday. Then a night FP2 from 22:00 / 17:00 BRT.[1]

But the real headline comes from test analysis: Motorsport.com calls the field "possibly the most competitive in the history" of the classic. On average, 11 cars from 7 different manufacturers were separated by just one second. The density of fast laps shows it even more clearly: laps under 3:30 minutes jumped from 178 (2025) to 362 (2026) — nearly doubled. Laps under 3:28 rose from 19 to 71, under 3:29 from 27 to 121.[2]

Why "laps under 3:30" matters: A race lap on the 13.6 km track takes just over three and a half minutes. How many cars beat this mark is a direct measure of the depth of the field — like a 100m race where suddenly not three but twelve runners dip under 10 seconds. The more cars running fast, the less predictable the race.

Fastest on Sunday was the #007 Aston Martin Valkyrie (Tom Gamble, 3:26.293) — just a hair over Toyota's 2025 best of 3:26.246. Caution remains warranted: test and qualifying times are skewed by fuel load and tire programs, and over 24 hours reliability trumps a quick lap. But the sheer tightness promises an open race like few others.

WEC: New 2026 qualifying rule — full driver lineup required [3]

Today's qualifying sees a revised format rule take effect for the first time, one that shifts strategy more than it sounds. The two-stage structure remains: from qualifying the top 15 (Hypercar) advance to Hyperpole 1 (20 min, top 10 go on) then Hyperpole 2 (15 min for pole).[3]

New is the driver assignment: the driver who qualifies the car cannot run Hyperpole 1 — and anyone in qualifying or H1 is barred from H2. In plain terms: a car that reaches the final pole stage must deploy all three nominated drivers across the three sessions. Previously, a team could field its fastest driver multiple times.

Why this matters tactically: Le Mans lineups are often unbalanced — a star driver plus two solid teammates. Before, the star could secure pole alone. Now the third driver must deliver in a qualifying session too. This levels the playing field slightly for teams with three equally strong drivers — and turns session driver selection into a poker game.

WEC: 94th Le Mans — Ferrari chasing a fourth straight win [5][6]

From Saturday the classic itself unfolds: the 94th edition of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (WEC round 3) on the 13.626 km Circuit de la Sarthe. Near-record grid: 62 cars, 186 drivers — 18 Hypercars from eight manufacturers (Aston Martin, Toyota, Cadillac, BMW, Genesis, Alpine, Ferrari, Peugeot), 19 LMP2, and 25 LMGT3.[5] The luxury brand Genesis (Hyundai) makes its Le Mans debut; Porsche is absent after the works withdrawal (hypercar field shrinking from 21 to 18).

Defending champion Ferrari won the last three editions (2023, 2024, 2025) with the 499P and brings three factory 499Ps again — though the #51 only managed P9 on test day, 24 hours tells a different story.[6] Toyota takes to the grid with the winter-revised, renamed TR001 Hybrid. The race start falls Sat 13.06. at 16:00 CEST / 11:00 BRT, the finish 24 hours later Sun at 16:00 / 11:00 BRT.

F1: Barcelona-Catalunya GP building up [7]

In parallel with Le Mans, the F1 weekend in Barcelona unfolds — now officially the Barcelona-Catalunya GP (the "Spain" name moved to Madrid in 2026). It's a standard weekend with no sprint, over 66 laps: FP1 Fri 12.06. 13:30 CEST / 08:30 BRT, FP2 17:00 / 12:00 BRT, FP3 Sat 13.06. 12:30 / 07:30 BRT, qualifying 16:00 / 11:00 BRT (right at the Le Mans start), race Sun 14.06. 15:00 / 10:00 BRT.[7]

Barcelona is pure aero track — fast here, fast everywhere — which is why teams traditionally bring big upgrade packages. Ferrari had announced a front wing package (~0.2 s) to close the gap on Mercedes and McLaren. Antonelli leads after five straight wins with 156 points (+66) chasing the record as the youngest champion. From 2026, the track alternates in even years with Belgium.

It's Wednesday, the tenth of June — and at Le Mans, the race operation kicks into gear today. Starting at two p.m. local time, nine a.m. Brazilian time, free practice gets underway, then tonight the first qualifying session for the top class, at seven thirty p.m. in France, two thirty p.m. in Brazil.

The big story of the day is the field's sheer tightness. Test analysis calls it possibly the most competitive in the history of the classic. Eleven cars from seven different manufacturers were separated by less than a single second on average. Even more striking is the density of fast laps: laps under three and a half minutes jumped from one hundred seventy-eight last year to three hundred sixty-two this year — nearly doubled. Fastest was an Aston Martin Valkyrie, just a hair over Toyota's benchmark from last year.

Today's qualifying also sees a new format rule take effect. Whoever wants their car in the final pole stage on Thursday must deploy all three nominated drivers across the three sessions — the qualifying driver can't run the first Hyperpole anymore. Previously a team could field its fastest driver multiple times. This slightly levels the playing field for teams with three equally strong drivers.

At the actual race starting Saturday, sixty-two cars line up, eighteen of them in the top class from eight manufacturers. Defending champion Ferrari has won the last three editions and is chasing a fourth straight victory. The luxury brand Genesis is even making its Le Mans debut. The start is Saturday at eleven a.m. Brazilian time.

In parallel, the Formula One weekend in Barcelona is building — a standard weekend over sixty-six laps. Qualifying on Saturday falls right at the Le Mans start, and the race is Sunday at ten a.m. Brazilian time. Kimi Antonelli leads the championship by sixty-six points after five straight wins.

In the other series there's a pause: no races since race Sunday, all standings unchanged. In the WEC this week will show whether BMW holds its slim lead over Toyota and Ferrari — Le Mans counts double. In MotoGP, the Aprilia duo holds the top, with the next race not until Brno.
