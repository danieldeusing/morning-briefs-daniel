WEC: Quali Shock — Alpine on Pole, Ferrari and Peugeot Out [1][2]

The first qualifying session Wednesday evening delivered the opening sensation of Le Mans week. Provisional pole went to Alpine: Ferdinand Habsburg posted a 3:23.135 in the #35 on his final lap with fresh soft tires — the fastest lap of the entire week — beating the #12 Cadillac of Louis Delétraz by just 0.013 seconds.[1]

But the real shocker is at the bottom of the top 15: The three-time defending champion Ferrari #83 (AF Corse) missed the Hyperpole as 17th — and for the second straight time. Phil Hanson put two wheels on the grass and picked up track-limit penalties. Even worse for Peugeot: Both 9X8s (P16 + P18) got eliminated — the brand has never made it into the Hyperpole at Le Mans. The other two works Ferraris (#50, #51) scraped through at P14/P15.[2]

Hyperpole, explained simply: The starting grid at Le Mans is awarded in two stages. First the regular quali — like a preliminary round that determines the fastest 15. Only those 15 get to compete in today's Hyperpole for the real pole shootout. Anyone like Ferrari #83 who fails in the preliminary is locked into their quali position and starts at the back — no matter how fast they might be over 24 hours.

Key context: A fast lap doesn't win a 24-hour race. Over the distance, tire management, fuel consumption, and reliability matter far more than a starting position. But the quali stumble costs the title defenders a good starting slot — and proves how tight and unpredictable this field is.

WEC: Tonight the Starting Grid is Decided [3]

Today (Thu 06/11) the Hyperpole determines the starting positions for Saturday. The format for the top class is two-stage: In Hyperpole 1 the 15 quali quickest fight for the top ten spots, then those ten battle in Hyperpole 2 for pole.[3]

Before that comes the third free practice (FP3) at 14:45 CEST / 09:45 BRT. The Hyperpole block opens with LMP2 and LMGT3 from 20:00 / 15:00 BRT, then the top class: Hypercar H1 from 21:05 CEST / 16:05 BRT, H2 from 21:40 / 16:40 BRT. The evening closes with night FP4 from 23:00 / 18:00 BRT — the only real darkness test before the race.

Times today (track = CEST) FP3 14:45 / 09:45 BRT. LMP2+GT3 Hyperpole from 20:00 / 15:00 BRT. Hypercar H1 21:05 / 16:05 BRT, H2 21:40 / 16:40 BRT. Night FP4 from 23:00 / 18:00 BRT.

Reminder of the new format rule for 2026: Anyone making it into H2 with their car must split all three nominated drivers across the sessions — the quali driver can't do H1, the H1 driver can't do H2. This levels the playing field slightly in favor of teams with three equally strong drivers.

WEC: 94th Le Mans — Ferrari Chases Fourth Straight Win [5]

Starting Saturday comes the classic itself: the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (WEC round 3) on the 13.626 km Circuit de la Sarthe. 62 cars, 186 drivers — 18 Hypercars from eight manufacturers (Aston Martin, Toyota, Cadillac, BMW, Genesis, Alpine, Ferrari, Peugeot), 19 LMP2 and 25 LMGT3.[5] Luxury brand Genesis (Hyundai) makes its Le Mans debut; Porsche is absent after its factory withdrawal.

Title defender Ferrari won the last three runnings (2023–2025) with the 499P and brings three works 499Ps again — but after the quali stumble of the #83, part of the fleet starts from the midfield. Over 24 hours, though, something else matters more than a starting spot, and the sheer tightness of the field (top 15 within 1.49s in quali) promises an open race. Start Sat 06/13 at 16:00 CEST / 11:00 BRT, finish 24 hours later Sun at 16:00 / 11:00 BRT.

F1: Barcelona-Catalunya GP Starts Tomorrow [6]

In parallel with Le Mans comes the F1 weekend in Barcelona — the Barcelona-Catalunya GP (the "Spain" name moved to Madrid in 2026). It's a standard weekend with no sprint, over 66 laps: FP1 Fri 06/12 13:30 CEST / 08:30 BRT, FP2 17:00 / 12:00 BRT, FP3 Sat 06/13 12:30 / 07:30 BRT, quali 16:00 / 11:00 BRT (same time as Le Mans start), race Sun 06/14 15:00 / 10:00 BRT.[6]

It is Thursday, June eleventh — and from Le Mans comes the first big surprise of race week. In the first qualifying session Wednesday evening, Alpine claimed the top spot. Ferdinand Habsburg posted a time of three minutes twenty-three point one three five in car number thirty-five — the fastest lap of the week — beating the quickest Cadillac by just thirteen thousandths of a second.

But the real shocker is at the bottom of the list. The three-time defending champion Ferrari, car number eighty-three, missed the Hyperpole as seventeenth — for the second time in a row. Even worse for Peugeot: both cars got knocked out, and the brand has never made the Hyperpole at Le Mans. Whoever fails in qualifying is locked into their position and starts at the back — no matter how fast the car might be over twenty-four hours.

Tonight it gets serious. The fifteen quickest cars battle in a two-stage Hyperpole for the starting grid. The top class runs the first stage at nine oh-five in the evening track time — that is four oh-five in the afternoon in Brazil; the final pole round starts at nine forty. Before that comes a third free practice session, and after that the night test session in the dark — the only real darkness test before the race.

At the actual race starting Saturday, sixty-two cars will line up, eighteen of them in the top class from eight manufacturers. Title defender Ferrari has won the last three runnings and is chasing a fourth straight win — but after the qualifying stumble, it starts part of its fleet from the midfield. Over twenty-four hours, though, something else matters more than a starting position, and the tightness of the field promises an open race. The start comes Saturday at eleven o'clock Brazil time.

In parallel, the Formula One weekend in Barcelona kicks off tomorrow — a standard weekend over sixty-six laps. Kimi Antonelli arrives with five wins from six races and a sixty-six point championship lead and is chasing his sixth straight victory. Barcelona is known as a pure aerodynamic circuit and delivers the clearest picture of the power balance since the season started — on a track where Mercedes has traditionally been strong.

The other racing series are on break: no championship races since race Sunday, all standings unchanged. In the WEC, this weekend decides whether BMW holds its narrow lead over Toyota and Ferrari — Le Mans counts double and can turn the season. In MotoGP the Aprilia pair holds the top, racing resumes in Brno.
