WEC: 94th Le Mans — the start falls today [1]

Today the classic itself unfolds: the 94th running of the 24 Hours of Le Mans (WEC round 3) on the 13.626 km Circuit de la Sarthe. 62 cars, 186 drivers — 18 Hypercars from eight manufacturers (Aston Martin, Toyota, Cadillac, BMW, Genesis, Alpine, Ferrari, Peugeot), 19 LMP2 and 25 LMGT3. On pole stands the BMW #15 (Vanthoor/Magnussen/Marciello) — BMW's first-ever Le Mans pole in factory history, inherited after Cadillac's deleted lap on Thursday (details below).[1]

Defending champion Ferrari won the last three runnings (2023–2025) with the 499P and brings three factory 499Ps again — but after the qualifying chaos, the armada starts scattered: the best Ferrari is the #51 in P8, the defending champion #83 starts from P17. Over 24 hours, though, different things matter than starting position: tire management, fuel consumption, reliability decide far more than a good grid slot. Luxury brand Genesis (Hyundai) makes its Le Mans debut from the top nine; Porsche is absent after its factory withdrawal.

Why pole matters less here: In a sprint race, the start decides almost everything. Over 24 hours, pole is just the first of ~340 laps. Like a marathon where the fastest sprinter at the start rarely wins — whoever manages their strength (saving tires, conserving fuel, avoiding breakdowns) takes victory in the end.

Recap: BMW's historic pole — Cadillac's record lap deleted [2]

A reminder of where the grid came from: In the Hyperpole on Thursday evening, Jack Aitken put the Cadillac #38 on top with a 3:22.559 — and beat Dries Vanthoor's BMW by just 0.005 seconds, the tightest pole margin in Le Mans history. But the stewards deleted Aitken's fastest lap for a pit-lane release infringement — the car was released into the fast lane too early. The #38 dropped to P10.[2]

BMW benefited: Vanthoor inherits in the #15 (3:22.564) BMW's very first Le Mans pole in factory history. In the Hypercar era (since 2023), a BMW factory car had never stood on pole at Le Mans before. The pair of #15 (P1) and #20 (P4) gives BMW a strong starting position — but as noted above: over 24 hours the grid position counts for little.

Brazil watch: Bortoleto & Colapinto in qualifying [3]

For the two Brazilians in the F1 grid, Barcelona qualifying today (11:00 BRT) is a benchmark: Gabriel Bortoleto (Audi) benefits from the double engine-upgrade allowance that the FIA grants Audi (like Ferrari and Honda) due to >4% deficit to Red Bull's power unit.[3] Franco Colapinto (Alpine, WM-P12 with 15 pts) hopes for aero updates to avoid falling further behind. At Le Mans no Brazilian top favorite stands in the Hypercar lead; in MotoGP, rookie Diogo Moreira (LCR-Honda) is off until the Brno opener (19–21/06).

F1: Barcelona — qualifying today, pace picture from Friday [4]

Parallel to Le Mans, today Barcelona's grid is decided. It's a standard weekend with no sprint, the race runs 66 laps. FP3 ran this morning at 07:30 BRT / 12:30 CEST, qualifying follows at 11:00 BRT / 16:00 CEST — exactly when Le Mans starts. Barcelona is a pure aero circuit (if you're quick here, you're quick almost everywhere), so teams bring big upgrade packages; the circuit delivers the sharpest picture of the power balance since the season opener.[4]

From Friday: In FP2, McLaren came back — Lando Norris (1:15.426) beat Russell by just 0.009 s, Piastri was third (+0.057), Leclerc fourth. Championship leader Antonelli complained of brake issues and stayed fifth (~0.6 s back); Hamilton was nearly a second behind Leclerc, only ninth. In FP1, Russell had been leading (1:16.363), but Norris, Antonelli, and Hamilton sat out the session for rookie runs — so FP1 times are barely useful as a pace gauge.

It's Saturday the thirteenth of June — and today is race day. At eleven o'clock in the morning Brazil time, that's four o'clock in the afternoon local time in France, the ninety-fourth running of the twenty-four hours of Le Mans gets under way. On pole is a BMW: car number fifteen driven by Dries Vanthoor, which inherited BMW's first-ever Le Mans pole in factory history after Cadillac took a penalty on Thursday.

Sixty-two cars are on the grid, eighteen of them in the top class from eight manufacturers. The weather is dry, around twenty-eight degrees in the afternoon. Important context: Over twenty-four hours, the grid position counts for very little. It's like a marathon — the fastest starter rarely wins. Tire management, fuel consumption, and above all reliability decide in the end. Defending champion Ferrari has won the last three runnings, but after the qualifying chaos starts scattered: the best Ferrari in eighth place, the defending champion even from seventeenth.

At exactly the same minute as the Le Mans start, qualifying for Formula One takes place in Barcelona — a timing clash for anyone wanting to watch both. From Friday's practice, we have the sharpest pace picture in weeks. McLaren has come back: Lando Norris set the fastest time in second practice and beat George Russell by just nine thousandths of a second. Mercedes is right there too.

Championship leader Kimi Antonelli, however, complained about brake problems and finished only fifth, nearly six tenths behind. Lewis Hamilton was almost a second back from his Ferrari teammate Leclerc and only ninth. Barcelona is a pure aero circuit — if you're quick here, you're quick almost everywhere — so qualifying today gives the first clear picture of the power balance since the season started.

In the championships, there's been no new race since race Sunday. But the Monaco result itself was revised retroactively: Alpine's appeal was successful and gave Pierre Gasly his third podium back, which moves him up in the standings, and Alpine climbs to fifth in the constructors' championship. At the top, Antonelli continues to lead Formula One by sixty-six points. In the WEC, this weekend will decide whether BMW holds its narrow lead against Toyota and Ferrari — Le Mans counts double and can turn the season on its head. In MotoGP, the Aprilia pair holds the lead, and the next race is at Brno from June nineteenth.

What comes next: tomorrow with the finish at Le Mans after twenty-four hours and the Formula One race from Barcelona in the morning Brazil time. Then we'll know whether BMW turns the drama into a real victory — and whether McLaren's Friday pace carries through to the race. Until then.
