Hamilton's first Ferrari victory

At Barcelona, in the seventh of 24 races in the 2026 season, Lewis Hamilton won for Ferrari — his first victory in the red car and at the same time the first non-Mercedes win of the entire 2026 season.[1] Winning time: 1:32:28.105. Behind him came George Russell (Mercedes, +19.561 s) in P2 and Lando Norris (McLaren, +23.719 s) in P3 — the first all-British podium since the US Grand Prix in 1968 (then Stewart/Hill/Surtees).[1] Max Verstappen (P4, +40.497 s) and Oscar Piastri (P5, +58.661 s) completed the top 5.

Strategically, Ferrari ran an aggressive three-stop for Hamilton. The decisive factor was a virtual safety car (VSC): it allowed Hamilton to make his final pit stop without losing the lead.[1]

Virtual Safety Car (VSC): a virtual safety car phase in which the entire field must slow down simultaneously without an actual safety car being deployed. If you pit during this phase, you lose significantly less time than normal — essentially a discount rate for a tire change. This is exactly what made Hamilton's three-stop strategy cheaper than it otherwise would have been.

Charles Leclerc (Ferrari) also suffered: he retired late with a power steering failure after running off track.[1] Franco Colapinto (Alpine) was penalized 10 seconds after the race (yellow flag violation), lost three championship points, and dropped from P8 to P10.[1][2]

formula1.com · racingnews365.com

Antonelli — retires while running second

The turning point for the championship: Kimi Antonelli was running in second place behind Hamilton and attempted to overtake George Russell five laps from the finish. He got past — but broke his front wing endplate in the process, lost pace, and eventually retired on lap 62 of 66.[1]

Endplate: the small vertical plate at the outer edge of the front wing. It directs air cleanly around the tire. If it breaks off, the aerodynamics at the front are disrupted — the car loses downforce and speed, rather like trying to steer with a bent rudder.

The championship impact is significant: Antonelli scores zero points from Barcelona but remains championship leader. His margin, however, shrinks to 41 points — Hamilton's win brought him 25 points closer, Russell's P2 another 18 closer. Antonelli's lead over Russell is now 50 points.

MotoGP heads to Brno — Czech Grand Prix

While F1 takes a break, MotoGP is in action: race 9 of the season takes place this weekend at Automotodrom Brno in the Czech Republic — in sprint format, meaning an additional short race on Saturday.[4]

MotoGP sprint format: since 2023, every MotoGP weekend has two races — a short sprint on Saturday covering roughly half distance (points only for the top 9), and the full main race on Sunday. Qualifying takes place Saturday morning. For the championship, both days count — the sprint can significantly shift the standings.

It's Tuesday, June sixteenth. The Barcelona weekend has been analyzed, and it has significantly scrambled the Formula One championship.

The big story: Lewis Hamilton has won his first race in a Ferrari. It's not just his first victory in the red car, but also the first victory of this entire season that doesn't go to Mercedes. Behind him came George Russell and Lando Norris on the podium — the first all-British podium since nineteen sixty-eight. In further positions, Max Verstappen and Oscar Piastri.

Strategically it was an aggressive three-stop for Hamilton. The decisive factor was a virtual safety car, which allowed him to make his final pit stop without losing the lead. His Ferrari teammate Charles Leclerc dropped out with a power steering failure.

The turning point for the championship: championship leader Kimi Antonelli was running second behind Hamilton. Five laps from the finish he attempted to overtake Russell — got through, but broke his front wing endplate in the process, slowed down, and retired four laps before the end. Zero points for him. He remains championship leader nonetheless, but his lead over Hamilton shrinks to forty-one points. Over Russell it's now fifty.

In the constructors' championship, Mercedes continues to lead clearly: two hundred sixty-two points ahead of Ferrari with one hundred ninety — a lead of seventy-two points. McLaren is third. Notably, Max Verstappen sits only in seventh place in the drivers' championship with fifty-five points — the two thousand twenty-six regulation reset has completely reshuffled the order.

While Formula One now takes a week off, MotoGP races. This weekend the Czech Grand Prix takes place in Brno, in sprint format with a short race on Saturday and the main race on Sunday. The main race starts Sunday, June twenty-first at two p.m. Central European Summer Time, that is nine a.m. Brazilian time.

In the MotoGP championship, Marco Bezzecchi leads with one hundred eighty points ahead of his Aprilia teammate Jorge Martin with one hundred sixty. That's the standing after the last race in Hungary on June seventh. There, Martin and Bezzecchi crashed into each other on the first lap and both retired — and Marc Marquez took advantage, won sprint and race, and celebrated his hundredth Grand Prix victory.

After Brno, Formula One returns: the following weekend, June twenty-sixth to twenty-eighth, in Austria at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg, classic format without sprint. The race starts Sunday at ten a.m. Brazilian time.

The question we'll be watching next week: was Hamilton's win a genuine turning point for Ferrari, or a lucky break thanks to the virtual safety car? And does Antonelli bounce back in Spielberg? Until then.
