Toyota wins Le Mans — first victory since 2022

In the endurance classic 24 Hours of Le Mans (June 13–14), Toyota has won again after four years. The #7 GR010 Hybrid with Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi and Nyck de Vries completed 381 laps and crossed the finish line after 24 hours 10.913 seconds ahead of second-place BMW #20 (Frijns/Rast/van der Linde).[1] P3 went to sister car Toyota #8 (Buemi/Hartley/Hirakawa) — a spectacular 1-2 for Toyota.[2]

Hypercar / WEC: WEC is the World Endurance Championship, its top class is called Hypercar. Unlike F1, each car has three drivers who rotate and share the wheel over the entire distance — at Le Mans that's 24 hours non-stop, day and night. The winner is whoever completes the most laps in that time. Le Mans is the season's flagship event and counts double toward the championship compared to a regular race.

For Toyota, this is the first Le Mans victory since 2022 — in the years in between, Ferrari (2023, 2024, 2025) and the growing competition from BMW, Cadillac, Porsche and Alpine dominated the class.[1] This very depth is what makes the current Hypercar era so compelling: seven manufacturers battling at the top.

F1: Hamilton's first Ferrari victory upends the championship

At the seventh of 22 races in 2026, Lewis Hamilton claimed his first victory with Ferrari in Barcelona — and with it, the first non-Mercedes win of the season.[5] Behind him: George Russell (Mercedes, +19.5 s) and Lando Norris (McLaren, +23.7 s) — the first all-British podium since 1968. For Hamilton, it was also his 106th F1 victory and his seventh in Barcelona.[6]

The deciding factor was a Virtual Safety Car late in the race, which gave Hamilton a favorable final pit stop.[5]

Virtual Safety Car (VSC): a virtual safety car phase — the entire field must slow simultaneously without an actual safety car on track. Anyone who pits during this phase loses far less time than usual — essentially a discount pit stop. That's exactly what made Hamilton's stop cheap.

The championship-deciding moment, however, was Kimi Antonelli's retirement: The championship leader was running second when a power unit failure stopped him in the closing laps. Zero points — his lead shrinks from previously comfortable to just 41 points.[5]

MotoGP heads to Brno — Czech GP

Round 9 takes place this weekend at Automotodrom Brno — in sprint format (short race Saturday, main race Sunday).[9] The main race starts Sun June 21 at 14:00 CEST / 09:00 BRT.

MotoGP sprint: since 2023, every MotoGP weekend has two races — on Saturday a short sprint over roughly half distance (points only for top 9), on Sunday the full main race. Both days count toward the championship, and the sprint can significantly shift the standings.

The last race was the Hungarian GP (Balaton Park, Sun June 7): There, Jorge Martin crashed into his Aprilia teammate Marco Bezzecchi in lap 1 — both out. Marc Marquez (Ducati) capitalized, won and celebrated his 100th GP victory.[10] Despite the zero, Bezzecchi remains championship leader.

It's Wednesday, the seventeenth of June. Behind us lies a packed motorsport weekend with two major stories — one from Le Mans, one from Formula One.

First, Le Mans: Toyota won the famous twenty-four-hour race, for the first time since twenty twenty-two. The car with starting number seven, driven by Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi, and Nyck de Vries, crossed the finish line after twenty-four hours just over eleven seconds ahead of a BMW. Toyota's sister car finished second — a one-two.

In the manufacturers' world championship of endurance racing, Toyota now leads clearly with one hundred thirty-two points, ahead of BMW with ninety-six and Ferrari with sixty-two. And for Daniel especially noteworthy: the next round is the six-hour race of São Paulo on July tenth, at Interlagos — practically on his doorstep.

The second major story comes from Formula One, from Barcelona. Lewis Hamilton won his first race in a Ferrari — and it was also the first victory of the season that didn't go to Mercedes. Behind him, George Russell and Lando Norris took the podium — the first all-British podium since nineteen sixty-eight.

Critical for the world championship was Kimi Antonelli's retirement. The championship leader was running second when an engine failure stopped him in the closing laps. Zero points. He remains championship leader, but his lead shrinks to just forty-one points. In the constructors' standings, Mercedes continues to lead with two hundred sixty-two points.

The next seven days belong to other series, because Formula One is on break. MotoGP races this weekend in Brno, Czech Republic, in sprint format. The main race starts Sunday at two p.m. Central European Summer Time, which is nine a.m. in Brazil. There, Marco Bezzecchi leads the world championship after a dramatic race in Hungary, which Marc Marquez won with his hundredth Grand Prix victory.

Also on Sunday, IndyCar races in Road America in the U.S. — there, Alex Palou dominates with a forty-nine point lead. And electric Formula E returns Saturday for the first time since twenty nineteen to Sanya, China, but at a deeply inconvenient time for Daniel, in the middle of Saturday night.

Then Formula One is back: the weekend of June twenty-sixth through twenty-eighth in Austria, at the Red Bull Ring. The race starts Sunday at ten a.m. Brazilian time. The question until then: Was Hamilton's victory a true turning point for Ferrari — and will Antonelli strike back in Spielberg? Until tomorrow.
