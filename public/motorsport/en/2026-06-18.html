F1: Hamilton's Ferrari Win Breathes Life into Championship

At the seventh of 22 races in 2026, Lewis Hamilton achieved his first win in a Ferrari in Barcelona — and with it, the first non-Mercedes victory of the season.[1] Behind him: George Russell (Mercedes, +19.6 s) and Lando Norris (McLaren, +23.7 s) — the first all-British podium since 1968.[1] With this win, Hamilton also becomes the oldest F1 winner since Jack Brabham in 1970.[2]

The decisive moment for the championship was Kimi Antonelli's retirement: The championship leader was running P2 when a technical fault stopped him in the closing stages — zero points. His lead shrinks to just 41 points (156 to 115).[1]

DNF (Did Not Finish): a retirement — the car doesn't reach the finish, usually due to a technical fault or accident. In F1, points are awarded only to the top ten finishers; a DNF means zero points, no matter how far ahead you were running. That's exactly what happened to Antonelli from P2 — the most costly zero of the season.

Three drivers retired in Barcelona: besides Antonelli, the second Ferrari of Charles Leclerc and Oliver Bearman.[3] Mixed feelings for Ferrari then — a double highlight (Hamilton's win) and double trouble (Leclerc's DNF) on the same day.

What Spielberg Decides

For the championship the question is straightforward: Does Antonelli strike back, or does Hamilton carry his Barcelona momentum forward? 41 points is no cushion with 15 races remaining, not when you can afford retirements. Mercedes has plenty of room in the constructors' championship (72 points lead) — there'd have to be a lot of things go wrong there.

The Red Bull Ring rewards engine power and hard braking zones; in the 2026 regulation-reset year with new power units, that's a real test of who has their power plant in check. Whoever runs up front here sends a signal for the second half of the season.

Le Mans: Toyota Reclaims the Classic

At the endurance classic 24 Hours of Le Mans (Sat 13–Sun 14 June), Toyota won for the first time in four years. The #7 with Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi, and Nyck de Vries crossed the line after 24 hours 11 seconds ahead of second-place BMW #20 (Frijns/Rast/van der Linde).[6] Third place went to sister car Toyota #8 (Buemi/Hartley/Hirakawa).[7]

Hypercar / WEC: The WEC is the world endurance championship, and its top class is called Hypercar. Unlike F1, three drivers per car share the wheel over the entire distance — at Le Mans that's 24 hours straight, day and night. Le Mans is the season's flagship event and counts double in the championship.

11 seconds ahead after a full day and night of racing — that's razor-thin and shows how tight the current Hypercar era is, with multiple manufacturers battling for victory.[6]

It's Thursday, the eighteenth of June. Formula One is on break, but the world championship is as wide open as it's been in a long time after the race in Barcelona — and the endurance series has just finished its biggest classic.

In Formula One, Lewis Hamilton won his first race in a Ferrari in Barcelona. Behind him were George Russell and Lando Norris on the podium — the first all-British podium since nineteen sixty-eight. For Hamilton it was also the oldest Grand Prix win by a driver since Jack Brabham in nineteen seventy.

But the decisive moment for the world championship was Kimi Antonelli's retirement. The championship leader was running in second place when a technical fault stopped him. Zero points. He remains in first place in the standings, but his lead has shrunk to just forty-one points, one hundred fifty-six to one hundred fifteen. In the constructors' championship, Mercedes continues to lead clearly with two hundred sixty-two points ahead of Ferrari and McLaren.

Racing resumes the weekend after next in Austria, at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg, from the twenty-sixth to the twenty-eighth of June. The race starts on Sunday at three o'clock Central European Summer Time, that's ten o'clock in the morning Brazilian time. A pleasant morning slot for Daniel, no midnight stream. The question until then: does Antonelli strike back, or does Hamilton carry the momentum from Barcelona forward?

The second big story comes from endurance racing. At Le Mans, Toyota won the famous Twenty-Four-Hour race for the first time since twenty twenty-two. The car with number seven, driven by Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi, and Nyck de Vries, crossed the finish line just eleven seconds ahead of a BMW after a full day and a night of racing. The sister car from Toyota finished third.

In the manufacturers' world championship of endurance racing, Toyota now leads clearly with one hundred thirty-two points ahead of BMW with ninety-six and Ferrari with sixty-two. And particularly interesting for Daniel: the next race is on July twelfth, the six-hour race at Interlagos in São Paulo — practically on his doorstep. See you tomorrow.
