F1: Hamilton's Ferrari Victory Gives the Championship Breathing Room

In race seven of 22 in 2026, Lewis Hamilton claimed his first victory with Ferrari in Barcelona — the first Ferrari win of this season at all.[1] Behind him: George Russell (Mercedes, +19.6 s) and Lando Norris (McLaren, +23.7 s) — the first all-British podium since 1968 (Stewart/Hill/Surtees then).[2] At 41 years old, Hamilton is also the oldest F1 winner since Jack Brabham in 1970; it was his 106th career victory and his seventh in Barcelona — no driver has won there more often.[2]

The decisive moment for the championship, however, was Kimi Antonelli's retirement: The leader was running P2 when a mechanical failure stopped him three laps from the end — zero points. His lead shrinks from 66 to just 41 points (156 to 115).[3]

DNF (Did Not Finish): a retirement — the car fails to reach the finish line, usually due to a mechanical failure or crash. In F1, only the top ten finishers score points; a DNF means zero points, no matter how far ahead you were. That's exactly what happened to Antonelli from P2 — the costliest zero of the season.

Charles Leclerc's second Ferrari also failed late in the race — a bittersweet day for the Scuderia: a highlight (Hamilton's win) and a disappointment (Leclerc's DNF) at once.[3] After the race, Franco Colapinto (Alpine) was penalized ten seconds for not slowing under yellow and dropped from P8 to P10.[3]

What Spielberg Decides

For the championship the question is simple: Does Antonelli bounce back, or does Hamilton carry his Barcelona momentum forward? 41 points is no cushion with 15 races left — if the leader has even one more retirement, the championship flips. Mercedes' constructor lead is another story at 72 points ahead of Ferrari; there's room for error there.

The Red Bull Ring rewards engine power and heavy braking zones. In the 2026 regulation reset year with new power units — Red Bull is running its own Red Bull Ford power unit for the first time — this is a real test of who has their engine sorted. The team sits fourth with 89 points; a strong home weekend would send a message for the second half of the season.[6]

Le Mans: Toyota Ends Ferrari's Streak

At the endurance classic 24 Hours of Le Mans (Sat 13–Sun 14.06., Race 3/8), Toyota claimed the win back. The #7 with Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi and Nyck de Vries took the checkered flag after 24 hours just 10.9 seconds ahead of BMW #20 (Frijns/Rast/van der Linde).[7] Third place went to the sister car Toyota #8 (Buemi/Hartley/Hirakawa, +20.4 s).[7] Ferrari's five-year hypercar winning streak at Le Mans comes to an end.[8]

Hypercar / WEC: the WEC is the world endurance championship, and its top class is called Hypercar. Unlike F1, three drivers share each car for the entire distance — at Le Mans 24 hours straight, day and night. Le Mans is the most prestigious single race of the season and the ultimate endurance trophy.

10.9 seconds ahead after a full day and night of racing — that's razor-thin and shows how competitive the current hypercar era is, with multiple manufacturers (Toyota, BMW, Ferrari, Cadillac) fighting for overall victory.[7]

It's Friday, June nineteenth. Formula 1 is still on break, but after the race in Barcelona the championship is as wide open as it's been all season — and the endurance racing world has already seen its biggest classic of the year.

In Formula 1, Lewis Hamilton won his first race for Ferrari in Barcelona. George Russell and Lando Norris rounded out the podium — the first all-British podium since nineteen sixty-eight. For Hamilton, at forty-one years old, it was also the oldest grand prix victory since Jack Brabham in nineteen seventy.

But the decisive moment for the championship was Kimi Antonelli's retirement. The leader was running in second place when a mechanical failure stopped him three laps from the end. Zero points. He stays in first, but his lead has shrunk from sixty-six to just forty-one points, one hundred fifty-six to one hundred fifteen. In the constructors' championship, Mercedes continues to lead comfortably with two hundred sixty-two points ahead of Ferrari and McLaren.

Racing resumes in exactly one week in Austria, at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg, from June twenty-sixth to twenty-eighth. The race starts Sunday at three PM track time, which is ten AM Brazilian time. A pleasant morning broadcast for Daniel, no night stream. The question between now and then: does Antonelli bounce back, or does Hamilton carry his Barcelona momentum forward? Forty-one points is no safe cushion with fifteen races still to come.

The other big story comes from endurance racing. At Le Mans, Toyota won the famous twenty-four-hour race and ended Ferrari's five-year winning streak. The car with the number seven, driven by Mike Conway, Kamui Kobayashi and Nyck de Vries, crossed the finish line after a full day and night just eleven seconds ahead of a BMW. Toyota's sister car came third.

In the manufacturers' championship for endurance racing, Toyota now leads decisively with one hundred thirty-two points ahead of BMW with ninety-six and Ferrari with sixty-two. And especially interesting for Daniel: the next race is the six-hour event in São Paulo at Interlagos on July twelfth — almost on his doorstep, with the race starting at half past ten Brazilian time.
