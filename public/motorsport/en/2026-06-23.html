Mercedes battery crisis: the mortgage on the title [3][4]

The two Mercedes lead the championship — but their biggest problem sits not in the competition, but in their own car. Antonelli and Russell are both already on their second power-unit battery (energy storage), and the regulations allow only three per season. The fourth incurs an automatic ten-place grid penalty.[3] With 15 races remaining, it's a ticking clock: if even one battery fails early, the championship leader drives half a season under the sword of Damocles.

Parts allowance & energy storage: In Formula 1, each car may use only a fixed number of certain powertrain components per season to cap costs. For the battery (officially "energy store", ES), that number is three. 2026 brings more electrical power than ever — the battery is heavily stressed, and that's exactly where Mercedes is struggling. Use a fourth and you're hit with a ten-place grid penalty.

Both recent Mercedes retirements trace back to this: Russell failed while leading in Canada, Antonelli in Barcelona four laps from the end while P2.[5] Technical chief James Allison says the source is found — an overheating battery module — and "most risk areas" understood; but the permanent fix is not yet on the car.[4] For Spielberg: quick enough to win, but with a reliability question mark that makes every DNF cost double.

Why this could cost the title Antonelli's cushion (41 pts) equals roughly one race win plus small change. Another battery DNF — like Barcelona — would nearly halve it; a grid penalty on top could turn a sure win into a midfield points struggle. The championship hinges on the durability of this one component.

Austrian GP, Spielberg · 26–28.06. [1]

Race 8 of 22 takes place at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg — Red Bull's home race, in the classic format with no sprint (three free practices, qualifying, race). The short, high-altitude circuit (4,318 km, ~700 m above sea level, three DRS zones) rewards engine power and hard braking zones; the race runs for 71 laps. The weather is forecast warm and dry all weekend, reaching 31.5 °C on Sunday — no rain lottery to even the odds.[1]

Barcelona wrap-up: Hamilton's breakthrough

The last race (Race 7, 14.06.) became a storybook moment: Lewis Hamilton claimed his first win for Ferrari — the 106th of his career, the first after 686 days without a victory.[6] With Russell P2 and Norris P3, it was the first all-British podium since 1968.[7] Hamilton (41) is also the oldest GP winner since Jack Brabham in 1970.[6]

Mercedes paid the price: Antonelli retired four laps from home while running P2 (technical failure) — exactly the DNF that halved his cushion and made the battery story (above) so critical.[6] Hamilton's margin at the finish: 19.5 s over Russell.[8]

MotoGP Brno: Márquez doubles up, Bezzecchi banned [9][10]

The Czech GP in Brno (Race 9, 21.06.) went to Marc Márquez (Ducati) — his second win in a row, finishing 0.4 s ahead of Ai Ogura.[9] On the podium came Ai Ogura (Trackhouse Aprilia) in second, ahead of Francesco Bagnaia (Ducati, +2.2 s) in third.[10]

But the headline was written by his absence: championship leader Marco Bezzecchi was banned from the race. In the sprint he hit a marshal after crashing; Aprilia's appeal against the ban was rejected.[11] With no points on Sunday, his championship cushion collapsed dramatically.

Why a race ban is so costly: In MotoGP there's a sprint on Saturday (half points) and the main race on Sunday (full 25 points for the win). A race ban means zero from the main event — while the chasing pack scores in full. Bezzecchi watched his lead evaporate: from a previous 65-point cushion over Márquez to just 40, and over second-place Martin to barely 8.

It's Tuesday, the twenty-third of June. Midweek is race-free, but on Sunday it gets busy: Formula One and MotoGP both run on the same day, almost at the same time.

In Formula One, Kimi Antonelli in the Mercedes still leads with one hundred fifty-six points. But the real flashpoint isn't the competition — it's Mercedes itself. Antonelli and his teammate George Russell are both already on their second of only three allowed batteries for the whole season. Whoever needs a fourth incurs a ten-place grid penalty. Technical chief James Allison says the cause is found — an overheating battery module — but the real fix isn't on the car yet. Both recent Mercedes retirements stem from this: Russell failed while leading in Canada, Antonelli in Barcelona four laps from the end while running second. Exactly that retirement halved Antonelli's lead, to just forty-one points ahead of Lewis Hamilton.

That was Hamilton's big moment. In Barcelona he took his first win for Ferrari, his one hundred sixth career victory, after six hundred eighty-six days without a win. With Russell second and Norris third, it was the first all-British podium since nineteen sixty-eight.

This weekend Formula One races the Austrian Grand Prix at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg, classically without a sprint, in warm, dry weather above thirty-one degrees. The race starts Sunday at three p.m. track time — that's ten in the morning for Daniel in Brazil.

In MotoGP there were two stories from Brno. Marc Márquez won for the second time running, narrowly ahead of Ogura, with Bagnaia third. And championship leader Marco Bezzecchi was absent: he was banned because he hit a marshal in the sprint after crashing, and Aprilia's appeal was rejected. Without Sunday points his championship cushion shrank to just eight points over Jorge Martin.

The next MotoGP race, the Dutch TT in Assen, falls on the exact same weekend. The race starts Sunday at two p.m. track time, one hour before Formula One, so nine in the morning for Daniel.
