Barcelona: Hamilton's first Ferrari win [1][2]

Round 7 of 22 (6/14, Barcelona) became a story for the ages: Lewis Hamilton claimed his first Grand Prix win for Ferrari — his first win ever since joining the Scuderia.[1] Behind him, George Russell (P2, +19.5 s) and Lando Norris (P3, +23.7 s) completed the podium — making it the first all-British F1 podium since 1968, when Stewart, Hill, and Surtees stood together at the USA GP.[2]

Behind them, Verstappen took fourth (+40.4 s), ahead of Piastri (P5). A 10-second penalty for Franco Colapinto (yellow flag violation) reshuffled the midfield after the race.[3] The downside fell to Mercedes: Antonelli retired from second place four laps before the end (technical failure), and Ferrari's Leclerc also retired three laps before the end.[1]

First non-Mercedes win of 2026 Until Barcelona, only one manufacturer had won in 2026: Russell opened with a win in Australia, then Antonelli took five straight wins (China, Japan, Miami, Canada, Monaco). Hamilton's success marks the first crack in Mercedes' dominance — and came right as the championship leader retired.

Antonelli's penalties: what counts — and what's open [4]

Antonelli's Barcelona weekend didn't end just with the retirement: he received a 5-second penalty after the race for four track limit violations.[4] Practically consequence-free — the penalty is added to a race time that had no points anyway due to the retirement. More important: it will not be converted to a grid penalty for Austria, because he had already completed >90 % of race distance before retiring.[4]

Track limits & conversion to a grid penalty: "Track limits" are the white track boundary lines — if a car crosses them with all four wheels, it's a violation. If a driver accumulates too many, he gets a time penalty. But if he retires before the penalty can take effect, the rulebook normally converts it to a grid penalty for the next race — but only if he completed less than 90 % of the distance. Antonelli was just over that threshold, so it lapses.
Still open One source mentioned an additional separate stewards review for "erratic driving" (unpredictable driving behavior). Whether it resulted in a separate penalty or was consolidated with the track limit case is not yet confirmed — we're carrying it forward as an open point rather than inventing a result.

WEC: Toyota leads after Le Mans dominance [7][8]

Alongside Formula 1, the endurance championship (WEC) runs in parallel, and its pinnacle just passed: At the 24 Hours of Le Mans (6/13–6/14, round 3), Toyota with car #7 (Kobayashi/Conway/de Vries) took victory and also secured third place — a 1-3 result that put the manufacturer at the top of the standings (132 pts, 36 clear of BMW).[7]

Hypercar & manufacturers' standings: "Hypercar" is the WEC's top class — Le Mans belongs to it. In the manufacturers' standings, it's not individual drivers who score, but the brands (Toyota, BMW, Ferrari…); Le Mans counts double because it's motorsport's most prestigious race. That's why one strong weekend there can flip the entire season standings.

It's Wednesday, June twenty-fourth. Midweek is race-free, but in two days the Austrian Grand Prix begins in Spielberg.

The headline comes from Barcelona. Lewis Hamilton claimed his first win in a Ferrari, his first ever since joining the Scuderia. Behind him, George Russell finished second and Lando Norris third, the first all-British podium in Formula One since nineteen sixty-eight.

The day was rough for the championship leader. Kimi Antonelli retired from second place in his Mercedes four laps before the end—a technical failure. This exact retirement nearly halved his points lead, dropping it from sixty-six to just forty-one points ahead of Hamilton. Antonelli also received a five-second penalty for track limit violations, but it has no consequences for Austria because he retired after more than ninety percent of the race distance. A separate review for erratic driving hasn't yet been confirmed.

This weekend, Formula One races at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg, in the classic no-sprint format. The race starts Sunday at three PM track time, that's ten in the morning for Daniel in Brazil. For Max Verstappen it's his home track, and he's still chasing his first win of this season.

The endurance championship continues in parallel. At the twenty-four hours of Le Mans, Toyota won with car number seven and also took third place. That puts Toyota at the top of the manufacturers' standings with one hundred thirty-two points, thirty-six ahead of BMW.

For Daniel, the next endurance race is the season's most exciting: the six hours of São Paulo at Interlagos on July twelfth. A home race right on his doorstep.
