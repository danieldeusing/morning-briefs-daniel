Assen: Bezzecchi's return after the Brno ban [1][2]

The hottest story of the weekend comes from MotoGP. Championship leader Marco Bezzecchi (Aprilia) competes in the Dutch TT at Assen (race 10 of 22) — right after being banned from the previous race at Brno: After a crash in the sprint at turn 3, he struck a marshal while retrieving his bike, prompting the FIM stewards to exclude him from the Grand Prix.[1] Aprilia's appeal was rejected. Bezzecchi himself said: "I lost control, I apologize. Now Assen."[2]

Track marshal: These are the orange-clad officials at trackside who retrieve crashed riders and bikes, wave flags, and secure the track — volunteer marshals, not paid race staff. Hitting one is considered a serious infraction in motorsport; that's why the ban was so harsh.
What's at stake Bezzecchi lost valuable points through the ban and leads by just 8 points over Martín. A weak Assen weekend — or another error — could cost him the lead, while Márquez presses hard from behind.

Brno: Márquez's comeback [3]

While Bezzecchi had to watch from the sidelines, Marc Márquez (Ducati) struck: He won the Brno main race (race 9, 21.06.) by 0.421 s over Ai Ogura, with Bagnaia taking third.[3] It was Márquez's third win in four races — and brought him within 40 points of Bezzecchi in just two weekends.

Assen question That's exactly the streak Bezzecchi wants to stop in Assen — his stated goal is to "end Márquez's winning run." If Márquez scores again while both Aprilias stumble, the championship picture could flip entirely before the summer break.

Austrian GP, Spielberg · 26–28.06. [6]

In parallel, race 8 of 22 in Formula 1 takes place at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg — Red Bull's home race, in the classic format with no sprint (two free practices on Friday, FP3 + qualifying on Saturday, race on Sunday).[6] The short mountain roller coaster (4.326 km, only 10 corners) sits in a natural valley in the Styrian foothills. For Max Verstappen, it's home turf — as championship seventh (55 pts), he's still waiting for his first win of 2026.

The double Sunday in focus

Rarely do both top classes overlap so closely. To catch both, Sunday 28.06. is best planned like this: first the MotoGP at 09:00 BRT (26 laps in Assen), seamlessly followed by Formula 1 at 10:00 BRT (71 laps in Spielberg). Saturday already offers the MotoGP sprint at 10:00 BRT right after F1 qualifying prep.

What to watch In MotoGP, the question is whether Bezzecchi shrugs off his misstep or whether Márquez finally challenges for the lead seriously. In Formula 1, it's about whether Hamilton's Barcelona win was an outlier or the start of a Ferrari surge — and whether Verstappen breaks through in front of his home crowd. After that, F1 heads straight to Silverstone (race 9, 03–05.07.).

It's Thursday, June twenty-fifth. This weekend, two championships run at the same time: Formula One races in Spielberg, MotoGP races in Assen — and both main races are on Sunday one right after the other.

The hotter story comes from MotoGP. Championship leader Marco Bezzecchi is back after being banned at the last race in Brno. Following a crash during the sprint, he struck a marshal while retrieving his bike, and the stewards excluded him from the Grand Prix. Aprilia's appeal was rejected. All Bezzecchi said was: I lost control, I apologize, now Assen.

While he had to watch, Marc Marquez won the Brno race by just over four-tenths of a second. It was his third win in four races, and in two weekends he's made up sixty-two points. Bezzecchi now leads the championship by only eight points over Jorge Martin, and Marquez as fourth is forty points back but in strong form.

In Formula One, the Austrian Grand Prix is happening at the same time on the Red Bull Ring, traditionally without a sprint. Kimi Antonelli leads with one hundred fifty-six points, but after his retirement in Barcelona, by only forty-one over Lewis Hamilton, who scored his first Ferrari win there. For Max Verstappen, Spielberg is home, and he's still waiting for his first win of the season.

For Daniel in Brazil, the double Sunday is well-timed: MotoGP at nine in the morning, then Formula One at ten, both in Brazilian time. The MotoGP sprint happens Saturday at ten in the morning.
