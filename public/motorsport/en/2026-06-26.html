Austrian GP starts: thin air tests the new engines [1][2]

Race 8 of 22 in Formula 1 begins today at the Red Bull Ring in Spielberg — Red Bull's home race, typically without a sprint (two Friday practice sessions, FP3 + qualifying on Saturday, race on Sunday). The short Alpine rollercoaster (4,326 km, 71 laps) has a genuine 2026 mystery: it sits at 678 meters altitude — the air is roughly 8 % thinner than at sea level, and this is the first time the new 2026 power units will race at this elevation.[1]

MGU-H & thin air: A turbocharger forces extra air into the engine — the thinner the surrounding air, the harder it has to work and the more severe the "turbo lag" (that split-second delay while pressure builds). Until 2025, a small electric motor called the MGU-H supported the turbo and kept it at high speed — like a running start before you pedal. In the 2026 regulations, this component is eliminated. At altitude, everyone loses this support — but not equally.

The effect hits manufacturers unevenly: whoever built a smaller, quick-spooling turbo (according to The Race, e.g. Ferrari) will handle thin air better; a larger turbo with higher peak performance (e.g. Audi) will struggle more at altitude.[1] That's exactly why today's practice times matter more than usual.

No lap times yet FP1 (13:30 MESZ / 08:30 BRT) and FP2 (17:00 MESZ / 12:00 BRT) start shortly — no practice times were available at deadline. We're deliberately not guessing; the first real best times will appear in tomorrow's brief.
Team news Charles Leclerc sits out FP1 — Ferrari gives its car to rookie Dino Beganovic for the first session.[2] Mercedes arrives with reported power-unit reliability concerns, and Red Bull brings a bigger upgrade for their home race. Norris won here in 2025 — McLaren has historically been strong at the Red Bull Ring.

Hamilton's Barcelona win: the point that changes everything [3]

The latest race explains the entire title picture in Spielberg. At the Barcelona GP (race 7, 14.06.), Lewis Hamilton claimed his first win for Ferrari — his 106th career victory. At 41, he is now the oldest F1 winner since Jack Brabham in 1970.[3] Behind him it got very British: Russell P2, Norris P3 — the first all-British podium since the 1968 U.S. Grand Prix.

The turning point Meanwhile, Antonelli crashed four laps from the end — a shock retirement that shrank his title lead from over 50 to just 41 points.[3] That's why Hamilton heads to Austria on a high: the big question for this weekend is whether Barcelona was an outlier or the start of a real Ferrari resurgence.

Assen: Bezzecchi's return — and Márquez crashes in FP1 [4][5]

Simultaneously, the season's most gripping MotoGP story unfolds. Title leader Marco Bezzecchi (Aprilia) is back at the Dutch TT in Assen (race 10/22) — right after his Brno suspension: following a crash in the sprint, he struck a track marshal while retrieving his bike, the FIM stewards ejected him from the Grand Prix, and Aprilia's appeal failed.[4]

Track marshal: These orange-clad helpers at trackside retrieve crashed riders and machines, wave flags, and keep the track safe — mostly volunteer work. Hitting one is considered a serious offense in motorsport, which is why the ban was so severe.

First sign this morning: Bezzecchi led FP1 at Assen, while his closest challenger Marc Márquez crashed.[5] Session times aren't yet dated — the order of the first real best times will appear in tomorrow's brief.

What's at stake Bezzecchi leads even after the ban by just 8 points over Martin. A weak Assen weekend could cost him the lead — and Márquez has won three of the last four races, closing from 102 down to 40 points in two weekends.

It is Friday, June twenty-sixth. Today, both premier classes take to the track: Formula One begins in Spielberg with two Friday practice sessions, and at the same time, MotoGP starts the weekend in Assen. No lap times yet — the sessions are just getting underway.

In Formula One, the Red Bull Ring is a genuine wild card. The track sits at nearly seven hundred meters, the air there is roughly eight percent thinner, and the new twenty twenty-six engines no longer have an MGU-H to support the turbo at altitude. This affects manufacturers unevenly: whoever built a smaller, quick-spooling turbo will manage better. That's why today's practice times are more revealing than usual. On top of that, there's a heat alert from the FIA with asphalt temperatures around fifty-one degrees.

The championship picture comes down to the last race. In Barcelona, Lewis Hamilton claimed his first Ferrari win at age forty-one, his one-hundred-sixth career victory, making him the oldest Formula One winner since Jack Brabham in nineteen seventy. At the same time, Kimi Antonelli crashed four laps from the end. Antonelli still leads the championship with one hundred fifty-six points, but his margin over Hamilton is now just forty-one.

In MotoGP, it's even tighter. Championship leader Marco Bezzecchi is back in Assen after being banned from the previous race in Brno for hitting a track marshal while retrieving his bike. This morning he led the first practice session, while his rival Marc Márquez crashed. Bezzecchi leads by just eight points over Jorge Martin, and Márquez in fourth has been closing hard recently.

For Daniel in Brazil, the double Sunday is perfect timing: MotoGP at nine in the morning, then Formula One at ten, both Brazilian time. The MotoGP Sprint happens on Saturday at ten in the morning.
