F1: Russell wins in Spielberg

Mercedes takes the double podium

George Russell won the Austrian Grand Prix (Round 8, Red Bull Ring, 28.06.) from pole position. Max Verstappen (Red Bull) crossed the line behind him with +1.611 s, third went Russell's Mercedes teammate Andrea Kimi Antonelli with +1.986 s.[1] That gives Mercedes a double podium — and Antonelli extends his championship lead despite finishing third, because none of his direct rivals got closer.

Behind them the field lined up as follows: Oscar Piastri (McLaren, +21.8 s) in 4th, Lewis Hamilton (Ferrari, +26.4 s) in 5th, Isack Hadjar (Red Bull) in 6th, Lando Norris in 7th, Charles Leclerc (Ferrari, +45.7 s) in 8th.[1] Ferrari had a dreadful day: Hamilton dropped from 3rd to 5th, Leclerc from 2nd to 8th.[2]

motorsport.com · gpfans.com

The controversial pole — and Mercedes' flawless streak

Saturday's qualifying already had plenty of drama. Russell claimed pole with 1:06.113 ahead of Leclerc (+0.236 s) and Hamilton.[3] Verstappen was eliminated in Q3 after hitting Turn 9 — and that very crash made the pole position contentious: Russell's decisive lap came under a single yellow flag, while Antonelli aborted his run. Race control later confirmed that Russell's time stands.[4]

Noteworthy Mercedes took pole in all eight races so far in 2026 — a flawless qualifying streak across the entire season.[2] In a year with a completely new ruleset (new chassis and power units), that's the clearest statement of dominance in the field.

MotoGP: Ogura's historic win in Assen

Japan's first MotoGP winner in 22 years

Ai Ogura (Trackhouse Aprilia) won the dramatic Dutch TT at Assen on Sunday (28.06.) — his first MotoGP victory ever. At the same time, it's the first win by a Japanese rider in the premier class since Makoto Tamada in 2004, 22 years ago.[6] Behind him, teammate Raúl Fernández (Trackhouse Aprilia) completed the double for the customer team in second; third went the new championship leader Jorge Martin (Aprilia Racing) with +3.512 s.[7] Francesco Bagnaia (Ducati Lenovo), meanwhile, endured a dreadful day: a brake problem forced him to retire around lap 15 — DNF, zero points.[7]

gpblog.com · crash.net

Bezzecchi throws away the championship lead in the gravel

The previous championship leader Marco Bezzecchi (Aprilia) crashed from fourth place early in the race and was out.[6] That cost him the lead: his brand colleague Jorge Martin took the top spot through the DNF — for the first time since the Austin race.[8]

ELI5 · What does "DNF" mean? DNF stands for "Did Not Finish" — the rider didn't cross the line, usually after a crash or mechanical failure. Like a board game where you crash one turn before the finish: all points for that race are gone, no matter how well you were doing before. That's exactly what happened to Bezzecchi — zero points, and the rival moves up.

MotoGP WDC after the Dutch TT [8]

Four riders now sit within 25 points — the title fight has never been tighter in 2026. Martin leads with 193 points, just 7 ahead of Bezzecchi (186). Lurking behind are Fabio Di Giannantonio (177) and the fresh winner Ogura (168).[8] Three different brands in the top four — Aprilia, Pertamina-VR46-Ducati, and Trackhouse — make the second half of the season wide open.

It's Tuesday, the thirtieth of June. Behind us is a busy double Sunday in motorsport — and ahead of us, two weeks when world championship racing even comes to Brazil.

In Formula One, George Russell won the Austrian Grand Prix in Spielberg, and he did it from pole position. Max Verstappen crossed the line behind him, barely a second and a half back, third was Russell's teammate Andrea Kimi Antonelli. Mercedes got a double podium — and something bigger with it: the Silver Arrows took pole in all eight races so far this year. A flawless streak that in a regulation year with completely new cars is a real statement of dominance.

Saturday's qualifying was controversial, though. Verstappen crashed in Q three, and Russell's decisive lap came under a yellow flag. Race control left the time standing in the end. In the championship, Antonelli still leads, with one hundred seventy-one points ahead of Russell with one hundred thirty-one — the gap has shrunk to forty points. Ferrari had a rough day and fell back.

Even more dramatic was MotoGP in Assen. Ai Ogura got his first win in the premier class ever — and it's the first Japanese victory since twenty oh four, since Makoto Tamada. Behind him his teammate Raúl Fernández completed the Aprilia double, third went Jorge Martin, and with that he took the championship lead. Francesco Bagnaia had to retire with a brake issue.

But the previous championship leader Marco Bezzecchi crashed early and was out. That cost him the lead: Jorge Martin took over, and now he's on top with one hundred ninety-three points, just seven ahead of Bezzecchi. Di Giannantonio and the fresh winner Ogura are lurking behind — four riders within twenty-five points. It's been tight like that for a while.

Next week Formula One goes to Silverstone, and as a sprint weekend — the first there since two thousand twenty-one. The main race starts Sunday at noon Brazil time.

And the weekend after, something special happens: the endurance championship runs six hours at Interlagos, in São Paulo. Toyota leads the manufacturers with one hundred thirty-two points. Race start is early Sunday morning, half past six local time. Watch out with the standings though: the WEC tables are still from Le Mans. MotoGP races at the Sachsenring in parallel, the last race before the summer break.
