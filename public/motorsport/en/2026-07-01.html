Silverstone: the sprint returns [1][2]

Round 9 — and the first sprint weekend since 2021

The British Grand Prix is the ninth round of the 2026 season — and the fourth sprint weekend of the year.[1] It will be held at the 5.891 km Silverstone Circuit.[1] The twist: for the first time since 2021, Silverstone will run the sprint format again.[2] For Daniel in Brazil, this works out well — the main race on Sunday starts just before eleven in the morning BRT.

Heads up · Sprint weekend At a normal Grand Prix weekend, only the long Sunday race counts. With the sprint format, there's an extra short race on Saturday (~100 km, separate points for the top 8) — like a dress rehearsal where real points are up for grabs. This means free practice gets cut down to just one session on Friday, and there's a separate "sprint qualifying" just for Saturday's sprint. But regular Sunday qualifying still happens.

Recap: Russell wins in Austria [3][4]

Mercedes double podium, Ferrari botches the strategy

At the last race before Silverstone — the Austrian Grand Prix (Round 8, Red Bull Ring, 6/28) — George Russell won from pole. Second was Max Verstappen (Red Bull) with +1.6 s, third Russell's teammate Kimi Antonelli (+1.9 s).[3] Once again, Mercedes had both drivers on the podium.

Behind them came Oscar Piastri (McLaren, +21.8 s), Lewis Hamilton (Ferrari, +26.3 s), Isack Hadjar (Red Bull), Lando Norris, and Charles Leclerc (Ferrari, +45.6 s).[4] It was a lost day for Ferrari: A mistake in pit-stop timing under the safety car cost the team, according to Motorsport Week, any shot at a top-3 finish and pushed Hamilton back in the order.[4]

Heads up · Safety-car pit stop When the safety car (a slow lead car after a crash) is out in front, everyone slows down and bunches up tight. Stopping for tires at exactly that moment is a trick: because everyone else is braking, you lose much less time on the tire change than normal. Miss the window — too early or too late — and you drop several places. Ferrari hit that wrong moment in Austria.

The picture heading into Silverstone: Antonelli leads the drivers' standings with 171 points — his lead over Mercedes teammate Russell (131) is 40 points.[5] Behind him, Lewis Hamilton holds down third in the championship (125) and keeps the pressure from afar. In the constructors' standings, the gap is even wider: Mercedes is pulling away from Ferrari with a 98-point lead (302 to 204). Red Bull, despite Verstappen's P2 in Austria, sits fourth — the 2026 title reset with completely new cars has shifted the balance noticeably in favor of the Silver Arrows.

WEC: 6 Hours of São Paulo [6][7]

Home-race feeling at Interlagos, July 10–12

For Daniel, this is the highlight of the next two weeks: The FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC) is coming to São Paulo — the 6 Hours of São Paulo at Autódromo José Carlos Pace (Interlagos), Round 4 of the season.[6] In the manufacturers' standings, Toyota leads with 132 points, 36 ahead of BMW (96) and 70 ahead of Ferrari (62).[7] Toyota also won the 24 Hours of Le Mans (Round 3) in mid-June with the #7 car (Kobayashi/Conway/de Vries) — the marque's first Le Mans win in four years.[7]

Data note · Start time The exact race start time has been confirmed by fiawec.com: Sun July 12, 06:30 BRT / 11:30 CEST (06:30 local time Interlagos). The standings above are also from after Le Mans (Round 3); São Paulo is the immediately following Round 4.
Heads up · WEC / Manufacturers The WEC is the World Endurance Championship: races lasting 6 or 24 hours, where multiple drivers per car take turns. Unlike F1, the emphasis here is on the manufacturer standings — which marque (Toyota, BMW, Ferrari, Porsche …) racks up the most points over the year. Toyota is in front, but has to defend that lead against a strong BMW factory team in Interlagos.

It's Wednesday, the first of July. A race-free day in the middle of the week — but this weekend, Formula One heads to Great Britain, and Silverstone is making its race something special.

For the first time since twenty twenty-one, a sprint weekend is back there. That means: not just one race on Sunday, but an extra short race on Saturday with its own points. To make room, there's only one free practice session and a separate qualifying session on Friday. For Daniel in Brazil, the timing works out — the main race starts on Sunday at eleven in the morning Brazil time, which is three p.m. at Silverstone and four p.m. in Central Europe.

On the sporting side, the picture heading into Silverstone is clearer than ever. Mercedes is running one-two in both championships. In the drivers' championship, rookie Kimi Antonelli is in the lead — with one hundred seventy-one points. His lead over teammate George Russell is forty points, and over Lewis Hamilton it's forty-six. In the constructors' standings, Mercedes is ahead of Ferrari by ninety-eight points — Red Bull, despite having Verstappen, is only fourth.

That was partly down to the last race: In Austria last Sunday, Russell won from pole, ahead of Verstappen and Antonelli. Ferrari, though, botched the pit stop under the safety car and threw away any shot at the podium — Hamilton fell back.

And for Daniel, the week that follows becomes a home race: The World Endurance Championship is bringing the six Hours of São Paulo to Interlagos on July twelve. Toyota leads the manufacturers with one hundred thirty-two points, and won Le Mans in mid-June too. The start time is confirmed: Sunday morning, half past six Brazil time, which is half past eleven Central European time.
