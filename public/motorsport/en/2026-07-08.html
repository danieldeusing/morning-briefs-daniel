6 Hours of São Paulo — not yet run, countdown ticking

Daniel's home race: the FIA World Endurance Championship runs the Rolex 6 Hours of São Paulo at the Autódromo José Carlos Pace — Interlagos, 4.309 km, 15 corners — as round 4 of the season this coming weekend.[1] The race starts Sunday, 07/12, at 11:30 BRT / 16:30 CEST and runs six hours, finishing around 17:30 BRT.

Heads up · no result yet The round has not been run yet. A direct pull of fiawec.com on 07/08 shows a live countdown to FP1 and no results section at all.[1] The "Cadillac wins São Paulo" snippet circulating online — already flagged as a false alarm yesterday — is still wrong. Who wins won't be known before Sunday evening at the earliest.
ELI5 · Hyperpole A two-stage qualifying format. In stage one, every car in a class runs; only the fastest advance. Stage two — the Hyperpole — is a separate shootout among just those best cars, and only there is pole position awarded. Like a semifinal and a final instead of one single race against the clock. That's why Saturday's schedule has four qualifying slots: a heat and a Hyperpole each for the Hypercar and LMGT3 classes.

What's at stake

In the manufacturers' standings, Toyota leads with 132 points ahead of BMW (96) and Ferrari (62).[2] The table is unchanged from after round 3 — the 24 Hours of Le Mans in June — because no WEC race has run since. Interlagos will be the first to move it. What's interesting is the crowd behind them: just 12 points separate Alpine (38), Cadillac (32), and Aston Martin (26), and an independent source confirms exactly that gap.[2] A good result at Interlagos could completely reshuffle that order.

ELI5 · WEC / manufacturers' championship The WEC is the World Endurance Championship: races lasting 6 or 24 hours, with multiple drivers per car taking turns — a relay race, just with cars. Unlike Formula 1, what matters most here is the manufacturers' championship: which brand racks up the most points over the year, not which individual driver.

Jorge Martin (Aprilia) leads the championship with 193 points — but only 7 ahead of fellow Aprilia rider Marco Bezzecchi (186), who crashed out at the last race in Assen and scored nothing.[4] Behind them, Fabio di Giannantonio (177) and Ai Ogura (168): four riders within 25 points, less than a single race weekend's haul. Defending champion Marc Márquez (Ducati) is fifth, 40 points back — there's no talk of Márquez dominance this year. The brand picture stands out too: four of the top six ride Aprilia.

Context · Assen (06/28) Ai Ogura (Trackhouse Aprilia) won his first premier-class race, ahead of teammate Raúl Fernández — a Trackhouse one-two. Bezzecchi, the championship leader up to that point, crashing out early handed the points lead to Martin.[4]

F1: Mercedes pulls away, the drivers' championship tightens up [5][6][7]

After Silverstone (round 9, Sun 07/05)

Charles Leclerc won at Silverstone ahead of George Russell and Lewis Hamilton; championship leader Kimi Antonelli started from pole, lost everything to a mechanical failure, and finished only P16.[5] Antonelli still leads with 179 points, but his margin over Russell (154) is down to just 25 points — teammate against teammate.[6]

The constructors' standings tell the other half of the story: Mercedes 333, Ferrari 255, McLaren 179 — a 78-point lead at the top.[7] Both Mercedes drivers score consistently; Ferrari spreads its points more broadly (Hamilton 147, Leclerc 108) but scores less overall. Red Bull has slipped to 128, with Verstappen seventh in the drivers' standings (76), far from title contention.

Context · regulation reset 2026 is F1's big regulation-overhaul year: new chassis, new power units, with Audi and Cadillac two new manufacturers on the grid. Whoever was ahead in 2025 says little about 2026 — the table below is exactly the argument for that. A single mechanical failure cost Antonelli nearly half his championship margin; with the number of races still to come, the drivers' title remains wide open, but the constructors' championship is already a Mercedes affair.

The World Endurance Championship stops in Interlagos this weekend. The six hours of São Paulo are round four of the season and start Sunday at half past eleven local time, or half past four in the afternoon Central European time. The full session schedule is out now: first free practice runs as early as Friday at eleven a.m., with the decisive Hypercar Hyperpole on Saturday at a quarter to four. One thing to keep in mind: this race hasn't run yet. A look at the official site shows a live countdown and no results.

What's on the line is mainly the manufacturers' championship. Toyota leads with one hundred thirty-two points, ahead of BMW with ninety-six and Ferrari with sixty-two. More interesting is the crowd behind them: just twelve points separate Alpine, Cadillac, and Aston Martin. A good result at Interlagos could completely reshuffle that order.

On a different clock, MotoGP runs its last round before the summer break at the Sachsenring. And that's the practical news of the day: the two races don't collide. The Sachsenring starts at nine in the morning Brazilian time and wraps up after about forty-five minutes. Interlagos doesn't get going until half past eleven. The only real overlap comes on Saturday, when the MotoGP sprint at ten o'clock coincides with the third free practice session in Interlagos. When in doubt, the sprint wins out, since that's the one handing out championship points.

The motorcycle world championship is as tight as it's been in years. Jorge Martin leads with one hundred ninety-three points, just seven ahead of Marco Bezzecchi, who crashed out in Assen. Behind them come Fabio di Giannantonio with one hundred seventy-seven and Ai Ogura with one hundred sixty-eight. Four riders within twenty-five points — that's less than a single weekend's haul. Defending champion Marc Márquez sits only fifth, forty points back. A reliable constructors' standings for MotoGP still couldn't be pinned down today, so we're deliberately not citing a number there as a confirmed table.

In Formula 1, the two championships are heading in opposite directions. The drivers' title race is tightening: Kimi Antonelli leads with one hundred seventy-nine points, but after his mechanical failure at Silverstone, his cushion over teammate George Russell is down to just twenty-five. The constructors' championship, on the other hand, is pulling away. Mercedes sits at three hundred thirty-three points, Ferrari at two hundred fifty-five — a seventy-eight-point lead. These numbers come today, for the first time, from a direct pull of the official table, and they match a second source exactly.
