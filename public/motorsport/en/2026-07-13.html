WEC: BMW wins in Interlagos — title fight back open [1][2]

6 Hours of São Paulo, round 4 — victory for BMW #15

Daniel's home race ended with a statement: Kevin Magnussen, Raffaele Marciello and Dries Vanthoor took the #15 BMW M Hybrid V8 (Team WRT) to victory in the six-hour endurance world championship race at the Autódromo José Carlos Pace — 242 laps in 6:00:26.[1] It's BMW's second win of the season. Behind them, the Ferrari #51 (Pier Guidi/Calado/Giovinazzi) crossed the line just 2.254 s back, followed by the two Cadillacs, #12 and #38. In the GT3 class (LMGT3), Racing Team Turkey by TF #34 (Yoluç/Dempsey/Eastwood) won — its second straight class win after Le Mans.

ELI5 · Why BMW gains 31 points at once In the manufacturers' standings, only each brand's best-placed car counts — not the sum of all its cars. So a win brings a large single chunk of points. BMW jumped from 96 to 127 points that way, while table leader Toyota came away empty-handed in São Paulo (no car finished high enough) and stayed on 132. Result: a 36-point deficit shrank to just five overnight.

What it means for the championship

Interlagos was round 4 of 8 — the season's midpoint. Before the race, Toyota led by 36 points; now only five points separate Toyota (132) and BMW (127), with Ferrari trailing on 88.[2] A solo run has turned into a duel, and BMW has the momentum. It's tight further back too: Cadillac (60), Alpine (41) and Aston Martin (40) are bunched close together. The next round isn't until September 6 in Austin (Lone Star Le Mans) — plenty of time for the five-point gap to sink in.

MotoGP: Márquez matches the Agostini record at the Sachsenring [3][4]

German GP — a lights-to-flag win

Marc Márquez (Ducati Lenovo) led the 30-lap Grand Prix from start to finish and won by 1.996 s over Ai Ogura (Trackhouse Aprilia); Raúl Fernández was third (+5.104 s), ahead of Pedro Acosta (4th) and championship leader Jorge Martín (5th).[3] It was Márquez's tenth premier-class win at the Sachsenring — matching Giacomo Agostini's record for most premier-class wins at a single circuit. Combined with pole and Saturday's sprint win, it was a perfect weekend. His closest sprint rivals didn't make it to the flag: Álex Márquez and Fabio Di Giannantonio both crashed out of the race, and Marco Bezzecchi also went scoreless.

The title fight tightens — Márquez from fifth to third

The win moves the championship noticeably: Márquez climbs from fifth to third with 190 points, now just 18 behind Martín (208).[4] The new runner-up is Ai Ogura (194, −14), who passes the now-scoreless Bezzecchi and Di Giannantonio. Notably, just 24 points separate the top five. At the season's halfway point (round 11 of 22), a clear Martín lead has turned into a tight race.

ELI5 · How 102 points became 18 After the Italian GP at Mugello, Márquez was still 102 points behind — all but written off. Two things fueled the comeback: his own wins (Hungary, Czechia, now Germany) and his rivals stumbling. At the Sachsenring he didn't just win — his opponents also gave away points by crashing. That's how a deficit shrinks twice as fast — momentum, not just math.

It's Monday, July thirteenth. The results from race Sunday are in, and both were big stories — one on Daniel's home track in Interlagos, the other at the Sachsenring.

In the endurance world championship, the BMW carrying car number fifteen won the six hours of São Paulo. Kevin Magnussen, Raffaele Marciello, and Dries Vanthoor ran two hundred and forty-two laps to claim BMW's second win of the season, just over two seconds ahead of the Ferrari of Pier Guidi and Calado. The two Cadillacs followed behind.

More important than the win is what it does to the standings. In the manufacturers' championship, only each brand's best-placed car counts, so a win delivers a big chunk of points all at once. BMW jumped from ninety-six to one hundred and twenty-seven points, while points leader Toyota came away empty-handed in São Paulo and stayed at one hundred and thirty-two. A thirty-six-point deficit shrank to just five overnight. The title fight that looked all but settled is wide open again.

At the Sachsenring, Marc Márquez was in a league of his own. He led the thirty-lap Grand Prix from pole to the checkered flag and won by just under two seconds over Ai Ogura. It was his tenth premier-class win at that track — matching Giacomo Agostini's record for the most wins at a single circuit. Combined with pole position and Saturday's sprint win, it was a perfect weekend.

And this time it also pays off in the championship. Márquez climbs from fifth to third, now just eighteen points behind leader Jorge Martín. The new runner-up is Ai Ogura. Márquez was helped not only by his own win but also by his rivals stumbling: Bezzecchi went scoreless, and his rival Álex Márquez and Di Giannantonio both crashed out. After the Italian race he was still one hundred and two points back — at the season's halfway point, it's down to just eighteen.

Formula One remains on its break. The standings are unchanged since Silverstone: Kimi Antonelli leads the drivers, Mercedes the constructors. The next stop is the Belgian Grand Prix in Spa, this coming Sunday, the nineteenth of July. Race start is three in the afternoon Central European time, ten in the morning for Daniel, a weekend without a sprint. The summer break, by the way, doesn't start until after that — not until after the race in Budapest a week later.
