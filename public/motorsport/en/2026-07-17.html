The wing that nearly caught a world champion twice [1][1b]

Red Bull pulls the plug on the experiment — ahead of the fastest weekend of the year

Today's freshest and, at the same time, most important story is the kind that rarely makes headlines, because nothing happened: Red Bull is going back to the conventional rear wing for the Belgian GP and setting aside the experimental "Macarena" wing — after Verstappen went off at high speed twice in a row with the part, in Austria and at Silverstone.[1] According to the report, the problem was identified during testing after Silverstone.

ELI5 · The "Macarena" wing & straight-line mode The rear wing on a 2026-spec car is adjustable: in a corner it stands up steep and presses the car onto the track; on a straight it folds flat so air flows past more easily and the car goes faster. The "Macarena" wing — first shown by Ferrari, then copied by Red Bull — takes that to the extreme: its upper flap rotates a full 180 degrees for straight-line mode. Picture your hand out of a car window — flat, it slices through the wind; tilted, the wind pushes it away. So the wing has to flip from "flat" back to "pressing down" the moment the driver turns in. That's exactly what it wasn't doing reliably.

Verstappen's own description is technically precise and, for that reason, unsettling: "while turning into the corner, the rear wing is not fully attaching, and you lose a lot of downforce for that. You just spin off the track."[1] In plain terms: the airflow doesn't "grab" the wing again, rear downforce is missing for a moment — and a moment is enough.

Flap rotates 180° (straight-line mode) → Turn-in → Airflow doesn't reattach → Rear downforce missing → Spin

His bottom line, verbatim: "At this point, it's super dangerous because I could have really hurt myself two times."[1] For a driver whose standard answer to just about any question right now is "Nothing" (see next door), that sentence is the clearest statement he's made all week.

The cost of the reversal — and why Spa of all places

And Spa of all places tightens the exact same motion that tripped up the wing: for the Belgian GP, the FIA has approved five straight-line-mode zones — a season high, tied with Australia (start/finish, the descent from La Source toward Eau Rouge, the Kemmel straight, the straight after Stavelot, Blanchimont through to the Bus Stop chicane).[1b] The second zone is the critical one — and above all, where it ends: it sits on the approach to Eau Rouge and does not run through the corner. Drivers have to close the wing again before Raidillon, so that full downforce is in place through the fastest kink on the track.[1b] That exact fold-back under load — flat, back to "pressing down," right before a flat-out corner — is the motion Verstappen's car twice failed to execute cleanly. Five zones mean five such transitions per lap, one of them at the exact point where a missing moment of downforce is least forgiving. The link to the wing is our own read — the FIA didn't place the zones because of Red Bull, and Fernando Alonso's warning about the same zones targets a different problem, energy deployment: "If you deploy at Spa from Turn 1 to 5, finito for the rest of the lap."[1b]

What Red Bull is doing here is a deliberate trade-off, and it isn't free: the whole point of the wing was straight-line speed — the 180-degree flap is a drag trick. Ditch it, and you give up top speed in exchange for a rear end the driver can trust on turn-in. That Red Bull makes this trade ahead of Spa of all places — a track where straight-line speed traditionally counts for a lot — says something about the urgency: the team is accepting a presumed time loss rather than risking a third crash. That's our read, not a team statement — Red Bull hasn't put a number on the expected time loss, and how much it really costs on Sunday will show up for the first time today, in FP1 and FP2.

What to watch today FP1 (08:30 BRT) and FP2 (12:00 BRT) are the first real data: does Red Bull, on the old wing, lag on the straights but run clean through the fast corners — or is it missing both? That will decide more about Verstappen's weekend than any question about his future.

Future: two signals from the same Thursday, neither official [2][3]

The manager talks — the driver doesn't

On Thursday, 07/16, two statements about Verstappen's future ran side by side, and they point in different directions. Signal one: his manager Raymond Vermeulen told the Austrian outlet oe24 that a lot is being written, "but the truth is, Max wants to finish his time with Red Bull. He has a contract until 2028 and would like to fulfil it." On the exit clause, he added: "The fact that this clause exists doesn't mean we'll activate it. We could have activated it in recent years as well, and we haven't."[3]

Signal two: Verstappen himself sat at the official FIA press conference in Spa that same Thursday — and didn't back up his manager with a single word. His script is a wall: "No." "Nothing." "Nope. There's nothing to say from my side." And at greater length, but just as empty: "Okay, I don't want to go here and say yes and no, and this and that, about my future. I've said already many times that if there was something new, I would say it myself."[2]

That's the whole story, and it deliberately has no ending. A manager who publicly pledges loyalty, and a driver who won't repeat that same pledge hours later — that can be read either way: as a driver who simply has no appetite for the topic (he says so himself), or as one who doesn't want to commit. Both readings are equally well supported today, so we're not picking one. What matters: there is nothing official from Red Bull, from Verstappen, or from any other team.

The clause math is now final [5]

The door is open — that says nothing about whether he walks through it

ELI5 · Exit clause An exit clause is an emergency hatch in a contract that only opens if an agreed condition is met. Like a lease with the addendum: "If the heating fails in winter, you can terminate immediately." Opening the hatch and walking through it are two different things. Verstappen's condition: if he isn't among the top two in the drivers' standings by the summer break, he can exit for 2027 (triggerable, per the report, in October).[5]

That condition can no longer be avoided as of now. Verstappen sits on 76 points in seventh place.[6] Exactly two races remain before the break: Spa on Sunday and Hungary on 07/26. Spa is confirmed without a sprint[4] — so two wins would be worth 50 points at most, a cap of 126. Above him, though, sit three drivers who wouldn't need a single further point: Antonelli 179, Russell 154, Hamilton 147.[6] Even generously assuming extra sprint points in Hungary, he'd still be short of Hamilton's 147. The top two are arithmetically out of reach — whatever happens on Sunday.

This is exactly where the math meets the driver-market story next door: it only decides that the hatch opens. Vermeulen's line — that they've had the clause in previous years too and never pulled it — is a reminder that this isn't a forecast. What's new today is only the finality: there's no longer any sporting result left that can settle the question on its own.
