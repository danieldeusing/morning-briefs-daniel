Today in Spa: qualifying decides more than it should [2]

A classic weekend — and a grid that will be hard to fix tomorrow

Formula 1 is running a conventional, sprint-free weekend at Spa-Francorchamps: FP3 this morning, qualifying this afternoon, and tomorrow's race over 44 laps.[2] Both fall at convenient times for Daniel: qualifying at 11:00 BRT, tomorrow's race at 10:00 BRT.

ELI5 · How an F1 qualifying session works Qualifying is a knockout race against the clock, run in three rounds — like a tournament where the slowest are eliminated after each round. In Q1, everyone runs, and the five slowest are out and start from the back. Q2 repeats the same process. In Q3, the remaining ten fight for pole position, meaning the first starting spot. Only each driver's single fastest lap counts — one mistake in four minutes can cost the whole Saturday.

Why that matters especially in Spa: the track is long and offers a classic overtaking spot on the Kemmel Straight — but it's also a track where a car with good straight-line speed can tow an entire train of cars behind it without anyone getting past. Whoever misses the front row today will have to hope tomorrow that strategy or the weather works in their favor. This is context, not a forecast — we don't have verified lap times from yesterday's FP1 and FP2 today, and we'd rather write nothing than a number we haven't checked.

The clause is live — and the clock is running until October [1]

From "mathematically almost unavoidable" to "confirmed triggerable"

ELI5 · Exit clause An exit clause is an emergency hatch built into a contract: it only opens if a pre-agreed condition is met — like a lease with a clause saying "if the heating fails in winter, you can cancel immediately." Opening the hatch and walking through it are two different things. Verstappen's condition: if he isn't among the top two in the drivers' standings by the summer break, he's allowed to leave for 2027.[1]

ESPN spells out the decisive sentence today, 07/18: since it has become mathematically impossible for Verstappen to be second by the summer break, he can pull the clause if he wants to.[1] The math behind it is trivial, and therefore unassailable: he sits seventh with 76 points, two races remain before the break (Spa tomorrow, Hungary on 07/26), and Spa is confirmed without a sprint. Even two wins would be 50 points — a cap of 126. Above him sit Antonelli with 179, Russell with 154, and Hamilton with 147, none of whom would need to score another single point.[3]

P7, 76 pts → max. 50 pts in 2 races → Cap 126 → P2 (154) out of reach → Clause condition met

What's new today is less the math than the timeline: according to ESPN, Verstappen doesn't have to tell Red Bull his decision until October.[1] That's the number that actually matters. It means the question won't be settled during the summer break but will stay open for roughly a quarter of a year — through Zandvoort, Monza, and the autumn races. For Red Bull, that means planning without certainty. For the competition: time to put together an offer. For Daniel as a reader, it mostly means that every report over the coming weeks should be treated as possible negotiation noise.

Where speculation ends Confirmed: the clause exists, its condition is met, and the notification deadline is October — that's how ESPN describes it. Also confirmed: coverage names McLaren as an interested party, and Verstappen has not publicly committed to 2027. Not confirmed: any statement about where he'll race in 2027, any figure attached to an offer, any "a deal is imminent" framing. The exact contract wording isn't public; we're reporting the condition the way ESPN describes it.

WEC: BMW wins and still trails — five points, four races [6][7]

Important context: the WEC isn't racing this weekend. The standings below are from after the Rolex 6 Hours of São Paulo (Sun 07/12, round 4); action resumes only at the Lone Star Le Mans in Austin, 09/04–06.[7]

ELI5 · Manufacturers' standings in endurance racing Unlike in Formula 1, every manufacturer in the top class ("Hypercar") fields multiple cars, each with three drivers who rotate during the race — nobody drives six hours straight alone. Only the best-placed car per brand counts toward the manufacturers' standings. That's why a manufacturer can win a race and still fail to move up the table: a win only scores points once, no matter how many of the brand's own cars finish behind it.

That's exactly what happened in São Paulo: BMW won — the M Hybrid V8 carrying car number 15 (Magnussen/Marciello/Vanthoor) took its second win of the season — and Toyota didn't score in that race.[7] Yet Toyota still leads, 132 to 127.[6] The cushion built up in Imola, Spa, and Le Mans is still carrying it. What makes the standings interesting now: with four races remaining — Austin, Fuji, Qatar, Bahrain — five points are worth less than a single good race. This championship is open, and for a month it wasn't.
