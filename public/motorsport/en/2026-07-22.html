Hungaroring: the place where Saturday counts [1][2]

A tight, slow circuit — and the last date before the break

The Hungarian Grand Prix is the next race: Spa is behind us, the Hungaroring lies ahead. It's a classic weekend without a sprint — two practice sessions on Friday, FP3 and qualifying on Saturday, the race on Sunday over 70 laps (or a maximum of 120 minutes).[1] After that, the summer break begins; the next F1 round, the Dutch Grand Prix, is a good three weeks away (21.–23.08.).[2]

On track, the Hungaroring is a distinctive test. The circuit is tight, twisty, and slow, with few real straights — overtaking here is traditionally as hard as almost anywhere on the calendar. That shifts the weight of the weekend clearly forward: whoever is at the front on Saturday usually controls the race on Sunday too.

ELI5 · Why "track position" is king here On a wide, fast circuit with long straights, a faster car can come from behind — it uses the slipstream and pulls past. The Hungaroring barely allows that: it's like a tight country-road course where even a clearly faster car gets stuck behind a slower one, because there's simply no room to get by. That's why it's often not the fastest car that wins here, but the one that nails the best lap in Saturday qualifying. For the title fight, that means: a weak Saturday is almost impossible to fix on Sunday.

The standings before the break: nothing has moved, everything's still to defend [3][4][5]

Antonelli enters the final act with a 45-point lead

Because no race fell between Spa and today, both tables are identical to yesterday's standings — we refetched them today from formula1.com and cross-checked against racingnews365, and they match row for row.[3][4] Antonelli leads the drivers' championship 204 to 159 ahead of Lewis Hamilton, followed by George Russell (154) and Charles Leclerc (126). In the constructors' standings, the picture is clearer. The real duel in this table remains Mercedes versus Ferrari — McLaren (195) and Red Bull (151) are already out of reach for the title.

Verstappen's clause: already decided before Hungary [6]

Verstappen's contract runs through 2028, but contains a performance-based exit clause that would need to be decided on in October. The sporting condition: he'd need to be at least second in the drivers' championship by the summer break.[6] When ESPN reported this on 16.07., two races still remained; after Spa, only Hungary is left — and that settles the math before a wheel turns at the Hungaroring.

The numbers are unambiguous: Verstappen is seventh with 91 points. Even a win in Hungary would add 25, for a maximum of 116. Second place stands at Hamilton's 159 — and Hungary can't shrink that total, since points aren't deducted. Russell (154) and Leclerc (126) also sit above Verstappen's best possible final tally. The best he can achieve before the break is fifth place.

What this means — and what it doesn't The sporting threshold the option hinges on is one Red Bull can no longer meet before the break — that much is solid today. What follows from it depends on contract wording that none of the sources we checked quote verbatim: if second place is the condition for Verstappen having to stay, the exit option now becomes live; he wouldn't have to use it — the decision would only be due in October. A headline circulating today claiming Verstappen has already "confirmed" his future sits behind a paywall and gets mixed up in search results with older 2025 reports about a different decision — we're not treating it as new news until an open, clearly 2026-dated source confirms it.
