GitHub breach — the attack reaches the IDE

This time the top story isn't in a registry, it's in the editor. On May 18 a compromised version of the popular Nx Console extension (nrwl.angular-console v18.95.0, >2.2M installs) appeared in the VS Code Marketplace — it was online for only 18 minutes (12:30–12:48 UTC), long enough. The embedded multi-stage stealer grabbed tokens and secrets from GitHub, npm, AWS, HashiCorp Vault, Kubernetes and 1Password and exfiltrated them over three channels in parallel: HTTPS, the GitHub API, and DNS tunneling. One caught Nx developer involuntarily handed over their GitHub credentials — which gave the attacker access to roughly 3,800 GitHub-internal repositories. GitHub confirmed the incident on May 19, but stresses: no customer, enterprise or user repo affected, and critical secrets were rotated overnight by priority [1][2].

IDE extension as attack surface: An editor extension runs with all the privileges of your login — it sees your tokens, your SSH keys, your open projects. So a malicious extension isn't a program knocking at the door; it's one you hand the master key to yourself when you install it. Here, 18 minutes in the marketplace was enough to pass that key to strangers.

DNS tunneling is the nasty part: stolen data is wrapped into innocuous-looking DNS queries that almost every firewall lets through — exfiltration through the one port nobody closes. The takeaway for anyone who updated Nx Console on May 18: treat every reachable token as compromised and rotate it [3].

TeamPCP — one actor, three trust layers

What turns the Nx breach from an isolated incident into the story of the month is the attribution: security firms tie it to TeamPCP — tracked by Mandiant/Google as UNC6780 — and thus to the same crew behind the npm worm Shai-Hulud and the GitHub Actions wave Megalodon on May 18. Three campaigns, one pattern: they never go after the "new malicious version" a reviewer would notice, but the implicit trust layer underneath. For Shai-Hulud it's the provenance signature (which the worm now forges), for Megalodon the CI bot identity, for the Nx breach the extension in the official Marketplace. Initial access reportedly came from a previously compromised TanStack npm package that leaked the dev credentials — so the chains are linked [1][2].

Practical reading: registry → pipeline → IDE are three doors into the same house, and all three assume "what looks official is real." That assumption is exactly the target — pinning extensions, verifying bot commits and pulling fresh packages with a cooldown defends one principle in three places [3].

KEV status — Drupal deadline Wednesday, Rift still left out

The CISA KEV is unchanged at version 2026.05.22 — no new entry on May 23, 24 or 25. That makes the Drupal entry (CVE-2026-9082, PostgreSQL-only SQLi) the most recent add, and its FCEB deadline is Wednesday May 27 — the day after tomorrow. Imperva still reports >15,000 attack attempts against nearly 6,000 sites in 65 countries; Drupal itself updated the advisory on May 22 confirming in-the-wild attempts. The framing matters: mostly reconnaissance and scanners mapping vulnerable Postgres-Drupal installs, not widespread takeovers yet. A public PoC scanner is circulating on GitHub [1][2].

Fragnesia: patched, no in-the-wild

The Linux kernel LPE CVE-2026-46300 (CVSS 7.8, "Dirty Frag" family, XFRM/ESP-in-TCP) has been patched across all major distributions since mid-May (Alma, CloudLinux, Debian, RHEL, SUSE, Ubuntu, Amazon Linux). There's a public PoC in the discoverer's repo, but no confirmed in-the-wild exploitation. Quick no-reboot mitigation: unload or denylist esp4, esp6 and rxrpc if unused [1].

OpenBSD 7.9 — the 60th release, proud of its edges

On May 19 Theo de Raadt announced OpenBSD 7.9 — the project's 60th release. The concrete changes: MAXCPUs on amd64 jumps from 64 to 255 (xAPIC limit), an initial Wi-Fi 6 scaffold (802.11ax), the graphics driver stack moves to the Linux 6.18 level, and there's delayed hibernation: when the battery gets very low, the machine wakes briefly and immediately hibernates. On board: OpenSSH 10.3 and LibreSSL 4.3.0, a new __pledge_open(2) syscall and stricter pledge(2)/unveil(2) rules, IPv6 SLAAC on by default, VLAN-aware bridges in veb(4), plus new arm64/riscv64 SoC support [1][2].

OpenBSD isn't a production system for Daniel, but for years the project has set the security defaults that become standard elsewhere later (privilege separation, pledge, W^X). A release tag worth noting as a senior dev [3].

pgBackRest — Postgres' backup tool gets a backup of its own

One of the better OSS stories of the week, and it hits Daniel's stack directly: pgBackRest, the most-used backup/restore tool for PostgreSQL, was sliding toward a bus-factor hole. After Snowflake's acquisition of Crunchy Data (June 2025), 13-year solo maintainer David Steele was left without a sponsor. On May 19 a coalition stepped up: AWS, Percona, Supabase, pgEdge (plus Tiger Data/Timescale and Eon.io) fund dedicated Steele time plus Percona engineering to onboard a second maintainer [1].

Bus factor: The number of people a project would have to lose (literally, get hit by a bus) before nobody understands it anymore. Bus factor 1 means one person carries everything — if they drop out, the project stalls. That was pgBackRest, a tool thousands of production databases depend on.

The pattern is familiar (xz-utils, OpenSSL before Heartbleed): critical infrastructure resting on a single maintainer. What's notable here is the proactive fix — before anything broke, not after. If pgBackRest is in your backup strategy, you can exhale [2].

CADA — Wednesday Brussels gets serious (maybe)

The Cloud and AI Development Act is on the agenda for the Commission meeting on Wednesday, May 27 — as part of the Tech Sovereignty Package, bundled with the Chips Act 2, an Open-Source Strategy and an "AI in Energy" roadmap. (A correction of our own: May 27 is a Wednesday, not Monday — yesterday's brief had the weekday wrong.) Important caveat: the package has already slipped twice (originally March, then April), with no reason given — so "expected," not "certain" [1].

Digital sovereignty: The question of who can pull the plug in an emergency. If government data sits on a US cloud, the provider is subject to US law — a foreign government could demand access or cut off the service. "Sovereignty" means keeping critical data under your own jurisdiction. Like a safe-deposit box in your own bank rather than in a country whose laws you don't get a vote on.

The sensitive question is still in the leaked draft (CNBC, May 7): considered is restricting the use of US cloud for sensitive government data (health, finance, justice) — explicitly not a ban, not for the private sector. So far that's only a single-sourced claim about internal discussions, in no published text. Anyone serving public-sector contracts in the EU reads the real text Wednesday — if it comes [2].

EU regulatory clock — DMA/Apple on Jun 1, CRA in the background

Two deadlines on the radar. DMA: Apple's interop deadline on Monday Jun 1 (notifications, proximity pairing, audio switching) stands; the statutory review (COM(2026) 178, Apr 28) holds that it's "premature" to extend messenger interoperability to social networks. CRA (Cyber Resilience Act): the next hard dates are the notification of conformity-assessment bodies on Jun 11 and — more important for software vendors — the start of reporting obligations via the ENISA platform on Sep 11 (24h early warning / 72h notification / 14-day final report). The latter is weeks away; it belongs on the radar, not in the 7-day track [1][2].

Small stuff with a lesson — API keys, Vivado, .de DNSSEC

Google API keys: a 23-minute afterlife

Researchers found that deleted Google API keys stay valid for ~23 minutes — a real abuse window after rotation or offboarding. If you automate key lifecycle on GCP or run incident response, build the delay into your assumptions [1].

AMD drops Linux from Vivado's free tier

AMD's new tiered license for the Vivado FPGA suite (2026.1) leaves the free Basic tier Windows-only — Linux support is now in the paid tiers only (from $1,200/yr). Heavy backlash from the FPGA/hobby/academic scene; a story about eroding developer-friendliness [2].

.de DNSSEC outage: the post-mortems

For the May 5 .de TLD outage (a DNSSEC KSK rollover mishap: three key pairs across three HSMs, one published DNSKEY tag but signed with all three → SERVFAIL on every validating resolver), detailed analyses from DENIC and Cloudflare are now out. Required reading if you run DNSSEC [3].

Comment — the assumption is the target

If you've been following the past weeks, the picture is clear: it was never an npm or GitHub problem, it's a trust problem. TeamPCP opened the same principle through three doors — registry, CI, marketplace. The move into the IDE is the most uncomfortable, because the extension runs with your privileges, not those of some server somewhere.

For Daniel's stack today, concretely: bring Postgres clusters to the May 14 level (and if Drupal-on-Postgres runs anywhere, patch by Wednesday). Pin VS Code extensions to known versions and treat auto-update with suspicion. Check whether CI verifies bot commits and tag provenance, and consider a cooldown for fresh packages. Catch up on the Linux kernel, unload esp4/esp6 if unused. And on Wednesday we'll see whether the leaked US-cloud clause becomes real CADA text — or whether Brussels slips a third time [1].
