Qualcomm Buys Modular — and Targets Nvidia's Software Moat

Qualcomm is acquiring the AI startup Modular in an all-stock deal worth roughly $3.92B (up to 19.2M new Qualcomm shares for Modular shareholders).[1] Modular builds two things: the programming language Mojo and the inference engine MAX. The punch: you write AI code once and run it across CPUs, GPUs, NPUs, and ASICs from different vendors, without locking yourself into Nvidia's vendor libraries.[2]

CUDA moat: CUDA is Nvidia's software layer for programming their GPUs — and almost the entire AI world writes in CUDA today. Imagine all the world's recipes were written only for a single stove brand: even a better competitor's stove stays useless because no recipes exist for it. That's exactly this lock-in — Nvidia's real moat isn't the chips, it's that all software is tuned to them. A hardware-agnostic compiler like Modular is an attempt to write "recipes for every stove."

Why this matters as a dev story and not just chip news: Qualcomm isn't buying a fab or a model — it's buying a toolchain. If the plan works, market power shifts from "which GPU" to "which compiler" — and for developers, that means less hardware lock-in on deployment over time. The deal is expected to close in H2 2026, subject to regulation and shareholder approval.[1] Skepticism remains warranted: Modular's own bet to broadly displace CUDA is still promise, not proof — and under a chip conglomerate, the "neutral" compiler could quickly become a Qualcomm house standard.

Micron: Record Quarter and $50B Outlook — the Memory Supercycle Is Real

Micron reports for its third fiscal quarter 2026 (ended May 28) a record revenue of $41.46B — versus $9.30B in the prior-year quarter, that's +346% YoY and nearly double the prior quarter ($23.86B).[3] GAAP net income: $28.24B, or $24.67 per diluted share.[3] For Q4, Micron guides $50.0B ± 1.0B at roughly 86% gross margin.[4]

But the really interesting part isn't the revenue — it's the structure: Micron has signed 16 "take-or-pay" customer contracts for roughly ~$100B in minimum revenue plus $22B in advance payments combined.[5]

Take-or-pay: A contract where the customer pays whether they take the goods or not — like a gym membership that's due even if you never show up. For Micron, that means guaranteed revenue regardless of memory price swings. That's precisely what's supposed to break the infamous memory cycle (boom-bust every half-year).

For infra planners, the message is simple and uncomfortable: HBM and DRAM capacity is pre-sold years out, the AI build-out is eating it up. If you're procuring servers in 2026/27, factor memory lead times and pricing as a standalone risk, not a line item.

SK Hynix Planning Record Nasdaq ADR — Proceeds Go Entirely to Fabs

SK Hynix is targeting a Nasdaq ADR listing for up to 45.45 trillion won (~$29B) — up to 17.79M new shares (~2.5% of capital), listing target July 10.[6] It would be one of the largest ADR offerings ever.

ADR (American Depositary Receipt): A kind of deposit certificate that lets a foreign company trade on a US exchange without a direct listing there. Like a coat check: you don't hold the Korean stock itself, you hold a receipt for it that you can buy and sell in New York.

The proceeds flow entirely into capex, per Bloomberg: the first fab (Y1) in the Yongin cluster, the Cheongju fab for advanced HBM packaging and EUV equipment.[7] Together with Micron's pre-sales, it paints the same picture — memory makers are burning every available dollar into capacity for the AI appetite.

Security Week: Three Active Burning Bugs [8][9][10]

If anything is genuinely urgent this week, it's these three. All are past theory — they're being exploited or hitting every affected system hard.

Cisco Unified CM — CVE-2026-20230 (CVSS 8.6, actively exploited)

An SSRF in Cisco Unified Communications Manager (and CME) can be escalated unauthenticated to file-write and thus root — precondition is an enabled WebDialer. Exploitation since June 21–22, added by CISA to the KEV list, federal deadline was June 28 — so yesterday.[8]

SSRF (Server-Side Request Forgery): You trick the server into making requests on your behalf that you couldn't make yourself. Like getting the janitor to unlock a door that's locked for you — the server has the keys, you use it as a proxy to reach internal targets.

Splunk Enterprise — CVE-2026-20253 (CVSS 9.8, actively exploited)

An endpoint of the bundled PostgreSQL sidecar skips authentication — an attacker can create or truncate files and open an RCE path. Affected: 10.2.0–10.2.3 and 10.0.0–10.0.6. WatchTowr published a PoC on June 12; CISA listed the bug June 18 in the KEV.[9] If you run Splunk: check version, this is not a "later."

SAP NetWeaver — CVE-2026-44748 (CVSS 9.9, patched)

An XML-signature-wrapping flaw in SAML auth on NetWeaver AS ABAP / ABAP Platform: an authenticated attacker can forge SAML responses and escalate privileges or access data. Affected: SAP_BASIS 702–919 — essentially all supported releases. SAP patched on patch day June 9 with note #3746332.[10]

XML Signature Wrapping: SAML logins are secured with an XML signature. In "wrapping," the attacker leaves the real, signed message untouched but places a second, manipulated one beside it — the server validates the signature on the real part but processes the forged one. Like a real, stamped envelope, then sneaking a second, fake letter inside it afterward.

Defender Zero-Day "RoguePlanet" — Still Without Patch

The Defender bug CVE-2026-50656 ("RoguePlanet", CVSS 7.8) exploits a race condition in the malware-protection engine to spawn a shell with SYSTEM privileges — on fully patched Win10/11 after the June patch day (Server is unaffected).[11] Microsoft confirmed the bug and is working on a fix but names no timeline; no patch was out by end of June.[12]

Important context against the panic: A public PoC exists (researcher "Nightmare-Eclipse"), but Microsoft reports no exploitation in the wild. That means: take it seriously, watch your patch window — but this isn't an active attack like Cisco or Splunk.

Heads up Irony on the nose: The defense layer itself (Defender) becomes an attack surface. That's precisely the pattern EDR software has been following for years — a privileged engine that can scan everything is a lucrative target.

EU CRA: Type-A Deadline Looming (Aug 30, 2026)

Standing thread regulation: horizontal (Type-A) products have Cyber Resilience Act compliance starting August 30, 2026 — roughly 62 days away. Vulnerability reporting obligations begin September 11, 2026; the main compliance kicks in December 11, 2027.[16]

CRA (Cyber Resilience Act): EU law mandating security minimums for products "with digital elements" — from updates to supply time to the duty to disclose exploited holes. For vendors, that means security becomes product liability, not elective.

If you ship software or connected devices in the EU, get your disclosure paths and update promises sorted by now.
