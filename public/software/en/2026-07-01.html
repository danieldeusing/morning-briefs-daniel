SimpleHelp: CVSS 10.0, ~1,000 servers exposed, a stealer for dev credentials

The biggest story today is an RMM flaw. CVE-2026-48558 in SimpleHelp carries the perfect maximum score CVSS 10.0 and is being actively exploited. The root cause: In OIDC login, the application simply doesn't verify the cryptographic signature of identity tokens — an attacker submits a forged token and gains access without any credentials.[1] Affected are versions 5.5.15 and earlier plus 6.0 pre-release builds; fixed in 5.5.16 and 6.0 RC2 at the end of May.[1][2]

RMM: Remote Monitoring & Management — the software IT service providers use to remotely access hundreds of customer machines. Think of it as a master key that fits every apartment in an entire building. Whoever cracks the RMM server sits right on that keyring — that's why RMM tools are a favorite target.

At the time of disclosure, roughly 1,000 vulnerable SimpleHelp servers were exposed online, according to BleepingComputer.[3] CISA has added the flaw to its Known-Exploited-Vulnerabilities catalog; US federal agencies must remediate by July 2, 2026 under BOD 26-04.[4]

Heads up What makes this interesting is not just the flaw, but the payload. Through the compromised servers comes a modular Node.js loader called TaskWeaver, which fingerprints the device and pulls down additional JavaScript modules from the C2 server.[3] It installs a fresh infostealer, Djinn Stealer, that explicitly targets cloud provider credentials, identity services, deployment platforms, and — explicitly — AI developer tools. Data is exfiltrated AES-256-GCM encrypted with RSA-2048.[5] This is why it belongs in the Software Brief and not the AI Brief: the attack surface is the hook, not a model. If you have dev-adjacent secrets in your environment, you're the declared target.

Langflow: unauthenticated RCE via exec(), exploited within 20 hours

Another case from the "agent tooling as attack surface" category. Langflow — the visual builder for LLM workflows — carries an unauthenticated remote-code execution in CVE-2026-33017 (CVSS 9.3). The endpoint POST /api/v1/build_public_tmp/{flow_id}/flow accepts attacker-controlled Python code and passes it without any sandboxing directly to exec() — no credentials, a single HTTP request.[6][7]

All versions through 1.8.2 are affected; the fix is in 1.9.0.[6] It was exploited within 20 hours of the advisory's publication.[7] Attackers drop a Monero miner (XMRig) under /var/tmp/.xlamb/ and — the unpleasant part — launch an SSH-key-reuse worm that traverses ~/.ssh/known_hosts and agent sockets, spreading itself via SCP to every reachable host.[8] Secondary sources indicate the flaw is listed in the CISA KEV catalog (we couldn't independently verify the exact listing date).

Remember: "public flow" here literally means without auth boundary. Anyone running Langflow with network access should put it behind an auth layer and not stay on 1.8.x — the worm component turns a single exposed node into a wildfire across SSH trust chains.

FBI: Russian intelligence services now harvesting Signal backup keys

The FBI published a Public Service Announcement on June 26 (I-062626-PSA): Two Russian threat groups, UNC5792 and UNC4221 — attributed to Russian intelligence services — are specifically phishing the "Secure-Backup" recovery keys from Signal.[9][10]

Heads up The bitter core: A stolen recovery key remains valid — even if the victim sets up a new Signal account on the same phone number, the compromised key does not become invalid.[11] So the classic "just re-register" reflex doesn't work here. The US Rewards for Justice program has offered $10M for tips leading to those responsible.[12]

Operation Endgame Phase 3: Europol dismantles the next stealer tier

Between June 15 and 19, the third wave of Operation Endgame — coordinated by Europol and Eurojust — took down the infrastructure behind the Amadey, StealC, and SocGholish/FakeUpdates malware families.[14] The tally: 326 servers disrupted, 142 domains taken offline, approximately $47M in crypto marked as criminal and 27 million stolen credentials recovered from over 385,000 compromised systems.[15][16][17]

Infostealer: Malware whose sole job is to grab stored passwords, cookies, crypto wallets, and tokens from an infected computer and ship them out. Like a pickpocket who only steals keys and vanishes silently — the stolen access is then bulk-resold.

In May alone, over 140,000 infected devices were linked to Amadey and StealC; the SocGholish disruption — connected to Evil Corp — cleaned up nearly 15,000 compromised websites.[14] Participants included Canada, Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, and the USA plus ten private partners.[14]

Windows 11 KB5095093: "Point-in-time Restore" as system rollback

A solid feature instead of a flaw: With the preview update KB5095093 (build 26100.8737 for 24H2, 26200.8737 for 25H2), released on June 23, Microsoft introduces "Point-in-time Restore".[18][19]

VSS snapshot: The Volume Shadow Copy Service periodically freezes a consistent image of system state — like a photo of your entire computer at one point in time. Point-in-time Restore lets you jump back to that photo if an update or change breaks something.

On consumer devices, snapshots are created automatically every 24 hours and kept for a maximum of 72 hours (Enterprise can set 4/6/12/16/24-hour intervals); the system needs at least 200 GB of storage.[19][20] The preview is live now; it becomes mandatory with the July Patch Tuesday on July 14.[20]

Databases & Infrastructure: Splunk RCE and PostgreSQL patch roundup

Splunk Enterprise has an unauthenticated RCE in CVE-2026-20253 (CVSS 9.8) via a PostgreSQL sidecar file endpoint. Affected are branches 10.0.0–10.0.6 and 10.2.0–10.2.3; fixed in 10.0.7 and 10.2.4.[21] The flaw is in the CISA KEV catalog — the (already-passed) agency deadline was June 21, mentioned here for context because active attacks began shortly after disclosure.[22]

For your stack Older but directly relevant: On May 14, PostgreSQL shipped a patch roundup across all branches (18.4, 17.10, 16.14, 15.18, 14.23) with 11 CVEs.[23] Two stand out with CVSS 8.8: CVE-2026-6637 — a stack buffer overflow in refint that lets a low-privilege user execute code as the database OS user — and CVE-2026-6475, a symlink-following flaw in pg_basebackup/pg_rewind that lets an origin superuser overwrite local files like .bashrc.[24][25] If you're still on a minor version below these, plan the upgrade.

The pattern behind today: Credentials are the prize

Three of today's stories — SimpleHelp/Djinn, Operation Endgame, and the Signal warning — tell the same story from three angles: the real value no longer lies in encrypting files, but in silently draining access credentials. Djinn targets cloud and dev tokens, Endgame recovered 27M stolen credentials, and even the Signal attack wants a key, not plaintext.

For a developer, practically speaking: The most valuable attack point on your machine is the folder with your tokens — ~/.aws, ~/.ssh, your Docker login, the .env in your project, your cached cloud CLI token. Short-lived tokens, hardware keys instead of passwords, and a watchful eye on unusual RMM or tooling connections cost little and strip all value from this category of prize.
