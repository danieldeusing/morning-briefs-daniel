SimpleHelp: CVSS 10.0, actively exploited — deadline is today [1][2]

The sharpest story of the day: SimpleHelp, a widely deployed remote support/RMM tool, has CVE-2026-48558, a vulnerability with the maximum possible score of CVSS 10.0 — an authentication bypass in the OpenID Connect flow. CISA added it to the KEV catalog on 29.06. and set a remediation deadline for federal agencies that expires today (02.07.).[1][2]

RMM / Remote support tool: Software that lets a service provider access and manage many customer computers remotely — like a janitor with a master key to every office in a building. Whoever steals that key is suddenly in all the rooms. That's why an auth-bypass hole here is so critical: the attacker logs in without a password and inherits the tool's access to everything.

Why this matters for you as a dev: the attack doesn't stop at access. After takeover, a two-stage payload is deployed, according to Blackpoint — TaskWeaver, a Node.js loader, and Djinn Stealer, a cross-platform info-stealer specifically targeting developer assets: SSH keys, infrastructure configs, source-control tokens, package-registry credentials, dev tooling, and crypto wallets.[1] This isn't generic ransomware, it's surgical theft of exactly the secrets that let attackers dig deeper into pipelines and repos later.

Affected versions are SimpleHelp 5.5.15 and older, plus 6.0 pre-releases; fixed in 5.5.16 and 6.0 RC2 (already released in late May).[1] Internet scans count roughly 14,000 externally reachable SimpleHelp servers, with an estimated ~1,000 directly vulnerable.[2] Not a "patch tonight" emergency, but the combo of CVSS 10, active exploitation, and credential theft makes this update a clear priority if SimpleHelp is anywhere in your stack.

Oracle PeopleSoft: Zero-Day, 100+ organizations compromised

Less headline-grabbing, more substance for anyone with enterprise backends: CVE-2026-35273 in Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools is a zero-day with CVSS 9.8 — remote, unauthenticated, exploitable over HTTP. The group ShinyHunters used it to compromise, by their own account, 100+ organizations (including University of Nottingham) across roughly 300 vulnerable instances.[3]

PeopleSoft lives in HR and finance systems at large institutions — the blast radius is proportional. Note: Oracle ships patches quarterly via Critical Patch Update; the next one drops in July. Until then, exposure reduction (no PeopleTools exposed directly to the internet) is the realistic mitigation.

Langflow vulnerability still being exploited for crypto-mining

CVE-2026-33017 in Langflow (CVSS 9.3) continues to be actively exploited to spread a Monero miner.[4] Langflow is a tool for building AI agents — the real news here isn't the model but the agent/tooling layer as an attack surface: exposed builder backends are a convenient entry point for cryptojacking. If you self-host such tools, they belong behind auth, not on the open internet. (AI agent details elsewhere in the ai-dev brief.)

Docker: old max-severity bug returns

CVE-2026-34040 (CVSS 8.8) is an incomplete fix for CVE-2024-41110 — the max-severity authz-plugin hole from July 2024. A crafted image can bypass authorization plugins and spawn a privileged container with root access to the host. This exposes AWS credentials, SSH keys, Kubernetes configs.[7]

Authorization-plugin bypass: An authz plugin is the bouncer checking every Docker request: "are you allowed to do this?" A bypass is like someone slipping through the back door — the bouncer never sees them. Now they're inside, waving a pass.

Reported in April 2026, so not a 24-hour item — but important context because Docker sits in your stack and "incomplete fix of an old critical" is the classic thing missed in adoption reviews. Check whether your engine/plugins are on the patched version.

Cyber Resilience Act: the clock is ticking (but slowly)

Standing thread in EU regulation: the Cyber Resilience Act is rolling out in phases. Chapter IV (notification to conformity-assessment bodies) has been in effect since 11.06.2026. The more relevant reporting obligations in Article 14 — 24-hour notification of actively exploited vulnerabilities and serious incidents to ENISA and the national authority — take effect 11.09.2026. Main obligations (products with "digital elements") start 11.12.2027.[8]

Still a ways off for solo devs and small shops, but now's the moment when "security by design" shifts from a nice-to-have to a mandate. Not a timeline item (too far out), but keep it in mind.

PostgreSQL: bundle update with 11 CVEs [9][10]

Straight into your stack: the Postgres releases 18.4 / 17.10 / 16.14 / 15.18 / 14.23 (from 14.05.) close a total of 11 CVEs, four of them high-severity at CVSS 8.8:[9]

  • CVE-2026-6473 — integer wraparound / undersized memory allocations lead to out-of-bounds writes.[9]
  • CVE-2026-6475 — symlink following in pg_basebackup/pg_rewind: a superuser can overwrite local files and take over the OS account.[10]
  • CVE-2026-6477 — libpq's lo_* functions overwrite the client-side stack: a malicious server can corrupt memory in the client process.[10]
  • CVE-2026-6637 — stack buffer overflow in refint: low-privilege DB users can execute arbitrary code or drive SQL injection.[10]

Seven weeks old, so no breaking news — but exactly the kind of patch-adoption context that a non-AI software brief should deliver: if your Postgres instances are still on an older patch level, those are three solid reasons to pull the update into your next maintenance window.

Caddy 2.11: ECH rotation, passive health checking, matcher fixes

For your reverse proxy: Caddy 2.11 has shipped (release started at v2.11.1 — the release process broke, 2.11.0 was skipped — now at v2.11.2).[11] Highlights: automatic ECH key rotation, host header rewriting for HTTPS upstreams, passive health checking for dynamic upstreams, tls_resolvers global option, and zstd log compression.

ECH (Encrypted Client Hello): In the TLS handshake, the client currently reveals in cleartext which domain it's contacting (the "SNI"). ECH wraps that info in encryption — like putting a letter in a second, opaque envelope so the mail carrier can't read the destination. Automatic key rotation means Caddy swaps out the keys needed for this on its own.

2.11 also fixes several CVEs — host-matcher case sensitivity, path-matcher normalization, a TLS client auth issue, and glob sanitization in file matching. Specific CVE IDs/CVSS scores for these couldn't be cleanly verified in research — so intentionally no made-up IDs here, just an "included, please update" note.[12]
