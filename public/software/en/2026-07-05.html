The ECJ seals Google's €4.1 billion fine — and it's more than just a number [1][2][3]

On July 2, the European Court of Justice (ECJ) rejected Google's final appeal in the Android antitrust case. That makes the fine of €4.1 billion final — Google has no further legal remedy.[1] The case has been running since 2018: the EU Commission originally imposed €4.34 billion, the EU General Court reduced the sum to €4.125 billion in 2022 while upholding the core allegations — and now the highest instance has cemented the whole thing.[2]

ECJ (Court of Justice): Think of the EU judiciary as a soccer match with two halves. The "EU General Court" is the first half, the ECJ is the second and final one — after this final whistle, there's no extra time. For Google, that means: the game is over, the €4.1 billion is due.

What the case was about: Google forced device makers to preinstall Google Search and Chrome just to get access to the Play Store, paid manufacturers and carriers for exclusive search-engine preinstallation, and hindered alternative Android forks.[3] That's the app-store/preinstallation debate that's followed us as developers for years — only now with a final, legally binding precedent behind it.

The real dynamite is in the fine print: under the EU Damages Directive, a final finding of infringement acts as binding proof in national civil proceedings that Google broke the law. Competitors and injured companies no longer have to prove that a violation occurred — only what damage it specifically caused them.[1] So the ruling isn't the end, but the starting gun for a wave of possible follow-on lawsuits.

Chrome 150: nearly 400 holes closed in one shot [4][5]

Google pushed Chrome 150 (build 150.0.7871.46/47 for Windows/macOS, .46 for Linux) into the stable channel, closing 382 security holes — 15 rated critical, plus 67 high, 169 medium and 131 low.[4] That's one of the largest single updates in Chrome's history. The lion's share (358 findings) came from Google's internal fuzzing; externally reported flaws earned around $90,000 in bug bounties.[5]

Use-after-free: The classic among critical Chrome bugs. Imagine you free up a parking spot but keep your old ticket — and meanwhile someone else parks their car there. If you come back with the old ticket and use "your" spot, you're accessing someone else's car. In memory terms: a program keeps using a block that's already been freed — and an attacker can slip in their own code right there.

The 15 critical CVEs (CVE-2026-13774 through CVE-2026-13788) are mostly such use-after-free bugs, spread across broad attack surfaces: extensions, the GPU layer (Dawn), WebUSB, Bluetooth and the Views component.[5] For us, that simply means: let Chrome (and every Chromium derivative — Edge, Brave, Vivaldi) restart so the update takes effect. Chrome 151 is expected around 07/28 on the usual four-week cadence.[4]

The supply-chain thread: "Miasma" hits Red Hat npm packages [10][11]

Slightly outside the seven-day window, but too relevant to leave out: in early June, a worm called "Miasma" surfaced under the npm namespace @redhat-cloud-services — at least 32 tampered package releases with roughly 80,000 weekly downloads combined.[10] Root cause: a Red Hat employee's GitHub account was hijacked via a malicious VS Code extension.

Supply-chain worm: Instead of attacking your machine directly, the attacker poisons an ingredient that thousands of projects pull in — like tainted flour that ends up in a hundred bakeries. A "worm" makes it worse: stolen credentials from one victim are automatically used to infect the next package. Miasma is a new iteration of the well-known "Shai-Hulud" worm family.

The good news: this was credential stealing, most malicious versions had been pulled by midday on June 1 — and according to Red Hat, no shipped Red Hat product was built with a compromised version.[11] Still, the pattern remains the core takeaway: the developer-tooling layer (IDE extensions, CI tokens) has become the preferred entry point into the supply chain. Anyone pinning npm packages and checking lockfiles against source repos sleeps more soundly.

Windows patch day: three critical RCEs — and a longer tail [6][7]

Microsoft's June patch day brought three remote-code-execution flaws rated critical: in the Windows kernel (CVE-2026-45657), the HTTP.sys driver (CVE-2026-47291) and the DHCP client service (CVE-2026-44815).[6]

RCE (Remote Code Execution): The worst bug class there is. The attacker needs neither a password nor a click from anyone — they send the vulnerable service a crafted packet over the network and run their own code directly on the target machine. With HTTP.sys and DHCP, the vulnerable service sits deep in the operating system and listens by default — hence the top severity rating.

More interesting than the three "standard" criticals is the tail that follows: researcher "Nightmare Eclipse" disclosed two more Windows flaws — YellowKey (CVE-2026-45585, CVSS 6.8), a BitLocker bypass, and GreenPlasma (CVE-2026-45586), a privilege escalation via CTFMON. Both are rated medium, affect current Windows 11 / Server 2025 builds, and were already patched on June 10.[7] YellowKey's trick: it abuses trusted behavior in the Windows recovery environment so an attacker with physical access can open an unrestricted shell during pre-boot recovery and reach the encrypted drive; GreenPlasma, by contrast, yields a local SYSTEM shell.

Heads up BitLocker bypasses require physical access — no remote attack, but a classic move for stolen laptops. Anyone selling BitLocker as "theft protection" should know this patch series and consider enforcing a pre-boot PIN.

And the one that remains open: "RoguePlanet" — a Defender privilege-escalation flaw that hits even fully patched Windows 10/11 and grants system rights by abusing Defender itself. It is now tracked as CVE-2026-50656 (assigned in mid-June); Microsoft's Defender update 1.453.20.0 only delivers an easily bypassed basic detection.[6] Microsoft told The Register it's aware of the reported flaw and is actively investigating "the validity and possible applicability of these claims."[8] Translation: there's no fix yet.

Docker Engine CVE-2026-34040: 2024's half-fix comes back to bite [9]

A straggler from April that affects the container world and thus belongs here: CVE-2026-34040 (CVSS 8.8) is an authorization bypass in Docker Engine — and explicitly an incomplete fix for the older CVE-2024-41110.[9] Attackers can use it to spin up privileged containers with host-root access and get at AWS credentials, SSH keys or the kubeconfig.

Authorization bypass: A Docker authorization plugin is like a bouncer checking which containers get which permissions. This flaw is the trick to slip past the bouncer with a crafted request — he's never even asked. Once through, you can start a container with full host rights, and from there the machine is yours.

In practice: anyone running Docker with authorization plugins (AuthZ) in multi-tenant environments should upgrade to the patched engine version. For a solo setup with trusted access, the risk is lower — but the point remains instructive: an "incomplete fix" is the most expensive bug, because everyone assumed it was handled.

SharePoint KEV deadline: expired yesterday — and to be taken with caution [12][13]

CISA had added the SharePoint deserialization flaw CVE-2026-45659 (CVSS 8.8) to the KEV catalog after confirmed active exploitation; the deadline for US federal agencies expired yesterday (07/04).[12]

KEV catalog: The US cyber agency CISA's "Known Exploited Vulnerabilities" list is a most-wanted list for flaws that are demonstrably already being attacked. If a flaw is on it, US federal agencies must patch it within a deadline. For us, the list is a strong prioritization signal: what's on it is being exploited in the wild — not just in theory.

The flaw lets an already authenticated attacker execute code via deserialization of untrusted data (vector AV:N/AC:L/PR:L). Affected are SharePoint Enterprise Server 2016, Server 2019, and the Subscription Edition below the respective fix builds.[13]

Sourcing note The specific 07/04 KEV deadline here rests on only one secondary source (The Hacker News, citing CISA BOD 26-04) — the CISA catalog page itself was unreachable (403) at the time of retrieval. The NVD publication date (05/22) and the CVSS score are primarily confirmed; the exact deadline date is not. So it's presented here with attribution, not as a hard agency commitment.

For self-hosters (not a US federal agency), the deadline carries no legal weight anyway — but an actively exploited SharePoint RCE is a reason to check your patch status regardless of the date.

Standing thread — EU: the CRA countdown is running [14]

Not a this-week deadline, but the thread shaping the coming months: from 09/11/2026, the first hard obligations of the EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) kick in. Manufacturers and developers of "networked software" distributed in the EU must report actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents to ENISA and the relevant national authority within 24 hours.[14] Full CRA compliance then follows by 12/11/2027.

CRA (Cyber Resilience Act): Like a mandatory ingredient list for software. Anyone selling a product with digital elements in the EU will have to answer for security across its lifecycle — and report incidents the way a restaurant reports food poisoning. The 24-hour deadline is the first part that goes live.

For solo developers and small shops, it's worth clarifying now whether and how you fall within scope — reporting channels should be in place before the real incident, not after.
