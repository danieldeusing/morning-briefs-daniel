Microsoft 365 gets more expensive — as of July 1

On July 1, Microsoft raised the list prices of its commercial Microsoft 365 plans.[1] The increases are tiered: Business Basic +16% ($6 → $7 per user/month), Business Standard +12% ($12.50 → $14), E3 +8% ($36 → $39), and E5 +5% ($57 → $60). The Frontline plans get hit hardest: F1 +33% ($2.25 → $3), F3 +25% ($8 → $10).

Microsoft justifies this with new features bundled in — more Defender, Copilot Chat, expanded Intune. For you as a customer, that means the bill goes up regardless of whether you touch the extra features.

Timing Existing customers stay on the old price until their next contract renewal after July 1.[2] If you have a renewal date on the calendar, check now which tier will hit — and whether down-tiering (Standard instead of E3, if the E3 extras go mostly unused) can offset the increase.

"Exploitarium": 100+ zero-days, no warning [3][4]

An anonymous researcher going by "bikini" has published a GitHub repo called Exploitarium — over 100 unpatched proof-of-concept exploits for flaws in 22 to 24 open-source projects, with zero vendor notification.[3][4] Affected projects include Firefox, Docker Engine, PHP, 7-Zip, FFmpeg, VLC, AnyDesk, libssh2, Gitea, OpenVPN, nmap, and ImageMagick.

Zero-day & "no vendor notification": A zero-day is a flaw that goes public before the vendor has a patch — defenders get "zero days" of head start. Normally you'd quietly report this to the vendor first (responsible disclosure), wait for the fix, then publish. Here that step was skipped: everything is out in the open before anyone had a chance to fix it.

The situation: as of July 2, 2026, about 13 of the flaws have received a CVE — starting with CVE-2026-55200 in libssh2, plus CVE-2026-58049 through -58058 as well as -58592 and -58593, spanning FFmpeg, 7-Zip, Gitea, MyBB, nghttp2, RustDesk, Flowise, nmap, Ladybird, and NodeBB.[4] The remaining findings still have neither a CVE nor a CVSS score, which makes triage and automation harder. And: two findings have been independently confirmed as high-risk, with observed active exploitation — the pre-auth heap write in libssh2 and an auth bypass in Gitea under default Docker configuration.[3]

Methodologically interesting: the researcher says they used an AI model to fuzz the code, but stresses the PoCs are "almost all hand-written."[4] They frame the whole thing as a recruiting/education effort — "to lure people into the field." Whether you read that as a contribution or as arson, anyone running libssh2 or an exposed Gitea instance (especially in a default Docker setup) should check now, specifically.

Ubiquiti UniFi: CVSS 10.0 with no login [5]

Ubiquiti's security advisory Bulletin 066 (published July 3) closes 25 vulnerabilities across UniFi Access, Connect, Network, Protect, Talk, and UniFi OS.[5] The headliner: CVE-2026-50746 in UniFi Connect, a command injection with the maximum score of CVSS 10.0 — exploitable over mere network access, with no authentication.

Command injection: An app passes input straight through to the operating system's shell without filtering it. It's like handing your order to a waiter, and if you say "Pizza; and open the register," they do both. The attacker smuggles their own commands into a field that was only supposed to hold data.

Affected are older version branches (including Access ≤ 4.2.28, Connect ≤ 3.4.16, Network ≤ 10.3.58, Protect ≤ 7.1.77). Since most of these flaws need only network access and no login, a UniFi panel reachable directly from the internet is the real risk — deploy the update and don't leave management interfaces exposed to the open internet.

SharePoint RCE lands in CISA KEV [6][7]

CISA has added CVE-2026-45659 — a remote code execution flaw in Microsoft SharePoint Server, CVSS 8.8 — to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog; added July 1, federal remediation deadline July 4.[6] The root cause is deserialization of untrusted data.

CISA KEV: A catalog maintained by the US cyber agency listing flaws proven to be actively exploited in the wild — not just theoretically dangerous. US federal agencies must patch these within a set deadline. For everyone else, a KEV entry is the most honest prioritization signal there is: "this one is being attacked right now."

The low bar is what makes this alarming: exploitable by an authenticated attacker with only "Site Member" rights — no admin role needed.[7] In most SharePoint deployments, half the workforce already has at least Site Member access, which drastically lowers the practical entry bar for an insider or a compromised account. The fix already shipped as an out-of-band update in late May; anyone who hasn't applied it yet needs to act now. Affected are SharePoint Server Subscription Edition as well as Server 2019 and 2016 — SharePoint Online (Microsoft 365) isn't affected in the same way, as a hosted service.

Chrome 150: 382 fixes — again

Google updated Chrome to 150.0.7871.46/.47 (Win/Mac), patching 382 vulnerabilities on July 1, 15 of them rated Critical; Google itself found 358.[8] Named explicitly is a sandbox escape (CVE-2026-13789, use-after-free in the GPU, High): an attacker who has already compromised the renderer process could break out of the sandbox via a crafted HTML page.

This is the second giant round in a row — the previous one (Chrome 149) closed 33 defects.[8] The fact that 358 of the 382 findings came from Google's own team is reassuring rather than alarming: the lion's share was found internally, not exploited in the wild first. For you: just restart the browser so the update takes effect — Chrome downloads it in the background but only activates the patch after a restart. No panic mode, but with an active GPU sandbox flaw, restarting today rather than next week is worth it. The shared Chromium core also affects Edge, Brave, and others — they typically catch up within days.

GitHub reliability: a trend worth watching

No new incident this week, but a pattern worth flagging: an analysis of GitHub's outage history shows reliability declining since December 2025 — the worst bug was a merge-queue regression on April 23 that rolled back already-merged PRs and affected 2,092 pull requests.[13] For anyone whose CI/CD depends on GitHub Actions and merge queue, that's an argument for not leaving critical merges entirely to automation, and for keeping a manual fallback ready.

Standing thread: EU Cyber Resilience Act

No single-day event, but a deadline getting closer: the Article 14 reporting obligation of the EU Cyber Resilience Act becomes mandatory on September 11, 2026 — from then on, manufacturers must report actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents.[14] In parallel, several ETSI/CEN/CENELEC draft standards (covering browsers, password managers, antivirus, VPNs, SIEM) have reached "mature draft" status. Anyone shipping software into the EU should set up the reporting process now, not in September.

It's Monday, July sixth. July first was a busy day — and the ripple effects are still rolling in. Today is mostly about cleanup.

Existing customers only pay more starting at their next contract renewal — so it's worth checking your calendar now for when a contract expires.

The bigger stir is around a repository called Exploitarium. An anonymous researcher published more than a hundred unpatched exploits, for flaws in roughly two dozen open-source projects — Firefox, Docker, PHP, libssh2, Gitea, and more. And without giving the vendors any warning. As of July second, about thirteen of the flaws have gotten an official identifier, starting with the one in libssh2. The rest still don't have one, and two findings have been independently confirmed as high-risk, with observed active exploitation: a memory bug in libssh2 and a login bypass in Gitea under default Docker configuration. Anyone running one of those should take a close look.

On top of that, three patch waves landed almost on the same day. Ubiquiti's Bulletin sixty-six closed twenty-five flaws in its UniFi products, including one with the maximum score of ten point zero — exploitable over mere network access, with no login at all. The US cyber agency CISA added an actively exploited SharePoint flaw to its catalog; here, an attacker only needs a basic member role. And Google's Chrome one-fifty patched a staggering three hundred eighty-two vulnerabilities, including a sandbox escape. So go ahead and restart your browser.

On the side: Angular shipped two point releases, nothing urgent. PostgreSQL nineteen is out as a beta, while version fourteen loses support in November. And in Brazil, Nubank is ending five years of full remote work and moving back to a hybrid model.

One date to mark down: on September eleventh, the reporting obligation of the EU Cyber Resilience Act becomes mandatory. Anyone shipping software into the EU should set up that process now. Tomorrow should be quieter — we'll be watching for stragglers from this week's patch waves.
