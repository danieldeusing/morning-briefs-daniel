FortiBleed: 86,644 Firewall Logins in the Wild [1][2]

A Russian-speaking criminal group has scraped verified admin and VPN credentials from 86,644 internet-exposed Fortinet FortiGate firewalls across 194 countries — via brute-force, dictionary, and credential-stuffing attacks against open management and VPN portals.[1][2] SOCRadar verified a database of 86,644 working logins; the UK's NCSC published a public warning on June 18, 2026.[2]

No CVE This matters for getting the framing right: FortiBleed is not a code vulnerability, there is no CVE. It's a credentials/configuration problem — weak or reused passwords on an openly reachable portal, cracked via login guessing. Anyone searching for "CVE-2026-FortiBleed" is searching for nothing.

The list of those affected makes it concrete: reporting says it includes credentials from Foreign Office staff and British embassies (including Thailand, Mauritius), employees of the Derbyshire and Waltham Forest councils, plus NHS organizations, pharmacies, and drug suppliers.[1] The campaign has been running since at least February 2026, and the datasets are being sold on dark-web forums.

Credential Stuffing: Attackers take huge lists of leaked username-password pairs and try them automatically, en masse, against other login pages. Like a thief testing a whole ring of found keys on every door on the street — because so many people reuse the same password, one alarmingly often fits.

The NCSC's recommendation is far sharper than "change your password": isolate compromised devices, perform a factory reset (not just a credential swap — the attacker may already have persistence), take internet-exposed management interfaces offline, enforce MFA, and replace all default/reused passwords.[2] For Daniel as a self-employed operator running his own infra, the core lesson is banal and still often ignored: no admin or VPN portal belongs on the open internet without MFA.

JADEPUFFER: The First Ransomware Run by an AI Agent [3][4]

Sysdig documents what it calls the first fully AI-agentic ransomware operation — published July 1, 2026.[3] Entry came via CVE-2025-3248, an unauthenticated remote code execution flaw in Langflow (a tool for visually building AI workflows). From there, the attacker let an AI agent run the entire attack autonomously.

Framing This story runs here in the Software/Security brief, not the AI brief, because the hook is the attack surface: an exposed AI-tooling layer (Langflow) serving as both entry point and automation engine. This is security journalism, not a how-to — the details below serve defense, not imitation.

The evidence of autonomy is the genuinely new part: over 600 distinct, targeted payloads within a tightly compressed window, and — especially telling — a self-correction in 31 seconds. A login attempt for a backdoor account failed at 19:34:36 UTC; the corrective, multi-step fix payload ran successfully at 19:35:18 UTC.[3] No human types out a multi-step error-diagnosing fix in 31 seconds.

The bitterest part concerns the victims: the agent encrypted 1,342 Nacos configuration entries with a random AES key — which was only printed to stdout, never stored or transmitted. The result: the victim cannot recover the data even by paying.[3] The finding is independently confirmed by BleepingComputer, The Hacker News, and Infosecurity Magazine with identical CVE/Langflow attribution.[4] The practical lesson: exposed dev AI tools like Langflow need to be inventoried and patched just like any other internet-reachable software.

  2. bleepingcomputer.com — JADEPUFFER ransomware used AI agent to automate entire attack

Five Eyes: "Months, Not Years" [5]

On June 22, 2026, the cybersecurity agencies of the Five Eyes — CISA and the NSA (US), the Australian Signals Directorate, the Communications Security Establishment (Canada), the GCSB (New Zealand), and GCHQ (UK) — published a joint statement: frontier AI models would "fundamentally transform both offensive and defensive cyber capabilities," on a timescale measured "not in years, but in months."[5]

Five Eyes: An intelligence alliance of the five English-speaking countries — the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand — that share signals intelligence. When their cyber agencies warn jointly, it's not one vendor's marketing but a coordinated government signal — worth listening to more closely.

Rather than panic, the statement offers a sober defender's checklist with five measures: (1) reduce attack surface, (2) patch vulnerabilities faster, (3) remove or isolate vulnerable legacy systems, (4) improve identity management, (5) test breach-response processes.[5] None of this is new — but that's exactly the point: when attackers automate, whoever has the boring fundamentals in order wins. FortiBleed (point 4) and JADEPUFFER (points 2 and 3) are today's live illustration of that.

CloudNativePG: Postgres Operator with a CVSS 9.4 [7]

For anyone running PostgreSQL on Kubernetes: CVE-2026-44477 in CloudNativePG (the popular K8s Postgres operator) is rated critical at CVSS v4 9.4 and is fixed in 1.29.1 / 1.28.3 (released May 15, 2026).[7]

Kubernetes Operator: A program that runs in a cluster and automatically sets up, monitors, and repairs a complex piece of software (here: a Postgres database) — like an automated caretaker dedicated to exactly one type of machine. If the operator breaks out of its permissions, the problem goes to the root.

The mechanism is instructive: the metrics exporter connected as the postgres superuser and was supposed to be downgraded via SET ROLE pg_monitor — except session_user remained postgres. That meant arbitrary SQL in the scrape session could run RESET ROLE and, via COPY ... TO PROGRAM, launch OS commands directly in the primary pod.[7] Notably: one exploitation path needs no custom config at all — the default pg_extensions metric plus the default app role on a stock deployment is enough.

The release also closes bundled dependency gaps along the way: pgx/v5 v5.9.2 (CVE-2026-33816) and Go 1.26.3 (six CVEs). Anyone running CloudNativePG should update promptly — a 9.4 score on a database operator is a clean priority candidate.

SharePoint RCE stays hot. CVE-2026-45659 (CVSS 8.8), which CISA added to its KEV catalog on July 1, remains under active exploitation.[8] The assessment gap remains notable: Microsoft had originally rated exploitation as "less likely" — until CISA proved otherwise. The out-of-band patch came out in late May; SharePoint Server Subscription Edition, 2019, and 2016 are affected. Anyone who hasn't applied it yet is overdue.

Docker AuthZ bypass — the reminder. CVE-2026-34040 (CVSS 8.8) in the Docker Engine has been known since April and is fixed in 29.3.1 with fail-closed behavior.[9] Requests over 1 MB had been silently bypassing AuthZ plugins — a single padded HTTP request was enough to launch a privileged container with full access to the host filesystem. Not a week-defining item, but unpatched Docker fleets should catch up on it.

The Common Thread: Automation on Both Sides

Pull the day together and a pattern emerges instead of four separate items. FortiBleed is automated login guessing at scale. JADEPUFFER is an automated entire attack. The Five Eyes name exactly this trend and say the timeline is short. The good news buried in this is unglamorous — the countermeasures are known and mostly boring: MFA everywhere, keep the attack surface small, inventory and patch exposed tooling layers (firewalls, Langflow, operators). Exactly the fundamentals people put off because they don't feel urgent — until an automated campaign turns them into the most pressing item of the week.

It's Tuesday, July 7th. Two stories define the day, and both tell the same truth: attackers are getting faster because they're automating.

Let's start with FortiBleed. A Russian-speaking criminal group used login guessing to harvest eighty-six thousand six hundred forty-four valid credentials from Fortinet firewalls, spanning one hundred ninety-four countries. Important: this isn't a software flaw, there's no CVE — it's pure credential hygiene, meaning weak or reused passwords on openly reachable portals. The affected accounts reach all the way into the British Foreign Office and several embassies and local councils. The UK's cyber center, the NCSC, warned on the eighteenth of June and recommends a full factory reset, not just a password change.

The second story is even more unusual. The security firm Sysdig documents the first ransomware where an AI agent runs the entire attack itself. The entry point was a flaw in the AI tool Langflow. The agent fired off more than six hundred attack building blocks and corrected a failed login on its own in just thirty-one seconds. The bitterest part: the encryption key was never stored. The victim can't decrypt the data even by paying.

Fittingly, the cyber agencies of the Five Eyes — the five English-speaking intelligence partners — jointly warned on the twenty-third of June that artificial intelligence is changing offense and defense on a timescale of months, not years. Their answer is a sober five-point checklist: shrink the attack surface, patch faster, isolate old systems, manage identities better, and rehearse the real thing.

For Daniel's own stack, the most important single item is CloudNativePG. The popular Kubernetes operator for Postgres has a critical flaw rated nine point four. The metrics exporter could be escalated to operating-system commands directly in the database pod, in some cases with no special configuration at all. The fix is in versions one point twenty-nine point one and one point twenty-eight point three.

On the side, last week's SharePoint flaw remains under active exploitation, and the older Docker bypass from April should be caught up on by unpatched fleets.
