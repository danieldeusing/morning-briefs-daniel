GitHub: when the public API becomes a corporate spy

The most interesting security story of the week isn't a zero-day, it's a method. Datadog Security Labs has documented a campaign running since October 2025 that systematically scrapes GitHub's public API to map corporate organizations and their repositories. Over 50 "ghost" accounts across multiple user agents have been confirmed; the activity runs in one- to three-week bursts and hits many companies at once, mostly via the GraphQL endpoint. The user agents in use carry telling names like GitHub-Company-Scraper, GitHubAnalytics/1.5, or GitHarvester.

Ghost-account reconnaissance: Picture a burglar who strolls unremarkably down the street for months before striking — nobody remembers his face. That's exactly what these accounts do: per Datadog, they're two to five years old before being activated for scraping. "An account with a multi-year history looks more legitimate than one that was registered the same week it starts scraping," says Datadog engineer Julie Agnes Sparks. Public repo metadata is harmless on its own — pieced together, it maps your whole company.

Two details push this beyond plain metadata collection. First, a parallel sub-campaign with stolen tokens: between late December 2025 and early January 2026, dozens of different legitimate GitHub accounts (compromised OAuth/PAT tokens) hit a single organization within minutes of each other. Second — and this is the part that stings — a user agent named repo-dumper ran git clones and API calls inside a private repository. That's no longer enumeration, that's confirmed exfiltration.

Who's affected and what to do: Any organization with public repos or public-facing metadata provides reconnaissance material — nothing stops that, that's the point of "public." The real lever is the tokens: inventory PATs and OAuth grants, cut them down to least privilege, rotate old/broad tokens, turn on secret scanning, and monitor unusual API bursts. The CSO Online analysis frames the whole thing as a mix of scanner tools, opportunistic credential abuse, and coordinated burner networks — not a single actor.

Sources: securitylabs.datadoghq.com · csoonline.com

GitHub's countermove: secret scanning for the whole public surface

Fittingly timed with the enumeration story, GitHub pushed a defensive feature into public preview on July 1: "Secret Scanning Public Monitoring for Enterprises." Instead of only checking its own repos, GitHub now monitors "the entire public surface of github.com in real time for leaked secrets" and attributes a find to the affected enterprise. Free for customers with GitHub Secret Protection. This closes exactly the gap the token sub-campaign above exploits: a secret that leaks from a private context into any public repo gets caught before a repo-dumper can use it.

Source: github.blog

Nextcloud GmbH: 367,000 internal records sat exposed on the internet

Important up front, so the headline isn't misread: this is not a bug in the Nextcloud software and not a hacked customer instance. What was affected was Nextcloud GmbH's own internal IT. According to deskmodder, the company had an internal database with around 367,000 records temporarily freely reachable from the internet. The publicly open Elasticsearch cluster amounted to nearly eight gigabytes.

Elasticsearch exposed on the internet: Elasticsearch is a search database — convenient because it's queryable over HTTP. That's exactly what becomes the trap when the port faces the internet without authentication: like putting the filing cabinet out on the sidewalk and leaving the drawer open. A classic misconfiguration, not an exploit.

Cybernews security researchers discovered the incident on May 18, 2026; after being notified, Nextcloud GmbH closed access within two days, and since May 27 the data has no longer been publicly reachable. The contents were sensitive: around 71,000 PDFs, 53,000 PNGs, and 23,000 Markdown files, including invoices, contracts, internal emails, employee data, and scripts for customer projects — per Cybernews, some with embedded database credentials. The lesson is unspectacular, and that's exactly what makes it frustrating: search clusters belong behind auth and on the internal network, full stop. (Cybernews' original report returned a 403 at fetch time; the figures here come from the German-language write-up.)

Source: deskmodder.de

CISA KEV: four new additions — deadline expires today

The most time-critical item today: for four flaws that CISA added to the KEV catalog on July 7, the patch deadline for US federal agencies ends on July 10 — that's today. The quartet is led by CVE-2026-48282, a path traversal flaw in Adobe ColdFusion that, per securityaffairs, was exploited within hours of disclosure. Add CVE-2026-48908 (JoomShaper SP Page Builder, unrestricted file upload), CVE-2026-55255 (Langflow, authorization bypass via a user-controlled key), and CVE-2026-56290 (Joomlack Page Builder, broken access control) — all for active exploitation.

KEV catalog: The "Known Exploited Vulnerabilities" list maintained by the US agency CISA — flaws with evidence that they're currently being actively exploited. For US federal agencies, that's a hard patch deadline; for everyone else, a very clear "fix this one first." Being on the KEV means: no longer theoretical.

Langflow is a builder for LLM workflows — but the news here is the CVE, not the AI feature; the tooling layer is attack surface too. Anyone running ColdFusion shouldn't put off the patch: a path traversal flaw already being exploited in the wild is the most uncomfortable combination there is. (The CISA alert page returned a 403 on direct fetch; the four CVEs and today's deadline are cross-checked against securityaffairs.)

Source: securityaffairs.com

SharePoint on-prem RCE (CVSS 8.8) on the KEV catalog

A week earlier, CISA had set the tougher anchor: CVE-2026-45659, a remote code execution flaw in SharePoint on-prem with a CVSS base score of 8.8 (high). Mechanism per NVD: deserialization of untrusted data allows an authenticated attacker to execute code over the network. Only on-prem is affected — SharePoint Server Subscription Edition, 2019, and Enterprise 2016; Microsoft had already shipped an out-of-cycle patch in May. CISA added the flaw to the KEV catalog on July 1, with a deadline of July 4 for federal agencies. Anyone still running an on-prem farm who hasn't applied the May patch should catch up — "authenticated" isn't a big hurdle on an internal collaboration server.

Source: nvd.nist.gov

Hoymiles inverters broadcast their password in plain text

A finding from the CCC crowd that reaches beyond the pure software world but is a textbook design sin. CCC researcher "Hunz" (Benedikt Heinz) showed that Hoymiles microinverters of the HM/HMS/HMT series exchange their radio access password — the last eight digits of the serial number — unencrypted over the 868 MHz and 2.4 GHz frequency bands. Hoymiles says it serves around 20% of the European microinverter market, so the problem is widespread.

Microinverter: The small box behind each individual solar panel that converts the panel's DC power into grid-compatible AC. It's configured over radio — and that exact radio channel is here practically unlocked.

The demo shows just how practical that is: a modified handheld scanner was enough to locate two dozen strangers' inverters, complete with their unique IDs, within 20 minutes. Since the radio range spans several hundred meters, such attack hardware could easily be strapped to a drone according to heise — which would let an attacker disrupt the feed-in of entire neighborhoods. Manufacturer contact has gone poorly: CCC and Germany's BSI are said to have tried warning Hoymiles since February 2026, with no patch so far (this part of the timeline rests on secondary reports, not a directly verified primary source). For those affected, there's little to adjust in the short term — RF hardening isn't a firmware update you just install. Realistically: awareness, manufacturer pressure, and, when in doubt, keeping the affected devices on the watch list.

Source: heise.de

Chrome: 382 fixes in one go, 15 rated critical

At the end of the month (June 30), Google shipped a stable update that's hard to overlook: 382 security fixes, 15 of them rated "Critical," in version 150.0.7871.46/.47 (Win/Mac) and .46 (Linux). The standout is CVE-2026-13789, a use-after-free in the GPU component that, via crafted HTML, can potentially lead to a sandbox escape.

Sandbox escape: The browser locks every web page into an isolated process — the sandbox — so it can't reach the rest of the system. A sandbox escape is breaking out of that cell: code that should only run inside the tab suddenly reaches the operating system. That's why GPU and rendering bugs specifically are so touchy.

A plain auto-update is enough here — but check that it actually went through instead of assuming it did. On July 8 another stable update for desktop followed; solid detail notes on it weren't available at the time of writing.

Source: malwarebytes.com

npm v12 locks down install scripts — the answer to the supply-chain year

The most relevant release of the month for anyone with a package.json: npm v12 is expected in July 2026 per the GitHub changelog and turns up default security significantly. Specifically: "npm install will no longer run preinstall, install, or postinstall scripts from dependencies unless explicitly allowed." On top of that, git dependencies (only with --allow-git) and remote-URL dependencies like HTTPS tarballs (--allow-remote) are blocked by default.

This is the direct response to the pattern behind this year's npm supply-chain attacks: a postinstall hook that fires the moment someone types npm install. The collateral damage is limited — only a small share of packages need install scripts at all (typically native modules via node-gyp). For Daniel's CI, that means: before upgrading, go through which deps actually need scripts and set an allow list — then nothing breaks in July.

Source: github.blog

OpenSSH 10.4: eight fixes and a taste of post-quantum

OpenSSH 10.4 (aka 10.4p1) shipped on July 6 and brings eight security-relevant fixes. The two most interesting ones concern file transfer: on an SFTP download via the command line (sftp host:/path .), a malicious server could redirect the file to an unexpected location; on a server-to-server SCP copy, a malicious server could write into the destination's parent directory. Add an sshd bug where long command lines for internal-sftp were silently truncated after the ninth argument — potentially dropping security-relevant options — and a client-side use-after-free.

Post-quantum signature: Today's signature schemes are considered breakable once powerful quantum computers exist. A composite scheme combines a classical one (Ed25519) with a quantum-resistant one (ML-DSA-44): the signature holds as long as at least one of the two schemes is secure — a seatbelt with a backup buckle.

That's exactly what 10.4 offers as experimental, opt-in support: a combined ML-DSA-44-plus-Ed25519 signature scheme. Not production-ready yet, but a clear signal of where things are headed. For Daniel's Linux/SSH routine, 10.4 is an unremarkable "grab it."

Source: openssh.org

Git 2.55: faster repacks and a `git history fixup`

A bit older (June 29), but worth catching up on: Git 2.55. Per GitHub's release post, the highlights are incremental repacking of the multi-pack index (faster maintenance for large repos), a new git history fixup, parallel hook execution, and batching for partial clone. Pure performance and ergonomics improvements, no security urgency — but git history fixup and the parallel hooks are the kind of quality-of-life you quickly won't want to live without.

Source: github.blog

Ubuntu: live kernel patching on Arm64 — a rollback on Rust coreutils

Canonical has extended its live kernel patching — security fixes applied to a running kernel without a reboot — to Arm64, both in regular Ubuntu 26.04 and in immutable Ubuntu Core 26 (The Register, July 9). A real operational win for Arm servers and edge devices. The Rust rewrite of coreutils went less smoothly: the Rust cp mishandled the -L flag, so the team has reverted to the classic C-based GNU cp for now. The goal of "100% Rust coreutils" isn't off the table, but it moves to Ubuntu 26.10 — a healthy sign that a regression actually triggers a rollback instead of being waved through.

On July 7, the UK Court of Appeal dismissed Microsoft's appeal against ValueLicensing: the reseller is allowed to resell used software licenses. Standing thread — EU CRA: reporting duty from September 11

The security week also shows why the next regulatory milestone is no paper tiger: from September 11, 2026, the reporting duty under Article 14 of the EU Cyber Resilience Act kicks in — a 24-hour early warning, a 72-hour report, and a follow-up report for actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents. Fines run up to €15 million or 2.5% of worldwide annual revenue. Every one of today's events — from the KEV SharePoint flaw to the open cluster — would fall under this clock going forward. The date deliberately isn't on the 7-day timeline (it's still two months out), but it belongs on the roadmap of every product with an EU market now. (Deadline via law-firm analysis, date solid; detailed interpretation still evolving.)

Source: crowell.com

It's Friday, July tenth. There's no single spectacular bug today, but there is a pattern: reconnaissance and negligence. I'm working through a security week that shows how much you can learn about a company without ever breaking in — and how often the door was left open anyway. And, as it happens, a patch deadline expires today too.

The core story comes from Datadog Security Labs. Since October of last year, a coordinated campaign has been scraping GitHub's public programming interface to map corporate organizations and their repositories. More than fifty so-called ghost accounts have been confirmed. The devious part: these accounts are two to five years old before they're activated — a long history simply looks more legitimate than an account registered the same week it starts scraping.

Two things push this beyond plain data collection. Between late December and early January, dozens of real but stolen GitHub accounts hit a single organization within minutes of each other. And a tool called repo-dumper demonstrably cloned data out of a private repository. This is no longer theory, this is confirmed exfiltration. The defensive lever sits with the tokens: inventory them, trim them down to the essentials, rotate the old ones. Fittingly, GitHub pushed a countermeasure into preview on the first of July that searches the entire public surface of github.com in real time for leaked secrets.

The second big story is a data leak at Nextcloud GmbH. Important: this is not a flaw in the Nextcloud software and not a hacked customer instance, but the company's own internal database. Around three hundred sixty-seven thousand records, nearly eight gigabytes, sat exposed on the internet through a misconfigured Elasticsearch search database. Invoices, contracts, internal emails, employee data. Discovered in May, closed within two days. The lesson is mundane, and that's exactly what makes it frustrating: search clusters belong behind authentication, full stop.

From the hardware world comes a finding from the Chaos Computer Club that shows just how bad radio security can get. A range of several hundred meters even makes a drone attack conceivable, one that could disrupt the power feed of entire neighborhoods. For four flaws that the US agency CISA classified as actively exploited on the seventh of July, the federal agencies' patch deadline expires today. The quartet is led by a path traversal flaw in Adobe ColdFusion that was exploited within hours of disclosure. A week earlier, a SharePoint flaw already took center stage: a code execution bug with a severity of eight point eight, only in the on-premise versions.

Chrome shipped three hundred eighty-two security fixes in one go, fifteen of them critical — the standout is a GPU bug with sandbox-escape potential.
