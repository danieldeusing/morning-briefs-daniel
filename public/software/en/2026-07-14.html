CISA lists a Cisco bug from 2008 — and that's the real story

Today's freshest hard-news item is a bit of curious software archaeology: on 07/13, the US agency CISA added the vulnerability CVE-2008-4128 to its catalog of actively exploited flaws. The number in the name gives away its age — 2008 is the year the CVE was assigned. It's a cross-site request forgery in the HTTP admin interface of Cisco IOS 12.4, specifically on the Cisco 871 ISR — a branch-office router from the mid-2000s that's been past end-of-life for ages.

CSRF (Cross-Site Request Forgery): If you're logged into a web interface — say, your router's config page — and open a crafted page in another tab, that page can send a command to the router on your behalf. The router sees your valid session and executes it. It's like a stranger slipping you a pre-filled wire transfer form while your online banking is still open — the bank just sees that you're logged in.

Why does an 18-year-old bug land in the catalog in 2026? CISA adds a CVE precisely when there's fresh evidence of active exploitation. So the signal isn't "here's a new flaw," but "this ancient flaw is being exploited again" — meaning enough of these long-discontinued Cisco boxes are still hanging open on the internet that automated scanning pays off. Botnets hunting for forgotten edge hardware are an old story; that a 2008-era CSRF is being retriggered specifically is a reminder that "end-of-life" doesn't mean "offline."

For Daniel's own stack this is practically irrelevant — nobody runs an 871 ISR voluntarily. The general lesson stands: the most dangerous device on the network is often not the one with the newest flaw, but the one nobody's thought about in years. Added under Binding Operational Directive 22-01, which triggers a mandatory patch or decommission deadline for US federal agencies (per SC Media).

KEV Catalog: CISA's "Known Exploited Vulnerabilities" list — flaws with evidence they're actually being exploited right now. For US federal agencies it's a hard deadline; for everyone else it's a very clear "fix this one first." Being on the KEV means it's no longer theoretical.

The rest of the KEV week

Elsewhere the catalog kept its usual pace. On 07/10, CVE-2026-48939 (iCagenda) and CVE-2026-56291 (Balbooa Forms) were added — both again the familiar Joomla/web-builder pattern of insecure file uploads. RoguePlanet finally gets its fix (CVE-2026-50656)

A neatly closed thread: the Defender privilege-escalation flaw RoguePlanet, which was still listed here as an open issue on 07/05 ("Microsoft still investigating"), has since been patched. Microsoft closed it on 07/09 — the fixed Malware Protection Engine 1.1.26060.3008 rolls out automatically via the normal definitions-update channel, and explicitly no action is required (per Help Net Security). The flaw was disclosed publicly on 06/10 — hours after the June Patch Tuesday, with no vendor fix.

Local Privilege Escalation (LPE): An attacker who already has some access on the machine uses it to elevate themselves to system rights. RoguePlanet made this especially bitter by abusing the antivirus software itself — through a faulty link resolution (symlink/junction) where Defender, while cleaning up a file, follows a planted reference and operates on the wrong target with its own very high privileges. The guard becomes the accomplice.

Context: this isn't a remote-access scenario — an attacker already needs a foot in the door. But building blocks like this are exactly what turns a harmless phishing click into a full takeover. The genuinely good part of the story is the quiet delivery path: because the fix lives in the Defender engine rather than the Windows kernel, it's already reached most systems without a reboot or a Patch Tuesday cycle. Time from public disclosure to fix: just under a month.

Source: helpnetsecurity.com

Frontend & releases: Angular 22.0.6

Angular 22.0.6 shipped on 07/08 — a pure maintenance patch for the v22 line, alongside the backports 21.2.18 and 20.3.26. No urgency. The real jump was the June major (v22, 06/03), which brought Signal Forms, the accessibility toolkit Angular Aria (twelve UI patterns), and the reactive resource/httpResource APIs to production readiness. Anyone still on v21 will find the usual path in the migration docs — but nothing's on fire.

Where the big weekly threads stand

To keep the picture complete without repeating myself here: the npm v12 hardening (install scripts off by default, Git/remote deps require a flag) has been rolling out since 07/08 and was covered in detail in the 07/12 brief. No fresh developments on any of these today.

NIS2: BSI extends deadline to 07/31

The next hard deadline for German mid-sized businesses: the BSI has set the NIS2 registration deadline to 2026-07-31 (pushed back from the original March date). Affected are medium and large companies (roughly 50+ employees or €10 million in revenue) across 18 critical sectors; failure to register risks fines of up to €500,000 (per solidaris.de). Just over two weeks left — tight enough to handle it now.

EU regulation: CRA reporting obligation approaching

Deliberately outside the 7-day window, but on the radar: the Cyber Resilience Act brings the 24-hour early warning and 72-hour full report requirement for actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents starting 09/11/2026. For anyone selling software with "digital elements" in the EU, that's the next structural deadline — still about eight weeks out.

Source: solidaris.de
