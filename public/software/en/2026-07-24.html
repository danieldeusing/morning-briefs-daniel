RefluXFS: a nine-year dormant root bug in the XFS filesystem

On July 22, Qualys disclosed a flaw in the Linux kernel's XFS filesystem that a local, unprivileged user can exploit to gain full root — CVE-2026-64600, CVSS 7.8. The bug is a race condition in XFS's copy-on-write path and has been in the tree since Linux 4.11 (February 2017) — just under nine years.

Reflink / Copy-on-Write: XFS can "copy" a file without actually duplicating the data — both names initially point to the same blocks, and only when one of them writes does the affected part get truly copied. Like a shared document that only splits apart once someone starts typing. A race condition means two operations access the same state at the same time, and whoever gets there first wins — the attacker deliberately wins that race.

Qualys estimates 16.4 million potentially affected systems worldwide — RHEL, Oracle Linux, Amazon Linux, and Fedora Server, everywhere XFS is the default root, which in practice means roughly half the enterprise Linux fleet. The kernel fix was merged into the tree on July 16, and distributions are currently backporting it.

No remote attack, no worm — this is the classic second stage: whoever already has a foot in the door (a compromised web package, phishing, a container escape) can use it to lever their way into the kernel. For Daniel's Docker hosts running on XFS, that means concretely: schedule the kernel update as soon as the distro backport lands. Urgent, but not a middle-of-the-night alert.

Five Eyes: Russian state actor has been emptying Zimbra mailboxes for a year

On July 23, CISA and the Five Eyes partners published the joint advisory AA26-204A: a Russia-attributed actor — tracked as either "Laundry Bear" or "Void Blizzard," depending on the source — has been exploiting a Zimbra flaw since at least July 2025 to spy on government, defense, education, and energy targets.

At the center is CVE-2025-66376, a zero-click XSS in Zimbra Collaboration's Classic UI (CVSS 7.2; CNA, NVD 6.1), already patched in November 2025 (10.0.18 / 10.1.13). Whoever didn't apply it back then is being harvested now: the exploit exfiltrates the last 90 days of email plus the entire Global Address List.

Zero-click XSS & GAL: "Zero-click" means the victim doesn't have to click anything — merely viewing a crafted email runs the attacker's code inside the webmail client. The "Global Address List" is the organization's central address book: whoever copies it instantly has every name, every role, every email address in the building.

The lesson is old and uncomfortable: a patched CVE isn't actually handled until it's actually deployed. Nine months of delay is enough to turn a "medium" flaw into a year of full access. Anyone running self-hosted Zimbra should check the version today — not the changelog line.

Malvertising via a trusted domain: "FakeAgent" slips in a RAT

On July 23, Huntress documented a campaign that shows just how little a "trusted" download host means these days. The attackers ran Bing search ads for a supposed "Claude Desktop" download — the twist: the ad led to a fake download page, published as a public artifact on the real claude.ai domain (valid TLS certificate, borrowed reputation). From there, a redirect funneled visitors to external attacker domains (claude.ai.download-app[.]us → downloading-api.it[.]com), which only then served the malicious ClaudeDesktop.exe carrying the infostealer/remote-access trojan SectopRAT. The binary itself was never hosted on claude.ai.

Malvertising & RAT: Malvertising is malware distribution through paid advertising — the top "hit" is an ad, not a genuine search result. A RAT (Remote Access Trojan) is a remote-control tool: once installed, the attacker essentially types alongside you at your keyboard — passwords, files, sessions.

7,100 downloads and at least 29 compromised organizations on July 21–22, before Anthropic removed the artifact. The defense doesn't lie in checking the domain, but in the binary's signature and provenance and in ad-network hygiene: always get official installers via the vendor link, never via the top ad.

Scope note This is a security and supply-chain story, not a model story — the model itself is irrelevant. The real pattern: platforms that host arbitrary user content under their own trusted domain (artifact hosters, Gist/Pages, CDN buckets) become the ideal bait-and-redirect point for a malware chain — the domain reputation is borrowed, the TLS padlock is genuine, and the actual payload sits elsewhere. KEV catalog: six newly, actively exploited flaws in two days

On July 21 and 22, CISA added six entries to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog — the mandatory patch calendar for US federal agencies, and also the best short list of what's actually being exploited right now.

  • July 21 — CVE-2026-0770 (Langflow), CVE-2026-63030 & CVE-2026-60137 (WordPress Core, including a SQL injection), plus a DD-WRT router flaw.
  • July 22 — CVE-2026-16232 (Check Point SmartConsole) and CVE-2026-50522 (SharePoint deserialization, CVSS 9.8). For the latter, the government deadline runs out on Saturday, July 25 — today is the last business day before it.

For self-hosters, what matters most are the WordPress Core fixes (Core, not just a plugin — so broad reach) and, for those running it, SharePoint on-prem. The rest is enterprise-appliance territory.

Three outages, one lesson: trust isn't availability

The past week brought three prominent outages — and in the two that matter for developers, the cause wasn't an attack but an operational failure in precisely the layer that's supposed to be responsible for stability.

GitHub Actions: an expired certificate takes down runners

From July 19, 23:05 UTC to July 20, 03:55 UTC (about 4h50m), self-hosted and "larger" runners couldn't connect to GitHub; standard and Mac runners were unaffected. Per the status page, the cause was an expired internal SSL certificate — a certificate-lifecycle lapse, fixed by rotation. For anyone with their own runner pool (Daniel's CI included), this is the classic self-inflicted outage.

AWS CloudFront: a Frankfurt AZ takes down the world

On July 16, CloudFront went down worldwide for about 3.5 hours (00:45–04:18 PDT) with elevated 5xx errors; affected services included Canvas, Blackboard, Hugging Face, and the UK National Lottery. The trigger was a fault in the VPC origins control plane, originating from a single Availability Zone in Frankfurt (euc1-az2) whose configuration wasn't correctly propagated across the global fleet.

Control plane vs. data plane: The data plane delivers the actual data (your file from the CDN). The control plane just manages the rules — who's allowed where, which config applies. Like waiters and the shift schedule: if the schedule fails, the waiters end up standing still even though the kitchen is running.

Meta: down worldwide, cause open

On July 22, around 13:00 GMT, Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp were disrupted or unreachable for millions of users in Europe, the UK, and the US. Meta hasn't published an official post-mortem; the theories circulating (DNS/BGP/config) are explicitly speculation. Mentioned here for completeness — the dev-relevant lesson is in the first two.

SAP & the EU Commission: ten years of loosened on-premise shackles

Somewhat lost among the security headlines, but relevant for the German enterprise world: on July 9, the EU Commission closed its competition investigation into SAP — with commitments that bind SAP worldwide for ten years. Customers with on-premise licenses get more room to breathe: free choice of third-party support, cancellation of unused "shelfware" licenses without a reinstatement fee, and capped back-payments when returning to maintenance (at most six months).

The catch: the commitments apply exclusively to on-premise — SAP's cloud business stays untouched. So this is less a change of course than a concession at the tail end: more freedom exactly where SAP is pushing customers toward the cloud anyway.
