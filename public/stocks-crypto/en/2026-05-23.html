Big Tech & the AI Trade

The quiet sensation of the week is Alphabet [Stocks]. While everyone stared at Nvidia, Google's parent climbed to number two in the world: market value $4.60T (as of 05/22) — just ahead of Apple ($4.54T) and now behind only Nvidia ($5.22T). The rest of the Mag-7: Microsoft $3.11T, Amazon $2.86T, Tesla $1.60T, Meta $1.55T. Together the seven are worth about $23.5T — roughly a third of the entire S&P 500. Alphabet is the group's YTD winner, Microsoft the laggard. The market is visibly rotating from the "picks & shovels" (chips) to the "users" (cloud, search AI, advertising) — the companies where AI investment already turns into revenue today.

"Picks & shovels": In the gold rush it wasn't the prospectors who got rich, but the people who sold shovels and pickaxes. Applied to AI: Nvidia sells the "shovels" (chips). But eventually what counts is whether the prospectors — Google, Microsoft & Co. — actually strike gold with those shovels, i.e. generate revenue. That's exactly where the money is moving now.

Nvidia delivered — and was sold anyway [Stocks]. Wednesday evening (05/20) brought the Q1-FY27 numbers: revenue $81.6B (+85% YoY, consensus ~$78.8B), non-GAAP EPS $1.87, data center $75.2B, gross margin 74.9%. The Q2 guidance of $91B ±2% came in well above the ~$86.8B consensus — and that's without a China contribution. Add a dividend raised 25-fold and a new $80B buyback program. And yet the stock fell about 1.5% after the print. Analysts, by contrast, lifted their price targets (Cantor to $350, Wells Fargo to $315, KeyBanc to $300).

"Priced in": When everyone expects a good result, they buy the stock beforehand — so the price rises before the news. Once the good news lands, the "fuel" is spent, and some sell to lock in gains. That's how a stock can fall on top-tier numbers. That's exactly what has happened to Nvidia several times running now.

Commodities — Oil & Gold

Oil ends the week in the red [Commodity]. Brent settled Friday at $103.54 (+$0.96 on the day, but >5% down on the week), WTI at $96.60. The driver remains Iran diplomacy: early in the week the price fell hard after the U.S. called off an imminent strike in favor of negotiations. On Friday Brent briefly spiked to ~$106 when Reuters reported Iran's leadership wants to keep its near-weapons-grade uranium in-country — then the spike faded again. U.S. Secretary of State Rubio tempered things: "some progress … but we're not there yet." The Strait of Hormuz remains blocked.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil is shipped. Picture the only bridge over which every bread truck in your city drives — block it and the bread isn't gone immediately, but everyone hoards out of fear, and the price shoots up. A peace hope lets the hoarding — and the premium — melt away again.

Recommendation — Oil exposure

Verdict: HOLD / deep dips only — the risk premium is unwinding, and last week's easy buy case has weakened.

Why: As long as Hormuz is unstable there's a floor. But the market is pricing a resolution; Goldman is sticking to its Q4 Brent target of $90, with strait normalization by end-June as its base case. Buying into strength here means potentially buying into a shrinking premium. Only a clear pullback — or a failure of the deal — makes energy clearly attractive again.

Timeframe: No fresh entry into a geopolitical recovery spike. Wait for the Iran negotiation outcome (mediation runs over the weekend) and the EIA inventory report on 05/28. Accumulate only if the deal collapses and the price escalates toward $115+ (then momentum) or on genuine normalization toward $85–90 (then value).

Forecast: Base (50%): the deal path holds, Brent glides toward $90–95 by Q4 (Goldman). Bull-for-oil (25%): the deal collapses, supply outage → $115+. Bear (25%): rapid full normalization of the strait → back to $85.

Evidence: Brent $103.54 / WTI $96.60 (settle 05/22), week >5% down; Friday spike to ~$106 (Reuters/uranium); Hormuz still blocked; Goldman Q4 target $90. cnbc · te brent

Gold consolidates at a high level [Commodity]. Spot ~$4,515/oz (05/22, −0.6% on the day) — the second straight weekly loss. Silver gave back more ($75.35, −1.7%). The reason for the near-term pressure: expensive oil stokes inflation worries, which raises the odds of a Fed rate hike in 2026 — and higher rates are poison for a yield-free asset. The structural driver, however, stays intact: central-bank diversification away from the dollar, fiscal-deficit risk, geopolitics. The big houses stay bullish — Goldman reaffirms $5,400; JPMorgan sees ~$5,000 by year-end (Q4 average $5,055).

Real yield: The yield after subtracting inflation. The 10-year U.S. note yields about 2.16% in real terms (as of 05/21). When the real yield rises, gold (which pays no interest) becomes relatively less attractive — when it falls, gold shines. Right now high real yields keep a lid on it, while central-bank buying builds a floor: a tug-of-war that holds the price sideways at a high level.

Spot-ETF outflows: A Bitcoin ETF is an exchange-traded pot that holds real bitcoin so you can buy it like a stock. When money flows out, the pot's manager has to sell real bitcoin — that's direct selling pressure, not just sentiment. Four negative days in a row means real coins are moving onto the market.

To the stocks: U.S. exchanges went into the long weekend at record highs on Friday. The quiet sensation of the week, though, is Alphabet, Google's parent: with a market value of 4.6 trillion dollars it has climbed to the most valuable company in the world after Nvidia, overtaking Apple. And yet the stock fell, because the good news had long been priced in. My take: this very weakness is the opportunity. The biggest worry — a cooldown of the AI boom — is off the table, but the price is no more expensive. I'd scale into Nvidia on red days.

The background headwind for everything risky is the new central-bank chief Kevin Warsh, sworn in on Friday. He's seen as a hawk who's thinking more about a rate hike than a cut. For the meeting in mid-June, the markets put a 97 percent chance on rates staying unchanged.
