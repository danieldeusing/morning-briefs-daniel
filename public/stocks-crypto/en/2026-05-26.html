Big Tech & the AI trade

Open again — and in risk-on [Stocks]. After the Memorial Day holiday, US markets reopen today. They start frozen at Friday's record (22.05): the Dow closed at an all-time high of 50,579.70, the broad S&P 500 (7,473.47) booked its eighth winning week in a row — the longest streak since late 2023 — and the Nasdaq stood at 26,343.97. Pre-market, the market points up: S&P 500 futures trade +0.5%, the Japanese Nikkei marked an all-time high overnight, and the 10-year yield has fallen to 4.50% (from 4.57% on Friday) — falling rates are a tailwind for tech. The question of the day: does the record mood carry into the first session since Friday, or does the look ahead to Thursday's PCE print put on the brakes?

The quiet sensation remains Alphabet [Stocks]. Google's parent is still the world's number two: market value $4.60tn (as of 22.05) — ahead of Apple ($4.54tn) and only behind Nvidia ($5.22tn). The rest of the Mag 7: Microsoft $3.11tn, Amazon $2.86tn, Tesla $1.60tn, Meta $1.55tn. Together the seven weigh roughly $23.5tn — crudely a third of the entire S&P 500. The market is visibly rotating from the "picks & shovels" (chips) to the "users" (cloud, search AI, advertising) — the firms where AI investment already turns into revenue today.

"Picks & shovels": In the gold rush it wasn't the prospectors who got rich, but the ones who sold the shovels and pickaxes. Applied to AI: Nvidia sells the "shovels" (chips). But at some point what counts is whether the prospectors — Google, Microsoft & Co. — actually find gold with those shovels, i.e. generate revenue. That's exactly where the money is moving right now.

Nvidia delivered — and was sold off anyway [Stocks]. The Q1-FY27 figures came on Wednesday evening (20.05): revenue $81.6bn (+85% vs. prior year), data center $75.2bn (+92%), gross margin 74.9%, EPS $1.87 (non-GAAP, above the $1.78 consensus). The Q2 guidance of $91bn was well above consensus — and that without a meaningful China contribution. On top of that, a raised dividend ($0.25/share, record date 26.06) and a new $80bn buyback program. And yet the stock fell: it closed Friday (22.05) at $215.33, −1.9% versus the prior day. Analysts, by contrast, lifted their price targets.

"Priced in": When everyone expects a good result, they buy the stock beforehand — so the price rises before the news. When the good news then arrives, the "fuel" is spent, and some sell to take profits. That's how a stock can fall on top-tier numbers. That's exactly what just happened to Nvidia (up +13.7% going into the print).

Recommendation — Nvidia (NVDA)

Verdict: BUY the dip — the fundamental uncertainty is smaller after the numbers, but the price isn't higher.

Why: Before Wednesday the setup was binary — now the central question ("is the AI boom slowing?") is answered by the $91bn guidance. That the stock fell anyway is positioning (priced in, up +13.7% beforehand), not fundamentals. The leverage remains: ~80% market share in AI accelerators, the Blackwell ramp, the $80bn buyback that supports the price. For a 6–12 month horizon, a weak phase with confirmed fundamentals is the better entry window than a euphoria spike.

Timeframe: Accumulate in tranches on red days over the next 4–8 weeks. Concrete risk dates: the PCE print on 28.05 and the first FOMC under Warsh on 16./17.06 — both can press tech lower again via yields and deliver better prices. Hold until the Q2 confirmation in July.

Forecast: Base (55%): stock consolidates ±5% around the current $5.2tn valuation ($215) until the market hunts for the next story. Bull (30%): yields fall further (falling oil, dovish PCE), rotation into quality → new highs, +10–15% over 6 months. Bear (15%): hot PCE + a hawkish Warsh presses the whole Mag 7 → −10 to −15%.

Evidence: Q1 revenue $81.6bn / EPS $1.87 non-GAAP / DC $75.2bn; Q2 guidance $91bn; stock $215.33 at the 22.05 close (−1.9% on the day, despite the beat); cap $5.22tn. 8-K (StockTitan) · companiesmarketcap

The flip side of concentration [Stocks]: That seven stocks carry a third of the index remains the real danger. If the AI narrative tips, the S&P 500 loses its support. Nvidia's Q2 confirmation in July and the broader Big Tech earnings season are the next real litmus test for the thesis "AI spending turns into revenue." Until then, the rotation into Alphabet & Co. is the healthier signal: the market is seeking AI winners with real cash flow, not just the story.

Commodities — oil & gold

Oil reverses higher — the "deal" frays [Commodity]. On Monday oil still fell ~5.5% on the deal hope (Brent below $98), but this morning it stands firmer again: Brent ~$99, WTI ~$92.6. Two things braked the slide. First: the US/Iran framework to reopen Hormuz is still unsigned — the announcement expected for Monday never came, the Iranians have not yet agreed, and the demining of the strait has not begun. Second: on Monday the US flew fresh "self-defense" strikes in southern Iran (near Bandar Abbas) against boats trying to lay mines and against missile positions; Iran threatens retaliation. The ceasefire in force since 08.04 formally stands, but is being tested. On the week, Brent is nonetheless down over 10% — the price now oscillates between deal hope and escalation risk.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil is shipped. Picture the only bridge over which all the bread trucks drive into your town — if it's blocked, the bread isn't gone immediately, but everyone hoards out of fear and the price shoots up. A hope of peace melts the premium away; fresh strikes bring it straight back.

Recommendation — oil exposure

Verdict: NEUTRAL / don't chase — the price is a pure headline bet as long as the framework is neither signed nor collapsed.

Why: Two weeks ago oil was a buy on the Hormuz premium (>$115). That's largely gone — and yet the price turns higher again on the fresh strikes. This is exactly the two-sided situation in which you do not chase: bet on further de-escalation and the next strike catches you; buy for escalation and the next diplomacy headline does. Energy only becomes clearly attractive again once the binary question is decided — signing (then further down toward $80–85) or collapse (then up over $115).

Timeframe: No new entry until a dated trigger brings clarity: the EIA inventory report on 28.05 (12:00 ET) and above all a concrete signal from the Iran talks (signing vs. failure). Accumulate only if the framework collapses and the price escalates toward $115+ (then momentum), or on real normalization toward $80–85 (then value).

Forecast: Base (55%): a stalemate — Brent oscillates $92–102, headline by headline, with no clear direction. Bull-for-oil (25%): framework collapses / supply outage → $115+. Bear (20%): signing + demining begins → toward $80–85.

Evidence: Brent ~$99 / WTI ~$92.6 (26.05), firmer again after Monday's −5.5% slide; on the week Brent over −10%; framework unsigned, fresh US strikes near Hormuz on 25.05, demining not begun. te brent · axios

In the risk-on reopen with falling yields, capital flows temporarily out of the safe haven — a mirror of yesterday's move, when gold still rose on the Iran uncertainty. Real interest rate: The rate after subtracting inflation. When it rises, gold (which pays no interest) becomes relatively less attractive — when it falls, gold shines. Right now the high US real rates (10Y nominal yield 4.50%) keep a lid on it, while central-bank buying builds the floor: a tug of war that holds the price sideways.

Spot ETF outflows: A Bitcoin ETF is an exchange-traded pot that holds real Bitcoin so you can buy them like a stock. When money flows out, the pot's manager has to sell real Bitcoin — that's direct selling pressure, not just sentiment. Six negative days in a row means real coins are moving onto the market. If the bounce turns, the ETF flow reversal is the signal you wait for.
