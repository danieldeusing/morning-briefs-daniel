Today's story: one number decides the afternoon

The most important event of the week is still ahead: today at 14:30 BRT (08:30 ET), the US Department of Labor releases the major jobs report (NFP) for May. It's the arbiter of Tuesday's JOLTS shock — when job openings jumped to a 2-year high and brought "higher for longer" back to the table. Consensus for today is strikingly weak: the broad Dow Jones survey expects only ~80,000 new jobs, the tighter FactSet reading (6 estimates) expects ~105,000 median (range 50k–125k, Goldman 60k, EY-Parthenon 50k, Vanguard just 20k), unemployment rate 4.3% — unchanged.[1]

Why is this number the switch for all my buckets today? The US Federal Reserve cuts rates when the labor market weakens — and cheap money drives stocks, gold, and crypto. JOLTS on Tuesday said "the labor market is hot," the NFP today checks that. Imagine two report cards in the same week: the first was surprisingly good (parents hold back allowance). Today the second card comes — if it's bad, the first was an outlier and the allowance (rate cuts) comes anyway. That's exactly the tension the market is trading: a weak NFP would paradoxically be good for risk assets.

The frame for the day: if the NFP confirms strength (well above ~105k), "higher for longer" locks in until the FOMC on June 16–17, and gold/crypto/bonds stay under pressure. If it disappoints (toward the 50–60k estimates), JOLTS was an outlier and risk assets get relief.[1] Because the report comes after my deadline, all stock prices below are Thursday's close; the reaction comes this afternoon.

The macro/FX side (BRL, DXY, Fed path in detail) belongs to the economy brief — here only the tradable lever on stocks, commodities, and crypto counts.

Big Tech: one chip crash swaps the whole tape (as of Thu 06/04)

The US market hasn't traded today (Fri 06/05) at my deadline (~06:00 BRT) — the prices below are Thursday's close (06/04). Beneath the surface, a rotation ran yesterday like the year rarely sees. Trigger: Broadcom (AVGO) crashed −12.59% to $418.91 — and this despite the company beating earnings expectations. The AI-chip outlook disappointed, and that was enough.[2] Investors read it as a warning shot for the entire AI complex and yanked capital out of chips — into "boring" value plays.

The result was a split market: The Dow closed +874.86 points (+1.73%) on record (51,561.93), led by UnitedHealth (+5%), JPMorgan (+3%), and Walmart. The S&P 500 rose just +0.41% to 7,584.31, while the tech-heavy Nasdaq −0.09% to 26,830.96 fell. Health Care (+3.1%) and Financials (+2.7%) led — exactly the sectors left behind by AI euphoria so far.[3]

What is a "rotation" — and why does the Dow rise while the Nasdaq falls? A rotation is when investors don't exit the market, but shift money from one corner to another. Picture a table with two bowls: out of the "AI-chips" bowl (Nasdaq-heavy) and into the "solid-old-economy" bowl (Dow: insurers, banks). The table stays full — but one bowl empties (Nasdaq red), the other overflows (Dow on record). That's exactly what happened yesterday, triggered by Broadcom's shock.

Against the chip headwind, two heavyweights held their ground: Nvidia $218.66 (+1.94%) defied the Broadcom shadow (premarket 06/05 then −1.5% to $215.45), and Alphabet jumped +3.68% to $372.19 and nearly wiped out Tuesday's capital-raise shock — Berkshire's entry at these levels acts as a floor.[4]

Oil: the Iran premium crumbles — but +4% on the week

After early-week escalation spike, oil pulls back a bit: Brent ~$94.90 (−0.14% on the day), WTI ~$92.76 (−0.30%) (TradingEconomics, 06/05).[6] The reason: diplomacy hopes. Per TradingEconomics, expectations that the US and Iran might find a solution and keep the Strait of Hormuz open are weighing on the risk premium. Despite today's loss, Brent is +4% on the week — the premium is dampened, not gone. On Sunday (06/07), OPEC+ meets and decides the production response.

Why does oil fall on "diplomacy hopes" when there's no deal yet? The oil market doesn't trade today's barrel, but the odds of future supply disruptions. One-fifth of the world's oil runs through the Strait of Hormuz — the "only bridge." While rockets flew, everyone covered defensively (price high). Now that negotiations are being discussed, the perceived risk of a closure falls — and some panic-buying is reversed. It's the expectation that falls, not physical supply that rises.

Recommendation — Oil Exposure (Brent/WTI)

Verdict: HOLD — no new position; the trade is binary and the premium is priced in.

Why: Oil hinges this week on two unpredictable switches — Iran diplomacy and the OPEC+ decision on 06/07 — either of which can flip both ways. Anyone who bought the spike paid panic prices; anyone shorting the de-escalation now is betting against a possible re-escalation. ELI5: opening an oil position right between rocket strikes and peace talk is like betting on the next gust on a windy day — the direction changes before you've traded. Only go long on a confirmed physical outage (actual Hormuz closure), not on headlines.

Timeframe: No position before the OPEC+ meeting on 06/07 — that's where the production call happens. Go long only on a physical outage, go short only if a concrete Iran deal and an OPEC+ output increase both happen.

Forecast: Base (50%): Brent $88–$100, high volatility as long as Iran unresolved without physical tanker losses. Why do rate expectations hit crypto harder than anything else? Crypto yields no interest and pays no dividend — its only appeal is price gain. When safe bond yields rise, rate-free, volatile crypto becomes relatively less attractive: why take the risk if the government pays "risk-free" more? Picture two piggy banks — one (bonds) pays more now, the other (crypto) only promises "maybe a lot later." With rising safe yields, money migrates to the first.
