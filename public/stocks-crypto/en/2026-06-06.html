The story of the day: NFP has killed the hope

In my Friday brief, the central tension was: would a weak labor report save risk markets? The answer came yesterday afternoon — and it's No. The US nonfarm payroll report (NFP) for May showed +172,000 new jobs, more than double the ~88,000 consensus expected; unemployment remained at 4.3%, hourly wages rose +3.4% year-over-year.[1] A hot labor market amid stubborn inflation is the central bank's worst nightmare — and the market drew the radical conclusion: for the first time in months, it's not just pricing in "no cuts," but openly betting on a possible rate hike this year.[2]

Why is a good jobs report bad for stocks? Normally strong growth is great for equities. But right now the logic is inverted ("good news is bad news"): The Fed cuts rates only when the economy weakens — and cheap money drives stocks, gold, and crypto. A strong labor market tells the Fed: "You don't need to ease, maybe you even have to tighten." Imagine everyone was waiting for a tax cut because the company was struggling — then the company reports record orders. Great for the company, but the hoped-for tax cut is off the table. That's exactly what happened yesterday: strong economy, dashed money hope, falling prices.

The result was a broad selloff. The tech-heavy Nasdaq crashed −4.18% to 25,709.43 — the worst trading day since April 2025; the S&P 500 fell −2.64% to 7,383.74, the Dow −1.35% (−695.15 points) to 50,866.78.[2] Crucially: unlike Thursday (pure rotation, Dow hit a record), this time the broad market fell too — the correction has spread from chips across the board. Since today is Saturday and the market is closed, the next market reaction won't come until Monday; but crypto keeps trading and is already showing where sentiment is headed.

The macro/FX side (BRL, DXY, the precise Fed path) belongs to the economy brief — here only the tradeable leverage on stocks, commodities, and crypto counts.

Big Tech: rotation becomes an AI correction

On Thursday, the Broadcom shock was still a rotation — money flowed out of chips into value, the Dow even hit a record. On Friday, with the NFP behind us, it became a broad retreat. Rising bond yields (the result of the strong jobs report) hit the richest growth names hardest — so the heavyweights led the declines: Nvidia −6.20% to $205.10 (−$13.56, cap $4.97T), Broadcom −7.92% to $385.73 — continuing last week's crash.[3] NVDA has now fallen below its 52-week midpoint (range $138.83–$236.54) and sits ~13% off its high.

Why do rising bond yields hit tech the hardest? A tech stock is a bet on profits that mostly come years away. Those future earnings are "discounted" — the higher the safe interest rate today, the less a distant profit is worth today. Imagine you're promised a gift in ten years: if the bank is also paying 5% safe interest, that future gift is worth less to you than if it paid zero. So when the expected interest rate rises (like after the strong NFP), the value of the priciest future bets shrinks — and those are the AI chip names.

Gold: the hedge breaks to 2026 year low

The biggest single move of the day was gold — and it went down: ~$4,331/oz, −3.22% (−$143.89) to the lowest level of 2026 (TradingEconomics, June 6; cross-check LiteFinance $4,328.40 on June 6, spread ~0.06%).[5] Over the week, gold is ~−4% in the red. The driver is clear: a strong labor market lifts interest-rate expectations and thus real rates — and the non-yielding metal loses appeal precisely when safe bonds start paying again. Middle East uncertainty (Strait, Israel-Lebanon) does support inflation risk, but couldn't come close to offsetting the rate boost.

Why does gold fall when rates rise? Gold pays no interest and no dividend — it just sits there. Its appeal is insurance against inflation and crisis. But when the "real rate" (the rate minus inflation) rises, suddenly you're getting actual returns from a safe bond for doing nothing — and that makes non-yielding gold relatively unattractive. Picture two vaults: one (the bond) now pays rent for your money being in it, the other (gold) doesn't. When rent appears, money moves to the first vault. That's exactly what the strong NFP triggered.

Oil: diplomacy + China pressuring — but +3% on the week

Oil is giving back the escalation spike: Brent ~$92.87 (−2.27% on the day), down 8.3% on the month (TradingEconomics, June 6).[6] Two forces are pressing: Iran diplomacy is making headway (Trump: talks are "going well"), and China's crude imports have hit the lowest level in a decade — a clear demand warning. Against the pressure stands geopolitics: uncertainty over the Strait of Hormuz and the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire keep a risk premium, which is why Brent is +3% on the week despite today's decline. On Sunday (June 7), OPEC+ decides the supply response.

Why does "China imports less oil" push the global price down? China is the world's largest oil importer. When its refineries process and buy less, a huge chunk of demand disappears — and with supply constant, that pushes the price for everyone down. Imagine a farmers' market whose biggest regular customer suddenly buys half as much: the vendors have to cut prices to clear their stock. That's the demand signal weighing right now.
