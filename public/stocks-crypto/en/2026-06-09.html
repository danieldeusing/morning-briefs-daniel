Story of the day: the market is waiting — and CPI is the switch

Yesterday's feared scenario — the US opens into a minefield of NFP shock and fresh Middle East escalation — didn't happen. Monday held: Nasdaq +0.86% to 25,929.66, S&P 500 +0.3% (7,405.73), carried by a semiconductor rally (Micron, down −13% on Friday, back around +10%; NVDA +1.73% after a memory partnership with SK Hynix).[1] Two weekend pressures have reversed simultaneously: Middle East tensions eased, and NFP jitters were partially digested. This isn't a boom signal — the rebound only clawed back part of Friday's plunge (−4.18%) — but the "minefield" is disarmed.

Instead, the market trades in wait mode today, and the reason is dated: Tomorrow, Wednesday 06/10, 08:30 ET, the BLS releases May CPI — the Consumer Price Index, the last major inflation print before the FOMC (06/16–17).[2] This one number decides which way the NFP debate resolves: if CPI confirms hot inflation, hike bets return and pressure on growth stocks and crypto rises; if it comes cool, it undercuts the NFP and we get the relief rally. Building big new positions ahead of such a number is coin-flip risk — that's why the sideways action across all buckets today.

Why is CPI the deciding number, not last week's NFP? CPI (Consumer Price Index) measures how much consumer prices have risen — inflation directly. NFP (the jobs report) only measures how many jobs were added; a strong labor market could fuel inflation, but doesn't prove it. The Fed steers on inflation, not jobs alone. Think of NFP saying "the kitchen is getting hot" — CPI is the thermometer reading whether the food is actually burning. Until the thermometer is read, no one keeps cooking and no one turns it down: everyone waits for the number. That's exactly what the market does today — holding its breath until tomorrow morning the thermometer shows.

My actual domain isn't the macro tape itself (the Fed path, BRL, DXY belong in the economy brief), but the tradeable leverage: which position survives both outcomes of the CPI coin flip best? That's the question guiding the calls below — and the answer is almost everywhere: don't trade big before this number.

The macro picture (Fed path, FX, yields) and the shooting war are the economy brief's domain; here only the tradeable stock/commodity/crypto leverage counts.

More important than the day-arrows is what hasn't improved structurally: Fear & Greed stays at 16 ("Extreme Fear"), and across the week BTC is nearly double-digit in the red.[4] On the flow side, a tender pulse: the record spot-Bitcoin-ETF outflow streak ran 13 days (05/15–06/03, cumulative −$4.33B / 59,351 BTC), ended 06/04, 06/05 brought the first net inflow (+$3.05M), and ETH-ETFs broke their 17-day outflow run with +$19.3M (entirely from BlackRock's ETHA).[4] This is a turn hint, not confirmation — single green days don't reverse a week-long series.

Why are ETF flows the more important signal than the day price? A spot-Bitcoin ETF is an exchange-traded fund holding real Bitcoin; when someone buys shares, the fund must buy Bitcoin, when they sell, it must dump some. Daily inflows and outflows show whether big institutional buyers are entering or exiting — unlike the day price, which can be short-term noise. Imagine a pool with an inlet and drain: as long as more flows out the bottom than comes in the top, the level drops, no matter how many ripples crease the surface. 13 consecutive outflow days means: the structural buyer was absent for weeks — the first green days in early June are just a sign the drain is turning, not a confirmed rising level.

Oil: War premium is gone — Brent back to ~$92.5

The oil market gives today the clearest proof yesterday's advice was right: don't chase headline spikes. Brent ~$92.48 (−1.97% day, −11.25% month), WTI parallel weaker — the entire Monday spike to around $97 is unwound (TradingEconomics, 06/09).[5] The reasoning is two-part: first, Middle East tensions eased overnight, so the fear premium evaporates. Second, structural headwinds are cutting through — the confirmed OPEC+ production step (+188,000 b/d from July, next meeting 07/05) and weak China demand are pushing the price back below month-open without the war bump.

Why does a war premium disappear so fast? A "geopolitics premium" is the markup the oil market pays for fear of a supply outage — not an actual one. As long as no tanker and no rig actually go offline, this premium is just a bet on escalation. When tensions ease, the bet collapses and price reverts to true supply-demand facts (here: more OPEC+ oil, weak China demand). Think of insurance against a storm that doesn't come: the moment the weather forecast clears, no one pays the high price anymore. That's what happened to oil in 24 hours.

Gold: the tell turns — insurance finds its floor

Yesterday gold was the "tell of the day" because it didn't rise despite war. Today that tell turns: Gold ~$4,327.52/oz, day +0.24% (+$10.56), month −8.62% — in the black for the first time in days, away from the year low (TradingEconomics, 06/09).[6] That's a small, but meaningful step: as soon as real-rate fears from the NFP ease — and Monday's rebound hints the market is partly taking back hike fear — the floor for zero-yield metal returns. That confirms yesterday's thesis: a year low on pure rate scare was the opportunity, not the break.

Why does slightly lower rate fear lift gold instantly? Gold pays no interest; its competitor is the safe bond paying real yields. "Real yield" is yield minus inflation — the true value gain. When real yields rise, money flows from zero-yield gold into bonds; when expectations fall, it reverses and gold rises. Imagine two piggy banks: one spits out coins (the bond), one stays the same weight (gold). Once the market believes the coins will be fewer (yields won't rise as much), the same-weight piggy becomes attractive again. That expectation has shifted slightly since Monday — and gold responds instantly.

Think of a restaurant opening in the middle of a slowdown: if it's packed anyway, that's a strong sign people are ready to spend again; if empty, it confirms the downturn. A $1.77 trillion debut in the middle of an AI correction is exactly that full-or-empty test — of the overall risk appetite, not just SpaceX.
