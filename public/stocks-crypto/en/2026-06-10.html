The story of the day: the market unloads risk — the CPI is the trigger

Yesterday, the market traded in calm waiting mode. Today that flips to active risk-off: overnight, fast hands are trimming across all buckets before the number comes. Gold down 2.17%, BTC down 2.5%, ETH down 3.2%, Brent sliding further — that's no coincidence, that's one bet flowing through all zero-coupon and risky assets simultaneously. The trigger has a date: Today, Wednesday June 10, 08:30 ET, the BLS releases the May CPI — the last big inflation number before the FOMC (June 16–17).[1] The consensus is hot: +0.5% month-over-month / ~4.2% year-over-year on the headline, +0.3% / ~2.9% on core — after 3.8% in April, the fastest year-over-year pace in nearly two years, and an April PPI that came in +6% annualized (energy +22.7%) signaling that companies are passing costs through.[1]

Why is the market selling before the number instead of waiting? The CPI (Consumer Price Index) measures inflation directly. If the market expects a hot number, it also expects the Fed to keep rates high for longer — and high rates are poison for anything that pays no interest itself (gold, crypto) or whose value sits far in the future (growth stocks). Imagine a class that knows a hard test is coming: the cautious ones hand in their risky cheat sheets beforehand, rather than waiting for the teacher to catch them. That's exactly what the market is doing this morning — it's handing in the risk before the number might punish it. If the test then comes easier than feared (cooler CPI), the hand-in was overdone and there's a relief rally; if it comes hard, the hand-in was smart.

My lane isn't the macro path itself (the exact Fed course, BRL, DXY, the war situation — that's the economy brief's job), but the tradeable lever: which position survives both outcomes of the CPI coin best? The answer is the same across almost all buckets — don't buy big before the number, but use the weakness in tranches where the long-term thesis holds (gold). That's the thread on which the recommendations below hang.

The macro picture (Fed path, FX, yields) and the political warfare belong to the economy brief; only the tradeable lever on stocks, commodities, and crypto counts here.

Gold: the floor breaks again — and that's exactly the next tranche

Gold had a small up day yesterday and seemed to be finding a floor. Today that reverses instantly: Gold ~$4,168.63/oz, day −2.17% (−$94.04), month −11.97% — yesterday's floor is gone, the metal is falling back toward the year-low (TradingEconomics, as of June 10).[2] The reason is exactly the CPI mechanics from above: a hot inflation expectation drives real-rate anxiety, and rising real rates pull money out of zero-coupon gold into the bond. This is no new fundamental break — it's the same expectation-driven rate reaction as after the NFP, just one notch sharper before the number.

Why does a hot inflation expectation press gold lower, when gold is supposed to be the classic inflation hedge? Over the long term, that's true: gold holds value when money loses purchasing power. In the short term, something else matters — the "real rate," the interest rate after subtracting inflation. If the market expects hot inflation and a Fed that keeps rates high because of it, then the real rate rises, and safe bonds become more attractive than zero-coupon gold. Think of two piggy banks: one spits out coins (the bond), one stays the same weight (gold). If the market suddenly believes the coins will be worth more (higher rates), the money migrates to the coin bank — short-term, until the Fed eventually has to ease. That's exactly the reflex you're seeing today, one day before the inflation number confirms or disproves it.

Whoever went long on every "Hormuz is closed" headline is sitting on losses after the last few days' pullbacks.

Why is oil falling when the world's most important oil chokepoint is blockaded? The Strait of Hormuz is the bottleneck through which roughly a quarter of seaborne oil flows. A blockade sounds like a mega-price driver — but two things brake it: first, the market has already priced in the fear premium and is reducing it again as a ceasefire offers hope. Second, the fundamentals (OPEC+ pumping more, China buying less) push the other way. Imagine a clogged garden hose while the faucet is being turned up further and the garden needs less water anyway: the pressure doesn't rise the way the clog would suggest. That's exactly the tug-of-war between fear premium and fundamentals keeping Brent under $92.

A fragile ceasefire between Iran and Israel, plus more OPEC oil and weak Chinese demand, press harder than war fears lift. Whoever went long on every headline is sitting on losses now. I'm staying on the sidelines — long fears the de-escalation, short fears the escalation.

Big Tech had a nervous, but directionless day yesterday. The Nasdaq fell just under one percent after Trump hinted at new Iran strikes, then recovered into the close. Nvidia and Google closed basically unchanged. For me, Google remains the better opportunity: it trades eighteen percent below analyst targets, and its own chips make it tough against a chip-shock that hits Nvidia. I'm holding Nvidia and buying Google gradually into the weakness.

After the Consumer Price Index comes the second switch: SpaceX goes public, final pricing Thursday after the close, trading starts Friday under ticker SPCX. At about one point seven-seven trillion dollars, it's the largest initial public offering of all time, more than three times Alibaba and above Tesla. If the debut goes well despite the backdrop, that's a strong signal for the whole AI complex; if it flops after a hot Consumer Price Index, it confirms the correction right before the Fed meeting. None of the three big private names are buyable for retail right now — the best lever on the AI thesis stays Google.

The sequence for the next few days is clear: this morning the inflation number, Thursday and Friday the SpaceX listing, mid-month the Fed decision.
