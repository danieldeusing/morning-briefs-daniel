Story of the day: the AI trillion-dollar trio surges to market simultaneously

Three of the largest values ever to come to or aiming for the US market are bunching into a single quarter — and today the first domino falls. SpaceX prices today after close at fixed price $135 (~$1.77 trillion) and launches 06/12; behind it stand the two largest private AI names marching in parallel toward confidential SEC filings: Anthropic filed a Draft S-1 on 06/01 — after a $65 billion Series H at ~$965 billion, which has overtaken OpenAI for the first time.[1] OpenAI confirmed its own confidential S-1 on 06/08–09 at ~$852 billion (after the $122 billion round in March), with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as underwriters; the listing window is Sep–Nov, but "nothing is fixed."[2] Both listings are at most in autumn — only SpaceX is tradeable starting 06/12.

What's a "confidential S-1" — and why does a pipeline of future IPOs press today's prices down? The S-1 is the mandatory document a company files with the SEC before it can go public — the official ticket in. "Confidential" means: only the SEC sees the numbers at first, not the public. But when three giants announce they're about to sell fresh stock, big investors have to set aside cash — and they grab it by selling elsewhere, often at the priciest Tech stocks. Picture a flea market where three mega-booths are opening at once: even before they set up, buyers shuffle their wallets and spend less elsewhere. That "offering rotation" is exactly what pushed the Nasdaq down yesterday — and that's why today's SpaceX oversubscription matters so much: it measures whether there's enough appetite for the whole wave.

The tradeable lever for Daniel is not the valuation of the private giants themselves (the macro picture of mega-raises belongs to the economy brief), but the read-through: None of the three is freely buyable for retail today. SpaceX becomes tradeable only from 06/12; Anthropic and OpenAI stay private until autumn. Anyone who wants to bet on the AI thesis can only do it through the already-listed infrastructure — and the question becomes: Which name best weathers the offering rotation? That's the thread on which the recommendations below hang.

Anthropic/OpenAI's AI product/model capabilities are the domain of the ai-dev/ai-usecases brief, the macro picture of mega-fundraises belongs to the economy brief; here only the tradeable market lever and sentiment read-through matter.

Single-name of the week: SpaceX (SPCX) — books ~2× oversubscribed, pricing today

The domino falling today has hard numbers: SpaceX prices today (06/11) after close at fixed price $135, ~555.6 million shares, ~$75 billion raise, target valuation ~$1.77 trillion — the largest IPO ever, more than three times Alibaba and above Tesla.[3] What matters is the demand side: order books closed on Mon 06/09, and demand is said to have hit around $150 billion — ~twice the $75 billion target, clearly oversubscribed.[4] Plus an unusually high retail quota of 30 % (vs. the single digits typical for deals this size) via Fidelity, Robinhood, and Schwab. The valuation is sporty — around 100× revenue (2025 $18.7 billion).[4]

What does "2× oversubscribed" mean, and why is that a test for the whole market? "Oversubscribed" means: more investors want to buy stock than there is to distribute. At $75 billion on offer and ~$150 billion in bids, on average everyone gets only half of what they wanted. That's a direct measure of risk appetite — you only buy a brand-new value rated at 100× revenue if you're optimistic. Picture a restaurant opening mid-slump that has twice as many reservations as seats on opening night: a strong signal people want to spend again. That's exactly what today's pricing day tests — and Friday's debut makes the result tradeable the same day.

Why it matters for Daniel SpaceX itself is only buyable from 06/12 — a direct buy on a 100× revenue debut is a high-risk bet, not core investing. The more valuable read is the throughline to the whole AI/Tech complex: if the oversubscribed debut on 06/12 runs well despite the nervous environment, that's a strong risk-on signal for NVDA/GOOGL and crypto core right before the FOMC; if it flops, it confirms the offering rotation and pushes sentiment further down. Also intriguing is the crosslink: SpaceX names Google (~$920 million/month) and Anthropic (~$1.25 billion/month) as major customers — the AI giants are already financing its revenue, so the trio props each other up.[4]

Gold: floor held — the mild CPI takes the edge off hike fears

After a painful month, gold holds the floor today: ~$4,085.40/oz, day +0.33 %, month −13.36 % — no new break, just stabilization near the year low (TradingEconomics, as of 06/11).[6] The reason is CPI mechanics from the other direction: because core CPI came below expectation (+0.2 % vs. +0.3 % month), real-rate fear eases, which had pressed the interest-less metal in May. The energy-driven headline jump (energy >60 % of the monthly rise) is exactly the kind of inflation the Fed tends to "see through" — that relieves gold.

Why does a mild core CPI support gold? In the near term, gold hinges on the "real rate" — the interest rate minus inflation. If the worry was the Fed must hold rates high longer because of hot inflation (which raises real rates and presses gold), then core CPI below expectation defuses that fear. Picture two piggy banks: one spits out coins (the bond), one stays the same weight (gold). If the market suddenly thinks coins won't come out more (rate fear drops), money stays with the gold pig. The headline jump comes almost entirely from energy — and energy inflation is the kind the Fed often treats as temporary, rather than fighting with rates.

Why does oil fall when the US president threatens new strikes against an oil state? A threat sounds like a price driver — but two things brake it: First, the market prices fear premium in and discounts it again as soon as no real supply break follows; worlds apart between threat and actual missing oil. Second, fundamentals (OPEC+ producing more, China buying less) press from the other side. Picture a garden hose someone threatens to step on while the tap is opened wider and the garden needs less water anyway: pressure doesn't rise the way the threat suggests. That tug-of-war is exactly what keeps Brent near $92.
