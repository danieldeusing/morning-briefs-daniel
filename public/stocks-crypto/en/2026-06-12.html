Story of the Day: Trump cancels Iran strikes — and the tape flips overnight

The power of a single headline was rarely more visible. On Wednesday, fear of US strikes against Iran had pushed the Dow below the 50,000 mark; last evening Trump wrote: "I have, as President of the United States of America, cancelled the scheduled strikes and bombings against Iran this evening" — and added that talks had been "brought to the highest level of Iranian leadership", venue and time "of the signing to be announced shortly".[1] The tape flipped instantly: Dow +1.8% (back above 50,000 to 50,848.75), S&P +1.7%, Nasdaq +2.5% — a classic relief bounce.[1] Beyond geopolitics, the inflation chapter of the week is also defused (May CPI came in-line, core came in below expectations), and the 06.16–06.17 FOMC stays in hold baseline.

How can cancelling a bombing lift stocks two percent — and crash oil four percent at the same time? Markets price not what is, but what they expect. For two days, investors had priced in a "war premium": higher risk, so out of stocks (risky) and into hedges like oil (an escalation in the Persian Gulf could choke supply and spike prices). When that premium vanishes, both flip — stocks get bought again, and the oil hedge becomes worthless because the feared supply risk disappears. Imagine a city expecting a storm: generators (oil) get expensive, cafés (stocks) empty. The weather service cancels the storm, the generator price crashes, and the cafés fill again. Exactly that happened today — except the "storm" hasn't officially been cancelled yet: the deal isn't signed.

The tradeable point for Daniel: This rally rests on a single, still-unsigned headline. Trump names a weekend for signing, but Tehran's formal sign-off is pending, and even a deal would need to clear mines from the Strait of Hormuz and repair damaged facilities before oil truly flows normally.[7] Translation: The risk-on is real, but fragile — it can flip just as fast with the next counter-headline. That's exactly why the recommendations below are keyed to staged moves and to the next dated event (FOMC, 06.16–06.17), not chasing the rally.

The geopolitical situation itself is the realm of economy/news context; here only the tradeable market read-through via stocks, oil, gold, and crypto counts.

Single-Name of the Week: SpaceX (SPCX) launches today — 4% float meets forced demand

The second domino of the AI/space wave falls today: SpaceX begins trading on Nasdaq under SPCX, priced last night at fixed price $135, ~$75B raise, valuation ~$1.75 trillion — the largest IPO ever, more than doubling the Aramco record ($29.4B, 2019).[3] What's actually tradeable isn't the headline, but the market mechanics: Only ~4% free float comes to market, and starting 06.13 (T+1) MSCI begins early index inclusion. Estimates suggest passive funds will hold ~30% of float within 15 trading days.[4] Bull/base/bear for the first 90 days (TradingKey): Week 1 base $140–175, month 1 base $130–165, 3 months $120–200.[3]

What does "4% float meets index forced buying" mean — and why is it an upside lever? The "float" is the share portion truly freely tradeable. At SpaceX it's only ~4% — the rest stays locked with SpaceX, employees, and early investors. So there's limited stock to trade. At the same time, index funds (which track an index like MSCI World) must buy the name once it's added — regardless of price, because their job is to track the index. When forced buyers meet scarce stock, it tends to push prices higher. Imagine an auction where only four of a hundred paintings are for sale — and a buyer is contractually forced to buy one: the price skyrockets. That's the bull thesis. The flip side: that same thin float makes the price extremely volatile in both directions — few sellers move it hard either way.

Why does oil crash when a war gets cancelled? An escalation in the Persian Gulf would have threatened oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz (the world's most critical oil choke point) — less supply, higher price. That risk was priced in as a "fear premium". A deal promising to keep Hormuz open removes the risk — and with it the premium. Imagine a city expecting gridlock on its only bridge into town; delivery services charge extra. The gridlock is cancelled, the surcharge vanishes instantly. Exactly that happened today: the "gridlock insurance" in the oil price became worthless.

Going long, meanwhile, fights intact structural downside (OPEC+ oversupply, China weakness) plus now-fading geopolitics.
