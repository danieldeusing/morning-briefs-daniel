Story of the Day: SpaceX delivers a textbook debut — the index mechanics come next

The largest IPO of all time delivered a nearly textbook debut yesterday: SpaceX (SPCX) opened at $150 (above the $135 fixed price), surged intraday to $176.52 (+31%) and closed orderly at $160.95 — +19%.[3] The firm raised ~$75B across over 555 million shares, with a valuation exceeding $2 trillion — more than double the Saudi Aramco record ($29.4B, 2019). Key point: The orderly, non-overextended move (no wild spike-and-fade) is itself the signal — the oversubscribed debut held up well in a jittery environment and broadly buttressed Friday's mood.

Why is an "orderly" 19% close better than a 40% spike that fades? In an IPO, a company sells fresh shares at a fixed price (here $135). If the stock rockets 40% on day one then falls back, it usually means: the fixed price was set too low, short-term traders piled in and out, and stability is absent. An orderly rise to +19% that holds through close signals instead real demand from buyers who plan to stay. Imagine two concerts: one erupts, then empties after three songs; the other fills steadily and stays full. The second is the healthier house. That's what SPCX was yesterday.

The truly tradeable lever hasn't kicked in yet — and here yesterday's assumption gets corrected: index forced-buys don't run "as of 06/13" but late June instead. MSCI adds SPCX to its Global Standard and Large-Cap indices on 06/29; Russell 1000/Top-200 on 06/26 (each after market close).[4] Only then must passive funds buy — into an even thinner float. Until then SPCX trades freely, without the mechanical support.

Recommendation — SpaceX (SPCX)

Verdict: WATCH / DON'T CHASE — don't mistake the strong debut as an entry signal; observe the index-mechanics effect from the sidelines and consider only as a small play-money tranche.

Why: The $160.95 close values SpaceX at roughly ~110× revenue (2025: $18.7B) — pure growth/story valuation, not a core investment. The orderly debut confirms real demand, but the structural upside support (MSCI/Russell forced-buys) doesn't kick in until 06/26–29; until then the thin float trades freely and thus extremely volatile. Buying now chases a +19% move without the mechanics at your back. The more valuable read for Daniel is the read-through: an oversubscribed mega-IPO running smoothly despite a jittery environment is a genuine risk-on signal for NVDA/GOOGL and crypto core — but it hinges on the same fragile Iran/CPI setup as everything else. ELI5: You don't buy the mini-float on euphoria day; you read what the debut tells you about the market's risk appetite.

Timeframe: The dated key event is index inclusion — Russell 06/26, MSCI 06/29 (passive forced-buys into thin float). Ahead of that, the FOMC (06/16–17) filters the macro direction. Earliest after debut volatility eases (≈ 1–2 weeks) and only as a small play-money position — not now on euphoria.

Macro Correction: May CPI was a 3-year high — and the oil crash becomes the disinflation hope

Yesterday's reading needs straightening. The May CPI (released 06/10) was not mild: the headline jumped to 4.2% yoy — the highest in three years, +0.5% mom.[2] The driver was almost wholly energy (+3.9% mom, +23.5% yoy, gasoline +40.5% yoy) — energy alone accounted for over 60% of the monthly rise. Yesterday was only correct on: the core CPI (ex-energy/food) stayed in line at 2.9% yoy. That's the real point: the inflation headline the market fears hinges almost entirely on oil — and oil is crashing right now.

Why is crashing oil good for stock sentiment when cheap oil hurts oil companies? Central banks set rates based on inflation. If inflation rises, higher or longer-high rates loom — and high rates poison stocks (especially growth). May CPI came in at 4.2%, almost entirely from expensive energy. If oil drops nearly 20% this month, next month's CPI energy component falls — headline inflation cools on its own without the Fed raising rates. Imagine a fever thermometer whose swing comes almost solely from a hot lamp beside it: turn off the lamp and the reading drops, even if the patient hasn't changed. Oil is the lamp. That's why stocks cheer the oil crash — it removes the Fed's argument for tightening.

The catch: both supports for this logic are not yet locked in. The oil crash rests on an Iran deal that isn't signed (Tehran calls reports "preliminary", nuclear stays separate).[2] And on 06/16–17 the FOMC decides: ~99% hold at 3.50–3.75% is set, but the risk is a hawkish-bias shift — facing a 4.2% headline, tone could shift from "cuts coming soon" to "neutral/tighter". It's the first meeting under Chair Warsh, raising uncertainty over tone. That's why the recommendations below anchor on the FOMC as a dated filter, not chasing Friday's rally.

The geopolitical situation itself belongs to the economy/news beat; here only the tradeable market read-through counts.

Oil: Brent ~$86.71 — the crash continues and becomes an inflation release valve

Brent ~$86.71 (day −4.06%, month −17.91%), WTI in parallel — last Friday print, lowest in nearly two months (TradingEconomics, 06/12).[7] Two forces pull the same direction: Iran-deal optimism obliterates the fear premium (an open Strait of Hormuz means more supply), and structurally OPEC+ oversupply and China weakness press down. The stocks-relevant side effect (see Macro section): exactly this energy weakness will cool the next CPI headline that spiked to 4.2% in May. But: nothing's signed — even a deal would first need to clear mines from the Strait before oil truly flows normally.

Why does oil crash when a war gets cancelled? Escalation at the Persian Gulf could have choked oil deliveries through the Strait of Hormuz (the world's tightest oil chokepoint) — less supply, higher price. That danger was priced in as a "fear premium". Now a deal promises Hormuz stays open, so the fear vanishes — and with it the premium. Imagine facing a traffic jam on the only bridge to the city; delivery services charge a rush fee. When the jam cancels, the fee disappears instantly.

Recommendation — Oil Exposure (Brent/WTI)

Verdict: HOLD — wait; don't short the crash or bet long on a bounce.

Why: The market has already priced out the fear premium — but on a still-unsigned deal. A short now bets the deal holds and Hormuz actually opens; risky because Tehran's formal sign-off is pending and the situation could flip in hours (then the premium snaps back). A long fights the intact structural headwind (OPEC+, China) plus fading geopolitics. Both sides fear the other's trump card — so you don't belong in this bet. ELI5: When a price swings four percent on a single unconfirmed headline, that's not a trend, it's a pendulum — you wait until it settles.

Timeframe: Wait for confirmation — either actual deal signature plus open Hormuz (then the structural short is cleaner) or a situation flip (premium snaps back). The next dated supply event is the OPEC+ meeting 07/05; until then day-to-day geopolitics around a possible weekend signature rules.

Forecast: Base (55%): Brent $82–90, structural headwind plus fading premium cap it as long as no flip occurs. Bear-for-oil / Downside (25%): < $80, if deal gets signed and Hormuz credibly opens — then OPEC+/China punch through (good for CPI, bad for oil longs). Bull-for-oil (20%): > $95, if Iran talks collapse and escalation fears snap back.

ETF flows show if big, slow buyers are entering or leaving — and they keep going out. Imagine a pool: wind ripples the surface upward (price), while poolside sentiment stays grumpy (fear index) and water drains below (ETF outflow). It's Saturday, June thirteenth. Stock and commodity markets are closed for the weekend, so all equity and oil readings are from Friday's close; only crypto trades around the clock. The week ended strongly in risk-on — but two of the assumptions propping up this good mood are thinner than the tape suggests.

SpaceX was the star of Friday. The largest IPO of all time delivered an almost textbook debut: opening at one hundred fifty dollars, intraday high nearly one hundred seventy-six, and a clean close around one hundred sixty-one dollars — nineteen percent above the offer price. The company is valued at over two trillion dollars. That the price rose orderly rather than spiking wildly then fading is itself the signal: real demand, not a trader spike. That lifted the indices — the Dow closed above fifty-one thousand.

For me, SpaceX means: don't chase it. The real lever comes late June when the big index providers are forced to add it — Russell on the twenty-sixth, MSCI on the twenty-ninth. That corrects what I said yesterday: the forced buying doesn't start today, it starts in two weeks. Until then the thin float trades freely and swings wildly. More valuable than buying is the read-through: that this mega-IPO ran so cleanly is a genuine risk-on signal for Nvidia, Google, and crypto.

The second, bigger correction is on inflation. Unlike what I said yesterday, May consumer prices weren't mild: the headline inflation jumped to four point two percent — the highest in three years. Almost all of that jump came from energy; gasoline cost over forty percent more than a year ago. Only core inflation ex-energy stayed in bounds. That makes one thing fascinating: the oil crash this week is now the key hope that inflation cools on its own. Brent fell four percent in a day and nearly eighteen percent for the month. Cheap oil presses exactly the energy component that pushed the index up.

But neither support is locked in. The oil crash rests on an Iran deal that still isn't signed — Tehran calls the reports preliminary, the nuclear issue stays separate. And on Tuesday and Wednesday the Fed meets. A rate cut is nearly off the table, but the risk is a tone shift toward tighter policy, especially with four point two percent inflation. It's the first meeting under the new Chair Warsh, raising uncertainty.

For Big Tech, Google stays my better buy — roughly twenty percent below analyst targets, has its own chips, resilient to a chip shock. Nvidia, the priciest name, I only hold and add after the Fed. Gold holds its floor around four thousand two hundred twenty and now has a fresh argument: a hot headline inflation and possible tighter Fed tone are classic gold terrain. That's my next tranche, staged in, biggest piece after the Fed.

In crypto the recovery firms up. Bitcoin sits around sixty-three thousand seven hundred eighty, up four and a half percent for the week, Ethereum leads slightly at nearly six percent. But the real problem stays: Bitcoin funds lost one point seven billion dollars in the week, the biggest outflow in over a year, and the fear index sticks at nineteen in extreme fear. One green week doesn't change that. I hold both and only re-enter after a real inflow week. On the margin, Bittensor jumped eleven percent — pure narrative rotation around the SpaceX IPO, no floor. That's play money, nothing more.

The sequence ahead: possibly a signed Iran deal this weekend, the Fed on Tuesday and Wednesday, SpaceX index additions late month. Monday I'm watching whether the deal actually closes and how the Fed's tone shapes up — because that decides whether the risk-on rally has substance or was just a strong week.
