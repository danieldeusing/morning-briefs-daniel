Story of the day: Two days before the FOMC — the rally stands on shaky legs

The SpaceX debut was the week's bombshell (SPCX +19% to 161.11 USD, ~75 billion USD raised, largest IPO of all time)[3] — but for the next 72 hours it's not the story. The story is the FOMC on Tuesday–Wednesday (June 16–17), the first monetary policy meeting under the new Fed chair. A rate cut is basically off the table; what matters alone is the tone: Does the Fed read the crashing oil price as a disinflation signal (good for equities), or does it emphasize geopolitical uncertainty and the recent inflation spike as a reason for more tightening — a hawkish-bias shift?

What's a "hawkish-bias shift," and how can it kill a rally without rates moving at all? The central bank (Fed) sets not just today's rate, but also signals where it's heading next — "dovish" means "we lean toward cuts," "hawkish" means "we lean toward tightening." A "bias shift" is when the Fed leaves the rate unchanged but flips its lean from dovish to hawkish. Stock valuations — especially pricey growth stocks — hang on expectations of future rates, not just today's. Imagine your boss says: "salary stays the same this month" — but in the same breath "and don't expect a raise next year." Your account doesn't change today, but your plans for next year do. That's exactly how the FOMC can flip sentiment without moving a single basis point.

The rally stands on two props, both rickety. First, the Iran deal: a fourteen-point draft circulates (oil sanctions down, Tehran's commitment to keep the Strait of Hormuz open within 30 days), but nothing is signed.[8] Second, the assumption that the oil crash cools inflation on its own — which only holds if the deal sticks. A telltale detail from Friday's tape: the Mag-7 closed mixed, not broadly green (AAPL −1.52%, AMZN −1.23% in the red). When the broad market rises but the largest names scatter, that's often the first sign that traders are already taking chips off the table ahead of the risk event. That's exactly why all recommendations below are pinned to the FOMC as a dated filter — not chasing Friday's euphoria.

The geopolitical situation itself is the territory of the economy/news context; here only the tradable market read-through counts.

Oil: Brent ~87.33 USD — the crash continues and becomes an inflation relief valve

Brent ~87.33 USD (day −3.37%), WTI 84.88 USD (−3.23%) — last print Friday (oilprice.com, June 12); Brent sits roughly 20% below the 2026 high.[7] The driver is Iran deal optimism: a fourteen-point draft envisions lifting oil sanctions and Tehran's commitment to keep the Strait of Hormuz open within 30 days.[8] Structural OPEC+ oversupply and China weakness pile on. The key equity side effect: this energy weakness is precisely what cools headline inflation in the next CPI. But: nothing is signed — even a deal would first have to clear mines from Hormuz before oil truly flows freely.

Why does oil crash when a war is called off? An escalation at the Persian Gulf could have threatened oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz (the world's tightest oil chokepoint) — less supply, higher price. This threat baked a "fear premium" into the price. If a deal now promises to keep Hormuz open, the threat vanishes — and so does the premium. Imagine a threatened traffic jam on the only bridge into the city; delivery services charge extra. The jam gets canceled, the surcharge disappears instantly.

Recommendation — Oil Exposure (Brent/WTI)

Verdict: HOLD — wait it out; neither short the crash nor go long betting on a reversal.

Why: The market has priced out the fear premium — but on an unsigned deal. Shorting now bets the deal holds and Hormuz actually opens; risky, because Tehran's formal commitment is missing and the situation can flip in hours (then the premium bounces back). Going long fights both the intact structural headwinds (OPEC+, China) and the waning geopolitics. Both sides fear the other's curveball — so you stay out of this one. ELI5: When a price drops three percent on a single unconfirmed headline, that's not a trend, it's a pendulum — you wait until it settles.

Timeframe: Wait for confirmation — either the actual deal signing plus Hormuz reopening (then the structural short is cleaner) or the situation reverses (then the premium bounces back). The next dated supply event is the OPEC+ meeting early July; until then day-to-day geopolitics around the possible Iran signing rules.

Forecast: Base (55%): Brent 82–90 USD, structural pressure plus waning premium cap, as long as no reversal hits. Bear-on-oil / Downside (25%): < 80 USD if the deal is signed and Hormuz credibly reopens — then OPEC+/China break through fully (good for CPI, bad for oil longs). Bull-on-oil (20%): > 95 USD if Iran talks collapse and escalation fear returns.

Evidence: Brent ~87.33 USD (oilprice.com, June 12, day −3.37%), WTI 84.88 USD (−3.23%), Brent ~20% below 2026 high; driver: fourteen-point Iran draft (oil sanctions down, Hormuz reopening in 30 days, unsigned) + OPEC+ oversupply + China weakness.

Why is a single day of ETF inflows so important — and why isn't it enough? Spot-Bitcoin ETFs are exchange-traded funds through which large, slow buyers (pension funds, asset managers) hold Bitcoin without self-custodying coins. Their net flows show whether this "big money" is coming in or pulling out. Thirteen consecutive days of outflows (~4.4 billion USD) was sustained selling pressure in the background, no matter what the day's price did. That Friday inflows finally resume flips the direction. But: one day is a swallow, not summer. Imagine a leaky basin where you've just turned the water back on — the level only rises once the intake consistently beats the leak. A real bottom takes an ETF inflow week, not a Friday.

Why do memecoins jump twelve percent in a day when nothing actually changed? Memecoins (DOGE, SHIB, PEPE, BONK) have almost no real utility — their price is almost pure sentiment. They're the market's "highest beta": when risk appetite rises, they jump the hardest; when it drops, they fall the fastest. So if Bitcoin and the ETF flows spark a little optimism, that gets leveraged into memecoins — not because a coin got better, but because playbook money hunts the cheapest, most exciting bet. Think of a theme-park swing ride: if the engine revs just a notch faster, the outer seats fly much farther than the inner ones. Memecoins are the outermost seats — they feel every twitch, both ways.

It's Sunday, June 14. Stock and commodity markets are closed over the weekend, so all equities and oil numbers are from Friday's close. Only crypto runs around the clock. The main story this week isn't the SpaceX debut anymore — it's the central bank meeting on Tuesday and Wednesday, the first under the new Fed chair.

Looking back: SpaceX delivered the largest stock market debut ever. Offering price one hundred thirty-five dollars, close around one hundred sixty-one — nineteen percent higher, roughly seventy-five billion raised. That lifted the indices, the Dow closed above fifty-one thousand. But the big tech names closed mixed, not broadly green: Tesla and Google higher, Apple and Amazon lower. When the largest names scatter while the broad index rises, that's often a first sign that traders are already being cautious before the key event.

And that event is the central bank. A rate cut is basically off the table. What matters is the tone. If the Fed reads the collapsing oil price as a signal that inflation is cooling on its own, that's good for stocks. But if it emphasizes the geopolitical uncertainty and recent inflation spike as reason for more tightening, the whole mood flips — without a single interest rate moving. The entire rally hangs on this tone.

And it stands on two shaky legs. First: the Iran deal that's pushing down oil prices is a draft — a fourteen-point text proposing lower oil sanctions and a commitment to reopen the Strait of Hormuz within thirty days. Nothing is signed. Second: the hope that the oil crash cools inflation on its own only works if the deal holds. Brent fell over three percent on Friday to around eighty-seven dollars and sits about twenty percent below the annual high.

For my recommendations, Google remains the stronger big-tech buy — protected by its own chips against any chip-outlook shock, I'm accumulating in tranches with the bulk after the Fed. Nvidia, the priciest name, I'm just holding. Oil is a clear wait-and-see: on an unsigned deal, neither shorting the drop nor betting on a bounce. Gold gave way — around four thousand two hundred dollars, down almost eleven percent for the month — but that pullback makes insurance cheaper, and a tough Fed tone would be a fresh gold argument. That's my next tranche, in steps.

In crypto, the bounce is building, and for the first time there's a structural sign: Bitcoin is around sixty-four thousand three hundred, up almost six percent over the week. More important — after thirteen days when Bitcoin funds lost around four point four billion, Friday saw inflows for the first time, around eighty-six million, not a single fund with outflows. That's the first crack in the weakness. But one day isn't a summer. I'm holding Bitcoin and I re-enter only after a confirmed inflow week. Ethereum is lagging and its funds keep flowing out; no reason to overweight it.

At the margins, memecoins jumped twelve percent, the artificial-intelligence token Bittensor around two hundred seventy-five dollars. That's pure risk appetite, no real floor — the outermost seats of the ride, feeling every little movement. Two days before the Fed and fresh off extreme fear, that's speculation, nothing more.

The sequence ahead: Monday is the first test whether risk appetite carries into the Fed week; Tuesday and Wednesday the central bank decides, filtering everything; end of month SpaceX joins the indices, triggering forced buying. I'll be watching especially for the tone the market takes into the Fed week. And as always: this is not investment advice, just my own research.
