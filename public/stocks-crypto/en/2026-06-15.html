Story of the day: Iran deal is signed — and tomorrow Warsh tests the rally

The thesis has shifted qualitatively since the weekend: The US-Iran peace treaty is no longer a circulating draft, it was signed on June 14th — a formal signing ceremony in Switzerland is planned, Iran's deputy foreign minister Gharibabadi confirmed the deal, and the Strait of Hormuz is to reopen by week's end.[6] That's the difference between hope and fact — and the market prices in fact: oil crashes, gold and crypto rise, appetite for risk returns.

But relief immediately hits the next, larger filter: On June 16–17 the FOMC meets for the first time under Kevin Warsh, sworn in on May 22nd as the 17th Fed chair, replacing Jerome Powell. A rate cut is practically off the table — the market prices ~98% odds for a hold at 3.50–3.75% (unchanged since Dec 2025). The only question is tone: If Warsh reads the oil crash as a disinflation gift that keeps the door open for later easing, risk-on carries. If he signals a hawkish shift — and that's exactly what's being debated in the market — sentiment flips, without a single basis point moving. Adding to the complexity: nobody knows how Warsh will stage the dot-plot and press conference. So all recommendations below are keyed to this dated filter — not chasing the relief rally.

Why does oil crash when a war ends? An escalation at the Persian Gulf could have threatened oil supplies through the Strait of Hormuz — the world's most critical oil chokepoint, through which about one-fifth of global oil flows. Less supply, higher price. This danger was baked in as "fear premium." If a signed deal now promises to keep Hormuz open, the threat vanishes — and the premium with it. Think of a threatened traffic jam on the only bridge into the city; delivery services demand a surcharge. When the jam is formally canceled, the surcharge drops right away.

Why do ETF flows matter more than daily price? Spot Bitcoin ETFs are exchange-traded funds through which large, slow buyers (pension funds, asset managers) hold bitcoin without self-custody. Their net flows show whether this "big money" is flowing in or out — a persistent current underneath daily price action. 13 days of outflows (~$4.33B) was sustained selling pressure. When that series ends, headwind stops — but "no outflow" is not yet "inflow." Imagine a leaking bucket: stopping the leak means the water level stops dropping — but it only rises when inflow outpaces the leak for multiple days.
