Story of the Day: Warsh's first decision comes today — the Dot Plot is the real news

This week's macro filter triggers today: At roughly 3:00 p.m. BRT (2:00 p.m. US Eastern), the FOMC announces its rate decision and updated Dot Plot; at about 3:30 p.m. BRT, Kevin Warsh holds his first press conference as Fed chair (sworn in May 22nd as the 17th chair).[13] Important: Nothing is public yet at press time. What follows is expectation and plan — not the outcome. A rate hold at 3.50–3.75% (fourth in a row) is virtually certain: CME FedWatch data prices it at ~97–98.4%.[13]

All the tension rests on tone and the Dot Plot. May CPI at 4.2% year-over-year — the highest since April 2023 — has cemented the hold and driven the probability of a December 2026 hike above 71% in the market.[7][13] Two open questions for tonight: Does the 2026 median erase the last priced-in cut (confirming a hold for the whole year)? And does a dot shift upward, formally putting a 2026 hike on the table?

What is a "Dot Plot" and why can it move the market more than the rate decision itself? Four times a year, each Fed member anonymously plots a dot on a chart: where they see the policy rate in the coming years. This cloud of dots (the Dot Plot) is the Fed's collective rate outlook. If today's rate is already locked in (a hold), the Dot Plot is the real news: if the dots shift upward, the Fed is saying "we'll stay higher or hike" — that hits stocks and crypto without a single rate move today. Think of it like a weather forecast: you already know it won't rain today; what matters is whether meteorologists are forecasting storms for next week.

A Warsh-specific angle: The new chair has repeatedly expressed skepticism about the usefulness of forecasts and specifically about the Dot Plot. It wouldn't surprise anyone if he omitted his own dot entirely — a largely symbolic but tone-setting move signaling that the Fed under his watch will focus more on incoming data than on forecasts.[14] Disinflation winds are coming from the oil crash (Brent −29.5% for the month) — cheap energy pushes the inflation rate down. If Warsh reads that as a gift, it's risk-on. If he stresses the stubbornness of 4.2%, sentiment shifts. That's why all recommendations today are pegged to this afternoon's filter.

Why does crypto often fall before and after Fed meetings? Bitcoin and ETH are "long-duration risk assets" — they pay no yield; their value sits entirely in future expectations. If rates rise (or expectations do), safe cash in the bank becomes attractive, and the most speculative end of the market sells first. Before a Fed meeting, many traders preemptively cut risk — they "de-risk" — and buy back once the fog clears. Think of it like beachgoers pulling in deck chairs before the weather report comes out: even if the sun stays, the beach is briefly empty.
