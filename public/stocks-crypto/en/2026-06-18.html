Story of the day: Warsh's first decision was hawkish — the dot plot is the news

The week's macro filter has tripped, and the answer is clear: Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Fed chair ended yesterday (6/17) with a rate hold at 3.50–3.75 % — unanimous 12:0 — but a materially hawkish dot plot. The median path for end-2026 rose to 3.8 % (from 3.4 % in March), and nine of eighteen members now see at least one rate hike by year-end. The statement also removed the last language hint that had been read as a nod toward easing.[1][2]

The market expected a neutral hold — the raised dot plot was the surprise. Warsh's opening message is thus: higher for longer. Justified by inflation that the Fed says will stay "several years" above the 2 % target. Anyone betting on rate cuts in 2026 had to reprice — and that hit first the asset classes that live most off future low rates: growth stocks and crypto.

Dot plot: A cloud of dots where each Fed member anonymously marks where they expect the fed funds rate at year-end. Imagine eighteen weather forecasters each pointing to a single temperature — the median, the middle forecaster, is the official expectation. When that middle point shifts higher, the Fed is telling the market: expect higher rates for longer. That's exactly what happened yesterday (3.4 % → 3.8 %).

Bitcoin ETF flows: A Bitcoin ETF is a traded fund holding actual Bitcoin — when people buy shares, they push the fund to buy more BTC (inflow); when they sell, it forces the fund to dump BTC (outflow).
