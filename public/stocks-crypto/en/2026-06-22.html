Nvidia's $25B bond: the capex signal [1][2][3]

Bucket 1, the story of the day. Nvidia announced a $25B bond offering on 06/15—subscriptions exceeded $85B, more than three times the offer, and it was Nvidia's first bond since 2021.[1]

Bond: Instead of issuing stock, Nvidia borrows money directly from the market—like a debt note with a fixed rate that large investors buy. A three-times oversubscription means for every dollar offered, investors wanted to lend three. That's massive confidence—and remarkable because Nvidia usually earns enough cash that it doesn't need debt. That it's raising $25B anyway signals: the company wants to max out its financing of the AI building boom.

The context explains investor appetite: Nvidia's fiscal 2026 revenue stands at $216B—versus $27B in 2021, when it last tapped the bond market.[2] Industry-wide AI capex is expected to exceed $700B in 2026, and Nvidia sits at the center of this cycle.[2] The stock closed 06/18 at $210.69 (+2.95%), market cap $5.10T (+47.4% YoY); pre-market on 06/22 opened at $207.33 (day range $206.50–$211.39).[3]

Why it matters: The bond is a louder signal than any price number. It says the AI infrastructure buildout looks so big and so safe that even the cash king borrows—and that large investors back it. The next hard catalyst is far off: NVDA earnings not until 08/26. Until then, NVDA is a macro and sector beta play, and the Fed hawk is headwind for exactly this high-beta segment.

Verdict: BUY on weakness · HOLD at current levels (don't chase the +3% day)

The hawkish Fed pivot remains the metronome [4][5]

The thread hanging over everything is the Fed. On Wednesday, 06/17, the FOMC held rates at 3.50–3.75%—but the "dot plot" turned hawkish.[4]

Dot plot: A scatter of dots where the FOMC members each anonymously place a dot showing where they see the rate at year-end—like if every student in a class secretly writes down tomorrow's temperature and you take the median. This time 18 projections were submitted (the chair submitted no dot). Now 9 of 18 projections see at least one rate hike in 2026, and 6 see two. A hike, not a cut—that's the pivot.

The Fed also lifted its 2026 inflation forecast to 3.6% headline / 3.3% core (from 2.7%)—and sees the 2% target not until 2028.[4] Result: The market now prices a September hike. The dollar reacted instantly: DXY topped 100 (100.909, +0.15% on 06/22)—strongest dollar since May 2025.[5]

DXY (Dollar Index): Measures the dollar against a basket of major currencies (euro, yen, pound, etc.)—like a scale showing if the dollar is stronger or weaker versus "the rest of the world." A rising DXY is headwind for almost everything priced in dollars: gold, oil, crypto, emerging-market currencies.

Why it matters: Higher rates for longer means costlier money, higher discounting of future profits (bad for high-beta tech), and a strong dollar as headwind for commodities and crypto. The Iran-deal relief rally masks the hawk—the PCE pressure on 06/25 is the next litmus test.

Oil: the Iran roadmap presses the price [8][9]

Bucket 2. A US-Iran 60-day peace roadmap conditionally opens the Strait of Hormuz—Kuwait lifted force majeure, Geneva talks were initially postponed on 06/19, then resumed.[9] Result: Brent $79.15 (−1.78%), WTI $75.24 (−2.70%) on 06/22.[8] WTI is −19.86% month-to-date, but still +9.83% YoY.[8] The Brent-WTI spread (~$3.91) is normal.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil ships—the bottleneck of oil logistics, like a single bridge one-fifth of all trucks in a country must use. Threat of blockade, oil price spikes (risk premium); strait opens, the premium vanishes—that's exactly what's happening with the roadmap.

Options expiry: Crypto options are bets on a future price; on a fixed day, they all "expire" at once. OTM ("out of the money") means the bet didn't pay off—the agreed price is too far away.
