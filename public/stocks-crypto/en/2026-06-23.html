Gold & Fed: From inflation hedge to rate victim [6][7][8]

Gold −2%: why it falls despite inflation

Spot gold traded 06-23 at ~$4,100 (CNBC: $4,099.84 −2.2%; TradingEconomics: $4,113.93 −1.85%). The driver is counterintuitive: with US CPI at 4.2% in May (highest since April 2023, driven by a 23.5% energy spike from the Iran conflict), gold should rise. Instead, the market is pricing in Fed rate hikes—and a strong dollar plus higher real rates are poison for a non-yielding asset. "Fed hike expectations outweigh Iran peace relief."[6]

Real rates: Nominal rate minus inflation. Gold pays no interest—when bonds suddenly yield more in real terms (because the Fed hikes), holding gold gets more expensive. That's why gold falls despite high inflation: not inflation itself, but what the Fed does about it.

It's Tuesday, June twenty-third. And this is a risk-off day the likes of which you don't often see so synchronized—stocks, gold, and crypto all falling at once, each for its own but related reason.

The biggest crack is showing in Big Tech. Alphabet dropped about six percent Monday to around three hundred forty-six dollars. Amazon about four percent. The trigger isn't a data point—it's a calculation the market won't ignore anymore: Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet, and Oracle plan over four hundred fifty-two billion dollars in AI investment this year, while free cashflow is collapsing. Alphabet's down forty-seven percent year-over-year, Amazon down ninety-five percent. For the first time the market is asking out loud whether this bet ever pays off.

Nvidia, the former darling, traded pre-market around two hundred two dollars, down almost three percent. The thing is, the semiconductor index has doubled this year, and Nvidia's dead last in it, while AMD is up one hundred fourteen percent. In South Korea the KOSPI cratered almost ten percent. Samsung and SK Hynix each down over twelve percent. That's a direct leading signal for US chips.

Crypto felt the pain of leverage. Bitcoin fell below sixty-three thousand, Ethereum down six percent. In a single day, somewhere between five hundred eighty million and seven hundred fourteen million dollars got liquidated—depending on the aggregator—and over one hundred forty-four thousand traders got wiped out, mostly optimists. That's the sixth straight week of outflows from Bitcoin ETFs, around two hundred twenty-six million dollars net. Fear is measurably extreme.

Gold dropped over two percent to around four thousand one hundred—and that's the most interesting story. Despite US inflation at four point two percent, the highest in over three years, gold is falling. Because the market is now pricing in Fed rate hikes, not cuts. Nine of eighteen Fed officials see at least one hike this year—Chair Warsh submitted no dot. A strong dollar and higher real rates are poison for an asset that pays no interest.

The one structurally calmer point is oil. Brent is around seventy-seven dollars, far from the Hormuz peak of one hundred twenty-six in March. Since the peace deal in Versailles on June seventeenth, the strait is open—structurally bearish, even if Iran claimed a closure again on the twentieth.

Bottom line: most calls today are hold and watch. With Bitcoin and gold, extreme fear is historically an accumulation zone, not a sell zone—but staggered, not all at once, and only once ETF flows turn.
