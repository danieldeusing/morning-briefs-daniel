Big Tech & Semiconductors [1][2][3]

Apple: −6.15% due to the "once-in-a-century flood" in storage

Apple lost −6.15% on June 25 to $275.15 (−$18.02) — the largest single-day loss in weeks and the main driver of the fourth Nasdaq losing day. The trigger: a round of price increases across Mac, iPad, Apple TV, HomePod, and Vision Pro, with the steepest hikes on MacBook, iPad Air, and iPad Pro. Premarket on June 26 essentially flat at $275.04 (−0.04%).

Why is hardware getting more expensive right now? Imagine all the world's bakers suddenly buying the same flour — then flour gets expensive for your pizza, even though you didn't change anything. That's exactly what's happening with memory chips (DRAM/NAND): AI data centers are draining supply, so prices spike for everyone — including iPhones and Macs. CEO Tim Cook calls it a "once-in-a-century flood" in storage and capacity costs.

The real investor debate: does the math work? Apple passes costs to customers — that protects margins only as long as buyers accept higher prices. Deutsche Bank warns storage scarcity could last "well into 2028." If customers balk, the cost pressure still eats into margins. Some investors were already buying the dip as oversold.

Recommendation — Apple (AAPL)

HOLD  Verdict: Hold, don't buy the dip — but no panic sell either.

Why: The 6% drop is a real margin question, not a demand collapse. Apple still sells, just at higher prices; the risk is price elasticity (will people buy anyway?) and a shortage that Deutsche Bank sees stretching to 2028. That's a multi-quarter margin story, not a one-day scare — so today's dip doesn't look like a clear bargain.

Timeframe: Wait for Q2 earnings (late July) — only the gross margin guidance there shows whether cost pressure bleeds through or gets passed on. No new position before then.

Outlook: Base case (~55%): sideways $265–285 until earnings, market digests the price hike. Bull (~25%): customers accept prices, margin holds, back toward $300. Bear (~20%): demand cracks + storage stays tight → test $250.

Evidence: Close 06/25 $275.15 (−6.15%); premarket 06/26 $275.04 (−0.04%) — overnight stabilization favors the base case.

AI Infra: the circular $6.3B deal [4][5][6]

SpaceX, Reflection AI, and Nvidia — all three in the same money circle

On June 22, SpaceX signed a compute deal with open-source AI startup Reflection AI for up to $6.3B: Reflection pays SpaceX $150M per month (Jul 2026 through 2029) for access to Nvidia GB300 chips in the "Colossus 2" data center in Memphis.

Circular trading: Imagine a car dealer lends you money to buy his car — the revenue looks big, but the cash circles back. Same here: Nvidia invested $800M in Reflection, which now gets access to chips Nvidia sold to SpaceX. Nvidia sits on both sides of the trade.

For the market, it cuts both ways: it shows demand (Colossus 2 has over $80B in committed revenue through 2029, including Anthropic, Google, Cursor, per TechCrunch) — but circular financing makes AI capex skeptics nervous, because it blurs real end demand from accounting revenue creation. NVDA closed June 25 at $195.74 (−1.59%), premarket June 26 $192.80 (−1.50%).

The hawkish Fed — the macro backbone [7][8]

From holding to hike signal

On June 17, the Fed held fed funds at 3.50–3.75% — but the Dot Plot flipped: the median sees rates at 3.8% by year-end, or above current levels. 9 of 18 members want at least one hike. The 2026 PCE inflation forecast rose from 2.7% to 3.6%.

Dot Plot: An anonymous survey of Fed members — each dots where they see rates heading next. Like an office betting pool: you see the scattered predictions, not who made them. When dots creep upward, it signals: tighter, not looser.

This is the regime explaining almost every market move today. Higher expected rates → stronger dollar → headwind for gold, crypto, commodities, and expensive stocks. Per FXStreet, the market now prices ~80% chance of a hike by December.

ETF outflows: A spot Bitcoin ETF is a basket that holds real Bitcoin so regular investors can play via their brokerage. When money flows out, the basket must sell Bitcoin — that directly pressures price. That's exactly what's happening now: a new outflow streak is running on June 24, $469M flowed out — the fifth straight down day and the biggest single-day exit since June 2. It's Friday, June twenty-sixth. And nearly every market sits in the same shadow today: money is getting expensive and scarce.

Earlier this week, the Federal Reserve under its new chief Kevin Warsh held rates steady at three point five to three point seventy-five percent. But members are now signaling a possible hike instead of a cut. The market is pricing roughly eighty percent odds of a rate increase by December. That's pushing the dollar to a fifty-two-week high and sending gold into its fourth straight losing week.

The standout stock is Apple. The shares fell over six percent Thursday, closing around two hundred seventy-five dollars. The reason is almost ironic: Apple has to raise hardware prices because memory chips are scarce and expensive. And the AI boom itself is draining the supply. CEO Tim Cook calls it a once-in-a-century flood in storage costs. The open question is whether customers will accept the higher prices.

Across big tech stocks, declines moved in lockstep. The Nasdaq notched its fourth straight losing day, the first such streak since February. Microsoft at least stabilized premarket Friday.

Nvidia has an interesting deal this week. SpaceX is buying computing power from startup Reflection for up to six point three billion dollars. Here's the twist: Nvidia itself invested eight hundred million in Reflection and is simultaneously selling the chips. The money goes in a circle. On one hand, that shows huge demand. On the other, skeptics of the AI capex boom are getting nervous.

In crypto, extreme fear now grips the market. Bitcoin has fallen below sixty thousand dollars, and Ethereum is much weaker. Right now a new outflow streak is running from Bitcoin exchange-traded funds: on June twenty-fourth alone, four hundred sixty-nine million dollars flowed out — the fifth straight down day and the biggest single-day exit since early June. The big players aren't back yet. Memecoins and AI tokens like Bittensor and Render fell broadly too.

Oil had a brief scare. A tanker was hit by a projectile off Oman, and Brent jumped nearly two percent. But Friday it completely reversed because oil is flowing through the Strait of Hormuz at record speed. Brent is now around seventy-two dollars.

My takeaway for today: wait. Almost all calls remain "hold" because the next real signal doesn't arrive until the Fed meeting in July and earnings from Nvidia and Apple at month-end. Until then, the dollar is calling the shots. In the next brief, we'll see if Apple can hold two hundred seventy-five dollars and whether Bitcoin finds a floor.
