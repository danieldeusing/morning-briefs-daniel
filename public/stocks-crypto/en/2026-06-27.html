Why does an unlisted company move the whole market? OpenAI is the poster child of the AI boom. An expected IPO works like a green light at a racetrack: as long as it's green, all AI bets floor it. If the signal gets pushed to 2027, investors suddenly wonder if the whole AI story is maturing slower than thought — and they take profits on linked stocks (chip makers, investors like SoftBank). The uncertainty itself is the trigger, not an actual business breakdown.

Embedded in an already-running chip slowdown: NVDA is down roughly −9% on a 30-day view (from 52-week high ~$232 to $192.53), and semiconductors are in their fifth losing week in a row. The AI bet is not broken, but it's being put through a reality check.

The Macro Backbone: PCE Kills Rate Hope [7][8][9]

Core inflation at three-year high — no rate cuts before 2027

The PCE inflation report for May 2026 — the Fed's favorite inflation gauge — was released Thursday, 06.25. (8:30 a.m. EDT). Core PCE accelerated to 3.40% year-on-year (from 3.30% in April) — the highest level since October 2023. Headline PCE stands at 4.1% (highest since April 2023). Month-on-month: headline +0.4%, core +0.3% — both in line with expectations.

Why 4.1% headline but only 3.4% core? The "headline" number measures all prices, including gas and food; the "core" number strips out exactly those two because they swing wildly. Picture your monthly shopping: if mainly gasoline got pricier, your whole bill looks scary (4.1%), but the underlying, sticky inflation (3.4%) is lower. For the Fed, the core number counts more — and it rose, didn't fall. The high headline is mostly driven by oil prices.

The consequence is clear: already the FOMC Dot Plot from 06.17. showed 9 of 18 members expecting at least one hike in 2026 — and no cuts before 2027. The PCE report confirms this hard line. Exactly this "higher for longer" regime explains weakness in gold, crypto, and expensive stocks: if cash still earns decent interest, risk assets have to adjust with lower valuations. Gold still reacted positively because the numbers just met expectations rather than surprising — the feared inflation shock didn't arrive.
