1 · Bitcoin: the historic ETF bleed

Live snapshot 29.06.2026 (CoinGecko) — crypto trades 24/7; ETF flows as of June 26.

Spot-Bitcoin ETF & why outflows matter: An ETF is an exchange-traded fund — here a pot that holds real Bitcoin, so you can invest in BTC via a normal brokerage account. "Outflow" means: investors pull money out, the fund has to sell Bitcoin. Like a coat-check line running in only one direction — seven consecutive days of outflows signal sustained, institutional selling pressure, not daily noise.

$1.79B out — second-worst week on record

US spot-Bitcoin ETFs posted net outflows of approximately $1.79 billion for the week of June 22–26 — the second-largest weekly exodus since launch in January 2024.[1] BlackRock's IBIT alone accounted for 73% of it (~$1.31 billion) and lost $444.5 million in a single day on June 26 — the largest single-day outflow since inception and the 7th consecutive outflow day.[2][3]

The bitter irony sits in IBIT's own numbers: since launch, $60.77 billion flowed in, but only $44.42 billion in assets remain (as of June 26). The ~$16 billion gap is pure price loss. The average IBIT investor is now sitting on roughly −40% — mid-2025 that same cohort was up 30%.[1]

Price: −52.9% from the high

Bitcoin trades at $59,459 (−0.8% on 24h, −7.1% on 7 days), market cap $1.19 trillion.[4] That puts BTC −52.9% below the October 2025 record of $126,272 — more than halved.[1] On Saturday (June 28) it traded around ~$60,750; the $60,000 level has since fallen again. Ethereum is bleeding: ~$1,565 (−0.9% on 24h); Ether ETFs too are bleeding for the 7th consecutive day.[4][5]

3 · Gold: fourth consecutive losing week

As of: 29.06.2026 (TradingEconomics spot) — macro as of June 18–27.

Gold traded around $4,051/oz on June 29 (−0.88% vs prior day); separate sources show ~$4,067 — a tight range.[10] For the week gold is down ~−3% — the fourth consecutive losing week.[10] The driver is the Fed: it held the policy rate at 3.50–3.75% on June 17 (under new Chair Kevin Warsh), but 9 of 18 members signaled at least one hike for 2026 — the market prices in three hikes, with ~62% odds on a first move in September.[11][12]

Real rates & why they pressure gold: The real rate is the interest rate after inflation is subtracted. If expectations rise that the Fed will hike rather than cut, the real rate climbs — and interest-free gold costs more in "foregone returns." Like a savings account that suddenly starts paying interest: that gold bar sitting in the vault earning nothing suddenly looks worse. That's exactly what's happening now.

The twist: May PCE inflation accelerated to 4.1% — but over 60% of the rise came from energy (+23.5% year-on-year). Core ticked up to +0.3% month-on-month: still restrained, but no longer clearly cooling. A hawkish Fed reacting to inflation whose core isn't spreading broadly — but the recent core stickiness adds nuance to that argument.[12] The DXY dollar index sat around 101.3 on June 29.[13]
