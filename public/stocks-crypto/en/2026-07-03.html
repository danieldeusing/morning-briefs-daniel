Big Tech: Rotation Is the Story

The most important chart of the day isn't a single price, but the divergence: The Dow Jones closed on July 2 at an all-time high of 52,900.07 (+1.14%, +594.83 points), while the Nasdaq Composite fell 0.80% to 25,832.67 and the broader Nasdaq-100 even lost −1.61%.[1][2][3] The S&P 500 landed dead flat at 7,483.24 (±0.00%)[4] — only because Apple masked a chip selloff.

Sector rotation: Think of the market as a buffet. For months everyone ate only at the expensive sushi station (tech/growth). Now they realize it stays pricey and switch to solid roast (industrials, banks, "value"). The money doesn't disappear — it moves. That's exactly what you see when the old-industrial Dow rises while the tech-heavy Nasdaq falls.

The drivers of the divergence:

Weak jobs report → softer Fed expected → money hunts cyclical value names → Dow up, Nasdaq down

Tesla: −7.5% Despite Delivery Beat

Tesla delivered 480,126 vehicles in Q2 2026 by reports — well above Wall Street's bar (~406,000). Yet the stock fell 7.49% to $393.45 (intraday briefly −8.3%).[6] The reason is textbook market mechanics: The stock had risen around 13% in the four trading days before — the good delivery number was priced in, and investors took profits.

"Sell the news": When everyone expects good news, they buy before. When the news actually hits — even if it's good — there's no new buyer left, and early movers cash out. The news was strong, the price falls anyway. No contradiction, just timing.

Recommendation — Tesla (TSLA)

Verdict: HOLD

Why: The delivery beat (480k vs. ~406k expected) is fundamentally solid — demand is there. The crash is positioning, not business deterioration. But Tesla stays expensive and volatile; a −7.5% day after +13% is noise rather than trend break. If you're in, no reason to sell; if you want in, don't chase the first rebound candle.

Timeframe: Clarity only after the Q2 earnings call (~2 weeks), when margins and Robotaxi/FSD guidance hit the table — those set valuation, not unit count alone.

Forecast: Base case (~50%): sideways 370–420 before earnings; delivery beat cushions pullbacks. Bull (~30%): strong margins + FSD news → back over 430. Bear (~20%): margins disappoint or rotation eats growth → test 360. Downside scenario: fails to hold 360, space opens to 330.

Evidence: $393.45 on 07.02.2026 (−7.49% day), after ~+13% in four prior days; Q2 deliveries 480,126 (beat).[6]

Gold: Above $4,100 — Weak Labor Market Helps

Gold jumped to $4,178.74/oz (+1.36%, +$57.66) — the best level in about a month, a rebound from an 8-month low.[8] An independent cross-check (Investing.com) confirms with $4,181.13 (daily range 4,121–4,196); the gap of 0.06% is well under the 0.5% threshold — the number is solid.[9]

The driver is the weak US labor market: The June jobs report showed only +57,000 new positions — the fewest in four months and far below the forecast of 110,000.[10]

Why does weak jobs lift gold? Fewer new jobs means: the Fed has less reason to keep rates high. Lower (expected) rates make gold — which pays no interest itself — relatively more attractive, because the rate-paying alternative (bonds, savings) yields less. Gold wins when the market expects falling rates.

Recommendation — Gold (XAU/USD)

Verdict: BUY (core hedge)

Why: Two tailwinds together: (1) weak labor market → softer-Fed expectation → falling real rates, the classic gold environment; (2) falling oil doesn't hurt gold — it actually supports the rate-cut narrative. Gold as portfolio insurance, not a speculative play.

Timeframe: Confirmation at the next FOMC meeting (~30 days) — if the Fed confirms the dovish tone, the breakout is durable; if it surprises hawkish, the pullback comes.

Forecast: Base (~55%): consolidation 4,100–4,250 as long as the rate-cut story holds. Bull (~30%): Fed confirms dovish + geopolitics → 4,300+. Bear (~15%): hawkish Fed surprise or strong dollar → back under 4,100. Downside scenario: fails to hold 4,100, risks a retest of the 8-month lows.

Evidence: $4,178.74 on 07.03.2026 (+1.36%); cross-check $4,181.13 (Investing.com), prior close $4,123.96; catalyst +57k jobs (vs. 110k expected).[8][9][10]

ETF Outflows: The Bear in the Room

June 2026 saw record net outflows from spot BTC ETFs of roughly $4.5B (independent sources cite ~$4.3B — same magnitude).[17] July 1 alone had ~$296M net outflow.[18]

Spot BTC ETF flows: An ETF is a stock-exchange-traded basket holding real Bitcoin, so you can buy BTC like a stock. "Net outflow" means: more people pull money out than push in — the ETF has to sell Bitcoin. That's sell pressure from big (institutional) wallets. $4.5B out in one month explains why the price tanked to a multi-month low.

Recommendation — Bitcoin (BTC)

Verdict: HOLD — accumulate only on flow reversal

Why: The rebound over $60,000 is encouraging, but the sell pressure is structural: $4.5B ETF outflows in June are institutional exit, not noise. As long as outflows persist, the price lacks a buyer base. The rebound could be a bear-market rally, not the bottom. No panic selling on the low, but also no free pass to buy blindly.

Timeframe: The key signal is ETF flows in the next ~30 days — if they flip from net outflow to net inflow, that's the trigger. Until then: watch, don't chase.

Forecast: Base (~45%): range 57,700–65,000 as long as flows stay negative. Bull (~30%): flows reverse → reclaim 70,000. Bear (~25%): outflows accelerate → break 21-month low, test 52,000–55,000. Downside scenario: fails to hold 57,700, technically room opens below.

Evidence: ~$61,460 on 07.03.2026 (+2.7% 7d); 21-month low $57,700–58,000 in late June; June ETF outflows ~$4.5B, 07.01 ~$296M.[14][15][17][18]

It's Friday, July 3rd. US markets are closed today for Independence Day, so we're working with Thursday's close on July 2nd — and it packed a punch.

The story of the day is a divergence. The Dow Jones closed at an all-time high of about fifty-two thousand nine hundred points, up one point one four percent. At the same time, the tech-heavy Nasdaq fell zero point eight percent. Money is rotating right now from expensive growth stocks into solid industrial and bank holdings. The broad S&P five hundred landed exactly flat — and only because a single stock rescued the rest.

That stock was Apple. It jumped nearly five percent to just over three hundred eight dollars, driven by reports of a foldable iPhone. The expected units for this year got raised to ten million. Without Apple, the broad market would have had a much worse day.

The big loser was Tesla, down seven point five percent to three hundred ninety-three dollars. Here's the twist: Tesla delivered over four hundred eighty thousand vehicles in the second quarter, well above expectations. Yet the stock fell — a classic "sell the news" after the shares had rallied thirteen percent prior. Meta gave back nearly five percent, Nvidia around one and a half.

On commodities, gold jumped over four thousand one hundred dollars an ounce, up one point four percent. The reason: the US labor market is weakening — June added just fifty-seven thousand jobs instead of the expected one hundred ten thousand. That makes the market expect a softer central bank — and that's good for gold. Oil, by contrast, slipped toward multi-month lows, Brent just under seventy-one dollars, because tensions in the Strait of Hormuz are easing.

In crypto, Bitcoin recovered from its twenty-one-month low back above sixty thousand dollars, now around sixty-one thousand five hundred. Important context: that's not an old price — it's a steep drop from the year's high of one hundred twenty-six thousand. The reason for the weakness is record outflows from Bitcoin funds — June saw around four point five billion dollars leave. Ethereum performed better, up ten percent in a week.

My take: on stocks, the rotation is the signal — Tesla hold, Apple interesting on pullbacks. Gold is core insurance in an environment of falling rate expectations. On Bitcoin, wait for the fund flows to reverse before you add, don't jump ahead.
