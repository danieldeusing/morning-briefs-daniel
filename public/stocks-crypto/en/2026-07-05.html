Crypto core: the turn holds over the weekend

Bitcoin trades stable on Sunday at ~$62,709 (+0.2% 24h, +4.4% 7d; market cap ~$1.258 trillion).[1] An independent check from the previous day confirms the magnitude at ~$63,121 (07/04, 14:30 EDT) — a ~0.6% difference, consistent.[2] But the real point today is what didn't happen: yesterday's ETF turn has not reversed over the weekend.

The trigger this brief had been waiting for The spot Bitcoin ETFs saw a net inflow of $221.7 million on Thursday, July 2 — the biggest single day in over two months and the first inflow after ten straight trading days of outflows that together had pulled > $2.7 billion out of the products. Fidelity's FBTC led with +$165.96 million, ARKB +$91.84 million; BlackRock's IBIT was the lone outlier at −$40.43 million. But: the year's picture stays ugly — 2026 still sits at −$5.4 billion in net outflows.[3]
Spot BTC ETF flows: An ETF is an exchange-traded basket that holds real bitcoin so you can buy BTC like a stock. "Net inflow" means more investors are putting money in than taking it out — the ETF buys bitcoin. After ten days of big (institutional) holders selling, a $221.7 million inflow is the first sign the buyers are back. A quiet weekend with no reversal makes the signal a notch more solid — though it's not proven yet.

Ethereum leads the recovery even more clearly: ~$1,764 (+0.4% 24h, +12.3% 7d; cap ~$213 billion).[4] +12.3% over seven days versus BTC's +4.4% is a clear outperformance — when capital rotates within crypto, ETH often leads. A source cluster spans ETH from ~$1,759 to ~$1,797 (~2% spread across several aggregators); the value used here sits mid-cluster.[4]

Gold: weak labor market keeps carrying it

Gold climbed on Friday (07/03) to ~$4,170/oz (+1.15% on the day) — the highest level since June 23 and a +2% on the week after four straight weeks of declines.[10] A second read from 07/05 confirms with ~$4,176 (~0.1% difference, consistent).[11] The driver is the weak US labor market: only +57,000 new jobs in June against ~113,000 expected — the market reads that as a more dovish Fed.

Why does a weak labor market push gold up? Fewer new jobs means the Fed has less reason to keep rates high. Lower (expected) rates make gold — which itself pays no interest — relatively more attractive, because the interest-bearing alternative (bonds, savings accounts) yields less. Gold gains when the market believes rates are falling.

Note: July 4–5 are holiday/thin trading days; Friday's value (07/03) is the last regular quote.

Dollar: DXY holds just above 100

The US Dollar Index (DXY) stands at ~100.86 (as of 07/03) — a soft dollar that supports gold's strength and gives risk assets room.[14]

DXY (Dollar Index): A basket that measures how strong the dollar is against six other major currencies (mainly the euro, yen). If the index falls, the dollar is weaker — that makes gold (priced in dollars) cheaper for the rest of the world and helps risk assets. A DXY around 100 is historically middling-to-weak.
