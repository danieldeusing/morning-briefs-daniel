OpenAI: $42.6B for the government — and an IPO nobody dates

The day's biggest tradeable-markets story involves a company that isn't even tradeable yet. OpenAI is discussing handing the US government a 5% stake — based on the latest valuation of $852B (March 2026 round), that comes out to roughly $42.6B.[1][2] The model is inspired by the Alaska Permanent Fund, which invests the state's oil revenue and distributes the returns to citizens. The idea: the government becomes a co-owner of AI's value creation instead of merely regulating it — the plan reportedly extends to Anthropic, Google, and Meta handing over similar stakes through a sovereign-wealth-style vehicle.

Why is this a markets story if OpenAI isn't public? Because it publicly anchors the valuation of one of the most important AI companies — and because OpenAI confidentially filed an S-1 IPO prospectus with the SEC back in June. A 5% government stake is like a neighbor buying into your house sale before it's even listed: it sets a price before the sign goes up in the yard. What matters to markets is twofold — the $852B price anchor for the entire AI infrastructure ecosystem, and the political cover that would make a later IPO approvable in the first place.
The IPO timing is genuinely disputed This is exactly where reports diverge, and the resolution matters. OpenAI's own documented statement on the S-1 (2026-06-09) reads: "We haven't settled on timing yet; it could still take a while, because some things are easier to do as a private company." Other reports cite a September 2026 target (close to Altman), while still others suggest a wait until 2027. There is no single primary source that resolves the date. I weight the direct company statement higher than third-party timing speculation: treat the IPO as coming, but undated — not as a fixed Q3 event.

The second data point grounding the thesis: OpenAI is burning cash fast — reportedly ~$2B in monthly revenue, but a loss of ~$1.22 for every dollar earned.[3] That's exactly the reason for "easier as a private company" — a loss-making giant wants to avoid quarterly disclosure obligations as long as possible.

SpaceX: Tuesday's Nasdaq-100 forced buy

On Tuesday, July 7, SpaceX (SPCX) joins the Nasdaq-100 — and this isn't a news event, it's a mechanical capital flow. Every fund that tracks the index (the QQQ ETF ecosystem chief among them) must buy SPCX to match the index. The estimate: roughly $4.3B of forced buying — into a stock with only 3-5% free float.[4]

Index forced buying & free float: An index fund promises to exactly mirror the index. When a stock gets added, the fund has no choice — it buys, no matter the price, or it deviates from the index. "Free float" is the portion of shares actually available to trade (the rest sits locked with founders/insiders). When $4.3B of mandatory buying hits only 3-5% of freely tradeable shares, it's like a busload of buyers pulling up to a kiosk with three bottles on the shelf: the price can jump sharply in the short term because a lot of demand meets very little supply.

SpaceX has only been public since June 12 — the IPO was priced at $135, closing its first day ~19% higher around $160.95 (day-one market cap ~$2.1T). Most recently (07-04), SPCX traded at $162.00 (prior close $157.54).[5] A stock this young, this tightly held, plus a dated mandatory buy is the textbook setup for a short-term squeeze — and for sharp volatility in both directions once the buying pressure clears.

Verdict: HOLD / don't chase the forced buy

Why: The index effect is real and dated — but it's known, so it's largely already priced in. Buying after the QQQ buying pressure on Tuesday often means buying the local top: once the mechanical buying is done, the demand engine disappears and the thin float swings both ways. Fundamentally, SPCX is extremely richly valued at ~$2.1T market cap for a three-week-old listing. Not a clean setup, except for very short-term event traders with a tight stop.

Timeframe: The event is pinpoint-dated for Tuesday, 07-07 — forced buying concentrates around the index rebalance. Re-evaluate in the 3-5 trading days after, once it's clear whether the price holds the buying pressure or falls back.

Bear (~25%): "buy the rumor, sell the news" — the buying was priced in, pullback toward the IPO zone of $135-150. Counter-scenario: if SPCX breaks below $150 after the rebalance, the index hype was the entire premium.

Price evidence: Nasdaq-100 inclusion 2026-07-07, ~$4.3B forced buying, 3-5% free float; SPCX $162.00 (07-04), IPO $135 (06-12), day one ~$160.95 (+19%), market cap ~$2.1T.[4][5]

Amazon: the quiet $20B chip thesis

Less headline-grabbing but fundamentally stronger than any single day's move: Amazon's own silicon (Graviton + Trainium + Nitro) has surpassed an annualized run-rate of over $20B — with > 100% year-over-year growth, according to CEO Andy Jassy on the Q1 2026 call (04-30).[8] Jassy's own math: if the chips were a standalone business sold to AWS and third parties at market prices, the run-rate would be ~$50B/year.[8]

Why are in-house chips a bullish case for AMZN? Right now AWS pays an Nvidia markup for every AI server. If Amazon builds the chips itself (Trainium for AI training, Graviton for standard compute), it saves that margin and becomes less dependent on a single supplier. It's like a supermarket chain launching its own house brand: same product, but the margin stays in-house instead of going to a supplier. And with OpenAI locking in ~2GW and Anthropic up to 5GW of Trainium capacity through AWS, the demand is already there.

The catalyst on the horizon: AWS AI chief Peter DeSantis told Bloomberg that AWS is in talks to make Trainium usable outside AWS as well — meaning selling chips externally.[9] That would turn an internal cost lever into a real revenue segment. Price context: AMZN traded pre-market on 07-06 at ~$242.67 (prior close $241.70).[10]

My quietest favorite story is Amazon. Amazon's own chips have crossed an annual run-rate of over twenty billion dollars, with more than one hundred percent growth. Sold at market prices, that would even be fifty billion. And AWS is currently negotiating to sell those chips externally too. That makes Amazon less dependent on Nvidia and supports cloud margins — an AI infrastructure lever the stock barely pays for yet. To me, that's a buy with room to add.

In crypto, the turn is holding. Bitcoin sits around sixty-three thousand four hundred dollars, and the first fund inflow in ten days — two hundred twenty-one point seven million dollars from July second — hasn't reversed over the holiday. Cautious accumulating remains justified, but I want to see several days of inflows. Ethereum, after its strong prior week, is now moving in lockstep with Bitcoin, its relative strength has flattened out.

In commodities, gold is slightly weaker at around four thousand one hundred fifty-three dollars but holding its elevated level — the weak labor market keeps supporting it. Oil stays soft, Brent around seventy-two dollars, near its lowest since late February, as tensions in the Strait of Hormuz have eased. Cheap oil, to me, is mainly a Fed signal: less inflation, more room to cut rates.

The next big date is tomorrow's SpaceX index inclusion, then this week the question of whether crypto inflows hold, and finally the central bank's rate decision on July twenty-ninth. That's what I'll be watching in upcoming briefs.
