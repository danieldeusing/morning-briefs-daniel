The jobs report carrying everything

The most important number of the week isn't a price number — it's a macro number. The US June jobs report (released Thursday, 07/02) showed just +57,000 new jobs — against a consensus of 115,000 and well below the revised May figure of 129,000.[1] The unemployment rate did fall to 4.2%, but for the wrong reason: labor force participation slid to 61.5% — the lowest level since March 2021.[1] On top of that, April (−31,000) and May (−43,000) were revised down: a combined 74,000 fewer jobs than originally reported.[2]

Why is a weak labor market good for stocks? It sounds contradictory, but it's the central market logic right now. The Federal Reserve raises rates when the economy runs hot and wages drive inflation. A weak jobs report says: the economy is cooling, no overheating risk — so no reason to raise rates, if anything room to cut. Lower rates make stocks, gold, and crypto more attractive (bonds then pay less). It's like driving: when the engine cools, the driver eases off the brake. "Bad" economic data is often "good" price news on the market — as long as the cooldown doesn't tip into a recession.

That's exactly why stock futures rose right after the report: traders lowered their expectations for a rate hike.[3] The policy rate has held steady since the last FOMC decision (06/17) at 3.50–3.75% (12–0 vote).[4] For the next meeting, the market is now pricing a 75.6% probability of a hold (CME FedWatch, as of 07/04).[5]

Why is Brazil falling while the US rises? The two markets don't move in sync. The Ibovespa is heavily driven by foreign capital: when international investors turn risk-averse or see better opportunities elsewhere, they pull money out of Brazil — the index falls even as US stocks rise. Add in local factors: nervousness over the Brazilian election cycle and the start of trade talks between Brazil and the US, whose outcome is still open. For Daniel, who lives in reais and partly thinks in euros/dollars, this matters: a weak Ibovespa plus foreign outflows can pressure the real — the same move that makes his euro bills cheaper or pricier in reais.

The drivers per the report: foreign capital outflows, growing concern over the Brazilian election scenario, and the start of Brazil–US trade talks.[7] It's not a Brazil-specific crisis, but the classic emerging-market sensitivity to global positioning — sharpened by local politics.

Crypto core: the ETF turnaround has broken down

The turnaround does NOT hold Flashback: US spot Bitcoin ETFs pulled in a net inflow of $221.7 million on Thursday, 07/02 — the first after a ten-day outflow streak (Fidelity's FBTC +$165.96M, ARKB +$91.84M; BlackRock's IBIT, meanwhile, −$40.43M, its 11th straight day of outflows).[9] That looked like a turnaround. Spot BTC ETF flows: An ETF is an exchange-traded basket that holds real bitcoin so you can buy BTC like a stock. "Net inflow" means more money in than out — the ETF buys bitcoin and supports the price. "Net outflow" is the opposite: the ETF sells, which is selling pressure. A single green day after ten red ones is like a ray of sunshine in a downpour — nice, but not a change in the weather. That's exactly confirmed by the $526.6 million outflow afterward: institutional buyers haven't come back yet.
