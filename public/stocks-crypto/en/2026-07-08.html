Oil: the calendar matters more than the headline

Brent rose on 08.07. to 76,35 $/Bbl (+2,95 % vs. the previous day).[1] Al Jazeera dates the September future on the same day at 76,07 $, "roughly 3 percent" higher and the highest level since 23.06.[2] — a 0,37 % gap between spot and future, so within the expected range and not a source conflict. The evening before (07.07., 17:01 ET), Forbes had Brent at 75,78 $ (+2,89 %) and WTI at 72,20 $ (+5,32 %).[3] Today WTI stands at 72,2–72,8 $ — the TradingEconomics page shows two slightly different intraday prints in its running text and its overview table (72,57 $ / +3,02 % vs. 72,80 $ / +3,37 %); I'm citing the range rather than a false precision.[6]

The trigger: the IRGC attacked three tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. The US responded with strikes on more than 80 targets — air defenses, command networks, coastal radars, anti-ship missiles, more than 60 IRGC fast boats — and the Treasury revoked the oil license.[4][5] Bloomberg calls it a "new test of the truce."[4] An analyst quoted by Al Jazeera expects Hormuz transit to stay "many months" below 50 % of pre-war levels.[2] That's the difference between a spike and a regime.

Why a revoked "license" weighs more than an airstrike: Sanctions normally forbid buying Iranian oil. A General License is the exception to that — an official permission slip that says: "these sales are okay." Two weeks ago, Washington issued license X as part of an interim peace deal; it would have run until 21.08. Now X is revoked — new purchases and loadings have been barred since 07.07. The much tighter license X1 only covers winding down already-authorized transactions, and it expires Fri, 17.07., 00:01 EDT.[2][5] An airstrike is a headline that fades. A permission slip that becomes invalid on a known date is an appointment in every oil trader's calendar: from that point on, barrels are missing, no matter how quiet the news cycle is. That's why this kind of premium sticks around — it has an expiration date, not a news cycle.
Hormuz attack→ License revoked (07.07.)→ Oil more expensive→ Headline inflation→ Fed hesitates→ Stocks/crypto lose tailwind

Gold reacts surprisingly quietly — and that's the information

Gold stands on 08.07. at 4.127,19 $/oz (+0,52 % vs. the previous day); over the month −3,14 %, over the year +24,55 %.[7] A second provider reads 4.128,32 $ at the same time — a 0,03 % gap, well under the 0.5 % flag, a clean cross-check.[8]

And that's exactly the point. On the same day oil jumps nearly 3 % because the US is bombing more than 80 targets in Iran, gold moves by half a percent. If this were systemic fear — war, flight to safety — gold would have to lead, not trail behind.

Why pricier oil pulls gold in two directions: Gold pays no interest, so it competes with bonds that do. When oil rises, measured inflation rises — and inflation erodes cash, which favors gold. But that same inflation forces the central bank to keep rates high — and the more bonds pay, the less attractive interest-free gold becomes. It's a tug-of-war between "money is losing value" (pulling up) and "bonds pay well again" (pulling down). That gold gains only 0.5 % while oil jumps 3 % means both ropes are pulling nearly equally hard. The market reads the Iran strikes as an oil-supply event, not the end of the world.

Tesla: a record quarter, the worst day in a year

Tesla closed on 07.07. at 402,90 $, −16,87 $ (−4,02 %).[9] That was already the second blow: on 02.07. the stock plunged −7,49 % — the worst trading day in almost a year.[10] The occasion was good news. Tesla reported 480.126 vehicles delivered in the second quarter that same day (against 451.758 produced and 13,5 GWh of storage deployment) — roughly 74.000 units above analyst expectations and a record.[10][11]

Why a record result wrecks the stock ("sell the news"): A stock price isn't a grade for the past, it's a price for the future. Whoever bought before the report already paid for the record. When it actually arrives, there's nothing left to gain — buyers sell and take the profit. Like a birthday present whose contents everyone already knows: unwrapping it is no longer an event. The amplifier is called the forward P/E: Tesla costs roughly 204 times the earnings expected over the next twelve months.[10] At a multiple like that, even a record quarter is just "as expected" — what's being paid for is a much bigger day after tomorrow. And that's exactly where the disruption hit: BYD has taken back the global EV sales lead from Tesla.[10]

The spread on 07.07. tells the rest of the story: while TSLA lost −4,02 %, META gained +2,55 %, AMZN +0,75 %, NVDA +0,71 %, MSFT +0,54 %, GOOGL +0,16 %; only AAPL slipped, −0,64 %.[12][13][14] Not a tech sell-off — a valuation problem with the name Tesla.

It's Wednesday, July eighth. Yesterday, cheap oil was still carrying the hope of falling interest rates. Today, that same oil is the crack in the thesis.

The United States struck more than eighty targets in Iran on Tuesday — retaliation for attacks by the Revolutionary Guard on three tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. At the same time, the Treasury revoked the license that had allowed Iranian oil sales. Brent jumped two point nine five percent to seventy-six dollars and thirty-five cents. That's the highest level since June twenty-third.

But more important than the price jump is a date. The revocation has been in force since July seventh; the narrow wind-down license expires on Friday, July seventeenth, at twelve-oh-one a.m. Eastern time. The old one would have run until August twenty-first. An airstrike is a headline that fades — a permit that expires on a fixed date sits in every oil trader's calendar. That's why this kind of premium sticks. I'm turning from neutral to buy here, but only with a small, staggered tranche. Whoever goes all-in the day after a three percent jump is buying other people's panic.

What this means for Daniel is the second-order effect. Pricier oil means higher inflation. Higher inflation means less room for the central bank. And it was exactly that hope for rate cuts that carried stocks, gold and crypto this week. The inflation report in mid-July is the tipping point. That's why I'm withdrawing yesterday's buy call on gold. Gold sits at four thousand one hundred twenty-seven dollars and gained only half a percent — on a day of real military escalation. If geopolitics can't lift gold, the geopolitical part of that thesis isn't being paid. Keep the core position, but stop adding.

The second big story is Tesla. The stock closed Tuesday at four hundred two dollars and ninety cents, down four point oh two percent. Five trading days earlier it had dropped seven point four nine percent — the worst day in almost a year. And that happened on the very day Tesla reported a record quarter: four hundred eighty thousand one hundred twenty-six vehicles delivered, roughly seventy-four thousand more than expected.

When the best operating news in quarters wrecks the stock, the market isn't talking about sales anymore, it's talking about valuation. Tesla trades at roughly two hundred four times expected annual profit. At that multiple, even a record is just "as expected." On top of that, BYD has reclaimed the global lead in electric vehicle sales. My call here is clear: sell or avoid, and trim existing positions before July twenty-second. That's when Tesla reports quarterly earnings, and the real topic will be margin, not the delivery number we already know. The rest of Big Tech, by the way, was green — Meta gained two point five five percent. This isn't a tech sell-off, it's a valuation problem with one name: Tesla.
