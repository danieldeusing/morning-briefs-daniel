And one that's decoupling: Nvidia +3.65% on an otherwise red day — the market is treating AI capex as secular growth, immune to the rate fear that hit the software Mag 7.

The split: why the Dow and Nasdaq diverged on the same day

08.07. wasn't a uniform day. The Dow lost 576.76 points (−1.09%) to 52,348.39 — two trading days after its record close of 53,055.91 (06.07.).[3] The broad S&P 500 gave up just −0.28% to 7,482.71[1], and the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite even added +0.20% to 25,870.65.[2] Three indexes, three different signs. The crack isn't random: it separates the rate-sensitive, cyclical old economy (Dow-heavy) from the secular AI growth story (Nasdaq-heavy).

"The market is pricing a rate hike": In the fed funds futures market you can bet on the next central-bank decision — every contract is essentially a vote on where rates will stand in three months. Like a betting exchange before a soccer match, except here the bet is on the policy rate. For weeks the bets sat on "the Fed cuts." This week they flipped: the majority is now betting on "the Fed hikes" — the Fed itself is holding at 3.50–3.75%, but nine of 18 members see at least one hike by year-end, median projection 3.8%.[9][10] Why that moves almost everything: a higher rate makes safe interest-bearing paper more attractive — and by extension makes anything that pays no interest (gold) or only earns far in the future (expensive growth stocks, crypto) relatively less attractive. Expensive oil is the accelerant: it lifts measured inflation, and inflation is exactly what keeps the Fed from cutting.
Iran oil pricier→ Inflation impulse→ Market prices rate hike→ Real-rate expectations ↑, dollar ↑→ Gold, Dow, crypto lose→ only AI hardware decouples

The Mag 7 show the split in pure form. Nvidia jumped +3.65% to $204.12 (52-week range $161.16–236.54)[5], TSM +1.02% to $436.98 and AMD +0.25% to $517.41 — the AI-hardware corner.[6] On the other side, META gave up −2.02% ($603.12), MSFT −1.41% ($383.34), GOOGL −1.39% ($361.92) and AMZN −0.96% ($243.62); AAPL held +0.88% ($313.39).[7][8] Important for honesty's sake: there's no single dated catalyst for Nvidia's daily gain — the source cites "AI infrastructure optimism." I'm not selling that as news, but as what it is: a rotation day where hardware was chosen over software.

Brent extended the Iran rally: the Yahoo future traded on 09.07. at ~04:57 UTC at $78.92[13]; Fortune read $78.17 at 8:30 ET on 08.07., up $4.88 from the previous morning.[14] The two reads sit ~0.96% apart with a ~20-hour sample gap — normal volatility, not a contradiction. WTI stands at $74.38 (09.07.), Yahoo's market board confirms "above $74."[15] The starting point before the escalation was ~$72 — so roughly +6 to +9% in a few days. The market is only now pricing the war premium back in, after having completely priced it out before.

The trigger remains the US Treasury's revocation of the Iranian oil export license after the US strikes — Iranian barrels are leaving the market on a calendar, not on a headline.[4] That's why this premium sticks instead of fading: a supply cut with an expiration date isn't a news cycle. And here's the point for Daniel: oil is today the only building block where the turning inflation/rate picture supports the position instead of undermining it — the same inflation that's capping gold and crypto is the price driver for oil itself.

The real information isn't in the price, it's in the reaction. On a day when the US strikes Iran and Trump declares the truce over, the crisis metal falls. If this were systemic fear, gold would have to lead. Instead, the rate thread wins out: the reversal from rate-cut to rate-hike expectations lifts expected real rates — and the more safe bonds yield, the more expensive it becomes to hold interest-free gold. Pricey oil compounds it twice over, because it keeps the Fed from cutting.

It's Thursday, July ninth. Yesterday it was an oil shock. Today it's the regime behind it that's flipping — and that explains three things at once that don't obviously fit together.

The core of it is a reversal in the rate bet. For weeks the market was betting that the central bank's next move would be a cut. This week it's betting on a hike. The Fed itself is holding at three point five zero to three point seven five percent, but the futures market is now pricing in roughly two-thirds odds of at least one hike by December. The trigger is pricey Iran oil, which is pushing inflation up and keeping the Fed from cutting.

The cleanest illustration is gold. On a day when the United States strikes Iran and President Trump declares the truce over, the crisis metal falls two percent to around four thousand seventy-two dollars — the lowest level since July second. When the safe haven doesn't rally in wartime but falls instead, the rate thread is pulling harder than the fear thread. I'm holding gold as insurance, but not adding. Fresh money belongs in oil this week.

Because oil is the one building block where high inflation helps the position instead of hurting it. Brent climbed further to around seventy-nine dollars, West Texas to just over seventy-four. After the revocation of the Iranian oil license, barrels are leaving the market on a calendar. I'm sticking with buying, but small and staggered — after roughly six to nine percent in just a few days, a good chunk is already priced in.

The second big story is the split in the stock market. The Dow lost five hundred seventy-six points, down one point oh nine percent, two trading days after its record close. The broad S&P gave up only a quarter of a percent — and the tech-heavy Nasdaq actually rose slightly. Three indexes, three different signs. The crack separates the rate-sensitive old economy from the AI growth story.

Big tech shows that split in pure form. Nvidia jumped three point six five percent to two hundred four dollars — the only winning outlier. Its chip neighbors TSM and AMD also gained, while Meta, Microsoft, Google and Amazon slipped. The market is treating AI hardware as secular growth, immune to the rate fear hitting the software names. I'm holding big tech, but not chasing Nvidia's day pop and not buying the software dip — that would mean buying against a regime that's only just turning. Worth being honest about: there's no single catalyst for Nvidia's jump, it was a rotation day.
