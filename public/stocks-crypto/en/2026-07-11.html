SK Hynix's record IPO lifts Nvidia — the green week-close

The last trading day of the week belonged to AI memory. SK Hynix — South Korea's chip giant and Nvidia's most important supplier of high-performance memory — brought its US shares public on 10.07.: opening at $170/share, a pop of over 14% above the issue price, raising $26.5 billion in total. That's, per the market report, the largest US IPO of a foreign company ever.[4] Riding the wake of it, Nvidia jumped +4.03% to $210.96 (+$8.18) — the biggest single move in the Mag 7 field that day.[2] The broader market followed quietly along: S&P 500 7,575.39 (+0.42%), Dow 52,637.01 (+0.29%), Nasdaq 26,281.61 (+0.29%).[1]

HBM (High Bandwidth Memory): This is the ultra-fast memory stacked right next to the AI chip. Picture the desk beside the worker, holding the papers for the current task — the bigger and faster that desk, the less the chip has to wait for fresh supplies. Nvidia's GPUs are only as fast as their HBM can feed them, and SK Hynix is the top supplier of exactly this memory stack. A record IPO by this supplier is therefore no isolated stock event: the market is paying up front for years of AI-memory demand — and that's also a bet on Nvidia's end demand. That's why Nvidia moves in tandem the same day.

The rest of the Mag 7 at the 10.07. close was quiet and mixed: AAPL $315.32 (−0.28%), MSFT $385.10 (+0.19%), GOOGL $357.18 (−0.48%), AMZN $245.34 (+0.15%), META $669.21 (−0.18%), TSLA $407.76 (practically unchanged), and in the AI-infrastructure corner AVGO (Broadcom) $399.97 (+0.11%).[3] Tesla holds above the $400 mark, but with no fresh catalyst — my SELL thesis stays unchanged; the formal re-rating falls on the Q2 numbers on 22.07., where margin is what counts, not the already-published delivery count. The real story of the day, though, remains Nvidia: after the ~$1 trillion valuation reset since its May record, the cheapened AI story gets, with the SK Hynix IPO, its first dated demand evidence from the supply side again.

Oil: the war premium breathes, but holds for the week

After the multi-day Iran run, oil closes the week a bit calmer: Brent trades around $76/bbl (10.07., −0.39% versus the prior day), WTI around $71.[6][7] The real context, though, is the weekly tally: despite Friday's pullback, Brent stands at +~5% for the week — the markup from the ceasefire breakdown is still in there.[7] The trigger was 08.07.: President Trump declared the ceasefire "over" after Iran attacked three merchant ships in the Strait of Hormuz and the US retaliated — that day, Brent jumped +5.43% to $78.19, WTI +4.37% to $73.52.[8]

Risk premium (oil): The markup the market pays not because less oil is flowing today, but because it could get scarce tomorrow. Like an insurance premium that rises the moment a storm appears on the horizon — even before a single drop of rain falls. As long as no fresh Hormuz headline shows up, this markup drifts down overnight (Friday −0.39%); the moment one does, it's back instantly. That's why Friday's pullback isn't a disproof of the thesis, but the expected breathing of the premium.

For my running position, that means: the framework is intact. The Strait of Hormuz — the passage for roughly one-fifth of the world's oil — remains the standing catalyst, and the license deadline on 17.07. flagged most recently (in yesterday's brief) would be a supply withdrawal by calendar, not by headline. Oil is also the only one of my long positions that benefits from an inflation impulse — a useful counterweight to gold and crypto.

Crypto core: the flow leg turns green — tentatively

Bitcoin trades on 11.07. at 05:06 UTC at $64,109 (+0.28% in 24h) (CoinGecko API); the Fortune cross-check from 10.07. 06:30 ET reads $64,340.78 — 0.36% deviation, consistent.[12] That puts BTC roughly 10% above its 21-month low of about $57,950, reached "earlier in the week" in early July (the lowest level in 652 days).[13] Ethereum tags along: $1,794.99 (+1.26% 24h, CoinGecko; Fortune $1,796.10).[16]

The real story is in the flows. June was the worst month for the US spot Bitcoin ETFs since their launch in January 2024 — roughly $4.5 billion in net outflows, part of an eight-week outflow streak topping $8.2 billion.[14] Early July turns the picture for the first time: a $221 million inflow on 03.07. (ending a ten-day, $2.73 billion outflow streak), followed by a $510 million run over three sessions (07.–09.07.) — recovering roughly 9% of the capital that exited in 2026.[15]

ETF flows: How much money flows net into or out of the regulated Bitcoin funds. Picture yourself counting at the shop door how many customers walk in with full bags and how many walk out empty-handed. A net inflow means more institutional buyers than sellers passing through the fund door — the best publicly measurable proxy for the "big money." After eight weeks in which more went out than came in, a $510 million run in one week is the first signal that the tide might be turning.
The trigger On 08.–10.07. I set two conditions for the second Bitcoin tranche: price above $64,000 and a real, multi-day inflow reversal (not a single print). Today both legs are tentatively green — the price ($64,109) above the line and the $510 million run over three sessions as flow evidence. The honest caveat: the flow numbers remain contested depending on the source (other sources read 08./09.07. as outflow days), farside.co.uk isn't independently verifiable due to a 403 error, and YTD still shows ~$5.4 billion in net outflows. So: green enough to start the second tranche staggered — not green enough to deploy it in one go.
