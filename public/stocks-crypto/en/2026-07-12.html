Nvidia's jump has a concrete, dated driver: Beijing is reportedly considering, for the first time again, a — though capped — release of the H200 chip to the China giants Alibaba, ByteDance, and DeepSeek. Under discussion is fewer than 200,000 units, less than half of what was requested.[3] That sounds small but is significant: under the previous export restrictions, Nvidia's data-center revenue in China fell from roughly $4.6 billion a year to practically zero. Any partial reopening brings back a market that the stock had long since written off — hence the +7.9%.

Export controls (chips): Washington bans Nvidia from selling its fastest AI chips to China — out of concern they could fuel the military and surveillance there. Imagine the best baker in town suddenly can't sell his premium bread in an entire neighborhood anymore: revenue there drops to zero even though customers want it. If the authorities now allow a partial shipment (the capped < 200,000 H200 units), a piece of that neighborhood comes back — and the stock is pricing in exactly that reopening.

Two notes of caution: according to Reuters, DeepSeek is simultaneously building its own AI chip to reduce its Nvidia dependence — so the reopening could be time-limited. Oil: A Real Crisis, a Surprisingly Calm Price

Brent closes at $76.01/bbl (10.07., −0.38% vs. the prior day), WTI at $71.41.[6][7] For the week, Brent is up +~5% — noticeable, but downright modest given the situation. Because the situation is serious: after the ceasefire signed on 17.06. broke down, the US struck more than 80 Iran targets in early July following tanker attacks in the Strait of Hormuz; shipping through the strait came nearly to a standstill according to the UN and Bloomberg, with around 6,000 seafarers stranded.[5] Roughly a fifth of the world's oil and gas normally passes through Hormuz; the IEA calls it the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market".[6]

And yet: no $100 oil, but $76 with a red Friday. That's the real story. The market is pricing the crisis as limited — it's betting that the conflict stays contained and the technical US-Iran talks keep going. So the low price isn't proof that nothing's happening, but a collective bet that it won't escalate.

Risk premium (oil): The markup the market pays — not because less oil is flowing today, but because it might get scarce tomorrow. Like an insurance premium that rises the moment a storm appears on the horizon, before a single drop falls. That Brent sits at just $76 despite a near-standstill in Hormuz means: the market rates the probability of a real, lasting blockade as low. If that assumption breaks — another tanker attack, collapsed talks — the premium snaps right back.

Recommendation — Oil Exposure (Brent/WTI)

Verdict: HOLD — keep a small core position; don't chase the thin premium, but don't sell the position into Friday's weakness either. A small tail hedge against real escalation is warranted.

Why (in detail): Two mistakes are pitted against each other here. Mistake one: chasing the crisis and paying for $90 oil that's already come back down — Friday's pullback (−0.38%) shows how fast the premium breathes. Mistake two: selling the position entirely and then getting run over by a real Hormuz blockade that carries Brent toward $95–100 overnight. The disciplined middle ground is HOLD: $76 is a fair, barely-overpaid hedge against exactly the escalation nobody can predict. Oil is also the only one of my long ideas that benefits from an inflation impulse — a useful counterweight to gold and crypto, both of which suffer under a hawkish Fed.

Timeframe: The binary catalyst is the trajectory of the US-Iran talks and Hormuz shipping this week (13.–17.07.) — every new tanker report moves it instantly. Macro litmus test: FOMC 28.–29.07.[9] (a hawkish, strong dollar caps oil). Hold horizon is weeks; add in tranches only on a confirmed escalation, not on the first headline.

Forecast: Base case (~55%): Brent ranges $72–82 while the market prices the crisis as limited and talks continue. Bull-for-oil (~20%): a real Hormuz blockade or new tanker attacks → > $95, the thin premium spikes. Bear (~25%): the ceasefire stabilizes or OPEC+ fills the gap → back to < $70, premium evaporates. Counter-scenario: if Brent falls below $70 despite an ongoing Hormuz disruption, the market would have written off the crisis completely — then the tail hedge is superfluous and I'd go neutral.

Prices as evidence: Brent $76.01 (−0.38% d/d, TradingEconomics 10.07., matching Yahoo futures BZ=F $76.01); WTI $71.41 (Yahoo CL=F 10.07.); weekly Brent tally +~5%; drivers: Hormuz near-standstill, US strikes on 80+ Iran targets after tanker attacks (UN/Bloomberg/CNN, 09.–11.07.), IEA "largest supply disruption in history".[5][6][7]

Gold: Well Below the Record, Stabilized — but Without Its Own Momentum

Gold sits at $4,121.05/oz (spot, TradingEconomics 10.07., −0.06%); the futures cross-check reads $4,113.70 (Yahoo GC=F) — a 0.18% spread, clean basis, no divergence flag.[10] That's roughly 27% below the all-time high of $5,589.38 (28.01.2026): gold briefly dipped below $4,000 in late June and has since stabilized around $4,100 — still supported by Gulf geopolitics and Fed uncertainty. But gold is missing its strongest driver: the dollar is holding firm (DXY 100.97, Yahoo futures 10.07.), and the Warsh Fed is flagging hike risk instead of cuts — rising real rates are gold's natural headwind.[10]

Real rates & gold: Gold pays no interest. When safe government bonds yield decently after inflation, money migrates there — gold becomes relatively unattractive. It's like the choice between an interest-bearing savings account and a gold coin in a vault: as the savings rate rises, the coin's appeal fades. That's why a Fed hinting at hikes instead of cuts is the lid on gold — even as a Gulf crisis pushes the other way.

Recommendation — Gold (XAU/USD)

Verdict: HOLD — keep the core position as insurance, don't add.

Why (in detail): Gold is doing exactly what insurance is supposed to do: after the roughly 27% decline from the January record ($5,589), it has settled around ~$4,100 and carries the geopolitical risk in the portfolio. The function is intact — you don't sell the fire extinguisher while the building's on fire. But there's no reason to add: a metal with no trend of its own, hanging on the rate/dollar thread, stays capped as long as the Warsh Fed flags hike risk and the DXY sticks around ~101. Fresh money this week belongs more in ideas with their own catalyst (the oil tail, the BTC flow question) than in trendless gold sitting 27% below its high — the risk/reward there is worse.

ETF flows & BTC dominance: ETF flows measure how much money net flows into the regulated Bitcoin funds — the best publicly measurable proxy for "big institutional cash." If you count more full bags than empty ones at the shop door, that's a net inflow. Dominance shows how big Bitcoin's share of the whole crypto pie is: if it climbs to 56%, money is hiding in BTC and fleeing the smaller coins — which is exactly what's happening right now while the altcoins bleed.
The Trigger The second BTC tranche still requires the double signal: price stable above $64,000 and a confirmed multi-week inflow. Last week's turnaround was fragile — exactly the kind of signal you don't bet the whole position on at once. I'm holding the first tranche and waiting for the new week's flow prints before adding.
