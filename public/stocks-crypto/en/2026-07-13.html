Oil: the premium missing on Friday is back

Friday, the calm was the news — Brent at $76 despite the Gulf crisis. Today, the premium's return is the news. Brent jumps +4.31% to $79.28/bbl, WTI +4.36% to $74.52 (Yahoo futures BZ=F/CL=F, 07-13 ~05:00 UTC; TradingEconomics reads Brent at $79.29, +4.32% — a spread of <0.2%, a clean basis).[5] The trigger: over the weekend the US struck Iran again — in retaliation for an Iranian attack on a Cyprus-flagged container ship on 07-11. In response, Iran's Revolutionary Guard (IRGC) declared the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly one-fifth of the world's oil and gas flows, "closed until further notice" on 07-12. The US CENTCOM disputes this explicitly — "Iran does not control the strait, traffic is flowing" — and enforcement remains an open question. Yet the real numbers show a break: according to Windward Maritime Intelligence, transits fell to around 21 on 07-11 — versus ~140 per day before the war.[6]

Risk premium (oil): The markup the market pays — not because less oil is flowing today, but because it might get scarce tomorrow. Like storm insurance whose premium rises the moment clouds gather on the horizon, even before a drop falls. On Friday this premium was surprisingly thin ($76); today, after fresh strikes, it shoots up ($79). That very mobility is the point: the premium breathes with every headline.

The counterweight comes from the supply side: OPEC+ is raising its August output targets by +188,000 bpd, and the UAE ramped its June production above 3.8 million bpd — the highest level since April 2020.[7] So two forces face off: geopolitical supply fear (Hormuz) against real additional supply (OPEC+). As long as the strait stays open, the August surge caps the price; if Hormuz truly closes, the extra barrel won't matter.

Recommendation — Oil exposure (Brent/WTI)

Verdict: HOLD — keep a small core position, but don't chase the +4.3% spike. Anyone who put on a tactical tranche into Friday's weakness should trim it here into strength.

Why (in detail): The tail-hedge idea — hold a small oil position against exactly the scenario the market considers unlikely — partly paid off over the weekend: the "~25% blockade" I was still pricing as a distant risk yesterday is today a declared reality. Iran has formally declared the strait closed, transits have collapsed according to Windward (~21 instead of ~140/day), and Brent is up +4.3%. But that is not a buy signal — it's the reason to hold rather than chase, for two reasons. First, the situation is genuinely binary and disputed: the US CENTCOM disputes the closure and reports traffic still flowing; if that's confirmed, the premium will evaporate as quickly as it appeared. Buying into a +4.3% spike that a single "traffic is flowing again" headline can wipe out is expensive. Second, the position shouldn't be sold either: if the declared closure is actually enforced, or further tanker attacks follow, OPEC+'s +188,000 bpd increase becomes irrelevant and oil runs meaningfully higher — and oil is the only one of my long ideas that benefits from an inflation impulse, a useful counterweight to gold and crypto, both of which suffer under a hawkish Fed. The disciplined middle ground is HOLD.

Timeframe: The binary catalyst is whether the declared Hormuz closure gets enforced this week (07-13 to 07-17) or whether CENTCOM keeps traffic flowing — every new tanker, transit, or de-escalation report moves it instantly. Supply litmus test: the OPEC+ August ramp-up, which takes full effect from August. Macro: FOMC 07-28 to 07-29.[9] (a hawkish, strong dollar caps oil). Holding horizon of weeks; add tranches only on confirmed enforcement of the closure, not on the declaration alone.

Forecast: Base case (~45%): the closure stays declared-but-disputed, transits suppressed but not at zero — Brent holds the premium and oscillates between $78–88. Bull for oil (~30%): the closure gets enforced / holds, or fresh tanker attacks follow → >$95, the premium explodes. Bear (~25%): CENTCOM turns out right — traffic keeps flowing, Iran backs down — plus the full August ramp-up → back below $74, the premium evaporates like it did Friday. Counter-scenario: if Brent falls back below $74 despite the declared closure, the market has believed CENTCOM and written off the closure as a bluff — then the tail hedge becomes redundant and I move to neutral.

Gold: falling despite the Gulf escalation — the dollar wins

The oddest chart of the day: the same Gulf escalation driving oil +4% is pushing gold −1.25% to $4,069.47/oz (TradingEconomics 07-13; Yahoo futures GC=F reads $4,066.40, −0.92% — a 0.07% spread, a clean basis).[8] On the month, gold is down −5.6%. Why does the classic crisis hedge fall while the crisis escalates? Because the other, stronger thread is pulling harder: the dollar is holding firm (DXY 101.07, Yahoo futures 07-13), and the Fed is flagging hike risk rather than cuts. A metal that pays no yield loses out against firm real rates — and today that thread outweighs geopolitics.

Real rates & gold: Gold pays no interest. When safe government bonds yield well after inflation, money migrates there — gold becomes relatively less attractive. It's like choosing between an interest-bearing savings account and a gold coin in a safe: when the savings-account rate rises, the coin's appeal fades. That's why gold can even fall while a Gulf crisis pushes the other way — as long as the dollar is firm and the Fed isn't signaling a cut.

But a metal with no uptrend of its own, chained to firm real rates, down −5.6% on the month and roughly 27% below its January record, gives no reason to add. This week, fresh money has better risk/reward in ideas with their own catalyst (the oil tail hedge) than in trendless gold.

Counter-scenario: if gold turns sustainably above $4,250 despite a firm dollar and a hawkish Fed, the market is buying it as insurance after all — then my rate-driven read was wrong and adding would be justified.

If the first big grower (Samsung) publicly doubts the boom will really continue, everyone fears the greenhouses could stand empty — and the construction suppliers (the chip stocks) fall first. That's exactly what the $290B day was.

Crypto core: Bitcoin below Strategy's cost basis — the big holder is selling

Bitcoin is trading on 07-13 at 05:07 UTC at $62,709 (−1.96% 24h) (CoinGecko API; Binance reads $62,786, a spread of ~0.12%).[10] The trajectory is sobering: from above $93,000 at the start of the year to a 21-month low under $60,000 in the last week of June, now at ~$62,700.[10] The total crypto market stands at $2.249T (−1.31% 24h), BTC dominance 56.0% — rising dominance means capital is fleeing smaller coins back into Bitcoin.[13]

The structural news is Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy): between 06-29 and 07-05 the company sold 3,588 BTC for roughly $216M — its largest Bitcoin sale since the 2022 tax transaction — to cover preferred-stock dividends and rebuild its USD reserve (~$2.55B as of 07-05). After the sale, Strategy still holds 843,775 BTC at an average cost basis of ~$75,476/BTC — with spot at $62,700, the position is thus about 17% underwater.[11]

Cost basis underwater & forced sellers: The "cost basis" is the average price someone paid — here ~$75,476. If the current price ($62,700) is below that, the position is "underwater": a paper loss. That alone would be harmless. It becomes critical because Strategy has to make ongoing payments (preferred-stock dividends) and is now selling Bitcoin instead of buying it to fund them. The market's biggest long-term buyer for years is turning into a seller — like the regular customer who always bought out half the bakery suddenly selling bread at the counter himself. That's a supply overhang hanging over the price.

On ETF flows the picture is now settled (Farside Investors): on 07-10, US spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded a net inflow of +$90.4M (BlackRock IBIT +$86.8M, VanEck HODL +$3.6M), with the ETH counterparts at +$18.4M. The monthly context stays weak: June brought roughly $4.5B in net outflows, with IBIT accounting for ~75% of redemptions.[12]

The US military disputes that and says traffic is still flowing — but actual transits through the strait, according to the shipping tracker Windward, have collapsed to around twenty-one, down from roughly one hundred forty a day before the war. Monday morning, Brent jumps four point three percent to seventy-nine dollars, West Texas right along with it. The thin war premium is suddenly back.

For me, the call stays hold. The tail hedge partly paid off — the very blockade I was still pricing as a distant risk yesterday is today's declared reality.
