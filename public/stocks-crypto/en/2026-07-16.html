"Sell the news": The market often runs on "buy the rumor, sell the news." When everyone already expects a piece of good news, it's priced in before it even happens; when it then actually arrives, there's no fresh reason left to buy — some traders even take profits. Like a kid who's been excited about a promised gift for weeks: the moment of unwrapping it is smaller than the anticipation. The fact that Nvidia only gains +0.33% on the now-confirmed China shipments means the market had already bought this approval long ago — the next push has to come from hard numbers, not from the headline.

The common thread stays the cool CPI: lower expected rates lift rate-sensitive growth names. But today's leadership is more defensive than yesterday's — Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet are the balance-sheet havens, not the speculative chips. Today brings the evidence test: TSMC reports its full Q2 numbers (07-16); the preliminary figure already stands at +36% y/y to $39.62B in revenue, with AI chips making up 61% of it.[4] A strong outlook would expose the flat Nvidia reaction as a mere pause; a weak one would confirm AMD's weakness and drag the whole chip cohort back down.

Oil: The premium bleeds off — but tomorrow the sanctions lever swings shut

Oil is slowly giving back its war premium: Brent trades at $84.56/bbl (−0.46% d/d), WTI at $79.38 (−0.28%) (Yahoo futures, 07-16 04:58 UTC; TradingEconomics read WTI at $80.17 on 07-15 — a ~1% spread over the ~12-hour sampling gap).[6] That's a pullback from the 1-month high of $85.92, which the September contracts marked at 08:00 GMT on 07-14, when the US struck Iran.[7] But the pullback isn't an all-clear signal — quite the opposite: the real lever doesn't swing shut until tomorrow. The permission granted last month to sell Iranian oil until August 21 will be revoked starting Fri 07-17 at 04:01 GMT (12:01 AM EDT on 07-17). The physical disruption is already real: Hormuz transits fell > 50% w/w (57 transits Fri–Sun versus the prior week).[7]

Sanctions waiver: A special permit allowing an otherwise-banned deal to go ahead for a limited time — here, buying Iranian oil despite sanctions until a set deadline. Like an exception permit with an expiration date: while it's in force, the oil flows legally; once it lapses, that supply vanishes from the world market overnight. That's why the revocation is a bullish oil catalyst — on 07-17 it pulls real barrels out of supply, into a Hormuz flow that's already disrupted.

That keeps oil the most valuable special role in the portfolio: the only long idea that benefits from an inflation impulse rather than suffering from it. Gold, crypto, and expensive growth stocks all like the "cool CPI → less rate pressure" thesis; oil is the natural counterweight. The offsetting force remains the supply side — OPEC+ keeps ramping up output from August, which can dampen the scarcity fear as long as the strait doesn't actually close.

The softer dollar (DXY ~100.5, −0.13% on 07-15) provides slight support.[9]

Why a cool inflation print can push gold down: Gold is insurance — among other things, against runaway inflation. When a number shows inflation is actually receding, the fear you were insuring against with gold shrinks — so some sell the policy and gold eases back. Like storm insurance whose price falls the moment the weather forecast gives the all-clear. That's why gold can fall on a good inflation day — the round $4,000 mark is the psychological line where it's decided whether it stays insurance.

Macro Anchor: the cool CPI and the road to the FOMC

The whole week's tone hangs on one number. The June CPI came in at 3.5% headline inflation (down from 4.2% in May), below the consensus of 3.8%; the monthly rate fell −0.4% — the largest one-month drop since April 2020 —, the core rate sits at 2.6% y/y (down from 2.9%).[1] It's the coolest print in months and the cause of the relief rally: less inflation means less rate pressure, and stocks, gold, and crypto all benefit from that at once — with oil as the one exception that lives off the inflation impulse.

CPI & core rate: The "Consumer Price Index" measures how much consumer prices have risen — the official inflation number. The "core rate" strips out the volatile energy and food prices to show the underlying trend. The Fed steers its rates off this number: if inflation is too high, it holds or hikes; if it falls, it can cut. That's why a single CPI morning can decide the direction of stocks, gold, and crypto all at once.

The cool print shifts the calculus for the next fixed point: the FOMC on 07-28–07-29 (decision Wed 07-29). CME FedWatch prices in a ~0% probability of a cut at that meeting — the Fed holds the 3.50–3.75% corridor; the first cut isn't priced in until Sep/Oct, with a year-end target of 3.25–3.50%.[14] So the soft CPI supports the hold thesis for July without pulling the cut forward. Until then, earnings season sets the tape's pace: TSMC today (07-16), then Tesla + Alphabet (07-22), Microsoft + Meta (07-29), and Apple + Amazon (07-30); Nvidia's own test follows on 08-26.[15] The common denominator across my hold calls stays: gold, crypto, and expensive growth stocks all breathe easier when the Fed holds rather than hikes — the one real headwind comes from Hormuz, where higher oil (amplified by tomorrow's sanctions revocation) could bring back the inflation that the CPI just calmed down.
