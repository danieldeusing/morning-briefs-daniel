Semiconductors: TSMC raises capex — and the market sells the chips

Today's trigger is a number that's actually good news. TSMC raised its capex plan for the current year to $60–64B — up from a prior $52–56B.[3] TSMC manufactures the chips Nvidia designs. When the foundry raises its fab spending by roughly a fifth, it's saying in plain terms: we're seeing so much demand that today's capacity isn't enough. The textbook reflex to that is: buy semiconductors.

The market did the opposite. The Nasdaq Composite fell −1.47% to 25,881.95, the S&P 500 −0.51% to 7,533.77, the Dow −0.20% (−105.67 points) to 52,552.97.[1] Nvidia lost −2.40% to $207.40 — roughly $123B in market value, remaining market cap $5.02T.[2] The CNBC wrap names the mechanism without hedging: "US stocks closed lower on Thursday, as weakness in the semiconductor sector and geopolitical risks overshadowed solid corporate results."[1]

Capex (capital expenditure): The money a company spends not on day-to-day operations, but on things that last for years — factories, machinery, data centers. Picture a bakery buying a second oven: that's capex. The oven says two things at once, and that's exactly where the ambiguity lies — it's a demand signal ("so many loaves that one oven isn't enough") and a cost bet ("this oven still has to earn itself back"). Until yesterday, the market read TSMC's ovens as a demand signal. Today it reads them as a cost bet.

This shift in interpretation is the real story — not the one red day. This is the second session in which clearly AI-demand-positive news fails to lift the chips — though I need to be precise here, because the weaker reading is the more honest one. The H200 news did work at first: when a US trade official confirmed the first H200 shipments to China before Congress on Tue 07-14 (volume: "trivial"), Nvidia jumped roughly +4.1% to $211.80.[24] Only none of it held: by Wed 07-15, the follow-through had already stalled at +0.33% to $212.50, and on 07-16 the stock falls −2.40% on a strong capex signal. So the finding isn't "no reaction" — it's the narrower one: the pop had faded within a single session, and the next piece of good AI news was traded straight into the red. That means I have to correct my own forecast from yesterday: my bear scenario required a weak TSMC outlook to pressure the chips. The outlook was strong, and they fell anyway. That's worse than my bear case, not better — it's not the fundamental that flipped, it's the valuation frame. A good number helps against a weak fundamental. Against a flipped frame, it currently doesn't.

The control check sits in the same price table. Apple rose +1.76% to $333.26 — another high above yesterday's record close — and Microsoft +1.38% to $401.10.[2] If this were broad risk-off, they'd be red too. They're not. Capital is not fleeing Big Tech; it's rotating out of semiconductors into names whose earnings don't depend on a data center's return. The VIX closed at 16.73 (+6.76%, prior day 15.67)[4] — higher, but historically calm. This isn't panic, it's a re-sorting — and that's more dangerous, because it doesn't price itself out in a single day.

Alphabet: $200 billion for a delivery date

Alphabet fell −4.44% to $354.46, making it the weakest Mag-7 stock of the day.[2] At a remaining market cap of $4.33T, that's roughly $200B in erased market value in a single session. The trigger: a Bloomberg report that Google is behind its own schedule delivering Gemini 3.5 Pro.[5] Aggregators name today — Fri 07-17 — as the targeted release date, unconfirmed by Google; the model itself, its specs, and its significance belong to the ai-dev brief.[6] Here, only what the ticker does with it counts.

And the ticker is making something out of it that doesn't fit the numbers. Gemini isn't a meaningful near-term revenue line — Alphabet's earnings come from Search and Cloud. Consensus for the Q2 results on Wed 07-22 (call 1:30 PM PT / 4:30 PM ET) stands at roughly $2.87 EPS, +23.8% year over year.[7] A model launch delayed by a few weeks changes practically nothing about that number. So the price loss is not an earnings event — it's a narrative event: the market isn't pricing worse quarterly numbers, it's pricing the fear that Google is losing the AI race.

On its own, that would be a classic overreaction — and thus a buy case. Two things hold me back. First, this isn't the first day of this kind: on 06-22, GOOGL fell as much as 7.2% intraday and closed around −5%, wiping out an estimated $225B at the time.[17] Two roughly 5% drops within a month on AI-execution doubts isn't an outlier, it's a pattern — and a valuation compression around "Google is falling behind" doesn't get fixed by a single quarter. Second, a binary event is due today, and I can't handicap its outcome: if Google delivers today, the delay narrative dies; if the date slips again, it extends. Buying into a binary whose odds you don't know is betting, not investing.

Recommendation — Alphabet (GOOGL)

HOLD Verdict: Hold the position, don't add into today's binary. This is simultaneously the clearest BUY candidate in the whole brief — but with a precise, dated trigger, not today.

Why (in detail): The discrepancy is real: roughly $200B in market value for a delayed model date, while the earnings base (Search + Cloud) is untouched and consensus for 07-22 stands at +23.8% earnings growth. Buying into today's unresolved release binary is buying a coin flip, not a thesis. Oil: a one-month high — and the market has already mispriced this story twice

Brent rose to $84.68/barrel (+0.53%), WTI to $79.75 (+1.02%), both captured on 07-17 at 07:50 UTC and at a one-month high.[8][9] The driver is concrete and dated: the US began a new wave of strikes on Iranian military installations on Wednesday, 07-15 — stated goal, to curb Tehran's ability to strike commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.[10] After the US also reinstated the naval blockade of Iranian ports, the Revolutionary Guards threatened "to close all other export corridors that benefit the U.S. and its allies."[10] The prior day's futures show the force of it: September Brent $84.78 (+$1.48 / +1.78%), August WTI $79.76 (+$1.62 / +2.07%) — both at their highest levels since the ceasefire signing of 06-17.[10]

Strait of Hormuz: A waterway between Iran and Oman, only about 33 kilometers wide at its narrowest point — through which a large share of the world's seaborne oil must pass. Picture the single door of a packed gym: as long as it's open, nobody thinks about it. The moment someone merely threatens to stand in front of it, everyone in the room instantly starts negotiating differently. That's exactly this week's oil price — not a single barrel has to actually go missing for the price to react; the credible threat is enough.

But the real lesson of the day only becomes visible if you look back 15 days: on 07-02, Brent was still under $71 — on progress in talks toward a lasting end to the war.[18] Fifteen days later: $84.68. That's roughly +20% in two weeks, purely on headlines, without a single barrel actually going missing. Anyone reading "the trend is up" out of that has the lesson backwards. It reads: this transmission from headline to price runs just as fast in both directions — a negotiating breakthrough yanks the premium back out as abruptly as it came in. That's exactly what happened in early July. Which makes a one-month high after a +20% run the worst conceivable entry point: you'd be buying the maximum premium into a binary, un-handicappable event. The next and only date where supply can predictably counter it is the OPEC+ meeting on Sun 08-02.[11] Until then, oil trades purely on headlines.

Recommendation — Oil exposure (Brent/WTI)

HOLD Verdict: Hold a small core position as a tail hedge. Don't buy into the one-month high — and don't panic-sell on the next de-escalation headline either. The position has exactly one purpose: to insure against the scenario where Hormuz actually shuts down.

Why (in detail): Oil is the only position in the brief that benefits from an inflation impulse — that's the entire reason the core position exists: it insures exactly the scenario that damages every other idea in the brief (gold via real rates, Big Tech and crypto via rate expectations). But adding to it now would be the wrong timing. The price is currently carrying the maximum war premium: +20% in 15 days, from under $71 on 07-02 to $84.68 today, without a single barrel actually going missing. That premium is a bet on a binary event whose probability nobody can seriously quantify — and in early July, the market lost that same bet just as sharply in the opposite direction. Entering after a 20% run means paying peak-of-the-move prices for insurance. The core position, by contrast, earns its keep exactly now: it was already in place when it was cheap.

Timeframe: The only plannable date is the OPEC+ meeting on Sun 08-02 — the cartel's chance to counter the premium with supply. Until then, only headlines move the price (the escalation has been running since Wed 07-15). Holding horizon: weeks; reassess position size after 08-02, once supply policy is a factor again. Adding would only be worth considering if Brent falls back into the $70s despite ongoing escalation — that is, once insurance gets cheap again.

Gold: the war escalates — and gold falls

This is the week's most striking non-move. Gold sits at $3,996.85/oz (+0.52%) — captured 07-17, 07:50 UTC — below the $4,000 mark and on track for more than −3% on the week.[12] In the exact same week the US is bombing Iranian military installations and oil jumps to a one-month high. Gold is the textbook war hedge. And it isn't showing up.

The obvious explanation would be: the haven bid went into the dollar instead. It doesn't hold up under scrutiny. The Dollar Index (DXY) sits at 100.6930 and is falling −0.07% (captured 07-17, 07:50 UTC) — trading near a one-month low and itself heading for a weekly loss.[13] The driver isn't strength, it's softening: US consumer prices rose weaker than expected in June, producer prices even fell unexpectedly — prompting traders to trim their rate-hike bets. The odds of a July hike stand at 11% per CME FedWatch, versus 25% a week ago; by December, still 26 basis points of hikes are priced in, versus 44 last week.[13] The Middle East escalation does support the dollar as a haven — but only enough to soften the inflation-driven decline, not reverse it.

Which makes the picture for gold worse, not better. Gold is priced in dollars; a softening dollar is normally a tailwind — the same ounce gets cheaper in every other currency, and demand rises. Gold lost over 3% on the week despite having that tailwind and despite a war escalating. Two drivers that should both be working for gold, and the price falls anyway. Exactly one channel remains to explain that — and it's the same one that moved the dollar: cooling inflation. Because that is gold's second job besides the fear hedge: it insures against currency debasement. If inflation comes in weaker than expected, exactly that demand disappears. The inflation channel was stronger this week than the fear channel and the currency channel combined.

Real rates & gold: The real rate is what's left of your interest after inflation takes its cut — 4% interest at 3% inflation is really 1%. And that's the actual price gold pays: gold pays no interest. It just sits there. Picture two piggy banks — one grows on its own every year, the other stays exactly the same size. As long as the first barely grows, the second is a fair alternative; once it grows nicely, the silent piggy bank looks expensive. That's why gold is less a bet on fear than a bet that saving doesn't pay off.

And that's exactly where the dated headwind sits. The Fed held its range at 3.50–3.75% at its June meeting (06-17) and raised the year-end-2026 dot-plot median to 3.8% (from 3.4% previously).[19] That projection remains the operative one until further notice — the July meeting doesn't update it: the Fed calendar carries new projections only in March, June, September, and December, so the next dot plot doesn't arrive until 09-15–09-16.[19] Higher-for-longer means higher real rates — a structural drag on gold, independent of Hormuz. The fact that markets priced out hikes this week (26 instead of 44 basis points by December) only helps gold if the nominal-rate expectation falls faster than the inflation expectation. This week it was the other way around. The month stands at −5.06%, the year still at +19.27%.[12] That makes today the second day in a row gold fails to jump on war headlines. Twice is a signal, not proof. A third time, though, would no longer call the gold price into question — it would call the insurance thesis itself into question: insurance that doesn't pay out exactly when the damage is in the news isn't insurance.

Macro anchor: why all seven verdicts hang on July 29

Six sections, seven hold verdicts, one common denominator. The FOMC meets 07-28–07-29, the decision lands Wed 07-29 at 2:00 PM ET.[19] At its June meeting (06-17), the Fed held the range at 3.50–3.75% and raised the dot-plot median for the year-end-2026 rate from 3.4% to 3.8%; for July, the market is pricing a hold (hike odds 11%).[19]

And here I need to temper an expectation I set myself: 07-29 brings no new dot plot. The Fed publishes its projections at only four meetings a year — March, June, September, December — and July isn't one of them; the next dot plot doesn't appear until 09-15–09-16.[19] The June median of 3.8% remains the valid projection until then, whether one likes it or not. So only two things are tradeable on 07-29: the rate move (known) and the tone of the statement and press conference. That's soft material — but this week showed how much it moves markets: soft June inflation data alone pushed the December hike expectation from 44 to 26 basis points and sent the dollar to a one-month low, with no Fed meeting involved at all. All seven verdicts hang on this expectation: gold via real rates, crypto via the same expectation, expensive growth stocks via the discount rate on their future earnings. Higher-for-longer weighs on all three simultaneously — and the next hard number on it doesn't arrive until September. Oil is the only idea in the brief working in the opposite direction: it lives off the inflation impulse — and is therefore exactly the insurance against the scenario that hurts the other six. That's not a coincidental pick, it's the reason the small oil position exists.

07-29 is also not a pure macro day: Microsoft (FQ4, call 2:30 PM PT) and Meta (Q2, call 1:30 PM PT) report the same evening after the US close.[23] That puts the Fed decision and the hardest test of the AI-capex thesis on the same Wednesday. Before it come Alphabet and Tesla on 07-22, after it Apple and Amazon on 07-30 (expected, not IR-confirmed), and Nvidia's own report not until late August — five weeks in which the market's most expensive AI bet is moved exclusively by other companies' numbers.[23] Anyone wanting to know whether the interpretive frame for AI capex has truly flipped gets their answer on 07-29 — not today.

It's Friday, July seventeenth. Yesterday's relief rally rotated into the big, balance-sheet-strong names. Today that rotation broke — and the break has a very specific shape. If you want to learn something today, don't watch what went up. Watch what didn't.

The trigger is a number that's actually good news. Taiwan Semiconductor raised its capital-spending plan for this year — to sixty to sixty-four billion dollars, up from fifty-two to fifty-six billion. This company builds the chips Nvidia designs. When it raises its factory spending by roughly a fifth, it's saying: we're seeing so much demand that today's capacity isn't enough. The textbook reflex to that is to buy semiconductors.

The market did the opposite. The Nasdaq fell almost one and a half percent, and Nvidia lost two point four percent, closing at two hundred seven dollars. Picture a bakery that buys a second oven. That oven says two things at once — it's a demand signal, but it's also a cost bet, because it still has to earn itself back. Until yesterday, the market read these ovens as demand. Today it reads them as cost. This is the second session where good news failed to lift the chips — and I need to be precise here, because the softer version of that claim is the honest one. On Tuesday, Nvidia jumped a good four percent on confirmed chip shipments to China. But by Wednesday, none of that held — just a third of a percent of follow-through, nothing more. So one piece of good news now buys only a single day. I also have to correct my own forecast from yesterday: my bear case called for a weak outlook to knock the chips down. The outlook was strong, and they fell anyway.

Even so, I'm not selling the chips. Yesterday I told Daniel not to chase Apple's record high — telling him today to chase-sell Nvidia's red day would be the same mistake, just in reverse. Trimming happens on a green chip day, not a red one. And the control check sits in the same table: Apple rose almost two percent to another high, Microsoft climbed nearly one and a half. Capital isn't fleeing Big Tech. It's rotating out of semiconductors.

The biggest single loser was Alphabet: down four point four percent, closing at three hundred fifty-four dollars. That's roughly two hundred billion dollars in market value — for a delayed release date. Bloomberg reported that Google is behind schedule on its next flagship model. But that model isn't a meaningful revenue line. Alphabet's earnings come from Search and Cloud, and consensus for next Wednesday's results calls for roughly twenty-four percent profit growth. So this isn't an earnings story, it's a narrative story. That would be a buy case — if it weren't already the second time this has happened within a month, and if there weren't a binary event due today whose outcome I can't handicap: Google is targeting today for the release, unconfirmed. Buying into a coin flip isn't investing. Alphabet is the clearest buy candidate in the brief — but only once it delivers and the numbers on the twenty-second hold up.

Oil hit a one-month high: Brent near eighty-five dollars, after the US began a new wave of strikes on Iranian military installations on Wednesday. Iran's Revolutionary Guards are threatening to close every export corridor that benefits the US and its allies. But here's the lesson you only see if you look back fifteen days: on July second, Brent was still under seventy-one dollars, on hopes for a lasting peace deal. Twenty percent in two weeks — purely on headlines, without a single barrel actually going missing. That doesn't mean the trend is up. It means this premium moves just as fast in both directions — a peace breakthrough would rip it right back out. I'm holding the small hedge, but I'm not chasing a one-month high.

And now the week's most striking non-move. Gold is falling. In the very week the US is bombing and oil is spiking to a one-month high, gold sits under four thousand dollars and is headed for a weekly loss of more than three percent. Gold is the textbook war hedge, and it isn't showing up. The obvious explanation would be that the flight-to-safety money went into the dollar instead. It didn't: the dollar index is sitting just above one hundred and is itself falling, near a one-month low, and on track for its own weekly loss. The reason is that American inflation came in softer than expected in June, producer prices actually fell, and traders trimmed their bets on future rate hikes. That makes things worse for gold, not better: a falling dollar is normally a tailwind, and gold is losing over three percent anyway, in the middle of a war. Two tailwinds that should both help gold, and the price falls regardless. Only one explanation is left, and it's the same one that moved the dollar: cooling inflation. Because gold's second job, besides insuring against fear, is insuring against currency debasement — and if inflation cools faster than expected, that demand disappears. On top of that, the Fed held rates steady in June and actually raised its own year-end rate forecast. Higher-for-longer means the non-interest-bearing metal looks expensive next to a savings account that's actually growing. I'm holding gold as insurance, not adding — and I'm watching closely now: insurance that doesn't pay out exactly when the disaster is in the headlines eventually stops being insurance.

For comparison, the entire prior week had brought in about two hundred million, ending an eight-week losing streak — that was the good news from two days ago. One day erased more than double that entire week. My line from yesterday, that a green week isn't a trend, is no longer caution — it's just what the data says. Bitcoin is trading in the low sixty-thousands to mid-sixty-thousands, deliberately shown as a range because two sources disagree by nearly two percent today.

With Ethereum, I have to be even more honest. Yesterday it was the strongest buy case in the whole brief, because it was visibly pulling away from Bitcoin. Today, two sources don't just disagree on the price — they disagree on the direction: one reads plus three percent on the day, the other minus four. That decoupling simply can't be measured today, and you can't upgrade a call based on something you can't measure. So I'm not upgrading Ethereum — not because the thesis is wrong, but because the data doesn't support it today. I'm pausing it, not abandoning it. On the smaller tokens, I have to check my own counter-scenario from yesterday, or it's just decoration: if the AI tokens rise together on a day Bitcoin falls, carried by genuinely good AI news, my thesis would be wrong. And a dog-themed meme coin rose exactly as much as the AI basket. This is a broad rotation within crypto on a weak Bitcoin day, not an AI trade. No new entries.
