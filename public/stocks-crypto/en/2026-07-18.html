Semiconductors: a free model sends the chip index into a bear market

On Thursday, 07/16, the PHLX Semiconductor Sector Index — the 30 largest US-listed chip stocks — stood at 11,876.50 points, 19% below its 06/22 record high. The threshold for an official bear-market call was a closing level of 11,707.78 points. On Friday, 07/17, the index broke through it.

Bear market: An index is considered to be in a bear market once it closes 20% below its most recent peak. That's a pure definition, not a forecast — like the mark of "fever starts at 100.4°F." It doesn't say things will get worse; it says things have formally gotten serious. In practice it still matters, because some funds are required to automatically cut their equity exposure right at that mark.

Working the 20% threshold backward implies a level of roughly 14,635 points for the 06/22 record high — meaning the index has shed nearly 3,000 points in barely four weeks. (This back-calculation is our own derivation from the cited 11,707.78 threshold, not a directly quoted figure.)

The trigger is the real story. Chinese startup Moonshot AI unveiled Kimi K3 on Friday, an open AI model that, per the vendor, competes with Western top-tier models. What's hitting the chip market here isn't the technology — it's the math behind it.

Open model catches up → Frontier lead shrinks → Doubt about the return on AI investment → Chip-demand expectations fall

Why that's logical: Chip valuations across the board rest on the assumption that Big Tech will keep pouring hundreds of billions of dollars into data centers for years, because it buys them a defensible product. If a freely available model reaches the same performance class, the expensively bought product becomes a commodity — and the willingness to fund the next data center wavers. It's not demand for compute that's in question. It's pricing power that's in question.

And that's exactly what the ticker tape shows. Nvidia closed on 07/17 at $202.81 (−2.21%) or $203.31 (−2.0%) depending on the source — an ordinary bad day. Over the entire downturn since 06/22, Nvidia was, per a Bloomberg report, the best performer among the major semiconductor stocks, while Marvell, ARM, and Intel lost over 30%. These Bloomberg figures — like the reported $3.3 trillion in destroyed market cap and the 105% rally from the March low — could not be directly confirmed due to an HTTP 403 and are explicitly carried here as attribution only.

This dispersion is the actionable information of the day. A bear market in which the largest name stays nearly flat while the second tier loses a third of its value is not an AI sell-off — it's a repricing of the substitutable suppliers. Nvidia sits on the compute base layer that you have to buy no matter whose model wins. A Marvell or an ARM sells into a layer where alternatives exist. When pricing power falls across an industry, it hits those without any first.

Why (in detail): The bear market is real, but it is not uniform, and that unevenness is the whole thesis. The market isn't pricing "AI is over" — Nvidia would have fallen with everyone else if it were. It's pricing "the substitutable supplier's margin is over." Oil: $88 — and why the market isn't paying 120 despite a "closed" strait

Brent rose 4.58% to $88.09 per barrel on 07/17, WTI 4.46% to $82.47 — the highest level in about a month. The cited driver is the escalated confrontation between the US and Iran around the Strait of Hormuz.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and Oman through which a very large share of the world's seaborne oil must pass. Picture the single exit of a huge parking garage: as long as it's open, nobody notices how important it is. The moment someone blocks it, everything stops — and there's no second way out.

Here's the context that matters, because the headline is misleading. The Hormuz crisis is not a fresh July event. Shipping traffic has been largely blocked since 02/28/2026, when the US and Israel launched an air campaign against Iran. Iran already declared the strait "closed" on 03/04/2026 and threatened to attack any ship passing through. A brief ceasefire collapsed on 07/08; on 07/14 Iran attacked three tankers, and the closure declaration was repeated "until further notice."

And yet oil keeps flowing. The US Department of Energy reported that 8.5 million barrels passed through the strait on a single day in July — under military escort. Traffic has collapsed massively: 57 transits over an entire weekend versus roughly 130 ships per day before the first attacks in February. But collapsed is not the same as stopped.

Iran declares closure → Traffic collapses → US escort holds up residual flow → Premium, not shutdown: $88, not $120

That's the investment takeaway. The $88 oil price is not the price of a closed strait — it's the price of a throttled strait plus a risk premium. An actual, enforced blockade would be a different price regime, and the market, at $88, is saying fairly clearly it doesn't believe in one. That cuts both ways: the upside is enormous if the escort fails — and the downside is just as large, because a negotiated resolution would wipe out the entire premium within days. That's exactly what happened once already in July, when the ceasefire held before 07/08.

Gold: the war escalates — and gold loses the week

Gold traded at $4,016.95 per troy ounce, +1.03% versus the prior day on 07/17. A second provider read about $4,010.65 — a 0.16% gap, unremarkable. So the day was green. The week was not: gold was on pace to close the week down more than 3%. On a monthly view it's down 4.59%, on a yearly view still up 19.87%.

That seems counterintuitive. A war is escalating in the Middle East, oil jumps 4.58% — and the classic crisis metal loses ground on the week. The resolution lies in the transmission channel.

Real interest rate: The interest rate you earn minus inflation. If you get 5% interest and prices rise 3%, your real gain is 2%. Gold pays no interest at all — its real rate is always zero. When bond real rates rise, gold becomes less attractive by comparison, even though nothing about gold itself has changed. It's like a savings account with no interest: tolerable as long as nobody else is paying anything either; annoying the moment the account next door offers 3%.
War → Oil +4.58% → Inflation expectations rise → Fed cuts less → Real rate rises, gold falls

This war is different for gold than most. A normal geopolitical shock lifts gold because capital seeks safety. But this shock runs, of all things, through the oil price — that is, through inflation. And inflation is the one argument that forbids the Fed the rate cut gold has been waiting on for months. The flight-to-safety demand is there; it's just being outweighed from the rates side. The +1.03% day against a more than 3% weekly loss is exactly that tug-of-war in fast-forward.

The $4,000 mark carries almost purely psychological weight here — gold held just above it on Friday but also traded below it intraday. Anyone treating that number as a technical signal is over-reading a round figure.

ETF flow: A spot Bitcoin ETF is an exchange-traded product that has to buy real bitcoin for every share it sells. The "flow" is the net money that moves in on a given day. Think of it as the water level in a tank: an inflow means bitcoin was actually bought — buying pressure you can measure instead of guessing from a chart.

But put that number in the right context. These $368M sit immediately after ten consecutive outflow days that had pulled $2.73B out of the funds — a streak that was only broken on 07/03 by a single inflow day of $221M. So the three good days recover roughly 13% of the preceding outflow. That's stabilization, not a trend reversal. Semiconductors and Big Tech are long-duration growth names — their value sits in earnings far out in the future, and the higher the discount rate, the less those are worth today. Gold loses directly against rising real rates because it pays no interest. Bitcoin reacts to liquidity expectations. And oil runs in the opposite direction: a high oil price fuels inflation, and inflation blocks rate cuts — oil is thus the one position that hurts the other six when it rises. One feature of this meeting deserves attention: no new economic projections will be published. The Fed releases its rate and growth forecasts only at every other meeting; the July date is not one of them.

Like an anonymous office pool before the game: you don't see names, but the spread of guesses reveals the mood. Without this sheet, all that's left is what the chair says — and how he says it.

Without the data set, every phrase Powell uses carries the full weight. It's Saturday, July eighteenth. The stock markets are closed, but Friday broke through a level that's going to shape this week: the world's most important semiconductor index is now officially in a bear market. And the trigger wasn't a rate decision or a bad earnings report — it was a free AI model out of China.

Here's how it played out. The PHLX semiconductor index tracks the thirty largest US-listed chip stocks. On Thursday it was still nineteen percent below its record high from June twenty-second. On Friday it fell below eleven thousand seven hundred eight points — the threshold for an official bear market, twenty percent below the peak.

The catalyst was the launch of Kimi K three, an open model from the Chinese company Moonshot AI that, according to the maker, can go toe to toe with the top Western models. The technical details belong in the AI-dev brief. For the markets, only the math behind it matters: if a freely available model delivers the same performance as one that costs billions in data centers to build, then it isn't demand for computing power that's in doubt — it's the pricing power of whoever is selling that power.

And that's exactly what the ticker shows. Nvidia closed around two hundred three dollars, down about two percent — an ordinary bad day. Over the entire slide since late June, Nvidia was, according to a Bloomberg report, the steadiest of the big chip stocks, while Marvell, ARM, and Intel lost more than thirty percent. We couldn't independently confirm those Bloomberg figures, so we're explicitly flagging them as reported only. But the pattern is the real story: a bear market where the biggest name barely budges while the rest get taken apart isn't a sell-off of artificial intelligence. It's a repricing of the suppliers who can be swapped out.

The broader market also pulled back. The S and P five hundred lost a bit over one percent to seven thousand four hundred fifty-seven, the Nasdaq one point four percent, the Dow just under eight tenths of a percent. Within the big seven tech names, though, the day was very uneven: Apple rose slightly, Meta lost nearly three percent. That's a ranking of AI-investment dependency, read from the bottom up. Whoever has the least money in data centers held up best.

Second big story: oil. Brent rose four and a half percent to eighty-eight dollars and nine cents, US benchmark WTI to eighty-two dollars and forty-seven cents. A one-month high, triggered by the escalating standoff between the US and Iran over the Strait of Hormuz.

A correction matters here, because the headline is misleading. This crisis isn't new. Shipping through the strait has been largely blocked since late February, and Iran already declared the passage closed back on March fourth. A ceasefire collapsed in early July, and in mid-July tankers were attacked again. But — and this is the point — the strait isn't sealed. The US Department of Energy reported eight and a half million barrels passing through in a single day, under military escort. Traffic has collapsed, not stopped. That's exactly why oil costs eighty-eight dollars and not a hundred twenty. The market is paying for a throttle, not a shutdown.

Gold delivers the puzzle of the day. It rose about one percent on Friday to four thousand sixteen dollars an ounce — and still lost more than three percent on the week, despite an escalating war. The resolution lies in the transmission channel. This war is working through the oil price, oil is pushing up inflation expectations, and inflation is exactly the argument that keeps the central bank from delivering the rate cut gold has been waiting on for months. Gold pays no interest. When rates rise elsewhere, it loses, no matter how bad the news gets.
