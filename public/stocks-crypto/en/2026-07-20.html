Oil: $90, Live, and the Stock Market Doesn't Know It Yet

Brent is trading this morning at $90.28 a barrel, WTI at $83.54 — live as measured on Jul 20 at 04:55 and 04:57 UTC respectively. Against the comparison close from the same source (Brent $84.95, WTI $79.60), that's +6.28% and +4.95%. An independent, Bloomberg-backed summary describes a rise of "nearly 4%" for Jul 20 and calls it the highest Brent level since mid-June — so level and direction agree across two sources.

The cause is named and dated. The US and Iran have further escalated hostilities: shelling of ships trying to pass through the Strait of Hormuz, and an attack on a major oil facility in Kuwait over the weekend. This isn't a sentiment story — it's a supply event.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and Oman that a huge share of the world's seaborne oil has to pass through. Picture the single exit of a massive parking garage: as long as it's open, nobody thinks about it. The moment it's jammed, everything grinds to a halt — and there's no second way out.

The number that explains the price isn't the price. Al Jazeera counted just six ship passages through the strait between Thursday and Friday as of Jul 14 — versus 18 to 22 daily earlier in the month. That's a drop of more than 60%, and it's the real reason the price isn't coming back down. At the same time: traffic is throttled, not stopped. That's exactly why Brent is at $90 and not triple digits.

Attack on Kuwait facility → Passages 6 instead of 18–22/day → Real throttling, not an outage → Brent $90

A week earlier, on Jul 14, Brent (September contract) stood at $85.92 — already the highest level since Jun 15 at the time. The move from there to $90 is therefore not a spike but a steady climb. For equities, that creates an uncomfortable asymmetry: the oil market has already priced in the weekend, while US cash markets don't open until 15:30 CEST today.

Recommendation — Oil Exposure (Brent/WTI)

HOLD Verdict: Hold the existing small core position; don't buy into the $90 mark. Anyone already in profit trims a third of the position above $95. New building only below $84.

Why (detailed): A Brent price of $90.28 already contains a substantial war premium — a week ago it was $85.92, two weeks ago noticeably less. Buying today means buying the news after it's been printed twice. The structure is nonetheless asymmetric and justifies the existing position: on the downside, a real throttling to six instead of 18–22 passages a day limits the pullback, because this supply can't be replaced within days. On the upside, the range is large, but it requires an event that hasn't happened yet — an actual closure rather than a throttling. That's exactly what a small position is for: it pays off if the improbable happens and costs little if it doesn't. Going bigger would mean betting on an escalation with an unknown timing. The second channel matters too: oil at this level is a cost shock for logistics, manufacturing, and data-center operations — so this position wins precisely when the rest of the portfolio loses. That's its purpose, but it also limits how large it should sensibly be.

Timeframe: Two dated waypoints and one undated pacesetter. First, today's US open, Jul 20, 15:30 CEST / 10:30 BRT — this shows whether the stock market reads the oil price as a cost shock or ignores it. Second, the FOMC decision on Jul 29 (20:00 CEST / 15:00 BRT), because oil at $90 feeds directly into the Fed's inflation argument. The real pacesetter — the situation at Hormuz — has no scheduled date. No action before today's US open; reassess position size after Jul 29.

Forecast: Base case (~50%): Brent holds an $85–96 range through the FOMC on Jul 29 — the throttling provides support, the lack of a full closure caps it. Bull (~22%): passages keep falling below six a day, or another Gulf facility gets hit → Brent above $105. Bear (~28%): a negotiation signal, and the premium unwinds quickly → Brent back below $80 within two weeks. Counter-scenario: If Brent closes today, Jul 20, below $88, despite a facility in Kuwait having been hit over the weekend, then the overnight jump was a thin Asia-session effect rather than a supply repricing. In that case the war premium is more short-lived than assumed here, and the position should be trimmed to the smallest possible hedge size — not added to.

Gold: Oil Shock, War — and Still Down Half a Percent

Gold trades live on Jul 20 at 04:57 UTC at $4,023.70 per troy ounce (COMEX future), down 0.50% from the comparison close of $4,044.00. Two independent spot trackers show $4,001.06 and $4,008.07 for the same day — 0.4 to 0.5% below the future. That's the normal future-spot gap, not a contradiction.

The decline is the story. On a morning when oil jumps above $90 because facilities are burning in the Persian Gulf, gold falls. The reason is uncomfortable: gold doesn't react to war, it reacts to real rates. An oil shock lifts inflation expectations, which lowers the odds of rate cuts — and that hurts gold. The war gives gold a flight premium with one hand and takes it back through the rate channel with the other.

Real rate: The interest rate you earn, minus inflation. If your savings account pays 4% and prices rise 3%, your real gain is 1%. Gold pays no interest at all, so it pays off mainly when real rates are low or negative. When real rates rise, holding zero-yield gold gets relatively more expensive.

The numbers behind that, as of Jul 13: the 30-year US Treasury is pushing toward a 4.902% yield, and US inflation ran at 4.2% year-over-year in May 2026 — high enough that the market is thinking more about hikes than cuts. Against that headwind stands a structural buyer: China's central bank bought 14.93 tonnes in June — the 20th consecutive month of buying and the largest single month since 2023.

The context not to lose sight of: gold sits roughly 28% below the January 2026 peak of $5,595.47 (assessment from Jul 13, when it stood at about $4,038). That's not a sideways market — it's a full-blown correction that has been finding a floor around $4,000 for weeks. The $4,000 mark is therefore the line where central-bank demand and real-rate pressure cancel out.

Recommendation — Gold (XAU/USD)

HOLD Verdict: Hold — don't sell, don't add. Buy only into weakness, and only below $3,900. Anyone buying gold today as war insurance is buying the wrong insurance — in this setup, oil is the more effective instrument for that.

Why (detailed): This morning is a clean experiment: maximum geopolitical stress, Brent above $90 — and gold gives back 0.50%. That's empirical proof that the rate channel currently dominates the flight channel. As long as US inflation sits at 4.2% and the 30-year yield runs toward 4.9%, gold is missing its decisive driver: the prospect of falling real rates. Selling would still be wrong. The case for a gold allocation is protection against currency and systemic risk, not the monthly return — and central-bank demand, with 20 consecutive months of buying, isn't a flash in the pan but a structural shift that demonstrably doesn't care about real rates. Adding would also be wrong, because you'd be buying against the currently dominant channel. This is the situation where doing nothing is the active decision: the price has corrected 28%, but the catalyst for the rebound — a credible expectation of falling real rates — is further away today than before the oil spike.

Timeframe: One hard date and one level. The date is the FOMC decision on Wednesday, Jul 29 (20:00 CEST / 15:00 BRT, from the official Fed calendar): that's where the tone for the rate path gets set, and because $90 oil feeds directly into the inflation argument, the odds of a restrictive tone are higher today than a week ago. No action before Jul 29; the next full reassessment belongs on the FOMC meeting of Sep 15–16, since that's the next time projections get published.

Counter-scenario: If gold rises more than oil this week without a measurable change in rate expectations, then the flight channel is more dominant than assumed here — today's weakness would then have been an outlier, and the HOLD rationale collapses.

Read the table from bottom to top and you're reading the supply chain. At the very bottom sit TSMC and Broadcom — the companies that build the chips. Above them, Nvidia, which designs them. At the very top, Microsoft and Amazon, which buy them — and the only two that closed in the green. This isn't panic about AI, it's a repricing of who makes the money in the AI chain. If the market fears data-center budgets will be capped, the sellers of the equipment lose and the buyers gain — because their biggest cost item would shrink.

Capex (capital expenditure): The money a company spends on long-lived assets — for the tech giants, that means data centers and the chips inside them. Think of it like buying a delivery van instead of renting a taxi: it costs a lot upfront, then sits on the books for years, and if you miscalculated, the van sits idle. For Nvidia and TSMC, that exact budget is their revenue.

Recommendation — Alphabet (GOOGL) & Tesla (TSLA) Ahead of Jul 22

HOLD Verdict: Carry both positions unchanged through the report. Neither add nor hedge. No orders between today and Wednesday evening — especially no positioning ahead of a result whose consensus isn't even verified here.

Why (detailed): The core of the argument is information economics. In two days, audited quarterly numbers will exist for both companies. Any position change today is a bet that you know those numbers better than the market — and in Alphabet's case, an additional bet on a capex statement that doesn't exist anywhere in advance. The weekly decline of 2.91% is too small to build a value argument on and too large to ignore. That can be an opportunity — but you get the better price by buying into a disappointment after the report, not into the nervousness before it. Hedging is also wrong: hedging costs peak two days before earnings, precisely because everyone wants the same thing at the same time. So you'd pay top price for insurance that becomes worthless after 48 hours. The special case is Alphabet: anyone holding semiconductors already carries exposure to Alphabet's investment decision through that position — an additional purchase here unknowingly doubles the same bet.
