Big Tech: A Narrow Chip Rally Heading Into the Report

Tuesday looked strong on the surface: the Nasdaq Composite rose 1.29% to 25,837.21 (+329 points), the S&P 500 0.89% to 7,509.20, the Dow Jones 0.74% to 52,224.64. That the tech-heavy Nasdaq beat the broader Dow is the whole story right there: this wasn't a broad-based advance, it was a move in semiconductors. All prices in this section: Tuesday's close, Jul 21, 4:00 p.m. EDT.

Market capitalization: The total value of all of a company's shares — price times share count. Imagine you wanted to buy the entire company: that's what it would cost you on the stock market. At $5.02 trillion, Nvidia is the world's heaviest company, which is why a 2% Nvidia gain moves the whole index noticeably — unlike with a smaller stock.

The engine was Nvidia. The chipmaker rose 1.97% to $207.29 after announcing an investment in cloud provider Nebius — and as the index's heaviest stock, it pulled the entire semiconductor segment along. But the real story sits in the column next door: the very companies that buy Nvidia's chips fell.

That's not conviction, it's positioning: capturing the chip momentum while keeping the cloud names' earnings risk out of the portfolio.

Capex (capital expenditure): The money a company spends on long-lived assets — for the tech giants, that means data centers and the chips inside them. Think of it like buying a delivery van instead of renting a taxi: very expensive up front, then it sits on the books for years, and if you misjudged demand, the van sits idle. For Nvidia and Broadcom, that customer budget line is literally their revenue — which is why their stock hangs on Alphabet's capex statement tonight.

Today's irony: the chips rallied on the hope that capex budgets are holding — but the one company that can confirm or break that hope tonight fell. Anyone who bought Nvidia on Jul 21 bet on Alphabet's report without waiting for it.

Oil: Back Above $90 Overnight — the War Premium Locks Back In

Brent is trading this morning at $92.23 per barrel (+1.35% versus the prior day), WTI at $85.47 (+1.33%) — live as measured on Jul 22 from an independent spot tracker. Against yesterday morning's figure (Brent $88.49 on Jul 21, 04:57 UTC), that's a gain of roughly 4% in just over a day. The diplomatic easing that had pushed the price down to $88 on Monday/Tuesday has evaporated.

The reason is the Strait of Hormuz. The ceasefire from June 17 has been broken since Jul 8; on Jul 14, the Revolutionary Guard struck three merchant ships in the strait. A 10-day ceasefire proposal put forward by regional mediators is on the table, but sources describe "major differences." As long as the strait carries the risk, the premium stays in the price — and any hint of diplomacy only takes sentiment out, not the physical situation.

Strait of Hormuz: A narrow strait between Iran and Oman through which a large share of the world's seaborne oil must pass. Picture the single exit ramp of a huge parking garage: as long as it's open, nobody thinks twice about it. The moment it's blocked, everything grinds to a halt — and there's no second way out. That's why the mere threat of a closure is enough to move the oil price by double digits.

The scale that has supported the price for months: at the peak of the crisis, Brent crossed the $100 mark on Mar 8, 2026 for the first time in four years, touching roughly $126 at its high. Today's price of $92 sits well below that — so the market is pricing in a throttling, not a full closure. That's exactly the lever in both directions: if Hormuz escalates, the path to $100 is short; if the ceasefire holds, the premium unwinds fast.

Gold: Green With Oil Today — the Safe-Haven Channel Takes Over

Gold trades live on Jul 22 at $4,128.25 per troy ounce, up +1.24% versus the prior day; a second reference from the same page shows $4,126.31 (+1.19%) — a gap under 0.05%. Against yesterday morning's comparison figure ($4,053), that's a gain of roughly 1.9%.

What stands out is that gold is rising together with oil today. On the prior days, gold moved as a mirror image of oil — falling when oil jumped, and vice versa: back then the rate channel was leading (falling oil → less inflation fear → gold's premium receding). Today the safe-haven channel is leading: both are rising at once, because the same Hormuz escalation is driving the oil premium and safe-haven demand for gold simultaneously. The dollar index stands at 101.13 (nearly unchanged, −0.05%) and isn't propping up gold against the trend — so it really is geopolitics, not the currency.

Real interest rate: The interest rate you earn, minus inflation. If your savings account pays 4% and prices rise 3%, your true gain is 1%. Gold pays no interest at all — it pays off mainly when the real rate is low. In calm weeks, this rate channel drives the gold price; in an acute crisis, the safe-haven channel takes over, and gold rises despite high real rates simply because investors want safety. That's exactly the switch on display today.

The context is still a recovery from a low, though: after the prior weeks' correction, gold remains well below its highs. A single green day driven by a war escalation doesn't make a new uptrend — it just shows that the safe-haven channel still works when the situation calls for it.

Why this isn't a trend reversal. June 2026 brought roughly −$4.5 billion in net outflows — the worst month since these products have existed. Across all of 2026 through mid-July, the net stands at −$5.2 billion. Two inflow weeks totaling under $300 million don't fill that hole — they only stop the bleeding, for now. The direction has turned, the magnitude still isn't something to build a position on.

Spot ETF flows: A spot bitcoin ETF is an exchange-traded fund that holds real bitcoin; when someone buys shares, it forces the fund to buy more bitcoin. "Flows" are the net of purchases and sales — an inflow means fresh money is pushing into bitcoin, an outflow means it's pulling out. Picture the water level of a reservoir: two days of rain stop the level from dropping further, but they don't make up for the dry months before.

It's Wednesday, July twenty-second. This brief runs on two clocks today. Everything related to stocks reflects yesterday's close, Tuesday evening — US markets don't open again until this afternoon. Oil, gold, and crypto, on the other hand, are live from overnight. And today's real pacesetter isn't a price at all — it's a date: tonight.

Tuesday looked strong, but only half of it was. The Nasdaq climbed a little over one percent, led by Nvidia's near-two-percent gain after the chipmaker announced an investment in cloud provider Nebius. At over five trillion dollars, Nvidia is the heaviest company in the world, so it dragged the whole index up with it. But the rally was narrow: the very companies that buy Nvidia's chips — Alphabet, Microsoft, Amazon — fell. So the market bought the data centers' suppliers and sold their biggest customers.

Both storylines converge tonight. Alphabet and Tesla report after the US close, around ten p.m. our time. For Tesla, it's about its own numbers, and Tesla went in with a strong prior day, up two and a half percent — so the confidence is already priced in. For Alphabet, it's about something bigger: the company is one of the world's largest buyers of AI compute capacity, and what it says about its investment budgets moves Nvidia and Broadcom more than any analyst opinion. That's exactly what the chip rally was buying ahead of — even as Alphabet itself fell more than one percent.

That's why this brief's headline recommendation is: hold, straight through the report. Don't sell, don't hedge, don't add. Trading in the final hours before a result you don't yet know is the classic mistake. The chips have already risen, partly pricing in a good result — if Alphabet disappoints, the correction hits those ahead-of-themselves chips first. The first possible action comes tomorrow, once Intel reports and the reaction is in.

Oil turned around overnight. Brent is back above ninety dollars, at ninety-two twenty-three, up roughly four percent from yesterday morning. The reason is the Strait of Hormuz: June's ceasefire has been broken since July eighth, on July fourteenth Iran's Revolutionary Guard struck three merchant ships, and a proposed ten-day ceasefire still shows major differences. The war premium that briefly eased out of the price two days ago is locked back in.

Gold is in the green today — and what's notable is that it's rising right along with oil. On the prior days, gold moved as a mirror image of oil; today both are up a little over one percent. The reason is a switch in the underlying mechanism: in calm weeks the rate channel leads, but in an acute crisis the safe-haven channel takes over. The same Hormuz escalation is now driving both the oil premium and demand for safe-haven gold at once. The dollar, meanwhile, is nearly unchanged — so this really is geopolitics.

Bitcoin is trading around sixty-six thousand two hundred dollars, a good one percent above yesterday, but its seven-day momentum has flattened from four and a half percent to just over two. Ethereum only beats bitcoin on the one-month view now, up almost twelve percent — over seven days and over twenty-four hours, that lead has disappeared, and today bitcoin is even running slightly ahead. Ethereum's strength is real, but it's fading at the current edge.
