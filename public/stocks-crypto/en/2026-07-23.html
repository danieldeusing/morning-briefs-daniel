The Capex Shock: Alphabet Beats the Numbers and Loses 7%

Alphabet reported a quarter on Wednesday evening that looked strong on paper: $9.11 in earnings per share on $119.8bn in revenue, and the cloud unit grew 82% to $24.8bn. On Thursday the stock still fell to $317.69 — -7.13% from Wednesday's close of $342.09, roughly $300bn in market cap wiped out in a single day. The reason isn't in the earnings line — it's in the capex line.

CFO Anat Ashkenazi raised 2026 capex guidance on the analyst call to $195 to $205bn — up from $180 to $190bn, versus a market estimate of $186.4bn. The midpoint of the new range sits roughly $13.6bn above expectations. At the same time it became clear where the money is coming from: free cash flow turned to -$5.855bn, buybacks fell from $13.2bn in the year-ago quarter to zero, and interest expense rose 390% year over year.

Free cash flow: the money that's actually left in the till after all operating costs and after capital spending. Picture your checking account at the end of the month, after rent, groceries, and the new washing machine are paid: whatever's left over is free cash flow. Alphabet is earning handsomely — but the data centers are so expensive that nothing's left at month's end; instead there's a $5.9bn hole. That's why buybacks have stopped and why interest costs are rising: the washing machine is being bought on credit.
Capex $195–205bn → FCF -$5.9bn → Buybacks = 0 → Interest expense +390% → Stock -7.13%

And now for the real story of the day. The sell-off didn't hit "tech" — it hit a very precisely defined group:

Tesla: Record Deliveries at a 1.4% Margin

Tesla lost 14.52% to $319.69 — implying a prior-day close of roughly $374 and a loss of roughly $200bn in market cap in a single day (our own calculation from the closing price and the $1.20T market cap). The trigger is a single metric: operating margin fell to 1.4%, down from 4.1% in the year-ago quarter.

Operating margin: what's left from $100 of revenue after production, development and administration are paid — but before interest, taxes and one-off items come into it. At 1.4%, a whole $1.40 sticks from every $100 of car sales. A normal automaker runs at 5–10%, a good one higher. 1.4% means: the factory is running, but it's earning almost nothing.

What's remarkable is the contradiction with unit volume: Tesla delivered 480,126 vehicles for the quarter — a record and +25% year over year — on $28.24bn in revenue (+26%). Record volume alongside a collapsing margin sounds like one single explanation — that volume was bought with price. The numbers say something more precise, and it's two things.

First, price. Gross margin fell from 17.2% to 16.8% — the average vehicle price softened and revenue from regulatory credits shrank. That's the pricing effect, and it's real. But it's small: 0.4 percentage points.

Second, and far bigger, Tesla's own AI build-out. Tesla's capex jumped 142% to $5.79bn for the quarter (versus $2.39bn a year earlier), and opex rose 47% to $4.35bn — against only +26% revenue growth. The money is going into Robotaxi, the Optimus robot, in-house chip manufacturing and AI compute; for full-year 2026, Tesla now guides to capex above $25bn (in January it was still "above $20bn") and is arranging credit lines of up to $30bn to fund it. Free cash flow turned to -$1.09bn. Breaking down the 2.7 percentage points of margin loss, roughly 0.4 points come from gross margin and roughly 2.2 points from opex growing faster than revenue (our own calculation from the reported figures).

This is exactly the Alphabet story, just smaller. A company with a growing business is pushing so much money into AI build-out that margin and free cash flow flip negative — and the market sends the bill to the buyer of the compute. For Alphabet, this brief calls that a HOLD, because a capex-driven margin dip resolves differently than a demand problem: it ends when the investment either produces revenue or gets stopped — both are dated events. The same mechanism applies to Tesla. The difference that still splits the verdict is in the next paragraph and in the recommendation.

A second point on earnings quality. A 1.4% operating margin on $28.24bn in revenue works out to roughly $0.4bn in operating profit — yet the reported net income was $1.114bn (our own calculation from the two reported figures). So the company earned more outside the car business than inside it. That's not fraud and not unusual in the industry, but it means: this quarter's profit mostly didn't come from selling cars. On adjusted earnings of $0.33 per share and a price of $319.69, roughly annualizing the quarter gives a price-to-earnings ratio of around 240 — an illustration of how far this could fall, not a forecast.

SELL Verdict: Trim the position into strength, staged down to no more than half — preferably into a recovery, not into today's crash. Half the position, not a full exit, and that's deliberate: part of the problem is investment, not demand. No new money, and under no circumstances a dollar-cost-average buy at this level.

Why (in detail) — two drivers, not one: the margin loss from 4.1% to 1.4% has two sources, and they're unequal in size. (a) Price and mix: gross margin fell from 17.2% to 16.8% — a lower average price per vehicle, less revenue from regulatory credits. Record volume alongside softening price points to weak pricing power. But it only explains about 0.4 of the 2.7 lost percentage points. (b) Tesla's own AI build-out: opex rose 47% to $4.35bn against only 26% revenue growth, capex rose 142% to $5.79bn, and free cash flow turned to -$1.09bn. This is the bigger chunk — roughly 2.2 percentage points — and it funds Robotaxi, Optimus, chip manufacturing and AI compute, not the car business.

Why this changes the reading of the SELL: a capex-driven margin dip resolves differently than a demand or pricing-power problem. It's temporary by construction — it ends when the investment produces revenue or when it's stopped. That's exactly the argument that keeps Alphabet at HOLD in this brief, and the same logic has to apply here: anyone who reads 1.4% margin as automatically "structurally broken" is reading past their own numbers. Three differences still keep the verdict at Sell: first, Alphabet has an active revenue channel — +82% cloud growth — where the investment visibly shows up; Tesla's investment targets (Robotaxi, Optimus) have no meaningful revenue line today. Second, Alphabet beat both revenue and earnings, while Tesla missed on earnings; the reported net income of $1.114bn also exceeds the calculated operating profit of roughly $0.4bn by several times over, meaning the profit mostly comes from outside the core business. Third, valuation: annualized, the price-to-earnings ratio sits around 240 — the stock is already paying for the payoff of the investment before it's been demonstrated. On top of that there's duration: Tesla plans more than $25bn for 2026 and signals further rising capex for the years ahead, backed by credit lines of up to $30bn. The burden is temporary, then, but not short. So why no panic sell today? Because trimming on the day of a -14.5% crash locks in the worst price, and because the macro backdrop can, for once, work in Tesla's favor: a non-hawkish FOMC on 07/29 lowers financing costs for car buyers and hits exactly the lever that volume depends on. Oil: Brent Closes Above $100 — First Time Since May

Crude oil jumped sharply on Thursday, and unlike the morning reading, the evening level is confirmed: Brent closed at $100.69 per barrel, up 7%, and touched $102 intraday — the highest level since May. Several independent outlets carry this figure in agreement: Washington Post, NBC News, CNBC, Forbes and oilprice.com.

A note on diligence, because it costs something here for once. In the morning, two readings stood against each other: roughly $94/barrel (Vantage Markets, headline "Brent $94, WTI $89: Crude Oil Prices Hold Near 6-Week High") versus $100.15 with a daily gain of 6.46% (TradingEconomics aggregation) — a gap of roughly 6%. Both stay in here, because both genuinely appeared. But they're not worth the same. The $100.15 sits practically on the confirmed close; the $94 is irreconcilable with it and, with high likelihood, an early-session read captured before the move had played out. This brief therefore works with $100.69 — and says so, rather than hiding behind a range. WTI traded between $86.50 (open) and $89.01 intraday; a confirmed WTI close isn't available in this run.

The trigger is clear-cut. The Iran-backed Houthi militia from Yemen claimed attacks on two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea. In parallel, the interim understanding between the US and Iran is falling apart: Washington has resumed blockading Iranian ports, Tehran has stepped up attacks on ships near the Strait of Hormuz. Over the month, both grades have gained roughly a fifth, with Brent up as much as roughly 30% measured from its July lows.

War premium in the oil price: the markup buyers pay, not because less oil is flowing today, but because less oil might flow tomorrow. Imagine there's plenty of bread at the store, but everyone hears the bakery might go on strike tomorrow — the bread already gets more expensive today. That's exactly why a headline out of the Red Sea moves the price harder than an actual supply disruption: the market is paying for the possibility.

Why this matters for the rest of the brief: oil is the most direct channel through which geopolitics reaches the inflation rate — and the Fed meets on 07/29. A Brent close above $100 is the one argument in the meeting room against rate cuts — and as of this evening it's no longer a rough estimate, it's a number. So today, the pricing of gold, of crypto and of the debt-financed AI capex wave from the left column all hang on this single commodity.

Gold: Falls, Even Though Everything Today Favors Gold

Gold traded at $4,053 per ounce on 07/23 at 9:25am EDT — $64, or 1.55%, below the level at the same hour the prior day ($4,117). An independent cross-check gives $4,045.33 (-2.06%); the two are roughly 0.2% apart, essentially matching. Year over year, though, gold is still +18.61% (prior year: $3,417).

This is the day's interesting contradiction. Stocks fell, oil jumped, tankers were attacked in the Red Sea — that's the textbook environment for rising gold prices. Gold fell anyway. The reason is a channel switch: higher oil prices lift inflation expectations and, with them, rate expectations; higher rate expectations support bond yields — and gold pays no interest. In calm weeks, the safe-haven channel leads (fear buys gold); today the rates channel led (rate expectations sell gold). It's the exact mirror image of the prior day, when gold had risen together with oil.

Why rates weigh on the gold price: gold pays neither interest nor a dividend — it just sits there. A government bond, by contrast, pays something every year. The more the bond yields, the more expensive it becomes to hold gold instead: you're giving up that interest. Picture two piggy banks — one grows a few percent every year, the other stays the same weight. If the first one suddenly grows faster, the second becomes less attractive, without anything about it having changed at all.

The bigger picture. Gold hit its all-time high on January 28, 2026, with an intraday high between $5,589 and $5,608 depending on the source (the LBMA reference price that same day was $5,405). From today's level, that's a gap of roughly 25 to 28%, depending on which peak you use. So gold is simultaneously a strong year-over-year gainer and a deep pullback from its high — both are true, and anyone who knows only one of the two figures draws the wrong conclusion.
