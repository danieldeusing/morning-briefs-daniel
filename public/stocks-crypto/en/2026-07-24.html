AMD Wins Every Major AI Deal — and Falls 4% Anyway

AMD held its "Advancing AI 2026" event in San Francisco on July 22 and 23 and delivered what was practically the perfect pitch. At the center was EPYC "Venice" — according to AMD, the first x86 server processor in volume production on TSMC's 2-nanometer node — flanked by the Instinct MI450 series/MI455X and the Helios rack systems ($5-5.5 million per rack, shipping starting in the second half of 2026). The customer list is the real news: Anthropic is taking up to 2 gigawatts of MI455X in Helios racks, OpenAI and Meta together account for roughly 12 gigawatts of AMD accelerator capacity, and Microsoft Azure is confirmed as the anchor customer for Helios, with Oracle as an early adopter.

Gigawatt (as a chip measure): A unit of power consumption — used here as shorthand for how many AI chips a customer is buying. A large data center full of accelerators draws as much power as a small city. Picture someone ordering not "a thousand graphics cards" but straight-up "the power bill of an entire plant's worth of them." 2 GW is already enormous; 12 GW from OpenAI and Meta combined is an industrial-scale statement — orders that stretch over years, not a quarter.

And the stock? It fell roughly 4% to $529.48 in trading on 07/23 — even though it's up more than 100% year to date and was still trading around $553 during the event, roughly 5.4% below its June high of $584.73. It's a textbook case of the "sell the news" pattern.

Sell the News: A stock rises before an expected event because everyone is already pricing in the good news. When the event then happens — even exactly as good as hoped — the reason to keep holding is gone, and whoever bought beforehand takes profits. Like Christmas: anticipation peaks on the 23rd; by the 25th, even the best gift is "just" the gift. AMD had run up more than 100% heading into the event — the flood of orders was the expectation, not the surprise.

Two other forces added pressure. First, the AMD position was crowded after the stock's doubling — a lot of fast money had to come out. Second, and more important, the same fear that cost Alphabet 7% on Thursday hit AMD indirectly too: once the market starts questioning the return on AI's billions, that's a risk for the buyers of AMD's chips — precisely the hyperscalers whose commitments AMD just celebrated. Announcements of 12 gigawatts only become revenue once the racks are installed and paid for.

Sources: ir.amd.com (Advancing AI 2026, primary source) · gurufocus.com (-4% to $529.48, 07/23) · fxleaders.com ($553 price, YTD, June high)

The Capex Complex, Now With OpenAI's $750 Billion

Thursday was the day the market stopped reading AI investment as purely good news. Alphabet had beaten its numbers the evening before (revenue $119.8bn, +24%, cloud +82% to $24.8bn) and still lost roughly 7.4% — because in the same breath it raised its 2026 capex guidance to $195-205bn (from $180-190bn) and quarterly capex jumped 101% year over year to $44.9bn. On Thursday, OpenAI followed: according to the Wall Street Journal, it raised its planned infrastructure spending to around $750bn by 2030 (from $600bn previously) and broke ground on "Project Camellia" — $20bn for its first self-built and self-operated data center (Effingham County, Georgia) instead of leased capacity from Oracle or AWS.

Capex (capital expenditure): The money a company spends on long-lived assets — here: data centers, chips, power. It doesn't count as an operating cost; instead it goes onto the balance sheet and gets depreciated over years. In plain terms: it's the difference between a monthly power bill (operating cost) and buying your own power plant (capex). Right now the market is asking: does the power plant pay for itself?

Thursday's sell-off had a clear signature. The buyers of AI compute got hit; the seller was spared:

Oil: The War Premium Holds at $100

Brent crude jumped more than 7% to around $101 on Thursday — its highest level since May 22 — after Iran-backed Houthi militias claimed attacks on two Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea and tension around the Strait of Hormuz increased. On Friday morning, Brent sits at $100.28 (-0.40% versus the prior day): the premium is holding but not widening further. Over the month, Brent is now up 36.5%, and up 45.5% year over year — a price that's almost entirely driven by geopolitics, not demand.

War premium (or risk premium): The markup the oil price carries just because a supply disruption is threatened — not because one has actually happened. Like trip cancellation insurance: you pay for the risk of something happening, even if the trip ends up going ahead. If Hormuz stays open and oil keeps flowing, this premium evaporates as fast as it arrived.

On the data, honestly: For WTI, the US grade, the dated TradingEconomics source cites $91.63 (-0.61%), while a second aggregator lists $86.42 for the same day — roughly a 6% gap with no clear timestamp. This brief uses the dated figure as the reference value; the gap could reflect front-month versus spot pricing, or different trading venues, but it's too large to leave unmentioned.

Gold: Falling Even Though Everything Favors the Safe Haven

Gold is trading at $4,027.35 per ounce on Friday morning — down 0.55% from Thursday's close of $4,049.77. That's the interesting part: gold is falling on a day when stocks are dropping and oil is jumping above $100 — both classic reasons to favor the safe haven. The reason is the rates channel: higher oil fuels inflation fears, inflation fears keep the Fed higher for longer, and higher real rates are poison for a metal that pays no interest. Today, that channel is beating the flight-to-safety channel.

Real Rate: The interest rate after subtracting inflation — what your money actually gains in purchasing power. It matters for gold because gold itself pays no interest: when the real rate rises, a government bond becomes more attractive than a gold bar sitting in a vault. That's why gold can fall of all times when the Fed is seen as "higher for longer" — the bond suddenly offers more real return.
Sources: tradingeconomics.com (XAU/USD $4,027.35, 07/24)

Recommendation — Gold (XAU/USD)

HOLD Verdict: Hold the gold allocation as a strategic hedge; pullbacks below $4,000 are a buying opportunity for the stagflation case — but don't buy into the current rate fear.

Why (in detail): In this environment, gold is the hedge against the most uncomfortable scenario — stagflation, meaning high inflation (oil-driven) alongside weakening growth. That's exactly when stocks and bonds fail together, and gold carries. The current decline isn't a loss of confidence, it's the rates channel: as long as the market prices "Fed higher for longer," the rising real rate works against gold. That's a headwind, not a broken trend — and it reverses as soon as the Fed signals it's tolerating the oil-driven inflation rather than fighting it.

Timeframe: The dated pivot point is the FOMC decision on 29 July (2:00pm EDT / 3:00pm BRT) and the tone from Chair Kevin Warsh. A hawkish tone (actively fighting oil-driven inflation) pushes gold down further in the short term; a tolerant tone (protecting growth) is the starting signal for a move higher. No new tranche until then.

Forecast: Base case (~50%): gold trades between $3,950 and $4,080 until the FOMC, caught between oil-driven inflation (supportive) and rate fear (a drag). Bull (~30%): a tolerant FOMC or an escalation in the Red Sea → back above $4,120. Bear (~20%): a rate hike (CME roughly 36.5%) or a clearly hawkish FOMC and falling oil → below $3,900. Counter-scenario: If gold rises while real rates simultaneously rise sharply, that would signal the market doubting sovereign creditworthiness (a fiscal premium rather than rates logic) — in that case the hedge is worth more than assumed here and the allocation should be increased, not just held.

Prices as evidence: XAU/USD $4,027.35 (live reading 07/24, mid-morning BRT; -0.55%; a single snapshot — a later re-pull may differ slightly), prior-day close $4,049.77. Spot ETF Inflows: A spot ETF is an exchange-traded fund that holds actual Bitcoin and trades like a stock — the convenient route for large investors who don't want their own wallet. "Inflows" means more money is coming in than going out, so the fund has to buy more Bitcoin. It's the water-level gauge for institutional demand — when it rises seven days in a row, the big players are buying again.

It's Friday, July twenty-fourth. Thursday confirmed a rule hanging over this whole week: right now it's not bad news getting sold, it's expensive news.

The clearest example is AMD. At its "Advancing AI" event, the chipmaker delivered maybe the strongest order book in the industry's history: Anthropic is taking up to two gigawatts of chips, OpenAI and Meta together about twelve gigawatts, and Microsoft Azure is confirmed as the anchor customer. And yet the stock fell about four percent, to five hundred twenty-nine dollars. The reason is the classic "sell the news" pattern: the stock had already climbed more than one hundred percent before the event even started. The flood of orders was the expectation, not the surprise.

That same day, OpenAI raised its planned infrastructure spending, according to the Wall Street Journal, to around seven hundred fifty billion dollars by twenty thirty, up from six hundred billion. Combined with Alphabet's capex shock the day before, it's the same story, just bigger: artificial intelligence costs staggering sums, and the market is sending the bill to whoever pays it. That's why the Nasdaq fell two point one five percent on Thursday — and the hit landed on the buyers of computing power, Alphabet and Amazon, while the seller, Nvidia, came through almost unscathed.

The second blow came from oil. Brent crude jumped above one hundred dollars after Iran-backed militias attacked two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea. On Friday morning the price sits at one hundred dollars and twenty-eight cents — the war premium is holding, but it isn't widening further. Over the past month, Brent is up more than thirty-six percent, a price almost entirely driven by geopolitics.

Gold is trading around four thousand twenty-seven dollars and slipping slightly — and that's the interesting part. It's falling even though stocks are sinking and oil is spiking. The reason is the rates channel: pricier oil stokes inflation fears, and that keeps the Federal Reserve higher for longer. Higher real rates are poison for a metal that pays no interest of its own.

In crypto, the news is what didn't happen. On a day the Nasdaq lost more than two percent, Bitcoin barely budged, sitting around sixty-five thousand dollars, with Ethereum around one thousand eight hundred seventy-seven. For the umpteenth time, crypto is decoupling from tech stocks. There's a real turn underneath it too: Bitcoin ETFs logged seven straight days of inflows, and Ethereum's eight-week streak of outflows has ended. Even so, retail sentiment stays fearful — the Fear and Greed Index sits at twenty-eight.

Across the smaller crypto sectors, the day was broadly red but calm. Dogecoin fell hardest, down almost five percent, while NEAR was the lone gainer, up a bit more than two percent against the tide.

Today's verdict is deliberately monotonous: five holds, not a single buy into the weakness. AMD, the Big Tech complex, oil, gold and Bitcoin are all sitting on hold. The reason is written on next week's calendar.

Because the real week hasn't even started yet. On Wednesday, July twenty-ninth, the Federal Reserve decides on interest rates; according to CME FedWatch, the odds of a hold have slipped to just over sixty percent, while the bet on a rate hike has climbed to just over a third. That same evening, Microsoft and Meta report earnings; Thursday brings Apple and Amazon. Four more capex announcements in one week, on top of the Fed. Buying today's dip means buying ahead of all of that. Tomorrow should be quiet — the next hard date is the twenty-ninth.
